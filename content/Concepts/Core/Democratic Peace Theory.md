# Democratic Peace Theory

## Definition
The empirical observation and theoretical claim that liberal democracies rarely — if ever — go to war with one another. The most empirically robust finding in IR, but its causal mechanisms and implications remain hotly contested.

## Intellectual Origins
- **Immanuel Kant** — *Perpetual Peace* (1795): Three "definitive articles" for peace: (1) republican constitutions, (2) commercial interdependence, (3) cosmopolitan law and international federation
- **Michael Doyle** (1983, 1986) — Revived Kant's argument; showed no war between liberal states since 1787
- **Bruce Russett & John Oneal** — *Triangulating Peace* (2001): quantitative evidence linking democracy, trade, and IGO membership to peace
- **Jack Levy** — "The absence of war between democracies comes as close as anything we have to an empirical law in IR"

## Two Versions

### Monadic Democratic Peace
- Democracies are inherently more peaceful, regardless of regime type of opponent
- Weak empirical support — democracies fight non-democracies as often as autocracies do

### Dyadic Democratic Peace
- Democracies do not fight *each other*, but do fight non-democracies
- Strong empirical support — almost no exceptions (debates: [[World War I]]? Finland vs UK?)
- This is the version most IR scholars accept

## Causal Mechanisms
1. **Institutional constraints** — Democratic leaders face checks (elections, legislatures, courts); war is costly to re-election
2. **Normative/cultural** — Democracies externalise norms of peaceful conflict resolution; expect other democracies to do the same
3. **Audience costs** — Democratic publics punish leaders who back down in crises, making threats credible; but same mechanism also makes escalation less likely between democracies

## Critiques

### Definitional/Conceptual
- What counts as a democracy? Thresholds determined after the fact to protect the theory ([[Constructivism]] — democracy is socially constructed)
- Colonial wars by democracies (Britain, France, US) are excluded by definition ("they weren't democracies then" or "it wasn't war")
- Cold War interventions ([[Vietnam War]], [[Iraq War]], coups in Iran/Chile)

### Empirical
- **Cold War nuclear peace** — Democracies didn't fight because they faced a common existential threat, not because of democratic norms ([[Realism]] critique)
- Rare near-misses: [[Cuban Missile Crisis]], India-Pakistan (both were democracies at times); US-France quasi-conflicts
- Dangerous peace — democracies fight non-democracies more intensely

### Theoretical
- Reverse causality — Peace causes democracy, not the other way around ([[Liberalism]] respondents: both)
- Spurious — It is not democracy but capitalism, wealth, or common interests that produce peace
- [[Feminism]] — Democratic peace ignores who *within* democracies benefits from militarism; the "peace" may be patriarchal order, not true peace

## Case Studies
- [[Cold War]] — Strongest evidence: US and USSR never directly fought despite intense rivalry; within the democratic bloc, zero wars
- [[Ukraine War]] — Democracies cooperate militarily, framing the conflict as defending the liberal order. Challenge: Russia (authoritarian) attacked a democracy
- [[Cuba Missile Crisis]] — Two nuclear democracies (US, arguably allies) nearly went to war over missiles — but didn't

## Key Questions
- Does the democratic peace hold in the 21st century, or is it an artefact of Cold War bipolarity?
- Are autocracies learning to fight democracies asymmetrically (hybrid war, information warfare)?
- Does democratic backsliding (Hungary, Poland, US under Trump) make the peace fragile?
