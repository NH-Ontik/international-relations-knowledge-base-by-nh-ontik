# Structural Violence

## Definition
Structural violence is harm built into the normal functioning of social, political, and economic structures — as opposed to **direct violence** committed by an identifiable actor. The term was coined by Johan Galtung (1969), who argued that peace research must look beyond war and physical harm to the systems that systematically disadvantage certain groups.

## Galtung's Triad of Violence
Galtung distinguishes three types, each feeding the others:

1. **Direct violence** — physical, visible, event-based (killing, war, assault). Has an identifiable perpetrator.
2. **Structural violence** — invisible, process-based, systemic. Embedded in social structures (legal systems, economic distributions, political exclusions). No single perpetrator.
3. **Cultural violence** — the cultural assumptions (religion, ideology, language) that legitimise both direct and structural violence.

Structural violence is thus **avoidable harm** — it is not fate or natural, but produced by human decisions embedded in institutions and structures.

## Key Characteristics
- **Unequal life chances** — Some groups have systematically lower life expectancy, health outcomes, education, or income due to structural position, not individual choices
- **No identifiable perpetrator** — Structural violence is anonymous. This does not mean no one is responsible, but responsibility is diffused across institutions, policies, and historical legacies
- **Normalised** — Because it is built into everyday institutions, structural violence often goes unrecognised or is accepted as "just how things work"
- **Measurable** — Galtung argues structural violence can be quantified: the gap between potential life expectancy and actual life expectancy in a given population

## Examples
- **Global economic inequality** — The [[Bretton Woods Institutions]] debt regime that keeps poor countries in cycles of repayment ([[Marxism]], [[Postcolonialism]])
- **Health apartheid** — COVID-19 vaccine distribution: 80% of doses went to wealthy countries while African nations received under 2%
- **Racialised policing** — Systems of incarceration that disproportionately target minority populations
- **Land dispossession** — Colonial land laws that legally stripped indigenous peoples of territory ([[Sovereignty]])
- **Gender inequality** — Legal and institutional barriers to women's education, employment, and political participation

## Related Concepts
- [[Peace and Conflict Studies]] — Structural violence is a core concept in the positive peace framework
- [[Marxism]] — Structural violence strongly overlaps with class exploitation and capitalist reproduction
- [[Distributive Justice]] — Structural violence is injustice by another name
