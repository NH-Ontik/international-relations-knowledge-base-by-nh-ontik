# Security Community

## Definition
A group of states among which the expectation of violent conflict resolution has become unthinkable. War is no longer a plausible instrument of policy between members. Coined by **Karl Deutsch** (1957), the concept explains how states transcend the [[Security Dilemma]] and achieve dependable expectations of peaceful change.

## Two Types (Deutsch et al., 1957)
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Amalgamated** | States formally merge into a single political unit with centralised decision-making | United States, Switzerland (cantons → federal state) |
| **Pluralistic** | States remain sovereign but maintain dependable expectations of peaceful change | US-Canada, Nordic states, NATO core members |

The concept focuses mainly on **pluralistic security communities** — how sovereign states make war among themselves unthinkable.

## Key Scholars
- **Karl Deutsch** — Originator; studied North Atlantic and Nordic regions (1957)
- **Emanuel Adler & Michael Barnett** — Revived and developed the concept for post-Cold War IR (1998)
- **Thomas Risse** — Focus on communicative action, democratic community

## How Security Communities Form
Adler & Barnett (1998) propose three tiers:
1. **Preconditions**: technological change, shared identities, economic interdependence, social communication
2. **Process**: transactions, institutions, social learning, trust-building
3. **Outcome**: dependable expectations of peaceful change — war becomes literally unimaginable

## Key Factors
- **Shared identities and values** — Liberal democracies develop mutual trust
- **Economic interdependence** — War becomes too costly ([[Complex Interdependence]])
- **Institutionalised interaction** — Regular meetings, joint decision-making, dispute mechanisms
- **Social communication** — Dense networks at elite and societal levels

## Key Debates
- **Is the concept Euro-centric?** — Most examples are Western liberal states. Can non-democratic states form security communities?
- **Scope conditions** — Does a security community require democracy? ([[Democratic Peace Theory]])
- **Expansion dilemma** — Does widening the community weaken its internal trust? (NATO expansion debate)
- **Reversibility** — Can a security community break down? Brexit, US-Europe tensions under Trump tested assumptions
- **Causality** — Does interdependence cause peace, or does peace enable interdependence?

## Case Studies
- **Nordic Community** — Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, Sweden. No plans for war against each other since 1800s. Dense social and cultural ties
- **NATO** — Core members (US, UK, Canada, Western Europe) form a pluralistic security community. Article 5 assumes members see threats as common
- **US-Canada** — Longest undefended border. War is unthinkable
- **EU** — The most institutionalised security community. Internal war among members is inconceivable despite centuries of prior conflict
- **ASEAN** — Debated case: "ASEAN Way" emphasises non-interference and consultation, but military conflict between members (Thai-Cambodia border clashes) suggests incomplete security community
