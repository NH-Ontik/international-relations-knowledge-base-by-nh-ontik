# Orientalism

Edward Said's foundational postcolonial concept — the Western academic, artistic, and political production of knowledge about "the Orient" as a unified, exotic, and inferior counterpart to the West. Said (1978) argued this body of representation is not neutral description but a discourse that enables and legitimises Western domination of the Middle East, Asia, and North Africa. In IR, orientalism appears in the framing of non-Western states as irrational, authoritarian, or culturally incapable of democracy or modern statehood — a pattern that postcolonial critics argue underpins interventions, sanctions, and unequal treaties. Orientalism connects discourse analysis directly to international politics: how the West *knows* and *represents* the non-West shapes how it *acts* upon it. The [[US War on Terror]], with its framing of Islamic terrorism as an existential threat from a monolithic "Islamic world," is cited as a contemporary orientalist discourse.
