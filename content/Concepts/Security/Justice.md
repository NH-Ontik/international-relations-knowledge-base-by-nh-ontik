# Justice

## Definition
Justice in peace and conflict studies extends beyond legal punishment to encompass how societies address harm, distribute resources, and rebuild relationships after violence. It is generally divided into three dimensions: **distributive justice**, **restorative justice**, and **transitional justice**.

## Retributive vs Restorative Justice

| Retributive | Restorative |
|-------------|-------------|
| Crime = violation of law | Harm = violation of people and relationships |
| State prosecutes the offender | Community involves all affected parties |
| Primary aim: punishment proportional to wrong | Primary aim: repair harm, restore relationships |
| Offender pays a debt to society | Offender takes responsibility, makes amends |
| Justice = what the offender deserves | Justice = what the community needs to heal |

Restorative justice draws on indigenous practices worldwide — Maori (New Zealand), Ubuntu in Southern Africa, Navajo peacemaking — where reconciliation rather than punishment is the default.

## Transitional Justice
How societies emerging from large-scale violence deal with the past. Mechanisms include:

- **Criminal prosecutions** — [[International Criminal Court]], hybrid tribunals, domestic courts. Accountable perpetrators may reduce the cycle of violence.
- **Truth commissions** — South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), Peru, Kenya. Focus on documenting truth rather than punishment.
- **Reparations** — Material compensation for victims, official apologies, memorialisation
- **Institutional reform** — Rebuilding judicial systems, security sector reform, constitutional change
- **Lustration/vetting** — Removing perpetrators from public office

**Core tensions:**
- **Peace vs Justice** — Can amnesties be justified for the sake of ending war? (South Africa's TRC traded prosecutions for testimony)
- **Universal vs Local** — Should justice be imposed from international standards, or rooted in local cultural practices?
- **Retributive vs Restorative** — Is justice done when perpetrators are punished, or when communities are reconciled?

## Justice and International Law
The [[ICC]] (2002) symbolises the international commitment to ending impunity for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity. However, the ICC has been critiqued as:
- A Western court prosecuting only African leaders (see [[Postcolonialism]])
- Lacking jurisdiction over great powers (US, Russia, China)
- Ineffective without enforcement mechanisms (the ICC has no police force)

## Key Thinkers
- **John Rawls** (*Theory of Justice*, 1971) — Justice as fairness: the distribution of goods should benefit the worst-off
- **Amartya Sen** (*The Idea of Justice*, 2009) — Contrasts transcendental institutionalism (perfect justice) with realisation-focused comparison (reducing manifest injustice)
- **Howard Zehr** — Restorative justice pioneer
- **Martha Minow** — Transitional justice: between vengeance and forgiveness

## Case Studies
- [[Rwanda Genocide]] — Gacaca courts attempted community-based justice for genocide perpetrators
