# Sovereignty

## Definition
The principle that states have supreme authority within their own territory and are not subject to any higher external authority. The foundational principle of the modern international system.

## Key Pillars (Westphalian Sovereignty)
1. **Internal sovereignty** — Supreme authority within a territory
2. **External sovereignty** — Independence from external authority
3. **Territorial integrity** — Borders are inviolable
4. **Non-intervention** — No state may intervene in another's internal affairs
5. **Legal equality** — All states have equal legal status, regardless of power
6. **Reciprocity** — Mutual recognition among states

## Historical Origins
- **Treaty of Westphalia (1648)** — End of 30 Years' War; conventional origin (disputed by [[Postcolonialism]] — Westphalia is Eurocentric)
- **Peace of Westphalia** established principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* → evolved into non-intervention norms
- Postcolonial critique: [[Postcolonialism]] — Westphalian sovereignty ignores the colonial world where sovereignty was systematically denied (Africa, Asia, Americas)

## Key Tensions / Debates
- **Sovereignty vs Human Rights** — R2P ([[Responsibility to Protect]]): sovereignty is not absolute; states have responsibility to protect populations — if they fail, international community may intervene
- **Sovereignty vs Globalisation** — States increasingly constrained by global markets, international law, institutions
- **Sovereignty vs Intervention** — Humanitarian intervention, peacekeeping, drone strikes all challenge sovereignty
- **Pooled sovereignty** — EU member states share sovereignty voluntarily
- **Empirical vs Juridical sovereignty** — Many states are legally sovereign but lack empirical capacity (failed states, weak states)

## Theories on Sovereignty
- **Realist**: Sovereignty is real and absolute — non-intervention is iron rule
- **Liberal**: Sovereignty is conditional — norms of human rights constrain it
- **Constructivist**: Sovereignty is a social construct — its meaning changes over time
- **Postcolonial**: Sovereignty was never extended to colonised peoples; contemporary sovereignty masks continued inequality
- **English School**: Sovereignty is the constitutive principle of international society

## Case Studies
- [[Kosovo (1999)]] — NATO intervention without UN authorisation challenged sovereignty
- [[Libya (2011)]] — UN-authorised intervention under R2P doctrine
- [[Ukraine (2022--present)]] — Sovereignty violated by Russia; debate over intervention
- [[EU]] — States pool sovereignty in supranational institutions
- [[Somalia / South Sudan]] — Empirically failed states, juridically sovereign
