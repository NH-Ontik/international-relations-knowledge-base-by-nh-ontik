# Transitional Justice

Transitional justice refers to the set of judicial and non-judicial measures used to address large-scale human rights violations after periods of conflict or authoritarian rule. It grapples with the fundamental tension between peace and accountability.

## Four Pillars

1. **Criminal prosecutions** — holding perpetrators individually accountable. Examples: the International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY), the International Criminal Tribunal for [[Rwanda]] (ICTR), and the permanent [[ICC]].
2. **Truth commissions** — official bodies investigating patterns of violence, not to punish but to establish an authoritative record. The most famous is South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) after [[Apartheid]].
3. **Reparations** — material or symbolic compensation to victims.
4. **Institutional reform** — purging human rights abusers from state institutions (e.g. security sector reform, lustration).

## Justice Models: Restorative vs Retributive

**Retributive justice** centres on punishment, trials, and proportional sentencing — the Nuremberg model. **Restorative justice** prioritises healing, dialogue, and community reintegration — the South African TRC model. Rwanda's *gacaca* courts blended both: community-based tribunals that sought truth and reconciliation while also imposing sentences.

## Critical Debates

Transitional justice is often criticised as victor's justice (ICTY), as too lenient (South Africa TRC), or as a neo-liberal template imposed from the North. Scholars in [[Peace and Conflict Studies]] ask whether it actually deters future atrocities or simply lets regimes buy stability.

## Related Concepts

Transitional justice sits at the intersection of [[Justice]], [[International Law]], and [[Peace and Conflict Studies]]. The experience of the [[Rwanda Genocide]] and the end of [[Apartheid]] shaped the field's core dilemmas.
