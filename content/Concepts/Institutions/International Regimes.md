# International Regimes

## Definition
"Sets of implicit or explicit principles, norms, rules, and decision-making procedures around which actors' expectations converge in a given area of international relations." — **Stephen Krasner** (1983). Regimes are issue-specific institutional arrangements that govern state behaviour in a particular domain, whether or not a formal organisation exists.

## Krasner's Four Components
| Component | Definition | Example (Trade) |
|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| **Principles** | Beliefs of fact, causation, and rectitude | Free trade increases welfare |
| **Norms** | Standards of behaviour in terms of rights and obligations | Non-discrimination, reciprocity |
| **Rules** | Specific prescriptions and proscriptions | Tariff binding commitments |
| **Decision-making procedures** | Prevailing practices for making and implementing collective choice | Voting, dispute settlement |

## Key Scholars
- **Stephen Krasner** — Edited *International Regimes* (1983), the foundational text; also developed structural realist critique
- **Robert Keohane** — *After Hegemony* (1984): regimes facilitate cooperation even after hegemony declines
- **Charles Kindleberger** — Hegemonic stability theory originator
- **Robert Gilpin** — War and Change in World Politics: hegemony as provider of stable regimes
- **Oran Young** — Typologies of regime formation (spontaneous, negotiated, imposed)

## Theoretical Perspectives: The Neo-Neo Debate
| Approach | View of Regimes | Key Scholar |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Neorealism** (Structural) | Regimes are epiphenomenal — reflect underlying power distribution. Largely irrelevant | Krasner, Waltz |
| **Neoliberal Institutionalism** | Regimes matter — they reduce transaction costs, provide information, enable reciprocity | Keohane, Nye |
| **Hegemonic Stability Theory** | Stable regimes require a hegemon to create and enforce them. Decline of hegemon → regime decay | Gilpin, Kindleberger |
| **Constructivism** | Regimes constitute interests and identities — not just constraints, but shaping actors | Kratochwil, Ruggie |

## Key Debates
- **Do regimes matter?** — Realists: regimes are "epiphenomena" of power. Institutionalists: regimes change state calculations independently
- **Regime change** — Under what conditions do regimes weaken, transform, or collapse?
- **Regime complexity** — Overlapping, nested regimes in the same issue area (e.g., trade and environment)
- **Enforcement** — Most regimes lack coercive enforcement; compliance is voluntary or reputational
- **Causality** — Do regimes cause cooperation, or does cooperation cause regimes?

## Hegemonic Stability Theory
- **Kindleberger (1973)**: The Great Depression resulted from British decline and US reluctance to lead. A hegemon is needed to provide international public goods (open markets, stable currency, lender of last resort)
- **Gilpin (1981)**: Hegemonic war occurs when rising powers challenge the regimes created by the dominant power
- **Critique** (Keohane): *After Hegemony* — cooperation can persist without a hegemon if regimes create sufficient institutional momentum
