# International Society

## Definition
The English School concept that states, while existing in a condition of anarchy, nevertheless form a **society** bound by common rules, norms, and institutions. Hedley Bull, in *The Anarchical Society* (1977), defines international society as existing "when a group of states, conscious of certain common interests and common values, form a society in the sense that they conceive themselves to be bound by a common set of rules in their relations with one another, and share in the working of common institutions."

## International System vs International Society
The English School distinguishes:
- **International system** — states simply interact, with enough contact to affect each other's calculations (e.g., Ancient Greece and Persia). A system exists when the behaviour of one state is a necessary element in the calculations of another (Bull).
- **International society** — states additionally share norms, rules, and institutions. They recognise common interests, accept binding rules of conduct, and participate in shared institutions.

Bull's test: an international society exists where there is a sense of belonging to a common enterprise.

## Pluralism vs Solidarism
A central English School debate:

| Pluralism | Solidarism |
|-----------|------------|
| States differ deeply in values and regimes | Humanity shares fundamental values |
| International society rests on minimum coexistence (sovereignty, non-intervention) | International society can enforce substantive norms (human rights, humanitarian intervention) |
| Order before justice | Justice is necessary for stable order |
| [[Sovereignty]] is the foundational institution | Human rights constrain sovereignty |
| Bull (pluralist moments) | Vincent, Linklater, Buzan |

**Pluralism** emphasises diversity: states have different religions, regimes, values — international society cannot impose one model. **Solidarism** argues that shared humanity creates a basis for collective enforcement of universal standards.

## Core Institutions of International Society
Bull identifies five institutions: [[Balance of Power]], [[International Law]], diplomacy, war, and the great-power management of international order. Later English School work adds [[Sovereignty]] and [[International Organisations]] as central institutions.

## Origins and Evolution
- **Grotián tradition** (Grotius, Vattel) — states in society bound by natural law
- **Via media** — the English School positions itself between realism (states in a system, not a society) and cosmopolitanism (world community beyond states)
- **Expansion thesis** — European international society expanded via colonialism to become global; debate over whether non-European states were genuinely admitted as equal members

## Key Critiques
- Is international society really European imperialism dressed up as law? ([[Postcolonialism]])
- Does the society actually bind powerful states, or just constrain the weak? ([[Realism]])
- Is the state-society distinction meaningful when transnational actors and global governance blur boundaries?
