# Just War Theory

Just War Theory is a tradition of ethical reasoning that seeks to regulate when and how states may resort to war and conduct themselves within it. It occupies a middle ground between [[Realism]]'s amorality and [[Pacifism]]'s absolute rejection of violence.

## Core Components

***Jus ad bellum*** — the justice of going to war. Requires: legitimate authority, just cause (self-defence or humanitarian intervention), right intention, last resort, reasonable prospect of success, and proportionality of ends.

***Jus in bello*** — conduct within war. Requires: distinction (combatants vs non-combatants), proportionality of means, and humane treatment of prisoners.

***Jus post bellum*** — justice after war. Encompasses peacebuilding, reconstruction, accountability for war crimes, and reconciliation.

## Key Thinkers

**Augustine of Hippo** first Christianised the just war framework, arguing war could express love of neighbour by restraining evil. **Thomas Aquinas** systematised *jus ad bellum* criteria in the *Summa Theologica*. **Michael Walzer** revived the tradition in modern form with *Just and Unjust Wars* (1977), emphasising non-combatant immunity and the moral equality of soldiers.

## Contemporary Debates

**Preemptive vs preventive war.** Preemption strikes against an imminent threat (e.g. Israel in 1967) is broadly permissible. Prevention against a distant or hypothetical threat (e.g. [[Iraq War]] 2003) is deeply contested.

**Non-combatant immunity.** Drone warfare and precision munitions blur the line between combatant and civilian. Critics argue signature strikes violate discrimination; defenders claim they reduce collateral damage.

**Humanitarian intervention.** The [[Kosovo War]] (1999) raised questions of whether force without UN Security Council authorisation can be just. The R2P (Responsibility to Protect) doctrine attempts to reconcile sovereignty with intervention.

## Relevance

Just War Theory remains the dominant ethical vocabulary for discussing military force in [[International Law]], [[Deterrence]] theory, and [[Strategic Studies]]. It informs debates on the [[Ukraine War]], the [[Kosovo War]], and the [[Iraq War]].
