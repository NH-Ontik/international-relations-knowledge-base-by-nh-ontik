# Quantum IR

## Core Premises
1. **Classical metaphors are inadequate** — The Newtonian/deterministic metaphors that dominate IR (billiard balls, balances, mechanisms) fail to capture the complexity and indeterminacy of social reality
2. **Quantum concepts provide better frameworks** — Superposition, entanglement, complementarity, and uncertainty offer more accurate ontological and epistemological tools for understanding international politics
3. **Complementarity over contradiction** — apparently contradictory theories (e.g., [[Realism]] vs [[Liberalism]]) may be complementary perspectives on the same reality, not mutually exclusive truths
4. **Entanglement of domains** — The domestic/international, material/ideational, agent/structure distinctions are not clean separations but entangled relationships
5. **Uncertainty as constitutive** — Uncertainty is not merely epistemic (we don't know) but ontological (reality itself is indeterminate); this has profound implications for decision-making under uncertainty

## Key Thinkers
- **Alexander Wendt** — *Quantum Mind and the Social World* (2015), argued that quantum mechanics provides a new ontology for social science; the mind is a quantum system and this shapes social interaction
- **Laura Zanotti** — Applied fuzzy-set social theory to IR, showing how social categories are not binary but graded and indeterminate
- **Jennifer Sterling-Folker** — "Competing Paradigms or Birds of a Feather?" (2000), argued that realism and liberalism are complementary rather than contradictory
- **Arash Heydarian Pashakhanlou** — "Quantum Approaches to International Relations" (2018), systematically reviewed the application of quantum concepts to IR theory
- **Miovic, P.** — "International Relations as Quantum Social Science" (2018), developed the epistemological foundations of quantum IR
- **Albert & Jahanbegloo** — Applied quantum thinking to non-Western IR theory and the problem of knowledge pluralism
- **Roland Bleiker** — "Quantum Theory and Hermeneutics" — explored the connections between quantum indeterminacy and interpretive methods in IR

## Applications to IR

### Complementarity of Realism and Liberalism
- Realism and liberalism may describe the same international reality from different perspectives — like wave-particle duality in physics
- States can be simultaneously self-help actors ([[Realism]]) and rule-following institutionalists ([[Liberalism]]; [[International Organizations]]); these are not contradictory but complementary descriptions

### Entanglement of Domestic and International
- Domestic politics and international pressures are not separate domains but entangled — each constitutes the other
- The inside/outside distinction that organizes IR theory is itself a classical assumption that quantum thinking dissolves

### Uncertainty as Constitutive
- Decision-making under uncertainty is not a failure of information but a fundamental condition of social life
- The nuclear "balance of terror" during the [[Cold War]] exemplified constitutive uncertainty — actors could never be certain of outcomes, and this uncertainty shaped the system itself

### Non-Linearity and Emergence
- Small actions can produce disproportionate effects; outcomes are not predictable from initial conditions
- [[Climate Change]] tipping points, financial crises, and revolutionary moments exemplify non-linear dynamics

## Strengths
- Challenges binary thinking — dissolves the false dichotomies (material/ideational, agent/structure, domestic/international) that constrain IR theory
- Captures ambiguity and complexity — social reality is genuinely indeterminate, and quantum frameworks acknowledge this rather than reducing it
- Bridges natural and social science — offers a new ontological vocabulary that connects physics and social theory
- Provides a framework for theory pluralism — multiple theories can coexist as complementary perspectives rather than competing paradigms
- Resonates with post-positivist IR — shares constructivist and critical theory insights about the limits of classical social science

## Critiques
- **Metaphorical overreach** — Quantum concepts developed for subatomic particles may not apply to social phenomena; the analogy may be misleading
- **Unfalsifiable** — Quantum IR makes few testable predictions; it is more a philosophical framework than an empirical theory
- **Impractical** — Policy-makers need clear guidance; quantum indeterminacy offers little practical decision support
- **Physicist imperialism** — Importing physics metaphors into social science may privilege one discipline over another
- **Wendt's quantum mind is controversial** — The claim that consciousness is quantum is debated even within physics; applying it to states is even more speculative
- **Lacks a research programme** — Unlike [[Realism]] or [[Liberalism]], quantum IR does not generate a clear programme of empirical research

## Related Concepts
- [[Critical Theory]] — Shares concern with the limits of positivist social science
- [[Practice Theory]] — Both challenge the separation of agent and structure
- [[Constructivism]] — Quantum IR extends constructivist insights about social construction with a new ontology
- [[Anarchy]] — Quantum thinking suggests anarchy is not a fixed structure but an indeterminate condition

## Opposing Theories
- [[Realism]] — Quantum IR rejects the deterministic, mechanistic assumptions of classical realism
- [[Neorealism]] — Structure is not fixed but indeterminate
- [[Rational Choice and Game Theory]] — Decision-making cannot be reduced to expected utility calculations when uncertainty is ontological
