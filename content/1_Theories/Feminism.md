# Feminism in International Relations

## Core Premises
1. **Gender is a structure of international politics** — IR cannot be understood without analysing gender relations
2. **Mainstream IR is masculinist** — Traditional IR theories reflect male experience and ignore women's
3. **Women are not absent; they are ignored** — Making women visible transforms understanding of war, peace, diplomacy, and economy
4. **Gender hierarchy is socially constructed** — Not natural; therefore changeable (overlap with [[Constructivism]])
5. **Intersectionality** — Gender interacts with class, race, sexuality, geography
6. **Emancipatory project** — Feminism seeks to liberate women, not just include them as variables

## Key Thinkers
- **Cynthia Enloe** — "Cheap labour is not cheap for the women who do it"
- **Ann Tickner** — *Gender in International Relations* (1992), critique of Morgenthau's realism as masculinist
- **J. Ann Tickner** — Feminist critique of security studies
- **Valerie Hudson** — *Sex and World Peace* — empirical links between violence against women and state behaviour
- **Carol Cohn** — Feminist analysis of nuclear discourse ("emasculation" of the Soviet Union)
- **Spike Peterson** — Global political economy, gender and globalisation
- **Jacqui True** — Transnational feminist politics, global gender equality governance

## Variants / Schools

### Liberal Feminism
- Focus on inclusion — women should be included in diplomacy, peacekeeping, leadership
- "Add women and stir" approach — does not challenge structures
- Critiqued by other feminisms for not transforming the system

### Critical / Postmodern Feminism
- Questions categories of "woman", "gender", "security"
- Deconstructs language of IR as gendered
- Overlaps [[Postcolonialism]], queer theory

### Postcolonial Feminism
- Western feminism is not universal; Third World women have different experiences
- Neocolonial — Western feminists sometimes speak FOR Global South women
- Overlaps [[Postcolonialism]]

### Socialist / Marxist Feminism
- Capitalism AND patriarchy together oppress women
- Overlaps [[Marxism]]

## Key Feminist Contributions to IR
1. **Security** — Women's security is often threatened by the same state that provides "national security"
2. **War** — Rape as a weapon of war; women disproportionately affected by displacement
3. **Peacekeeping** — UN Security Council Resolution 1325 on Women, Peace and Security
4. **Diplomacy** — Women under-represented in diplomacy; this shapes outcomes
5. **Global economy** — Export processing zones, care economy, domestic work — all gendered
6. **Nuclear weapons** — Masculinity and the "missile gap"

## Strengths
- Explains *who is missing* from IR and why that matters
- Empirically grounded — peace negotiations: fewer women = less durable peace
- Intersectional analysis captures complexity
- Connects personal (violence, household) to international (war, trade)

## Critiques
- **Empirical difficulty** — Not domestically in the mainstream
- **Not a theory of everything** — Does not explain all IR
- **Diversity within** — Feminists disagree with each other
- **Mainstream dismissal** — Often treated as "special interest" not "core IR"

- [[Postcolonialism]] — Shared critique of Eurocentrism, tension around universal "woman" concept
