# Liberalism

## Core Premises
1. **Optimistic view of human nature** — Cooperation is both possible and natural; progress in international relations is achievable
2. **Multiple actors** — States are not the only significant actors; IOs, NGOs, MNCs, and individuals matter
3. **Interdependence** — Economic, social, and institutional connections reduce the likelihood of conflict
4. **International institutions matter** — Institutions facilitate cooperation, reduce transaction costs, and provide information
5. **Domestic politics shapes foreign policy** — Democratic states behave differently ([[Democratic Peace Theory]])
6. **Absolute gains** — States care about their own gains, not just relative advantage

## Key Thinkers
- **Immanuel Kant** — *Perpetual Peace* (1795), outlines republican constitution + commercial interdependence + international law for peace
- **Woodrow Wilson** — League of Nations, self-determination
- **Michael Doyle** — Democratic peace theory revival
- **Robert Keohane** — *After Hegemony* (1984), neoliberal institutionalism; institutions facilitate cooperation after hegemony declines
- **Andrew Moravcsik** — Liberal theory of international relations, three variants: commercial, republican, ideational
- **Francis Fukuyama** — *The End of History and the Last Man* (1992), liberal democracy as endpoint of history
- **Joseph Nye** — [[Soft Power]], complex interdependence

## Variants / Schools

### Commercial Liberalism
- Trade and economic interdependence reduce war
- States benefit from cooperation and lose from conflict
- Cobden, Bright, contemporary globalisation theorists

### Republican Liberalism
- Democratic states are less likely to go to war with each other ([[Democratic Peace Theory]])
- Institutional constraints on executives; public accountability
- Doyle, Russett, Oneal

### Neoliberal Institutionalism
- Institutions enable cooperation by reducing transaction costs and information asymmetries
- Even under anarchy, cooperation is possible through regimes and institutions
- Keohane, Nye — [[Complex Interdependence]]

### Ideational Liberalism
- Ideas, values, and social purposes shape state preferences
- Liberal states have different preferences than illiberal states

## Strengths
- Explains cooperation that realism misses (EU, WTO, climate agreements)
- Accounts for the decline of great-power war since 1945
- Democratic peace has strong empirical support (very few wars between democracies)
- Captures the role of non-state actors and global civil society
- Explains systemic change (end of Cold War, decolonisation)

## Critiques
- **Empirically weak** — WWI disproved early optimism; wars still happen; democratic peace may be spurious (shared interests, not democracy per se)
- **Ethnocentric** — Assumes Western/liberal model is universal; ignores non-Western traditions
- **Institutions are weak** — IOs have limited independent power, dependent on states (realist critique)
- **Naive about power** — Underestimates conflict, dark side of interdependence (they depend, you coerce)
- [[Marxism]] critique — Liberalism is ideology of capitalist class; interdependence = exploitation
- [[Postcolonialism]] critique — Liberalism ignores colonial origins of international order
