# Assemblage Theory and Actor-Network Theory

## Core Premises
1. **Heterogeneous assemblages constitute international politics** — International politics is produced by networks of human and non-human actors; no a priori distinction between the social and the natural, the human and the technological
2. **Agency is distributed** — Power is not located in states or individuals but emerges from relations within actor-networks; agency is relational and emergent
3. **No a priori social structure** — Society is not a pre-existing container but is assembled and reassembled through ongoing processes of translation and negotiation
4. **Flat ontology** — All entities (humans, technologies, institutions, ecosystems) are ontologically equal; none is privileged a priori as the unit of analysis
5. **Heterogeneity and complexity** — Social phenomena are always constituted by diverse, heterogeneous elements that cannot be reduced to a single principle

## Key Thinkers
- **Bruno Latour** — *Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory* (2005), foundational text of ANT; "follow the actors themselves" and trace the networks they assemble
- **Manuel DeLanda** — *A New Philosophy of Society: Assemblage Theory and Social Complexity* (2006), applied Deleuze's concept of assemblage to social theory; emergent properties of assemblages
- **Michel Callon** — "Sociology of Translation" (1986), developed the concept of translation as the process by which actor-networks are assembled and stabilized
- **Andrew Barry** — *Political Machines: Governing a Technological Society* (2001), applied ANT to political institutions and technology governance
- **Sandra Braman** — *Information Policy and Power* (2006), applied assemblage thinking to global information governance
- **Benjamin Mueller** — *Networks and States: The Global Politics of Internet Governance* (2010), applied actor-network theory to internet governance
- **Bruno Latour** — *Politics of Nature: How to Bring the Sciences into Democracy* (2004), argued for a "parliament of things" in which non-human actors have political representation

## Applications to IR

### Global Governance of Technology
- AI governance is not a matter of states regulating technology but of assembling heterogeneous networks of algorithms, institutions, users, and regulators
- Internet governance (ICANN, IETF) emerges from actor-networks, not state treaties

### Infrastructure Politics
- Ports, cables, satellites, and supply chains are actor-networks that constitute international politics; infrastructure is not the "background" but the substance of global order
- [[Belt and Road Initiative]] can be understood as a China-led assemblage of infrastructure, finance, and diplomacy

### Climate Change as Human-Nonhuman Assemblage
- [[Climate Change]] is not just a policy problem but an assemblage of atmospheric processes, scientific institutions, fossil fuel networks, and political movements
- The atmosphere itself is an actor in the network; climate science is a practice of assembling knowledge from heterogeneous sources

### Supply Chains as Actor-Networks
- Global supply chains are actor-networks of factories, logistics systems, labor, regulations, and financial instruments
- Disruptions (pandemics, wars) reveal the fragility of these assemblages

## Strengths
- Dissolves IR's anthropocentrism — non-human actors (technologies, ecosystems, infrastructures) are given analytical weight
- Captures materiality — attends to the physical, technical, and environmental dimensions of international politics that state-centric theories ignore
- Explains technological disruption — AI, cybersecurity, and digital governance require frameworks that take technology seriously as an agent
- Provides a micro-sociology of global governance — traces how specific networks assemble, stabilize, and dissolve
- Challenges state-centrism without abandoning it — states are important actor-networks, but not the only ones

## Critiques
- **Relativism** — If all actors are ontologically equal, how do we distinguish between powerful and powerless? ANT struggles with hierarchy and inequality
- **No room for power asymmetries** — The "flat ontology" ignores the fact that some actors (states, corporations) have disproportionate power
- **Difficulty with normative analysis** — If everything is an assemblage, on what basis do we make normative judgments about justice, rights, or emancipation?
- **Empirical opacity** — "Following the actors" is methodologically demanding; ANT studies are often descriptive rather than explanatory
- **Not a theory** — ANT is often accused of being a methodology or sensibility, not a falsifiable theory with clear predictions
- **Neglects discourse and meaning** — ANT's materialism may understate the role of ideas, norms, and language in constituting international politics

## Related Concepts
- [[Critical Theory]] — Shares a concern with power and structure but assemblage theory rejects the humanist subject
- [[Global Governance]] — Assemblage theory reframes global governance as a network phenomenon, not a state-led project
- [[International Organizations]] — IOs are assemblages of people, documents, buildings, and technologies
- [[Constructivism]] — Both share a social constructionist ontology, but ANT is more materialist

## Opposing Theories
- [[Realism]] — States are not the primary unit; agency is distributed across networks
- [[Neorealism]] — Structure is not anarchy but heterogeneous assemblage
- [[Rational Choice and Game Theory]] — Actors are not individual agents making rational choices; agency is distributed and emergent
