# Marxism

## Core Premises
1. **Historical Materialism** — Economic relations (mode of production) underpin all social and political structures
2. **Class is the primary unit** — International relations are fundamentally about class struggle between capital and labour
3. **Capitalist World System** — The global economy is a capitalist system that exploits peripheral regions for core accumulation
4. **State as instrument of capital** — States serve capitalist interests, not national interests
5. **Imperialism** — Capitalism drives expansion, leading to imperial/colonial domination
6. **Capitalism breeds war and inequality** — Competition among capitalist states and exploitation of the periphery drive conflict
7. **Revolutionary change** — The system can and should be transformed

## Key Thinkers
- **Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels** — *The Communist Manifesto* (1848), *Capital* (1867)
- **V. I. Lenin** — *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism* (1917) — capitalism's need for new markets drives imperial expansion
- **Antonio Gramsci** — *Prison Notebooks* — [[Hegemony]] in international relations; ideas as well as force maintain capitalist order
- **Immanuel Wallerstein** — [[World-Systems Theory]] — core, semi-periphery, periphery
- **Robert W. Cox** — Neo-Gramscian IR; "theory is always for someone and for some purpose"
- **Stephen Gill** — Discipline in the global political economy, constitutionalism
- **André Gunder Frank** — *Development of Underdevelopment* — dependency theory

## Variants / Schools

### Orthodox / Leninism
- Imperialism as the monopoly stage of capitalism
- Economic determinist — economic base determines political superstructure

### World-Systems Theory (Wallerstein)
- Single capitalist world economy since 16th century
- Division: core (exploits), periphery (exploited), semi-periphery (intermediate)
- Nation-states are compartments within the world system

### Neo-Gramscianism (Cox, Gill)
- Hegemony = coercive domination + ideological consent
- Transnational capitalist class
- International organisations as mechanisms of global capitalist order

### Dependency Theory (Frank, Dos Santos)
- Peripheral development is constrained by core-periphery relations
- "Development of underdevelopment" — trade and investment maintain inequality

## Strengths
- Explains persistent global inequality (core-periphery patterns persist 500 years)
- Explains ideological power — why states accept a "global order" that serves the few
- Connects international to domestic political economy
- Powerful critique of "free trade" discourse

## Critiques
- **Economic determinism** — Overlooks non-economic factors (identity, religion, security)
- **Weak on post-Cold War** — Collapse of USSR weakened the empirical claim
- **Does not explain cooperation** — Why do states cooperate, why do institutions exist?
- **Class reductionism** — De-emphasises gender, race, ethnicity ([[Feminism]] [[Postcolonialism]])
- **Revolutionary bias** — Overstates crisis, understates capitalist adaptability

## Relationship to Other Critical Theories
- **Shares with [[Feminism]]**: focus on structural inequality, emancipation
- **Shares with [[Postcolonialism]]**: critique of Eurocentric world order
- **Differs from [[Constructivism]]**: Marxists are materialist, constructivists are idealist
