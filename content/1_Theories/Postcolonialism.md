# Postcolonialism

## Core Premises
1. **IR is Eurocentric** — The discipline of IR was built from European experience and presents it as universal
2. **Colonialism made the modern world** — The international system originated in colonialism and imperialism, not Westphalia alone
3. **The "international" is racialised** — Race and colonial hierarchies are central to how international relations work
4. **Coloniality persists** — Even after formal independence, colonial power structures remain (economic, political, discursive)
5. **Epistemic violence** — European knowledge systems have devalued and erased non-European thought
6. **Subaltern agency** — Colonised peoples have always resisted and shaped the international
7. **The nation-state form is itself a colonial imposition** — Borders drawn by colonisers persist and cause conflict

## Key Thinkers
- **Edward Said** — *Orientalism* (1978) — how the West constructs the "East" as inferior, exotic, other
- **Frantz Fanon** — *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961) — colonialism, violence, liberation
- **Mahmood Mamdani** — *Citizen and Subject* — colonial governance in Africa, "divide and rule"
- **Partha Chatterjee** — *The Nation and Its Fragments* — postcolonial nationalism
- **Homi K. Bhabha** — *The Location of Culture* — hybridity, mimicry, the "third space"
- **Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak** — "Can the Subaltern Speak?" — the subaltern cannot be heard
- **RBJ Gibson** — postcolonial IR, critique of mainstream discipline
- **Siba N'Zatioula Grovogui** — postcolonial IR, Africa in international thought

## Variants / Schools

### Postcolonial Theory (Cultural Turn)
- Focus on representation, discourse, knowledge production
- "The Orient" as a Western construction (Said)
- Resistance through art, writing, reclaiming history

### Decolonial Theory (Material Turn)
- Goes beyond discourse to material and economic structures
- "Coloniality of power" (Quijano) — race and labour are intertwined
- Modernity/coloniality as one side of the same coin
- Mignolo, Quijano, Lugones

### Subaltern Studies
- Focus on those who cannot speak within dominant frameworks
- Writing history from below, recovering resistance

## Key Concepts
- **Coloniality of power** — Race-based classification of the world population underpins capitalism
- **Orientalism** — Western production of knowledge about the East that enables domination
- **Epistemicide** — Destruction of indigenous knowledge systems
- **Hybridity** — Cultures are never pure; the coloniser and colonised influence each other
- **Mimicry** — Colonised societies imitate colonisers but imperfectly, creating ambiguity
- **Strategic essentialism** — Temporarily adopting essentialised identity for political goals
- **The state is artificial** — Borders drawn in Berlin 1884-85 (Scramble for Africa)

## Strengths
- Explains inequalities that Eurocentric IR theories cannot (persistent Global South disadvantage)
- Makes visible actors, histories, thinking that mainstream IR excludes
- Connects colonialism to contemporary international law, trade, institutions
- Empirically grounded — Scramble for Africa, Sykes-Picot, Berlin Conference are real

## Critiques
- **Risk of essentialism** — Treats "the West" and "the non-West" as monolithic
- **Pessimistic** — Focus on constraints on agency, persistent victimhood narrative
- **Difficulty operationalising** — How does one "do" postcolonial IR empirical research?
- **Contradictory relationship** — Many postcolonial states support structures created by colonialism (state system, international law)
- **Overstating uniqueness** — Post-colonial problems may have non-colonial causes too
