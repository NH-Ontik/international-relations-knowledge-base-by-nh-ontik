# Practice Theory

## Core Premises
1. **IR is constituted through practices** — International politics is not merely the result of ideas, interests, or structures, but is performed through repeated practices, routines, and embodied knowledge
2. **Agent-structure co-constitution** — Agents and structures are mutually constituted through practice; neither is ontologically prior
3. **Knowledge is performative** — Knowing international politics is not just cognitive but is enacted through doing — diplomacy, intelligence analysis, peacekeeping operations are all practices that produce knowledge
4. **Tacit knowledge matters** — Much of international political life operates below conscious deliberation; practitioners carry embodied, unspoken knowledge that shapes action
5. **Practices have histories** — They are not static but evolve through repetition, innovation, and contestation

## Key Thinkers
- **Emanuel Adler & Vincent Pouliot** — *International Practices* (2011), foundational volume defining practice theory for IR; practices are "competent performances" that constitute international life
- **Christian Bueger** — "Practice Theory and the Analysis of International Societies" (2014), systematically applied Bourdieu and Schatzki to IR
- **Pierre Bourdieu** — *Distinction* (1979), *The Logic of Practice* (1990), concepts of field, habitus, and capital; practice is the "art of the possible" within structured social spaces
- **Jutta Weldes** — *Constructing National Interests* (1999), showed how US national interests were constructed through bureaucratic practices during the Cuban Missile Crisis
- **Alexander Wendt** — *Quantum Mind and the Social World* (2015), extended practice theory to the micro-level of cognition and interaction
- **Theodore Schatzki** — Social ontology of practices as the fundamental units of social life; practices as organized human activity
- **Vincent Pouliot** — *International Security in Practice* (2010), applied practice theory to NATO expansion and diplomatic security practices

## Applications to IR

### Diplomacy as Practice
- Diplomacy is not just an institution but a set of practiced skills — negotiation, ceremony, protocol — that constitute international order
- Diplomatic practices (summits, handshakes, communiqués) are not symbolic add-ons but constitutive of international relations

### Intelligence as Practice
- Intelligence analysis is a practice of interpretation, pattern recognition, and judgment under uncertainty
- The "intelligence community" is constituted through shared analytical practices, not just organizational structure

### Peacekeeping as Practice
- [[Peacekeeping]] is constituted through on-the-ground practices: patrols, checkpoint operations, community engagement
- UN peacekeepers learn and perform peace through embodied routine, not just mandates from [[United Nations]] headquarters

### Sanctions Implementation as Practice
- Sanctions regimes are not just legal instruments but practiced bureaucracies — implementation, monitoring, and enforcement are skilled performances

## Strengths
- Bridges the agent-structure gap by treating both as constituted through practice rather than prior to it
- Captures the "doing" of IR — moves beyond ideas and interests to the embodied, material dimensions of international politics
- Accounts for tacit knowledge and practitioner expertise that formal theories ignore
- Connects micro-level interactions to macro-level outcomes through the concept of practices
- Provides a middle way between rational choice (too individualist) and structural theories (too deterministic)

## Critiques
- **Micro-level focus** — Difficult to theorize macro outcomes like systemic change, wars, or revolutions from the practice level
- **Bourdieu's concepts don't translate cleanly** — Field, habitus, and capital were developed for French society; applying them to the international level is analytically problematic
- **Difficult to operationalize** — Practices are often tacit and embodied, making them hard to observe and measure
- **Tendency toward conservatism** — Practices reproduce existing structures; practice theory struggles to explain innovation and radical change
- **Lacks clear methodology** — No established research programme; many practitioners rely on ethnographic or interpretive methods that are hard to replicate

## Related Concepts
- [[Constructivism]] — Practice theory shares constructivist ontology but adds a material, embodied dimension
- [[Agent-Structure Problem]] — Practice theory claims to dissolve the agent-structure problem through co-constitution
- [[International Organizations]] — IOs are constituted and reproduced through the practices of their members
- [[Critical Theory]] — Shares a concern with how power operates through everyday practices

## Opposing Theories
- [[Rational Choice and Game Theory]] — Practices are not reducible to strategic calculation; much is tacit and unreflective
- [[Neorealism]] — Structure does not determine behaviour; practices mediate between structure and action
- [[Liberalism]] — Institutions are not just rules but performed through practice
