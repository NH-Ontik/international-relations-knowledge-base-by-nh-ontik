# Disability Studies in IR

## Core Premises
1. **Disability is a political and international category** — Disability is not merely a medical or individual condition but is produced by political, social, and international structures
2. **The "normal" body is a political construct** — Sovereignty, citizenship, humanitarianism, and development are all organized around an implicit norm of the able-bodied subject
3. **Ableism structures international institutions** — International law, development policy, and humanitarian practices reproduce ableist assumptions about whose body counts and what constitutes a "good" life
4. **Disability intersects with other axes of oppression** — Disability is shaped by race, gender, class, colonialism, and geography; disability justice requires intersectional analysis
5. **Disabled people are agents, not objects** — Disabled people are political subjects with rights, not passive recipients of charity or development "beneficiaries"

## Key Thinkers
- **David Reeve** — *War, Disability and Rehabilitation in 20th-Century Britain* (2014), showed how war and disability are historically linked through rehabilitation and citizenship
- **Jane Darcy** — *Disability and International Development* (2009), applied disability studies to international development policy
- **Nirmala Erevelles** — *Disability and Difference: Global Studies in Education* (2011), connected disability to postcolonialism, race, and education
- **Kristen Fritsch** — "Queering Anti-War Politics" (2019), linked disability, queerness, and anti-militarism
- **Sara Ahmed** — *Living a Feminist Life* (2017), "feminist killjoys" and the politics of the "happy disabled person"; disability as a figure of discomfort
- **Schalk, S.** — *Bodyminds Reimagined: (Dis)ability, Race, and Gender in Black Women's Speculative Fiction* (2018), theorized disability justice in intersectional terms
- **Aimi Hamraie** — *Building Access: Universal Design and the Politics of Disability in Early America* (2017), traced how built environments produce disability

## Applications to International Politics

### The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)
- The CRPD (2006) is a landmark international norm that redefined disability from a medical to a human rights model
- Represents a norm cascade in disability rights; intersects with [[International Law]] and [[Responsibility to Protect]]
- States ratify the CRPD but implementation varies; the gap between ratification and practice reveals ableist structures

### Disability in Conflict Zones
- Armed conflict produces mass disability; disabled veterans and civilians are often excluded from peace processes and reconstruction
- The [[Humanitarian Intervention]] framework often fails to address disabled populations' specific needs

### Ableism in Humanitarian Aid
- Humanitarian organizations operate on assumptions about "normal" bodies and minds; camps, shelters, and aid distribution often exclude disabled people
- Development "beneficiaries" are implicitly imagined as able-bodied; disability is an afterthought in [[International Organizations]]' programming

### Pandemic Disability Impacts
- COVID-19 revealed how disabled people are disproportionately affected by public health crises and by triage decisions that devalue disabled lives
- The "underlying conditions" discourse during the pandemic reproduced ableist assumptions about whose lives are worth saving

### Development and the Ableist Subject
- International development is organized around the figure of the productive, self-sufficient, able-bodied subject
- Disability is framed as a "problem to be solved" rather than a form of human diversity; this reproduces colonial and ableist logics

## Strengths
- Intersectional analysis — connects disability to [[Feminism]], [[Postcolonialism]], and [[Critical Theory]]
- Challenges the universal subject of IR — the implicit "normal" body that organizes sovereignty, security, and humanitarianism is exposed as a political construct
- Connects to broader decolonizing IR — disability studies intersects with critiques of Eurocentrism and colonial modernity
- Empirically grounded — connects to real policy failures (humanitarian exclusion, development gaps, conflict impacts)
- Opens new questions about the body, embodiment, and materiality in IR

## Critiques
- **Marginal within IR** — Disability studies remains a very small subfield; it is not yet integrated into mainstream IR theory
- **Limited explanatory power for structural issues** — Disability studies is strong on micro-level exclusion but less developed on macro-level questions (war, hegemony, global capitalism)
- **Difficult to operationalize** — "Ableism" and "the normal body" are difficult to operationalize for empirical research
- **Tension between medical and social models** — The social model of disability (disability is produced by social barriers) may understate the reality of bodily impairment
- **Risk of fragmentation** — Adding disability to an already crowded field of critical approaches risks further fragmentation

## Related Concepts
- [[Feminism]] — Shared focus on the body, gender, and the politics of the "normal"
- [[Postcolonialism]] — Disability intersects with colonialism, race, and the production of the "other"
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — Humanitarianism's assumptions about the able body
- [[Responsibility to Protect]] — R2P's scope and its limits for disabled populations
- [[International Law]] — The CRPD and the development of disability rights law

## Opposing Theories
- [[Realism]] — Disability is invisible in state-centric security theory
- [[Neorealism]] — Structural analysis does not account for the body or embodiment
- [[Liberalism]] — Liberal rights theory may incorporate disability rights but reproduce ableist assumptions about the "normal" subject

- Critique: Is disability studies too marginal to IR to have significant explanatory power?
- Compare: The medical and social models of disability and their implications for IR
- Evaluate: What would a truly disability-inclusive IR theory look like?
