# Latin American Dependency Theory

## Core Premises

1. **Underdevelopment is not a stage but a product** — The global economic system structurally produces underdevelopment in the periphery while generating wealth in the core (Frank, 1966: 13).
2. **Development and underdevelopment are two sides of the same coin** — The prosperity of the "developed" world is causally linked to the exploitation of the "underdeveloped" world (Frank, 1966: 9).
3. **The international division of labour is exploitative** — Peripheral countries export raw materials; core countries export manufactures with high value-added (Prebisch, 1950: 7).
4. **Domestic elites are complicit** — The peripheral bourgeoisie acts as a comprador class, mediating between foreign capital and domestic labour (Dos Santos, 1970: 82).
5. **Dependency is not merely economic** — It encompasses political, cultural, and epistemic dimensions; the international system is not a market of equals (Cardoso, 1979: 21).

## Key Thinkers

### Raúl Prebisch (1901–1986)
- Chief economist at ECLAC (UN Economic Commission for Latin America)
- *The Economic Development of Latin America and Its Principal Problems* (1950) — Terms of trade thesis
- Argued that the prices of primary commodities decline relative to manufactured goods over time (Prebisch-Singer thesis)
- Advocated import substitution industrialisation (ISI) as a development strategy (Prebisch, 1950: 7)

### André Gunder Frank (1929–2005)
- *The Development of Underdevelopment* (1966) — The most influential dependency text
- "Metropolis-satellite" model: colonial powers structured the periphery to extract surplus
- Argued that underdeveloped countries are not "behind" but actively underdeveloped by the system (Frank, 1966: 13)

### Fernando Henrique Cardoso (1931–)
- *Dependencia y Desarrollo en América Latina* (1969) — Associated-dependent development
- Later revised his position: some dependent development is possible (Brazil's "miracle")
- As President of Brazil (1995–2003), implemented neoliberal reforms — irony of a dependency theorist becoming a pragmatic reformer (Cardoso & Faletto, 1979: 21)

### Theotonio Dos Santos (1936–2018)
- *Dependencia e Marxisão* (1970) — "New dependency" theory
- Distinguished between old colonial dependency and new technological-industrial dependency
- Argued that multinational corporations, not colonial states, now enforce dependency (Dos Santos, 1970: 82)

### Celso Furtado (1920–2004)
- *The Economic Formation of Modern Brazil* (1959) — Colonial economic structures persist
- *Economic Development of Latin America* — Critique of modernisation theory
- Argued that development requires structural transformation, not mere growth (Furtado, 1970: 12)

### Orlando Ferraris
- Dependency theory and its application to Southern Cone economics
- Contributed to the structural analysis of Argentine dependency

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Significance |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Core-periphery | Structural division between developed and underdeveloped countries | Explains global inequality as systemic, not developmental |
| Metropolis-satellite | Colonial centre-periphery relationship that persists after independence | Frank's model; dependency is reproduced, not overcome |
| Associated-dependent development | Dependent development is possible but structurally limited | Cardoso's refinement; avoids economic determinism |
| Uneven exchange | Peripheral exports are systematically undervalued relative to core exports | Prebisch-Singer thesis; terms of trade deteriorate over time |
| Import substitution industrialisation (ISI) | Replace imports with domestic manufactures | Policy prescription; implemented across Latin America in 1960s-70s |
| Comprador bourgeoisie | Domestic elites who serve foreign capital interests | Marxist-influenced; explains elite complicity in dependency |
| Resource nationalism | State control over natural resources as a tool of liberation | Nationalisation of copper, oil, minerals as resistance |

## Applications

### Latin American Debt Crisis (1980s)
- Structural adjustment programmes (SAPs) imposed by IMF/World Bank
- Dependency theorists argue SAPs deepened dependency: privatisation, austerity, export orientation served core interests (Stiglitz, 2002: 52)
- "Lost decade" — GDP per capita declined across Latin America

### Commodity Dependence
- Latin America remains a major exporter of raw materials: oil (Venezuela, Mexico), copper (Chile), soy (Brazil, Argentina)
- Price volatility in commodity markets destabilises peripheral economies
- The "resource curse" as a modern form of dependency

### Chinese Investment in Latin America
- New pattern of dependency: China as the new metropolis?
- Infrastructure-for-resources deals in Ecuador, Venezuela, Argentina
- Dependency theorists debate whether Chinese engagement replicates or disrupts core-periphery patterns (Gallagher, 2016: 38)

### Pink Tide (2000s–2010s)
- Chávez (Venezuela), Morales (Bolivia), Correa (Ecuador), Kirchner (Argentina)
- Nationalisation of resources, rejection of IMF conditionality, regional integration
- Dependency theory as the intellectual foundation of Pink Tide politics (Burgues, 2008: 45)

### Structural Adjustment Programmes (SAPs)
- IMF/World Bank conditions: privatisation, trade liberalisation, fiscal austerity
- Dependency interpretation: SAPs as instruments of core-periphery reproduction
- Led to the "Washington Consensus" critique and post-Washington Consensus reforms (Williamson, 1990)

## Strengths

- Explains structural inequality that modernisation theory cannot account for
- Links domestic and international politics through class analysis
- Provides a Global South perspective on the causes of underdevelopment
- Historically grounded: colonial extraction, trade patterns, and institutional legacies are real
- Policy influence: ISI, resource nationalisation, and regional integration were directly inspired by dependency thinking

## Critiques

- **Economic determinism** — Overemphasises economic structures; neglects political agency, culture, and ideas (Brenner, 1977: 76)
- **Underestimates agency** — Treats peripheral countries as passive victims; ignores the role of local elites and domestic politics
- **Romanticises nationalism** — Nationalisation and state-led development have often produced inefficiency and corruption
- **Failed policy prescriptions** — ISI led to inefficiency, debt, and inflation; many dependency-influenced economies eventually liberalised
- **Internal contradictions** — Cardoso's "dependent development" undermines the claim that dependency is total; East Asian developmental states defy the framework
- **Eurocentrism in reverse** — Dependency theory reproduces a binary (core vs periphery) that is as reductive as the modernisation models it critiques

## Related Concepts

- [[Marxism]] — Dependency theory draws heavily on Marxist class analysis and surplus extraction
- [[International Political Economy]] — Dependency theory is a foundational IPE framework from the Global South
- [[Global South]] — Dependency theory provides the intellectual foundations for Global South solidarity
- [[Postcolonialism]] — Shared critique of Western universalism; dependency theory is materialist where postcolonialism is often culturalist
- [[Development Studies]] — Dependency theory as a counter to modernisation theory (Rostow, Parsons)
- [[Neoliberalism]] — Dependency theory as the primary critique of neoliberal globalisation
