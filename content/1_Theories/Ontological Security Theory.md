# Ontological Security Theory

## Core Premises
1. **States seek ontological security** — Beyond physical survival, states require a stable sense of identity, narrative coherence, and routinized social existence
2. **Anxiety as a driver of state behaviour** — Existential anxiety about identity disruption motivates actions that appear "irrational" from a materialist perspective
3. **Routine and ritual maintenance** — States maintain ontological security through repeated practices, diplomatic rituals, and institutional routines that reinforce identity
4. **Narrative coherence** — States construct self-narratives (historical memory, national mythology, constitutional identity) that must be protected from disruption
5. **Identity before interests** — Who a state *is* precedes what it *wants*; ontological insecurity produces pathological behaviour at the international level

## Key Thinkers
- **Brent Steele** — *Ontological Security in International Relations* (2008), foundational text arguing that states seek self-identity continuity as a primary motivator of foreign policy
- **Jens Mitzen** — "Ontological Security in World Politics" (2006), showed how great powers pursue ontological security through institutionalized practices like the UN Security Council
- **Karin Kinnvall** — "Globalization and Religious Nationalism" (2004), linked ontological insecurity to the rise of religious fundamentalism in South Asia
- **Jeffrey Huysmans** — *The Politics of Insecurity* (2006), connected securitization to ontological anxiety about borderlessness and identity dissolution
- **Erving Goffman** — *Interaction Rituals* (1967), micro-sociological foundation: individuals maintain self-image through interaction rituals; states mirror this at the system level
- **Lene Hansen** — Applied ontological security to identity construction in foreign policy discourse

## How Ontological Security Theory Explains "Irrational" Behaviours

### Russia's Invasion of Ukraine
- Russia's self-narrative as a great power with spheres of influence was disrupted by Ukrainian westernization; the invasion serves identity-continuity with Soviet-era status rather than rational cost-benefit calculation
- The narrative of "protecting Russian speakers" functions as an ontological security strategy — maintaining the story of Russia as a civilizational protector

### China and Taiwan
- The unfinished Chinese civil war narrative constitutes a core element of PRC identity; abandoning the Taiwan claim would destabilize the legitimacy narrative of the CCP
- Taiwan's de facto independence is an ontological threat — it disrupts the "one China" narrative that underpins domestic political order

### Brexit
- British identity as an independent sovereign power, distinct from continental Europe, drove the Leave vote despite economic costs
- The desire to "take back control" reflects ontological anxiety about the erosion of national identity through European integration

## Strengths
- Explains behaviours that rational choice and [[Realism]] cannot — why states pursue costly policies that do not maximize material interests
- Bridges individual-level psychology and state-level behaviour through the concept of identity maintenance
- Accounts for emotional dimensions of statecraft — pride, shame, humiliation as drivers of foreign policy
- Connects to [[Constructivism]] while adding a psychological mechanism for identity persistence
- Explains path dependency — why states cling to costly alliances and identities long after material conditions change

## Critiques
- **Unfalsifiable** — Ontological security is difficult to test empirically; any behaviour can be attributed to identity maintenance
- **Operationalization problems** — How do we measure ontological insecurity? What counts as evidence?
- **Priority question** — Is ontological security prior to, or secondary to, physical security? States that lose physical security cannot maintain ontological security
- **Over-rationalizes the irrational** — May impose a rational framework (identity maintenance) on what is genuinely irrational or pathological
- **State-centrism** — Predominantly applied to states; less developed for non-state actors, transnational identities, or individuals

## Related Concepts
- [[Constructivism]] — Ontological security extends constructivist insights about identity with psychological mechanisms
- [[Security Dilemma]] — Ontological insecurity can exacerbate the security dilemma through identity anxiety
- [[Sovereignty]] — Sovereignty itself is an ontological security practice — maintaining the boundary between domestic and international
- [[Anarchy]] — Anarchy is not just a structural condition but an existential condition that generates ontological anxiety

## Opposing Theories
- [[Realism]] — Physical security is prior; ontological security is a luxury good
- [[Rational Choice and Game Theory]] — Actors do not always seek identity coherence; they maximise utility
- [[Neorealism]] — Structural位置 determines behaviour, not identity narratives
