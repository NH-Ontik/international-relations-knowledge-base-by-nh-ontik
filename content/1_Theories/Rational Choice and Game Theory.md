# Rational Choice and Game Theory

## Core Premises
1. **Methodological individualism** — All social phenomena, including international outcomes, must be explained by reference to individual actors making rational choices
2. **Utility maximization** — Actors are rational agents who order preferences transitively and select the option that maximizes expected utility given available information
3. **Strategic interaction** — Outcomes depend not only on one actor's choices but on the choices of others; actors anticipate and respond to each other's strategies
4. **Equilibrium concepts** — Stable outcomes emerge when no actor has an incentive to unilaterally deviate; the Nash equilibrium is the foundational solution concept
5. **Information and uncertainty** — Actors operate under varying degrees of information; incomplete and asymmetric information shape strategic behaviour and institutional design

## Key Thinkers
- **Thomas Schelling** — *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960), pioneered the application of game theory to conflict, deterrence, and brinkmanship; introduced the concept of credible commitment
- **Robert Axelrod** — *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984), demonstrated that cooperation can emerge in iterated prisoner's dilemmas through tit-for-tat strategies without central authority
- **Robert Keohane** — *After Hegemony* (1984), applied rational choice to international institutions; regimes reduce transaction costs and information asymmetries, enabling cooperation under anarchy
- **James Fearon** — "Rationalist Explanations for War" (1995), showed war can be a rational outcome of commitment problems, information asymmetries, and indivisibility of issues
- **Robert Powell** — *In the Shadow of Power* (1990), analysed the absolute gains problem: states care about relative as well as absolute gains, complicating cooperation
- **Kenneth Oye** — *Cooperation Under Anarchy* (1985), applied game-theoretic frameworks (prisoner's dilemma, coordination, assurance games) to international relations
- **Anne Sartori** — Applied rational choice to alliance formation and the logic of institutional design

## Variants / Schools

### Formal Modelling
- Uses mathematical models and game trees to derive testable predictions about state behaviour, bargaining, and institutional design
- Emphasises deductive logic and equilibrium analysis

### Mechanism Design
- Asks: what institutional arrangements can induce desired cooperative outcomes? The "reverse" of game theory — designing the rules of the game
- Applied to [[International Organizations]], treaty design, sanctions regimes

### Evolutionary Game Theory
- Actors do not need to be perfectly rational; cooperation can evolve through natural selection of strategies across populations
- Axelrod's tournaments demonstrated this; connects to institutional evolution

### Behavioral Critiques
- Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky) shows actors are loss-averse, not utility-maximising
- Bounded rationality (Simon) — actors satisfice rather than optimise
- Psychological biases (anchoring, availability, framing) distort strategic decision-making

## Application to Contemporary Issues
- **Deterrence as credible commitment** — Nuclear deterrence works because retaliation is a credible commitment; Schelling's "threat that leaves something to chance"
- **Alliance formation as coordination game** — NATO allies face a coordination problem: each benefits from collective defence, but free-riding is tempting ([[Balance of Power]])
- **Trade cooperation as iterated prisoner's dilemma** — The [[World Trade Organization]] reduces transaction costs and creates repeated interactions that sustain cooperation
- **Civil war as commitment problem** — Fearon's model explains why civil wars persist: warring factions cannot credibly commit to power-sharing agreements
- **Climate negotiations** — The [[Climate Change]] problem is a multiplayer prisoner's dilemma with collective action challenges

## Strengths
- Provides rigorous, falsifiable predictions about state behaviour
- Generates elegant parsimonious explanations for complex phenomena
- Bridges theory and policy — can be applied directly to institutional design
- Captures strategic互动 in ways that purely structural or normative theories cannot
- Compatible with empirical testing through formal hypotheses

## Critiques
- **From [[Constructivism]]** — Norms, identities, and culture are not reducible to strategic preferences; actors do not always calculate consequences
- **From [[Feminism]]** — Rationality is gendered; the rational actor model reflects masculine ideals of detachment and calculation
- **From [[Postcolonialism]]** — Rational choice assumes a Western liberal subject; non-Western actors may operate under different logics of action
- **Overly rational** — Real actors are cognitively limited, emotionally driven, and culturally shaped (behavioral critique)
- **Cannot explain change** — Preferences are exogenous; the model cannot explain how interests themselves transform
- **Strategic interaction is not all of politics** — Many IR outcomes emerge from habit, routine, and path dependence rather than strategic calculation

## Opposing Theories
- [[Constructivism]] — Rejects methodological individualism; preferences are socially constituted
- [[Marxism]] — Structural, not individual, explanations; class, not rational choice
- [[Postcolonialism]] — Challenges universal rationality assumptions
