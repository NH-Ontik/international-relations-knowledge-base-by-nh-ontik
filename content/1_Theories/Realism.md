# Realism

## Core Premises
1. **Statism** — The state is the primary unit of analysis and the principal actor in international politics.
2. **Anarchy** — The international system is anarchic (no overarching authority above states), forcing states to rely on themselves.
3. **Self-Help** — In an anarchic system, states must prioritise their own survival and security above all else.
4. **Power Politics** — International relations is fundamentally about the pursuit, maintenance, and exercise of power.

## Key Thinkers
- **Thucydides** (Athens-Melos dialogue in *History of the Peloponnesian War*) — "The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must"
- **Niccolò Machiavelli** — *The Prince* (statecraft, raison d'état)
- **Thomas Hobbes** — State of nature as "war of all against all"
- **Hans Morgenthau** — *Politics Among Nations* (1948), six principles of political realism
- **Kenneth Waltz** — *Theory of International Politics* (1979), neorealism/structural realism
- **John Mearsheimer** — *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001), offensive realism
- **Stephen Walt** — Defensive realism, balance of threat theory

## Variants / Schools

### Classical Realism
- Human nature (lust for power) drives state behaviour
- Morgenthau's six principles: politics governed by objective laws rooted in human nature; national interest defined as power; power is omnipresent

### Neorealism / Structural Realism
- International system structure, not human nature, drives behaviour
- Distribution of capabilities (polarity) shapes outcomes
- **Defensive realism** (Waltz) — states seek survival, not maximum power; balancing is rational
- **Offensive realism** (Mearsheimer) — states maximise relative power to achieve hegemony; insecurity drives expansion

### Neoclassical Realism
- Foreign policy explained by system pressures filtered through domestic variables (perceptions, state-society relations)
- Rose (1998) — "The state is the transmission belt between systemic pressures and foreign policy"

## Strengths
- Powerful explanatory framework for war, security competition, and great power politics
- Elegant parsimony — explains much with few assumptions
- Track record on Cold War bipolarity, balancing behaviour

## Critiques
- **Empirically weak** — Many predictions wrong (end of Cold War, persistence of cooperation, decline of great power war)
- **Neglects non-state actors** — Ignores IOs, NGOs, MNCs, transnational networks ([[International Organizations]] [[NGOs]])
- **State as black box** — Ignores domestic politics, ideology, perception ([[Foreign Policy Analysis]])
- **Static, determinist** — Cannot explain systemic change
- **Neglects gender, race, identity** ([[Feminism]] [[Postcolonialism]])
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy** — Treats conflict as natural, making it so
