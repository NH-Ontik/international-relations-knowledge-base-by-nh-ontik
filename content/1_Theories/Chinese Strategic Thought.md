# Chinese Strategic Thought

## Core Premises

1. **Harmony (*he*) over anarchy** — Chinese strategic thought privileges relational order and harmony over the structural anarchy that dominates Western IR theory (Qin, 2007: 96).
2. **Relational ontology** — The self is constituted through relationships (*guanxi*), not as an atomised unit in an anarchic system (Yan, 2011: 42).
3. **Strategic patience** — Long-term positioning, incremental gain, and the avoidance of premature confrontation are preferred over short-term power maximisation (Johnston, 1995: 18).
4. **Tianxia as alternative to Westphalia** — The "all-under-heaven" (*tianxia*) framework offers a hierarchical, inclusive model of international order distinct from sovereign equality (Zhao, 2005: 38).
5. **Domestic and international are continuous** — Internal order (*nei*) and external order (*wai*) are not sharply divided; state-building and world-building share a common logic (Shi, 2013: 148).

## Key Thinkers and Texts

### Sun Tzu (*The Art of War*, c. 5th century BCE)
- Strategic deception: "All warfare is based on deception" (Sun Tzu, trans. Sawyer, 1994: 103)
- Winning without fighting: "Supreme excellence consists of breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting" (Ibid.: 109)
- Intelligence and foreknowledge as prerequisites for strategy
- Influenced Mao's protracted war and Deng Xiaoping's "hide and bide" strategy (Handel, 2001: 58)

### Confucius (*Analerta*, c. 5th century BCE)
- *Ren* (humaneness) and *li* (ritual propriety) as the basis for moral order
- Hierarchical harmony: Relations between states mirror those within families (father-son, ruler-subject)
- The tributary system as Confucian international order: hierarchy, not equality, is the natural state (Fairbank, 1968: 3)

### Han Fei (Legalism, c. 3rd century BCE)
- *Shi* (formidable power/positional advantage) — Power derives from institutional position, not personal virtue
- *Fa* (law) and *Shu* (technique of statecraft) — The ruler must control through impersonal mechanisms
- Realist foundation: "The enlightened ruler does not rely on the benevolence of others, but on the power of his own position" (Han Fei, trans. Lai, 2008: 172)

### Mao Zedong (1893–1976)
- *On Protracted War* (1938) — Strategic patience, turning weakness into strength over time
- *On Guerrilla Warfare* (1937) — People's War: asymmetric conflict as strategy, not desperation
- "Hide your strength, bide your time" — Attributed to Deng, rooted in Maoist patience (Shambaugh, 2013: 24)
- Combined Clausewitzian and Sunzuian traditions into revolutionary praxis

### Qin Yongmin and Modern Chinese IR
- Qin Yaqing — Relational theory of world politics; *guanxi* as the basis of international relations (Qin, 2018: 32)
- Tang Shiping — "World politics in the long term" — Chinese historical experience as IR theory (Tang, 2013: 127)
- Amitai Etzioni — "Harmonious world" as Chinese grand strategy (Etzioni, 2017: 5)

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Significance |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| *Tianxia* (天下) | All-under-heaven; a world inclusive of all peoples | Alternative to Westphalian sovereign equality; hierarchical inclusivism |
| Tributary system | Hierarchical order where peripheral states acknowledge centrality of civilisational centre | Pre-Western Asian international order; non-sovereign-equality model |
| *Wei qi* (围棋) | The game of Go — strategic encirclement and positional strategy | Metaphor for Chinese strategic behaviour: surround, not confront |
| *Guanxi* (关系) | Relationships and networks | Relational ontology; diplomacy as relationship management |
| *He* (和) | Harmony | Organic social order; contrast with Western "balance of power" |
| Peaceful rise/development | China's development does not require hegemonic war | Deng's "hide and bide"; Xi's "new type of great power relations" |
| *Shi* (势) | Positional advantage / formidable power | Legalist concept; power as situational, not merely material |

## Applications

### Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
- Framed as *tianxia* logic: inclusive, connectivity-based order, not military alliance
- Network of economic relationships as *guanxi* at global scale (Yan, 2019: 73)
- Critics see neo-imperial debt diplomacy; Chinese framing emphasises mutual benefit

### South China Sea
- Assertive but not expansionist in Chinese framing: defending historical rights (*zhongguo*, "middle kingdom" centrality)
- Island-building as *wei qi* strategy: positional advantage without confrontation (Kaplan, 2014: 289)
- Overlaps with territorial disputes involving Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei

### Xinjiang, Tibet, Hong Kong
- Domestic = international in Chinese IR: internal stability is prerequisite for international order
- "Core interests" doctrine: sovereignty non-negotiable (Foot & Walter, 2011: 82)
- Challenges Western human rights framing as interference in domestic affairs

### US-China Competition
- Mearsheimer's offensive realism predicts inevitable conflict; Chinese strategists invoke *he* (harmony)
- "New type of great power relations" — avoid Thucydides Trap (Allison, 2017: 25)
- Technological competition (chips, AI) as *shi*-building: positional advantage in the long term

## Strengths

- Alternative to anarchy-centric IR that captures relational dynamics
- Explains long-term strategic behaviour that Western theories struggle to account for
- Historical depth: Chinese statecraft is 2,500+ years old, offering a genuine non-Western tradition
- *Tianxia* provides a positive vision of world order, not merely a critique of Western order
- Captures the role of patience, timing, and positioning in strategy

## Critiques

- **Instrumentalises Confucianism** — Selective reading of Confucian thought to serve state interests (Zhang, 2015: 211)
- **State-centric despite relational claims** — The PRC is the primary agent; the "relational" framework serves state power
- **Tianxia as neo-imperialism** — Hierarchical ordering may reproduce imperial tributary logic (Zhao, 2006: 197)
- **Operationalisation problem** — Concepts like *shi* and *he* are difficult to translate into falsifiable hypotheses
- **Romanticises pre-modern order** — The tributary system was not always peaceful or consensual (Wang, 2013: 64)
- **Selective history** — Ignores periods of Chinese aggression (e.g., Vietnam 1979, border wars with India)
