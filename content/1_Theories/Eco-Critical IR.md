# Eco-Critical IR

## Core Premises
1. **The nature-culture binary is false** — IR has been built on a false separation between "nature" (the environment) and "culture" (politics); ecological crisis collapses this distinction
2. **Ecological crisis constitutes political possibility** — [[Climate Change]], biodiversity loss, and resource depletion are not "low politics" issues but reshape the very conditions of sovereignty, security, and political community
3. **The Anthropocene as ontological shift** — Human activity has become a geological force; this fundamentally alters the assumptions on which IR theory was built
4. **Ecological interdependence** — Environmental problems are inherently transboundary and cannot be contained within the state system; they demand new forms of political organization
5. **Environmental injustice** — Ecological crisis is not evenly distributed; it falls disproportionately on the Global South, marginalized communities, and future generations

## Key Thinkers
- **Simon Dalby** — *Environmental Security* (2009), reframed environmental issues as security concerns; challenged the state-centric security paradigm
- **Anthony Burke** — "Security and Ecology in the Post-Cold War Era" (2013), argued that security must be rethought in ecological terms
- **Nicola Detraz** — *Environmental Security and Gender* (2014), connected environmental security to [[Feminism]] and gender analysis
- **Jon Barnett** — *The Meaning of Environmental Security* (2001), developed a human-security approach to environmental politics
- **Alf Hornborg** — *Global Ecology and Unequal Exchange* (2011), linked environmental degradation to global economic inequality and colonial legacies
- **Andreas Malm** — *Fossil Capital* (2016), traced the origins of the fossil fuel economy to capitalist accumulation, not technological determinism
- **Kyle Whyte** — *Indigenous Environmental Justice* (2017), centred Indigenous knowledge and justice in environmental politics
- **Robyn Eckersley** — *The Green State* (2004), theorized the ecological transformation of the state

## Variants / Schools

### Green Realism
- Environmental degradation as a national security threat; resource scarcity drives conflict
- "Water wars" thesis; climate migration as security risk
- Risks instrumentalizing environment for state-centric security goals

### Green Liberalism
- Sustainable development, international environmental regimes, green governance
- [[Climate Change]] as a collective action problem solvable through institutions and market mechanisms
- Overlaps with [[Liberalism]] and [[International Political Economy]]

### Green Radical / Eco-Socialism
- Capitalism is the root cause of ecological crisis; degrowth and systemic transformation required
- Overlaps with [[Marxism]]; challenges the growth paradigm
- Malm's fossil capital thesis; ecological debt from Global North to Global South

### Eco-Feminism
- Environmental destruction and gender oppression share a common logic of domination
- Nature and women are both "othered" and exploited by patriarchal capitalism
- Overlaps with [[Feminism]] and [[Postcolonialism]]

## Applications to Contemporary Issues
- **Climate security** — [[Climate Change]] as a "threat multiplier"; displacement, conflict, and state fragility
- **Water wars thesis** — Whether water scarcity will drive interstate conflict; contested empirically
- **Climate refugees** — The legal and political status of people displaced by environmental change; no current legal category
- **Resource conflicts** — Oil, rare earth minerals, and conflict; resource curse theory
- **Green energy transition as geopolitics** — Lithium, cobalt, and the geopolitics of renewable energy; new dependencies
- **COP process critique** — International climate negotiations and their limitations; the gap between commitments and action

## Strengths
- Addresses the defining issue of the 21st century — IR theory is incomplete without ecological analysis
- Challenges anthropocentrism — places human activity within ecological systems rather above them
- Connects material and ideational — ecological crisis is both material (warming, scarcity) and ideological (growth paradigm, denial)
- Provides an intersectional analysis — connects environmental justice to [[Postcolonialism]], [[Feminism]], and [[Global South]] perspectives
- Expands the concept of security — from state security to planetary security

## Critiques
- **Difficult to integrate with existing theories** — Ecological concerns don't fit neatly into [[Realism]], [[Liberalism]], or [[Constructivism]]
- **Tension between state-centrism and ecological universalism** — The state system is poorly equipped to address global ecological problems
- **Green realism is problematic** — Instrumentalizing environment for state security goals may reinforce the very structures that cause ecological crisis
- **Degrowth is politically unrealistic** — Radical alternatives face enormous political obstacles
- **Knowledge politics** — Who speaks for nature? Western science vs Indigenous knowledge

## Opposing Theories
- [[Realism]] — Eco-critical IR challenges state-centrism and the nature-culture binary
- [[Neorealism]] — Environmental issues are not reducible to power politics
- [[Liberalism]] — Sustainable development may be insufficient; systemic change is required
