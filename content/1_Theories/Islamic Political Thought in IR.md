# Islamic Political Thought in IR

## Core Premises

1. **Sovereignty belongs to God** — The concept of *hakimiyya* (God's sovereignty) challenges the Westphalian assumption that the state is the supreme authority within its territory (Esposito, 2003: 53).
2. **The *ummah* transcends the state** — Islamic political thought posits a transnational community of believers whose solidarity supersedes national borders, challenging state-centrism (Esposito & Mogahed, 2007: 24).
3. **Justice (*adl*) is the foundation of political order** — Legitimate governance requires adherence to divine law (*sharia*) and the pursuit of justice, not merely the maintenance of power (Hashmi, 1997: 21).
4. **Religion and politics are inseparable** — The Western secular-religious divide is a historical contingency, not a universal truth; Islamic governance integrates the two (Maududi, 1960: 12).
5. **Community cohesion drives civilisational rise and fall** — Ibn Khaldun's *asabiyya* provides a cyclical theory of political power rooted in group solidarity (Ibn Khaldun, 1377/1967: 91).

## Key Thinkers

### Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406)
- *The Muqaddimah* (1377) — Foundational text of sociology and political science
- Developed *asabiyya* (social cohesion/group feeling) as the engine of political power
- Civilisations rise through strong *asabiyya* (desert/Bedouin groups) and fall when luxury weakens it (sedentary/city dwellers)
- Often called the first true IR theorist from the non-Western tradition (Malik, 2012: 18)

### Sayyid Qutb (1906–1966)
- *Milestones* (1964) — Radical critique of modern *jahiliyya* (pre-Islamic ignorance)
- Argued modern societies had reverted to *jahiliyya* despite appearing secular or religious
- *Takfir* (declaration of unbelief) — Excommunication of nominal Muslims
- Influenced political Islam movements across the globe (Kepel, 2002: 56)

### Muhammad Iqbal (1877–1938)
- *The Reconstruction of Religious Thought in Islam* (1930) — Islamic modernism
- Concept of *khudi* (selfhood) as the basis for Muslim political renewal
- Advocated *ijtihad* (independent reasoning) to adapt Islam to modernity
- Inspired the Pakistan movement and Islamic revivalism (Nasr, 1996: 78)

### Abul A'la Maududi (1903–1979)
- Founded Jamaat-e-Islami (1941) — Blueprint for an Islamic state
- *Islamic Law and Constitution* (1955) — *Khilafa* (stewardship) as political institution
- Argued sovereignty rests exclusively with God; human legislation must conform to *sharia*
- Influenced Zia ul-Haq's Pakistan and transnational political Islam (Maududi, 1960: 34)

### Khaled Abou El Fadl (1963–)
- *Reasoning with God* (2014) — Islamic governance and human rights
- Distinguishes between *sharia* (divine path) and *fiqh* (human jurisprudence)
- Argues for *siyasa shar'iyya* (governance in accordance with sharia) that is consistent with human dignity and rights
- Challenges both Islamist authoritarians and secular critics (Abou El Fadl, 2014: 89)

## Historical Applications

### Ottoman Millet System
- Non-Muslim communities (Christian, Jewish) granted semi-autonomous governance under Ottoman suzerainty
- Practised pluralistic coexistence within an Islamic framework (Braude & Lewis, 1982: 15)
- Challenges the narrative that pre-modern political orders were necessarily exclusionary

### Mughal Empire
- Akbar's *sulh-i-kul* (universal peace) — Inclusive governance across religious lines
- Dara Shikoh's syncretic philosophy — Bridge between Islamic and Hindu thought
- Demonstrates diversity within Islamic political practice (Richards, 1995: 112)

### Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC)
- Founded 1969 — 57 member states, second-largest IO after the UN
- Charter invokes Islamic solidarity; tensions between Arab and non-Arab, Sunni and Shia
- Limited enforcement capacity; functions more as a diplomatic forum (Dodge, 2002: 48)

### Political Islam Movements
- **Muslim Brotherhood** (Egypt, founded 1928) — Hassan al-Banna; social services + political Islam
- **Hamas** (Palestine, founded 1987) — Resistance + Islamic governance
- **Hizbollah** (Lebanon, founded 1982) — Shi'a political party with armed wing
- Demonstrates the diversity of political Islam across contexts (Bayat, 2013: 42)

## Strengths

- Challenges the secular-Western assumptions that underpin most of IR theory
- Explains transnational religious politics that state-centrism cannot capture
- Provides an alternative to the state-centric, anarchy-driven ontology of mainstream IR
- Ibn Khaldun's *asabiyya* offers a compelling theory of civilisational dynamics independent of materialist explanations
- *Siyasa shar'iyya* provides a framework for legitimacy that is neither purely divine-right nor purely popular sovereignty

## Critiques

- **Essentialises Islam** — Treats a diverse, 1,400-year-old tradition as a monolith (Sayyid, 2003: 87)
- **Conflates religion and politics** — Ignores secular Muslims, secularisation within Islamic societies, and the diversity of Islamic political thought
- **Difficulty with internal diversity** — Sunni-Shia divide, Sufi-Salafi tensions, Arab vs non-Arab perspectives are undertheorised
- **Securitisation bias** — Islamic political thought is often studied through the lens of terrorism and security, distorting its intellectual richness (Kundnani, 2014: 34)
- **Operationalisation problem** — How does one build empirical research programmes around concepts like *ummah* or *hakimiyya*?
- **Normative tension** — *Sharia*-based governance may conflict with liberal human rights norms (Mahmood, 2012: 176)
