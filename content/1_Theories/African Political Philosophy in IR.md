# African Political Philosophy in IR

## Core Premises

1. **Communal personhood (*Ubuntu*)** — "I am because we are"; the individual is constituted through community, challenging the Western liberal ontology of atomised selfhood (Ramose, 1999: 53).
2. **Pan-African solidarity** — African peoples share a common history of colonialism and a common interest in continental liberation and unity (Nkrumah, 1963: 217).
3. **Anti-imperialism as foundational** — The international system was constructed through colonial violence; decolonisation is not merely historical but an ongoing process (Fanon, 1961: 35).
4. **African solutions to African problems** — External intervention (humanitarian, military, economic) has historically served foreign interests, not African ones (Nyerere, 1973: 18).
5. **Knowledge decolonisation** — Western IR theory has marginalised African political thought; recovering it is both an intellectual and political project (Ndlovu-Gatsheni, 2013: 11).

## Key Thinkers

### Desmond Tutu (1931–2021)
- Popularised *Ubuntu* in the context of South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)
- "A person is a person through other persons" — communal identity as basis for justice and reconciliation
- Demonstrated *Ubuntu* as a practical framework for transitional justice (Tutu, 1999: 31)

### Mogobe Ramose (1944–)
- Philosophical foundation of *Ubuntu* as an ontology: being is co-being
- Critiques Western individualism as the philosophical root of colonialism and apartheid
- *Ubuntu* as a political philosophy, not merely a cultural value (Ramose, 1999: 52)

### Kwame Nkrumah (1909–1972)
- Pan-Africanism: "Africa must unite" — Continental political and economic integration
- *Neo-Colonialism: The Last Stage of Imperialism* (1965) — Formal independence without economic sovereignty is a fiction
- Advocated a United States of Africa with supranational authority (Nkrumah, 1963: 218)

### Frantz Fanon (1925–1961)
- *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961) — Decolonisation as total social revolution, not merely political transfer
- Violence as the cleansing force: the colonised must violently reject the colonial psyche
- *Black Skin, White Masks* (1952) — Psychology of colonial oppression; decolonisation of the mind (Fanon, 1961: 35)

### Julius Nyerere (1922–1999)
- *Ujamaa* (African socialism) — Self-reliance and communal agriculture as alternatives to capitalist development
- "The villagisation" programme in Tanzania: practical attempt to build *ujamaa*
- Argued that development must be rooted in African communal values, not imported models (Nyerere, 1968: 12)

### Thandika Mkandawire (1940–2015)
- Critiqued the "one-size-fits-all" development prescriptions imposed by the Bretton Woods institutions
- *African Economies and the Crisis of Development* — Context-specific development strategies
- Argued African states have been "thinking" about development, but their ideas are systematically excluded (Mkandawire, 2010: 47)

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Significance |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| *Ubuntu* | Communal personhood — "I am because we are" | Relational ontology; basis for justice, diplomacy, and governance |
| Pan-Africanism | Continental solidarity and unity | Political and cultural movement against colonialism and division |
| *Neo-colonialism* | Formal independence without economic sovereignty | Nkrumah's concept; explains persistent inequality in post-colonial Africa |
| *Ujamaa* | African socialism — communal self-reliance | Alternative development model rooted in African values |
| African Renaissance | Cultural, political, and economic renewal of the continent | Mbeki's vision; links knowledge production to political liberation |
| African solutions to African problems | Self-determination in conflict resolution | AU principle; challenges interventionism |
| Decoloniality | Ongoing dismantling of colonial power structures | Intellectual and political project; knowledge, culture, economy |

## Applications

### African Union Peace and Security Architecture (APSA)
- African Standby Force, Continental Early Warning System, Panel of the Wise
- Operationalises "African solutions to African problems" in practice
- Mixed record: success in Darfur mediation, limited capacity in Somalia (Adebajo, 2011: 156)

### African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)
- Largest free trade area by number of countries (54 signatories)
- Attempt to reduce commodity dependence and intra-African trade barriers
- *Ubuntu* economic logic: shared prosperity through interconnectedness (ECA, 2019)

### Ubuntu Diplomacy
- South Africa's TRC as a model of *Ubuntu*-based transitional justice
- Rwanda's *gacaca* courts as communal justice rooted in African tradition
- Challenges the Western liberal model of individual accountability (Clark, 2010: 24)

### African Development Challenges
- Structural adjustment programmes as neo-colonialism in practice
- Chinese investment in Africa: new partner or new imperial power?
- Youth bulge, urbanisation, and demographic dividend as opportunities and threats

## Strengths

- Challenges Western universalism by offering a genuinely different ontology (*Ubuntu*)
- Recovers marginalised intellectual traditions and makes them visible
- Explains patterns of solidarity and resistance that Western IR cannot capture
- Decolonial approach connects past and present, theory and practice
- Provides a communal alternative to both Western liberalism and Marxist materialism

## Critiques

- **Essentialises "African" thought** — Africa is 54 countries, 2,000+ languages, enormous diversity; "African philosophy" risks homogenising (Wiredu, 1996: 2)
- **Romanticises pre-colonial** — Pre-colonial African societies were not egalitarian utopias; hierarchy, slavery, and warfare existed
- **Limited policy prescriptions** — *Ubuntu* is compelling philosophically but difficult to translate into specific policy recommendations
- **Tension between sovereignty and solidarity** — Pan-Africanism requires ceding sovereignty; African states have resisted this
- **Instrumentalisation by elites** — *Ubuntu* and Pan-Africanism can be co-opted by authoritarian regimes to legitimate power
- **Undertheorised IR framework** — African political philosophy is rich, but an "African IR theory" remains underdeveloped (Acharya, 2014: 95)
