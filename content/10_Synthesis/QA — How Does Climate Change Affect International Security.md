# QA — How Does Climate Change Affect International Security?

**Q: How does climate change affect international security?**

**A:** Climate change affects international security through three primary pathways:

**Resource scarcity → conflict**: The "water wars" thesis (Klare 2001) argues that declining freshwater supplies, desertification, and agricultural disruption create competition for resources. Climate change reduces arable land, disrupts food production, and intensifies water scarcity — particularly in arid regions (Middle East, Sahel, Central Asia). The empirical evidence is mixed: [[Homer-Dixon 1999]] argues resource scarcity causes conflict; [[Theisen 2008]] finds the direct link is weak, mediated by governance quality.

**Migration → state instability**: Climate-induced displacement creates pressure on host states. [[Climate refugees]] — people displaced by sea-level rise, drought, or extreme weather — number in the millions. The IPCC projects 200+ million climate migrants by 2050. Urbanization pressure in developing states strains infrastructure and governance. [[Reuveny 2007]] finds climate migration correlates with conflict risk in receiving areas.

**Geopolitical → new arenas**: Arctic competition intensifies as ice melts — Russia, US, Canada, and China vie for shipping routes and resources. Energy transition geopolitics reshapes power: oil states (Saudi Arabia, Russia) face decline; lithium and rare earth producers (DRC, Chile, China) gain leverage. [[Climate diplomacy]] — COP negotiations, Paris Agreement, loss and damage — becomes a arena of great-power politics.

**Securitization of climate** (Buzan & Wæver 2008): Climate change has been securitized — framed as an existential threat requiring emergency action. [[Dalby 2009]] and [[Burke 2009]] argue for "environmental security" as a framework. The [[CNA Military Advisory Board]] (2007) termed climate a "threat multiplier."

**Key critique**: The climate-conflict link is mediated by **governance quality** — poor governance + climate stress = conflict; rich states adapt. Climate change is not deterministic but increases risk in fragile contexts.
