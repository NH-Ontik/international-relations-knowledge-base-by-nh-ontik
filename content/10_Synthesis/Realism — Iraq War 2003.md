# Realism — Iraq War 2003

Realist scholars diverged sharply on whether the 2003 Iraq War supported or undermined their theories.

## Mearsheimer's Critique: Strategic Error, Not Realism

Mearsheimer (2005) argued the invasion was a catastrophic strategic error driven by neoconservative ideology, not by realist logic. Realists generally opposed the war: Walt and Mearsheimer published a 2003 open letter arguing Iraq posed no direct threat to the US. The war violated realist principles — it weakened US relative power, destabilized the region, and distracted from the actual balance-of-power challenge in East Asia. From this view, the Bush administration's decision was liberal interventionism masquerading as security policy.

## Neoclassical Realist Explanation

Neoclassical realists (Rose 1998, Schweller 2004) offered a more nuanced reading. Structural permissiveness — US unipolarity after 1991 — created the conditions for adventurism by removing external constraints. Domestic factors: the [[neoconservative]] movement within the Bush administration, ideological commitment to democracy promotion, and the post-9/11 security environment created a permissive structure for preventive war logic. [[PO 2003]] — the "Powell Doctrine" of overwhelming force and clear objectives was abandoned in favor of Rumsfeld's light-footprint approach.

## Classical Realist Lens: Hubris and Power

Classical realists (Morgenthau, Niebuhr) would recognize the war as a manifestation of imperial hubris — the corruption that power brings to those who wield it unchecked. The unipolar moment created what [[Ikenberry 2001]] called the "unipolar dilemma" — the absence of countervailing power tempted the lone superpower into overreach.

## The Core Debate

Was the Iraq War about WMD (security), oil (material interest), or democracy promotion (ideology)? Realists disagree: Mearsheimer emphasizes ideology; Posen emphasizes bureaucratic politics; [[Walt 2005]] emphasizes the Israel lobby's influence. The war illustrates a key realist insight: when structural constraints are weak, domestic politics and ideology fill the explanatory gap — but this makes the case difficult for purely structural realism to handle.
