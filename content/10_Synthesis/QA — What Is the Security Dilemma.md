# QA — What Is the Security Dilemma?

**Q: What is the security dilemma and how does it differ from the spiral model?**

**A:** The security dilemma, formulated by [[Herz 1950]] and [[Butterfield 1951]], describes a structural predicament: defensive measures by one state are perceived as offensive threats by another, creating unintended mutual insecurity. States arm themselves for protection, but their arming makes neighbors feel less secure, prompting reciprocal armament. No state intends conflict, but the logic of anarchy produces it.

The **spiral model** ([[Jervis 1976]]) adds the offense-defense balance: when offensive military capability has the advantage, the dilemma intensifies because defensive and offensive preparations are indistinguishable. A missile silo is "defensive" but looks "offensive." The spiral model emphasizes misperception and miscommunication as amplifiers — states misread intentions and assume worst-case scenarios.

**Key distinction**: The security dilemma is **structural** — it arises from anarchy and uncertainty regardless of state intentions. The spiral model is **perceptual** — it depends on misperception, miscommunication, and the offense-defense balance. All spiral model cases involve the security dilemma, but not all security dilemma cases involve spiral dynamics.

**Wendt's constructivist critique** ([[Wendt 1992]]): The dilemma is not inevitable but socially constructed. Identity and rhetoric determine whether states perceive others as threats. "Anarchy is what states make of it" — hostile identities produce security dilemmas; friendly identities do not. The dilemma can be mitigated through trust-building, transparency, and shared identity formation.

**Contemporary application**: The US-China relationship exhibits classic security dilemma dynamics — Chinese military modernization (defensive in Chinese perception) appears offensive to the US, prompting US countermeasures that China reads as encirclement.
