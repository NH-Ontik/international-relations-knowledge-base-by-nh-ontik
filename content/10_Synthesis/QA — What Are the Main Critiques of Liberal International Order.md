# QA — What Are the Main Critiques of Liberal International Order?

**Q: What are the main critiques of the liberal international order?**

**A:** The liberal international order (LIO) — the post-1945 system of [[UN]], [[WTO]], [[IMF]], [[NATO]], and rule-based norms — faces critiques from multiple theoretical traditions.

**Realist critique** (Mearsheimer 2001, Layne 1993): The order is maintained by **US hegemonic power**, not institutions. Institutions are epiphenomenal — they reflect the distribution of power, not constrain it. When US power declines, the order will erode regardless of institutional design. [[Hegemonic stability theory]] (Gilpin 1981) argues the order depends on a single dominant power.

**Marxist critique** (Wallerstein 1974, Harvey 2003): The order serves **capitalist exploitation**. [[WTO]] rules protect intellectual property in ways that disadvantage developing states. [[IMF]] structural adjustment programs impose austerity that benefits creditors. The order reproduces core-periphery inequality at the global scale.

**Postcolonial critique** (Acharya 2014, Shilliam 2010): The order is **Eurocentric** — it imposes Western norms (sovereignty, human rights, free trade) on non-Western states without their consent. The "rules-based order" reflects Western preferences, not universal values. [[Decolonizing IR]] requires recognizing alternative normative systems.

**Populist critique** (Rodrik 2018, Tooze 2018): The order **benefits elites** at the expense of domestic workers. Free trade, capital mobility, and migration have hollowed out middle classes in Western democracies, fueling nationalism and protectionism. [[Brexit]] and [[Trump]] are symptoms of domestic backlash against the LIO.

**Chinese/Russian critique** (Yan 2019, Putin 2007): The order is a **tool for US dominance**, not neutral rules. US military interventionism, unilateral sanctions, and financial hegemony (dollar weaponization) demonstrate the order's one-sidedness. [[Multipolarity]] is needed to restore balance.
