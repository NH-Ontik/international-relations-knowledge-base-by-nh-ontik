# QA — What Is the Responsibility to Protect?

**Q: What is the Responsibility to Protect (R2P) and when was it adopted?**

**A:** R2P was adopted at the **2005 UN World Summit** (UNGA Resolution 60/1) by all 191 UN member states. It emerged from the **International Commission on Intervention and State Sovereignty** (ICISS 2001), established in response to the failures to prevent Rwanda (1994) and Srebrenica (1995).

**Three pillars**:

**Pillar I** — State responsibility: Every state has the responsibility to protect its populations from four crimes: genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity. This is the sovereignty-as-responsibility principle — [[sovereignty]] entails obligations, not just rights.

**Pillar II** — International assistance: The international community has the responsibility to assist states in fulfilling this duty through capacity-building, early warning, and diplomatic engagement. Prevention is the priority.

**Pillar III** — Timely and decisive response: When a state **manifestly fails** to protect its populations, the international community must be prepared to take collective action, including military intervention as a **last resort** (Chapter VII of the UN Charter). This pillar is the most contested.

**Origins**: [[ICISS 2001]] reframed "humanitarian intervention" as "responsibility to protect" — shifting the focus from the intervener's right to the protected population's needs. The sovereignty-as-responsibility principle built on [[Francis Deng 1996]] and the African Union's [[non-indifference doctrine]].

**Implementation**: R2P has been invoked in Darfur (Sudan), Côte d'Ivoire, Kenya, and Libya. **Criticisms**: Libya (2011) showed R2P's third pillar can be abused for regime change. Russia and China have blocked R2P implementation in Syria since 2011. R2P's selective application (Libya bombed, Syria protected) undermines its legitimacy. The principle oscillates between [[internalization]] and [[rejection]].
