# QA — How Do Sanctions Work?

**Q: How do economic sanctions work and when are they effective?**

**A:** Economic sanctions are coercive tools designed to change target state behavior through economic pressure. They operate through three mechanisms:

**Cost imposition**: Sanctions impose economic pain — trade restrictions, asset freezes, financial exclusion — to make the target state change behavior. The logic is straightforward: raise the costs of non-compliance until compliance becomes cheaper. [[Baldwin 2016]] identifies this as the primary mechanism.

**Signaling**: Sanctions demonstrate resolve and establish norms. Even when sanctions fail to change behavior, they signal that violations have costs. They communicate to the target, allies, and adversaries that the sender is serious about the issue. [[Drezner 1999]] emphasizes the signaling function.

**Audience costs**: Domestic audiences punish leaders who back down after imposing sanctions. Once sanctions are imposed, escalating or maintaining them becomes politically necessary. This creates a commitment problem — sanctions are hard to lift without achieving the goal.

**When sanctions succeed** ([[Hufbauer 1985]]): Sanctions succeed approximately **34% of the time**. They work best when: (1) The target is **democratic** (publics punish leaders for economic pain). (2) The issue is **low-stakes** for the target. (3) Sanctions are **swift and decisive** (gradual escalation signals indecision). (4) The sender has leverage over the target's economy.

**Modern evolution**: "Smart sanctions" (targeted asset freezes, travel bans on individuals, sectoral sanctions) replace comprehensive sanctions (embargoing entire economies). This reduces humanitarian costs while targeting decision-makers.

**Critique**: Secondary sanctions harm third parties; sanctions resistance (import substitution, alternative payment systems) reduces effectiveness; humanitarian costs fall on civilians, not elites. [[Russia 2022]] sanctions demonstrated unprecedented scope but limited behavioral change — Russia adapted through energy leverage and alternative markets.
