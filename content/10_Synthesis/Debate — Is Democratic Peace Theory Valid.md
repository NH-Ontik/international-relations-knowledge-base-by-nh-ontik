# Debate — Is Democratic Peace Theory Valid?

The democratic peace theory (DPT) is among the most empirically tested propositions in IR.

## PRO — Russett, Doyle, Kant

**Claim**: Democracies rarely, if ever, go to war with each other. The empirical record is robust: no consolidated democracy has fought another since 1900 (Doyle 1983, Russett 1993).

**Three mechanisms**: (1) Institutional constraints — democratic leaders face elections, public opinion, and legislative checks that make war costly. (2) Normative expectations — democratic culture produces shared norms of peaceful conflict resolution and compromise. (3) Commercial peace — economic interdependence raises the cost of war (Oneal & Russett 1999).

**Evidence**: [[Doyle 1983]] examined Kant's three conditions (republican constitutions, commercial society, international federation) and found strong empirical support. [[Oneal 1997]] meta-analysis confirmed statistical significance across 150+ years.

## CON — Rosato, Mearsheimer

**Selection bias** (Rosato 2003): Democracies are wealthy, and wealthy states rarely fight anyone — the peace is about prosperity, not regime type. The correlation is spurious.

**Backward causation** (Mearsheimer 1990): Peace creates democracy, not vice versa. Stable states develop democratic institutions; war-prone states don't. The causal arrow runs backward.

**Hypocrisy** (Spencer 2008): DPT is used ideologically to justify intervention — democracies have fought many wars against non-democracies. The "democratic peace" becomes a tool of liberal imperialism.

## SYNTHESIS

DPT is empirically robust for **dyadic** peace (democracies don't fight each other) but weak as a **monadic** theory (democracies still fight non-democracies). Best understood as a liberal institutional claim, not a universal law. Its scope conditions matter: established democracies with strong institutions, economic interdependence, and shared norms maintain peace; newly democratized states with weak institutions may be more war-prone.
