# Constructivism — Ukraine War

Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine presents a case where rational choice models struggle but constructivist explanations illuminate.

## Identity Politics: "Russkiy Mir"

Putin's July 2021 essay "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians" frames the conflict as identity-based: Ukraine is not a real nation but part of a broader "Russian world" (Russkiy Mir). This is not instrumental rhetoric — constructivists argue it reflects a genuine elite identity schema where Russian greatness requires territorial and cultural suzerainty over former imperial spaces. [[Hopf 2021]] documents how Russian elite identity has been reconstructed since 2012 around civilizational distinctiveness and anti-Westernism.

## NATO as Identity Threat

From a constructivist perspective, NATO expansion threatened Russia not primarily through military capability but through identity. [[Wendt 1992]] argued "anarchy is what states make of it" — Russia's alliance with Ukraine was identity-defining. Ukraine's potential NATO membership represented not just a security threat but an existential identity challenge: losing Ukraine meant losing the narrative of Russia as a great civilization-state.

## Norms: Sovereignty Violation

Russia's invasion violated the foundational norm of [[sovereignty]] and [[territorial integrity]]. The nuclear taboo — the norm against nuclear use — has been eroded through Russian nuclear signaling. The invasion represents what [[Mazarr 2015]] calls "norm entrepreneurship" in reverse: a major power attempting to normalize spheres-of-influence politics.

## Ontological Security

[[Steele 2008]] argues states seek not just physical security but ontological security — continuity of identity. Russia's invasion, while materially irrational (sanctions, isolation, military costs), served ontological security by restoring a great-power narrative. The war maintained the "biographical narrative" of Russia as a civilizational power that cannot be subordinate to Western institutions.
