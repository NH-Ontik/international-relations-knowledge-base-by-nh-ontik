# Debate — R2P After Libya

The 2011 Libya intervention transformed R2P from an emerging norm into a deeply contested one.

## PRO — Evans, Bellamy

**R2P saved lives** (Evans 2008, Bellamy 2009): Without intervention, Gaddafi would have massacred civilians in Benghazi as he promised. The UN-authorized NATO operation prevented mass atrocity. R2P's first two pillars (state responsibility, international assistance) are uncontroversial. The third pillar — timely and decisive response — proved its value in Libya.

**Sovereignty is conditional** (Patten 2010): The 2005 World Summit unanimously endorsed R2P (UNGA 60/1). Sovereignty entails responsibility; when states manifestly fail to protect populations, the international community is authorized to act. This is not regime change but humanitarian protection.

**Normative progress** (Welsh 2013): R2P emerged from the failures of Rwanda (1994) and Srebrenica (1995). Abandoning R2P means returning to a world where mass atrocity goes unchecked.

## CON — Russia, China, African Union

**Libya became regime change** (Russian Federation 2011): The NATO operation exceeded the UN mandate — UNSC 1973 authorized a no-fly zone and civilian protection, not Gaddafi's overthrow. Russia and China argue this constitutes abuse of R2P for regime change — "humanitarian imperialism" (Mazower 2013).

**Double standards** (AU 2011): R2P is applied selectively. Libya was bombed; Syria's Assad was protected by Russian vetoes. The principle is not applied consistently, revealing it as a tool of Western power rather than universal norm.

**R2P's legitimacy deficit** (Chomsky 2012): The "Responsibility to Protect" becomes the "Right to Project Power." Iraq (2003) demonstrated how WMD claims could manufacture consent for intervention; Libya showed the same pattern with civilian protection claims.

## SYNTHESIS

R2P's **third pillar** (timely and decisive response) remains deeply contested. The first two pillars — prevention and assistance — retain broad support. The norm is in oscillation between **internalization** (institutionalization in AU, ASEAN) and **rejection** (Russian/Chinese vetoes, BRICS alternatives). R2P's future depends on whether the international community can prevent its abuse for regime change while maintaining its capacity for genuine humanitarian response.
