# English School — ASEAN

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) is a paradigmatic case for the English School's concept of international society.

## International Society: Shared Rules and Institutions

[[Bull 1977]] defined international society as a group of states bound by common rules and institutions. ASEAN embodies this: its members share norms (the [[ASEAN Way]]), institutions (ASEAN Regional Forum, East Asia Summit), and a共同 identity as Southeast Asian states. Unlike [[realism]], which sees only anarchy, the English School recognizes that ASEAN states have developed a society with genuine normative content.

## The ASEAN Way

The [[ASEAN Way]] — consensus, non-interference, peaceful settlement, non-use of force — is a set of shared norms that constitutes ASEAN's international society. [[Acharya 2014]] argues these norms are not merely behavioral patterns but reflect a genuine normative community. Consensus decision-making (unanimity) and "consultative" diplomacy (musyawarah, mufakat) represent a regional diplomatic culture distinct from Western multilateralism.

## Regional Security Complexes

[[Buzan 1998]] developed the concept of Regional Security Complexes (RSCs) — clusters of states whose security interdependence makes them a distinct unit. Southeast Asia constitutes an RSC: the security of Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, and others is deeply intertwined through proximity, history, and institutional membership. ASEAN manages this complex through dialogue and confidence-building rather than military alliances.

## Weak Institutionalization as Strength

Unlike the EU, ASEAN has no supranational institutions, no binding dispute settlement mechanism, and no enforcement capacity. The English School interprets this as strength, not weakness: ASEAN's minimal institutionalization preserves sovereignty while enabling cooperation. [[Kent 2007]] argues ASEAN's "pluralistic security community" holds together through shared identity, not legal obligation.

## Tensions

The sovereignty norm conflicts with [[human rights]] protection: ASEAN's non-interference principle prevented effective response to Myanmar's Rohingya crisis. The [[East Timor]] intervention (1999) required overcoming the non-interference norm — a rare exception that proves the rule.
