# Postcolonialism — Scramble for Africa Legacy

Postcolonial analysis reveals how the Berlin Conference (1884-85) constituted Africa's modern state system and continues to shape its politics.

## Berlin Conference as Constitutive Act

The Berlin Conference did not merely divide territory — it constituted the political categories through which Africa would be governed. [[Herbst 2000]] argues that European powers drew borders without reference to existing political communities, creating states that were administrative units of colonial empires, not expressions of self-determination. These borders became the [[sovereignty]] framework inherited at independence — the AU's 1964 Cairo resolution codified [[uti possidetis]] (existing borders are sacrosanct).

## Arbitrary Borders → Ethnic Conflict → Weak States

Colonial borders deliberately divided ethnic groups (the Somali across five states, the Ewe across Ghana and Togo) and forced hostile groups into single polities (Nigeria, DRC, Sudan). [[Mamdani 1996]] argues colonial governance produced "decentralized despotism" — indirect rule through customary authorities that became authoritarian institutions at independence. Post-independence conflicts (Nigeria-Biafra, Rwanda, South Sudan) trace directly to colonial border construction.

## Fanon's Psychology of Colonialism

[[Fanon 1961]] analyzed how colonialism deforms the psyche of both colonizer and colonized. The "colonial subject" internalizes inferiority through cultural domination. Liberation requires psychological decolonization — not just political independence. [[Fanon 1952]] "Black Skin, White Masks" examines the identity crisis produced by colonial modernity.

## Neo-Colonialism

[[Nkrumah 1965]] argued that political independence without economic independence produces "neo-colonialism" — states formally sovereign but materially dependent on former colonial powers. [[CFA franc zone]] in West Africa exemplifies: 14 former French colonies use a currency pegged to the euro, with reserves held in the French Treasury. Contemporary [[resource extraction]] in Africa (oil in Nigeria, minerals in DRC, cobalt in Congo) reproduces dependency structures.

## Contemporary Tensions

The AU's non-interference principle (border non-change) conflicts with self-determination claims: [[Western Sahara]], [[Somaliland]], [[Ambazonia]]. Postcolonial theory argues this is not merely a sovereignty question but reflects the enduring legitimacy crisis of borders created without consent.
