# QA — How Does Neorealism Explain Alliance Formation?

**Q: How does neorealism explain why states form alliances?**

**A:** Neorealism explains alliance formation as a response to the distribution of power and threat in anarchy.

**Waltz (1979)**: Alliances form to **balance** against threatening states. States band together to prevent any single state from achieving hegemony. Balancing is the default behavior under anarchy — states prefer to maintain the balance of power rather than allow a single state to dominate. [[Bandwagoning]] (joining the强者) occurs only when balancing is impossible or when the threat is overwhelming.

**Walt (1985)** refines: states balance against **threat**, not just power. Threat is a function of four factors: (1) **Aggregate power** — the size of a state's military and economy. (2) **Geographic proximity** — nearby threats are more dangerous than distant ones. (3) **Offensive capability** — the ability to project military force. (4) **Offensive intentions** — perceived aggressive intent (the most important and most subjective factor).

**Alliance dynamics** ([[Snyder 1997]]): Alliances create two classic dilemmas: **entrapment** — being dragged into an ally's conflicts that are not in one's own interest — and **abandonment** — the fear that an ally will desert you in a crisis. These dilemmas explain why alliances are both necessary and unstable.

**Ukraine case**: NATO expansion can be read as **balancing** against a perceived Russian threat (Walt's framework). Russia views NATO expansion as **encirclement** — a spiral model dynamic. The US-Russia security dilemma over Ukraine illustrates how alliance formation by one side is perceived as offensive by the other.
