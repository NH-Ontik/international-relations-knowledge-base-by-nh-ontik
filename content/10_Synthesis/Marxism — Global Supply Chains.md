# Marxism — Global Supply Chains

Marxist and IPE frameworks reveal the structural exploitation embedded in global supply chains.

## World-Systems Theory: Core-Periphery

[[Wallerstein 1974]] argues the global economy operates as a world-system divided into core, semi-periphery, and periphery. Global manufacturing exemplifies this: Apple designs in California (core), Foxconn assembles in China (semi-periphery transitioning to core), and raw materials are extracted in the DRC (periphery). Each position in the hierarchy captures different value. The world-system is self-reproducing: core states set the rules of global trade through institutions like the [[WTO]] and [[IMF]].

## Dependency Theory: Rare Earth Minerals

[[Amin 1976]] and [[Frank 1967]] argued underdevelopment is not a stage but a product of exploitation. The global rare earth supply chain illustrates this: 70% of mining occurs in China, 90% of processing, yet the highest value capture occurs in Western technology firms. The DRC produces 70% of cobalt — essential for batteries — yet retains minimal value. Dependency persists because peripheral states are structurally locked into primary commodity exports.

## Labor Exploitation

[[Silver 2003]] documents how global supply chains relocate production to sites of cheap labor, reproducing Marx's "reserve army of labor" at the global scale. Foxconn's conditions in Shenzhen, garment factories in Bangladesh (post-Rana Plaza), and agricultural labor in the Global South all demonstrate the global proletariat's exploitation. Amazon's warehouse logistics represent a Taylorist intensification of labor discipline.

## Unequal Exchange

[[Emmanuel 1972]] formalized unequal exchange: when wages differ between countries but commodity prices are set globally, surplus value transfers from low-wage to high-wage nations. This is not market failure but the mechanism of global capitalism. [[Amin 1976]] extended this to show how the international monetary system reinforces unequal exchange through dollar hegemony.
