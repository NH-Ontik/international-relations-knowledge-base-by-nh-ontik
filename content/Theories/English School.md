# English School

## Intellectual Origins

The English School emerged in post-war Britain through the British Committee on the Theory of International Politics (1959–1985). Key founders were [[Martin Wight]] and [[Hedley Bull]]. Wight's lectures at the London School of Economics and Bull's seminal work *The Anarchical Society* (1977) laid the foundations. The School synthesised historical, legal, and philosophical approaches to IR, rejecting the stark divide between [[Realism]] and [[Idealism]] dominant in American IR scholarship.

## Core Arguments

The English School's central claim is that states exist in an **international society** — not merely a system of interacting units, but a society bound by shared norms, rules, and institutions. Bull defined international society as: "a group of states, conscious of certain common interests and common values, form a society in the conception of themselves as bound by a common set of rules in their relations with one another, and share in the working of common institutions."

The School distinguishes **three traditions**, drawn from Wight:

1. **Hobbesian/Machiavellian (Realist)** — International politics is a state of war; no moral restraints bind states.
2. **Grotius/Grotian (Rationalist)** — States are bound by international law and diplomatic norms; they form a society.
3. **Kantian/Revolutionist** — Humanity transcends the state system; cosmopolitan justice and universal community are the ultimate goals.

## International System vs International Society vs World Society

Bull and later [[Barry Buzan]] conceptualised three levels:

- **International system** — states interact enough to affect each other's calculations (e.g., [[Ancient Greece]] and Persia).
- **International society** — states share rules, institutions, and a sense of common interest (e.g., the modern [[Sovereignty]]-based order).
- **World society** — individuals, non-state actors, and a global consciousness transcend the state system; this remains aspirational.

## Pluralism vs Solidarism

The central internal debate: can international society go beyond coexistence to pursue justice?

- **Pluralism** (Bull) — international society is thin; states agree on minimal rules (sovereignty, non-intervention, diplomacy) but disagree on deeper values. Order trumps justice. Humanitarian intervention is suspect.
- **Solidarism** ([[Nicholas Wheeler]], [[John Vincent]]) — states can agree on substantive values (human rights, humanitarian intervention). The society can enforce collective norms, even against sovereign states.

## Variants/Offshoots

- **Classical (Wight-Bull)** — focuses on diplomatic history, international law, and normative order.
- **Structural (Buzan)** — systematises the School into a framework compatible with [[Neorealism]] and [[Constructivism]].
- **Normative (Vincent, Wheeler)** — pushes toward solidarism, human rights, and humanitarianism.
- **World Society approach (Buzan)** — extends analysis beyond states to non-state actors and individual identity.

## Critiques

- **From [[Realism]]** — international society is thin; states act on interest not norms; great powers ignore rules when vital interests are at stake (e.g., the [[Ukraine War]]).
- **From [[Critical Theory]]** — the English School naturalises the state system and ignores structural inequality, [[colonialism]], and [[patriarchy]].
- **From [[Postcolonialism]]** — international society was built through colonisation; non-European states were excluded from "society" for centuries. The "standard of civilisation" was a tool of empire.
- **Methodological** — the School lacks a rigorous causal theory; it is more descriptive than explanatory.

## Key Questions

- Can international society survive challenges from rising powers (e.g., China) that reject liberal norms?
- Does solidarist intervention undermine the very sovereignty that makes international society possible?
- Is world society a realistic goal, or does state sovereignty remain the only viable basis for order?
- How does the [[Responsibility to Protect]] (R2P) fit into the pluralist-solidarist spectrum?
