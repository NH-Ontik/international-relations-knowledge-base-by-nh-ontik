# International Political Economy

## Intellectual Origins

International Political Economy (IPE) emerged as a distinct subfield in the 1970s, driven by the collapse of the [[Bretton Woods Institutions|Bretton Woods]] system, the oil crises, and the growing interdependence of national economies. Scholars sought to bridge the gap between economics and politics, arguing that markets and states are mutually constitutive. Foundational works include [[Robert Gilpin]]'s *U.S. Power and the Multinational Corporation* (1975), [[Charles Kindleberger]]'s *The World in Depression* (1973), and [[Susan Strange]]'s work on structural power.

## Three Main Perspectives

### 1. Economic Liberalism
Rooted in [[Liberalism|classical liberal]] thought ([[Adam Smith]], [[David Ricardo]]). Core claims:
- Free trade produces mutual gains through comparative advantage.
- Markets are efficient and self-regulating.
- States should minimise intervention and allow capital, goods, and labour to flow freely.
- International institutions ([[World Trade Organization|WTO]], [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]]) facilitate cooperation and reduce transaction costs.

Key scholars: [[Richard Cooper]], [[Robert Keohane]] (after his institutionalist turn), [[Jagdish Bhagwati]]. The perspective underpins [[globalisation]] theory and contemporary trade liberalisation.

### 2. Economic Nationalism / Mercantilism
Rooted in [[Realism|realist]] thought ([[Alexander Hamilton]], [[Friedrich List]]). Core claims:
- The economy is a tool of state power; markets serve national interests.
- The state should protect strategic industries, manage trade balances, and use economic leverage.
- Free trade is not naturally occurring but imposed by powerful states that have already industrialised.

Key scholars: [[Robert Gilpin]] (hegemonic stability theory), [[Eric Helleiner]]. [[Donald Trump]]'s tariff policies and [[Xi Jinping]]'s state-led development model reflect this perspective.

### 3. Marxism / Dependency / World-Systems Theory
Rooted in [[Marxism|Marxist]] thought. Core claims:
- Capitalism is a global system of exploitation between core (wealthy) and periphery (poor) states.
- Free trade perpetuates inequality through unequal exchange.
- The state serves capitalist class interests.

Key variants:
- **Dependency theory** ([[Andre Gunder Frank]], [[Fernando Henrique Cardoso]]) — development in the core causes underdevelopment in the periphery.
- **World-systems theory** ([[Immanuel Wallerstein]]) — the modern world-system is a single capitalist economy divided into core, semi-periphery, and periphery.

## Key Debates

### Hegemonic Stability Theory
[[Charles Kindleberger]] argued that a liberal international economy requires a single hegemonic power to provide public goods (open markets, stable currency, security). The UK (19th century) and US (post-1945) served this role. [[Robert Gilpin]] refined the theory, arguing that hegemony eventually declines as the costs of provision exceed benefits. Critics ([[Robert Keohane]]) argue that cooperation can persist after hegemony through international institutions.

### Dependency vs Modernisation
Dependency theorists challenged the [[modernisation]] thesis ([[W.W. Rostow]]) that all countries follow a linear path from traditional to modern. Instead, they argued that the global economic structure actively blocks development in the periphery.

### Globalisation Debate
- **Hyperglobalisers** ([[Kenichi Ohmae]]) — the state is obsolete; global markets govern.
- **Sceptics** ([[Paul Hirst]], [[Grahame Thompson]]) — the global economy is less integrated than in the 19th century; states retain power.
- **Transformationalists** ([[David Held]], [[Anthony Giddens]]) — globalisation is real but contingent, reshaping sovereignty rather than ending it.

## Critiques

- **From [[Feminism]]** — IPE ignores the reproductive and care economy; the household is treated as invisible.
- **From [[Postcolonialism]]** — both liberal and Marxist IPE are Eurocentric; non-Western agency and knowledge are erased.
- **From [[Constructivism]]** — IPE under-theorises identity and norms; economic interests are socially constructed.
- **Methodological** — the three-perspective framework is too rigid; contemporary phenomena (global supply chains, digital trade, climate finance) blur these boundaries.

## Key Questions

- Is US-China economic competition best explained by liberalism (efficiency), nationalism (power), or dependency (exploitation)?
- Does the [[World Trade Organization]] benefit all members or primarily the core?
- Can the [[Global South]] achieve development within the current capitalist world-system?
- Is deglobalisation/de-risking (post-2020) a cycle or a permanent shift in the global economy?
