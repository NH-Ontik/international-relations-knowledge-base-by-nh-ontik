# Critical Theory

## Intellectual Origins

Critical Theory in IR draws primarily from the **Frankfurt School** of social theory — particularly [[Max Horkheimer]], [[Theodor Adorno]], and later [[Jürgen Habermas]]. Horkheimer's 1937 essay "Traditional and Critical Theory" drew the foundational distinction: traditional theory seeks to describe, predict, and control; critical theory seeks to emancipate.

The Frankfurt School developed in the Weimar and Nazi periods, combining [[Marxism]] (especially the young Marx), Freudian psychoanalysis, and [[Kantian]] critique. Their project was to understand why the working class failed to revolt and why modernity produced fascism, not freedom.

Critical IR theory emerged in the 1980s as a rejection of the positivism and problem-solving orientation of mainstream [[Neorealism]] and [[Neoliberal Institutionalism]]. Two landmark texts: [[Robert Cox]]'s "Social Forces, States and World Orders" (1981) and [[Andrew Linklater]]'s *Men and Citizens in the Theory of International Relations* (1982).

## Core Arguments

### "Theory is always for someone and for some purpose"
Cox's most famous dictum encapsulates the core claim: no theory is neutral. All knowledge is produced from a standpoint and serves particular interests. Mainstream IR theory serves the interests of the powerful — it takes the existing order as natural and asks how to manage it better.

### Problem-Solving vs Critical Theory
Cox's distinction:

| Problem-Solving Theory | Critical Theory |
|-----------------------|-----------------|
| Takes the world as given | Questions how the world came to be |
| Works within existing institutions | Stands apart from existing order |
| Serves the status quo | Seeks emancipation |
| Fragmented, specialised | Holistic and historical |
| Examples: Neorealism, Neoliberal Institutionalism | Examples: Cox, Linklater, Ashley |

### Emancipation
For critical theorists, the goal of inquiry is **emancipation** — freeing people from unnecessary constraints, including:
- Material exploitation (economic domination)
- Discursive domination (who gets to speak, whose knowledge counts)
- Institutional domination (oppressive structures like patriarchy, colonialism, the states system)

### Critique of Positivism
[[Richard Ashley]]'s "The Poverty of Neorealism" (1984) attacked neorealism's claim to scientific objectivity. He argued that neorealism's positivism:
- Naturalises the state system as though it were a law of nature
- Erases human agency and history
- Serves as an ideology of power politics

## Variants/Offshoots

- **Gramscian IR** (Cox, [[Stephen Gill]]) — applies [[Antonio Gramsci]]'s concept of hegemony to global politics: power is exercised not just through coercion but through consent and the spread of ideas. The [[Washington Consensus]] is a hegemonic ideology. Gill's "new constitutionalism" describes how neoliberal rules constrain democratic choice.
- **Critical Security Studies** ([[Booth]], [[Keith Krause]], [[Michael C. Williams]]) — broadens security beyond the state to encompass individuals; emancipation replaces power as the goal.
- **Habermasian Critical Theory / Discourse Ethics** (Linklater) — uses Habermas's theory of communicative action to argue that states should be held to norms of democratic dialogue.
- **Frankfurt School fourth generation** ([[Axel Honneth]]) — recognition theory applied to IR: domination is not just economic but a failure of mutual recognition between states, cultures, and peoples.

## Critique of Positivism and Post-Positivism

Critical theory was the first wave of **post-positivist** IR, opening the door for [[Constructivism]], [[Poststructuralism]], [[Feminism]], and [[Postcolonialism]]. It shares with these approaches:

- The rejection of the fact/value distinction
- The claim that knowledge is socially constructed
- The emphasis on reflexivity (theorists must interrogate their own position)

However, critical theory distinguishes itself by maintaining a normative commitment to **emancipation** that some post-structuralists reject as impossibly modernist.

## Distinction from Marxism

Critical theory in IR is often confused with [[Marxism]], but the differences are significant:

| Marxism | Critical Theory |
|---------|----------------|
| Focus on economic structure (base) | Focus on ideas, knowledge, culture (superstructure matters) |
| Class is the primary contradiction | Multiple axes of domination (class, gender, race, species) |
| Revolution as the goal | Emancipatory reform; communicative consensus |
| Positivist/scientific in some variants | Explicitly anti-positivist |
| Deterministic historical trajectory | Contingent and open-ended |

## Critiques

- **From [[Neorealism]]** — critical theory provides no concrete policy guidance; emancipation is vague and unworkable in an anarchic world; great powers will not be "emancipated" into cooperation.
- **From [[Poststructuralism]]** — critical theory retains an Enlightenment faith in reason and progress that is itself a form of Western domination; "emancipation" can become a new metanarrative.
- **From [[Postcolonialism]]** — critical theory remains Eurocentric; Habermas's discourse ethics assumes a Western model of rational dialogue; Cox insufficiently addresses [[colonialism]] and race.
- **From [[Feminism]]** — critical theory's "emancipation" tends to be gender-blind; the subject of critical theory is implicitly male.
- **From [[Pragmatism]]** — the distinction between problem-solving and critical theory is overdrawn; all theory is both.

## Key Questions

- What would an emancipated world order actually look like?
- Can critical theory move beyond critique to offer positive institutional design?
- Does the concept of "hegemony" (Gramscian) help explain the endurance of [[neoliberalism]] despite its crises?
- How can critical security studies address new threats (climate change, pandemics, AI) without falling into securitisation traps?
