# Constructivism

## Core Premises
1. **Ideational factors matter** — Ideas, norms, identities, and culture are as important as material factors (power, resources)
2. **Identities shape interests** — Who states *think they are* determines what they *want*
3. **Anarchy is what states make of it** — The international system is not inherently conflictual or cooperative; it depends on shared ideas (Wendt's key contribution)
4. **Intersubjective knowledge** — Meaning is constructed through social interaction, not given by nature
5. **Change is possible** — If identities and norms can be constructed, they can be reconstructed
6. **Agent-structure co-constitution** — Actors and structures shape each other mutually

## Key Thinkers
- **Alexander Wendt** — "Anarchy is what states make of it" (1992), *Social Theory of International Politics* (1999)
- **Nicholas Onuf** — *World of Our Making* (1989), coined "constructivism" in IR
- **Martha Finnemore** — Norms, international organisations as teachers of norms
- **Kathryn Sikkink** — Transnational advocacy networks, norm cascades, "The Power of Principles"
- **Ted Hopf** — Identity and foreign policy, social construction of Cold War
- **Emanuel Adler** — Communities of practice, epistemic communities
- **Friedrich Kratochwil** — Rules, norms, and the role of language

## Variants / Schools

### Conventional Constructivism
- Focus on shared norms, identities
- Still takes states seriously, works within social science
- Wendt, Finnemore — closer to mainstream IR

### Critical / Postmodern Constructivism
- Focus on language, discourse, deconstruction of power/knowledge
- How does discourse *constitute* reality?
- Campbell, Doty — closer to [[Postcolonialism]] and post-structuralism

## Key Concepts Introduced by Constructivism
- **Logics of consequences vs. logics of appropriateness** — Do actors act based on expected outcomes, or because norms tell them certain action is "right"?
- **Life cycle of norms** — Emergence → cascade → internalisation
- **Identity formation** — A state's identity (e.g., "great power" vs. "victim") shapes foreign policy
- **International society** — Not impersonal system; a society with shared norms (English School overlap)

## Strengths
- Explains *change* in IR — where realists say "always the same", constructivists show how norms *evolve* (slavery, colonialism, chemical weapons, women's rights)
- Explains non-material factors that matter — credibility, legitimacy, reputation
- Bridges IR and social theory — connects to broader sociology
- Captures the power of legitimating discourse — why states justify their actions

## Critiques
- **Hard to measure** — Norms, identities, ideas are hard to operationalise empirically
- **Teleological** — Norm evolution stories often imply inevitable progress
- **Overestimates norm power** — States frequently violate norms when it suits them (realist critique)
- **Agnostic about norm content** — Norms can be bad (colonialism, Nazism) — constructivism cannot evaluate them
- **Methodologically weak** — Predominantly interpretive, hard to falsify
