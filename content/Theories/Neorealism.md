# Neorealism

## Intellectual Origins

Neorealism (or structural realism) was developed by [[Kenneth Waltz]] in his landmark *Theory of International Politics* (1979). Waltz sought to correct [[classical realism]]'s reliance on human nature as an explanatory variable. Drawing on [[microeconomics]] ([[Milton Friedman]]'s market analogy) and [[sociological theory]] ([[Émile Durkheim]]'s concept of structure), Waltz built a parsimonious theory grounded in the structure of the international system.

The impetus was the failure of classical realism to explain the persistence of the [[Cold War]] — a period of bipolar stability that human-nature accounts could not predict. Waltz offered a scientific, law-like theory that could generate testable predictions.

## Core Arguments

### The Three Elements of Structure

Waltz defines the structure of the international system by three principles:

1. **Ordering principle** — the system is **anarchic** (no central authority). This distinguishes international politics from domestic politics, which is hierarchical. See [[Anarchy]].
2. **Functional differentiation** — none. In anarchy, all states are functionally alike: they perform the same basic tasks (defence, extraction, rule-making). Unlike firms in a market (which specialise), states in anarchy do not specialise — they must all provide their own security.
3. **Distribution of capabilities (polarity)** — the number of great powers in the system. The key variable is whether the system is unipolar, bipolar, or multipolar. See [[Polarity]].

### The Logic of Anarchy

In an anarchic system, states face an **uncertainty problem**: they can never be certain of others' intentions. This forces states to:

- **Self-help** — each state must rely on its own capabilities for survival.
- **Security-seeking** — survival is the primary motive.
- **The [[Security Dilemma]]** — actions taken to increase one state's security (e.g., arming) decrease others' security, triggering arms races and conflict.

The system constrains and socialises states. Those that behave as the system requires survive; those that do not are eliminated. Over time, all states converge on similar balancing behaviour.

### Static Theory

Unlike classical realism, neorealism is static. The distribution of capabilities changes, but the logic of anarchy is constant. Waltz was not interested in explaining specific foreign policies (a unit-level phenomenon) but in the **recurrent patterns** of international outcomes — above all, the tendency toward [[Balance of Power]].

## Offensive vs Defensive Realism

The central division within neorealism:

| Offensive Realism | Defensive Realism |
|------------------|-------------------|
| **[[John Mearsheimer]]** (*The Tragedy of Great Power Politics*) | [[Stephen Walt]], [[Jack Snyder]], [[Robert Jervis]] |
| Maximise power; hegemony is the goal | Security is the goal; too much power provokes balancing |
| Revisionism is inevitable | Status quo is possible; states are security-seekers not power-maximisers |
| Anarchy forces states to be aggressive | Anarchy forces caution; aggression backfires |
| Explains the [[Ukraine War]] as great power competition | Explains NATO enlargement as triggering Russia unnecessarily |
| Predicts US-China conflict (unipolar moment will end in war) | Predicts balancing constraints will moderate conflict |

### Why Offensive and Defensive?

Offensive realists believe the security dilemma is **tight** and **indivisible**: any gain for one side is a loss for the other. Defensive realists argue the dilemma can be mitigated by:
- The [[offence-defence balance]] (Jervis) — when defence dominates, states can be secure without aggression.
- Strategic restraint and reassurance (sending costly signals).
- Geography (Walt's [[balance of threat]] theory: threats are a function of capability + geography + perceived intentions).

## Distinction from Classical Realism

| Classical Realism | Neorealism |
|------------------|-----------|
| [[Thucydides]], [[Hans Morgenthau]], [[Reinhold Niebuhr]] | Kenneth Waltz, John Mearsheimer, Stephen Walt |
| Human nature (lust for power, fear, honour) | System structure (anarchy, polarity) |
| States seek power because humans do | States seek security because the system forces them to |
| Foreign policy theory (explains specific decisions) | International politics theory (explains system-level outcomes) |
| Pessimistic but allows for statecraft | Deterministic — structure dictates outcomes |
| Normative: how should states behave? | Positive: how does the system work? |

## Key Propositions

- **Balancing** (not bandwagoning) is the dominant behaviour — states ally against threats, not with them.
- **Bipolarity is more stable than multipolarity** — fewer major powers means fewer potential conflict dyads and less miscalculation.
- **Nuclear weapons reduce the likelihood of major war** — Waltz's controversial argument for nuclear proliferation (more nukes = more caution).
- **Unipolarity is the least durable structure** — other powers will eventually balance against the hegemon.

## Critiques

- **From [[Liberalism]]** — neorealism ignores the domestic politics, international institutions, and economic interdependence that shape state behaviour. The [[Democratic Peace Theory]] shows that democracies do not balance against each other.
- **From [[Constructivism]]** ([[Alexander Wendt]]) — "anarchy is what states make of it." The logic of anarchy is not fixed; it depends on whether states hold friends or enemies. Structural change is possible.
- **From [[Critical Theory]]** — neorealism naturalises the state system and forecloses emancipatory possibilities. It serves the interests of great powers.
- **From [[Feminism]]** — the theory is gender-blind; concepts like "security" and "survival" are masculine; the state is implicitly male-gendered.
- **Empirical failures** — neorealism predicted the [[Cold War]] would persist (USSR collapsed peacefully); predicted that [[European Union]] integration would fail (it deepened); predicted that [[NATO]] would dissolve after the Cold War (it enlarged).

## Key Questions

- Does neorealism explain the [[Ukraine War]], or are unit-level factors (Putin's ideology, NATO decision-making) more important?
- Can neorealism explain rising powers like China and India, or does it underestimate domestic transformation?
- Is the current system best described as unipolar, multipolar, or something new (non-polarity, G-zero)?
- Does neorealism's static structuralism leave any room for agency, resistance, or historical change?
