# Apartheid

Apartheid (Afrikaans: "apartness") was a system of institutionalised racial segregation and discrimination enforced by the South African government from 1948 to 1994. It is a central case study for [[Postcolonialism]], [[Structural Violence]], and [[Peace and Conflict Studies]].

## Structure of Oppression

The Population Registration Act (1950) classified all South Africans into racial groups: White, Black, Coloured, and Indian. The Group Areas Act enforced physical separation of residences and businesses. The Pass Laws controlled Black movement into "white" areas. Non-whites were denied the vote, access to quality education, and freedom of movement. **Galtung's concept of [[Structural Violence]]** is vividly illustrated here: violence embedded in the legal, economic, and political architecture itself, not requiring direct physical force to produce harm.

## Domestic Resistance

The African National Congress (ANC), led by figures including Nelson Mandela and Oliver Tambo, organised strikes, civil disobedience, and ultimately armed struggle after the Sharpeville Massacre (1960). The Soweto Uprising (1976) marked a turning point, as schoolchildren protested compulsory Afrikaans instruction and were met with lethal force.

## International Dimensions

Apartheid became a global cause. The UN imposed arms embargoes (1977), cultural and sports boycotts, and economic sanctions. Divestment campaigns pressured Western corporations to leave South Africa. The anti-apartheid movement demonstrated the power of transnational civil society and is a key reference in [[International Law]].

## Transition and TRC

F.W. de Klerk unbanned the ANC in 1990 and released Mandela. The 1994 democratic elections ended apartheid. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), chaired by Desmond Tutu, pursued restorative rather than retributive [[Justice]], offering amnesty in exchange for full truth. This model of [[Transitional Justice]] has been studied and replicated globally.
