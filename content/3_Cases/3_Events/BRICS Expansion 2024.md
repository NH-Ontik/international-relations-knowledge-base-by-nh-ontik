# BRICS Expansion 2024

## Overview
The expansion of BRICS in January 2024, admitting Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates (with Argentina declining under President Milei), represents the most significant restructuring of the grouping since its founding in 2009. The expansion transforms BRICS from a five-member emerging-market coalition into a broader coalition of states seeking to reshape the architecture of global governance, de-dollarise international finance, and provide an institutional alternative to Western-dominated institutions such as the G7, IMF, and World Bank (Stuenkel, 2016; Cooper & Thakur, 2023).

## Key Themes
- **Original BRICS** — Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa joined the grouping primarily as an economic coordination mechanism, but its geopolitical significance grew as the five states' collective share of global GDP surpassed that of the G7 in purchasing power parity terms (IMF, 2023)
- **2024 expansion** — the addition of Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE dramatically expanded BRICS' geographic, demographic, and resource base, particularly incorporating major Middle Eastern oil producers (Cooper & Thakur, 2023)
- **De-dollarisation agenda** — BRICS states have explored alternatives to dollar-denominated trade, including bilateral currency swaps, the New Development Bank's lending in local currencies, and discussions of a common BRICS currency, though implementation remains limited (Subacchi, 2023)
- **New Development Bank (NDB)** — headquartered in Shanghai, the NDB provides an alternative to the World Bank and IMF for development finance, though its lending volume remains a fraction of its Western counterparts (NDB, 2023)
- **Alternative governance order** — BRICS explicitly positions itself as an alternative to the G7-led liberal international order, though internal divergences (India-China border tensions, Saudi-Iran rivalry) limit its coherence as a counter-hegemonic bloc (Stuenkel, 2016)
- **Internal heterogeneity** — the expanded BRICS includes democracies (India, Brazil, South Africa) and autocracies (China, Russia, Saudi Arabia, Iran, UAE), market economies and state-directed systems, and states with conflicting strategic interests, challenging the grouping's coherence (Malamud, 2023)

## Related Theories
- [[Global Governance]] — BRICS expansion reflects the growing demand for governance reform in institutions designed in the post-1945 era; the expansion demonstrates that non-Western states are creating parallel institutions rather than reforming existing ones from within (Ikenberry, 2018)
- [[Hegemony]] — BRICS challenges US-led hegemony through institutional multiplication rather than direct confrontation, demonstrating that hegemonic challenge can take institutional rather than military form (Hegel, 1837/1956; Keohane, 1984)
- [[Middle Powers]] — several BRICS states (Brazil, India, South Africa) fit the middle-power category; their participation in BRICS amplifies their collective influence beyond what any single middle power could achieve (Cooper, 1997)
- [[Complex Interdependence]] — the economic interdependence of BRICS states with Western economies constrains their capacity for radical institutional disruption, illustrating the limits of institutional counter-hegemony (Keohane & Nye, 1977)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- First BRIC summit (2009) — Russia, China, India, and Brazil established the grouping in Yekaterinburg, framing it as a coordination mechanism for emerging-market interests
- South Africa's inclusion (2010) — transformed BRIC into BRICS, incorporating the African continent's largest economy and enhancing the grouping's global South credentials
- Johannesburg II summit (2018) — first formal discussion of expansion, signalling BRICS' ambition to grow beyond its founding membership
- BRICS expansion announcement (August 2023) — the Johannesburg summit agreed to admit six states (Argentina later declined), effective January 2024
- New Development Bank expansion (2024) — NDB membership expanded to include new BRICS members, increasing the bank's capital base and lending capacity (NDB, 2023)

## Key Questions
1. Can BRICS maintain internal coherence given its members' divergent strategic interests?
2. Is de-dollarisation a realistic objective, or aspirational rhetoric?
3. Does BRICS expansion strengthen or dilute the grouping's strategic purpose?
4. How does BRICS compare with the G7 as a governance mechanism?
5. Can the New Development Bank become a genuine alternative to the IMF and World Bank?
