# Bangladesh Liberation

## Overview
The Bangladesh Liberation War (March–December 1971) was the armed conflict in which East Pakistan became the independent nation of Bangladesh after decades of political, economic, and linguistic marginalisation by West Pakistan. It is a critical case study for [[Self-Determination]], genocide, and [[Humanitarian Intervention]] in international relations.

## Background
After the partition of India (1947), Pakistan comprised two geographically and culturally distinct territories separated by 1,600 km of India:
- **West Pakistan** — dominated the military, civil service, and economy
- **East Pakistan (now Bangladesh)** — larger population (~56% of total Pakistan) but politically and economically marginalised
Linguistic repression (Urdu imposed over Bengali), economic exploitation (export revenues from East Pakistan's jute used for West Pakistan's development), and democratic denial (election results annulled) fuelled a growing independence movement led by Sheikh Mujibur Rahman and the Awami League.

## The War
Key events:
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| March 25, 1971 | Operation Searchlight — Pakistan military crackdown in Dhaka; genocide begins |
| March 26, 1971 | Mujib declares independence; arrested |
| April–November | Bangladesh liberation forces (Mukti Bahini) with Indian support wage guerrilla war |
| December 3, 1971 | India intervenes militarily (Indo-Pakistani War) |
| December 16, 1971 | Pakistan surrenders; Bangladesh is independent |

## Genocide
The Pakistan military systematically targeted Bengali civilians, intellectuals, students, and Hindus. Estimates: 300,000 to 3,000,000 killed. Systematic rape used as a weapon of war (~200,000–400,000 women). 10+ million refugees fled to India. It is widely regarded as a genocide, though it lacks formal UN recognition.

## Humanitarian Intervention Debate
India's intervention in December 1971 is a key case in the [[Humanitarian Intervention]] literature:
- **Justifications**: Massive civilian suffering, refugee burden, Pakistan's inability to govern its own territory, military crackdown (indiscriminate and disproportionate)
- **Critiques**: India had strategic interests (weakening Pakistan, creating a friendly state, regional hegemony); intervention was selective — no intervention in Tibet, Cambodia, etc.
- **Significance**: 1971 is often cited by proponents of a responsibility to protect civilians from mass atrocities, but it was unilaterally justified without UN authorisation

## Self-Determination
The Bangladesh case is one of the most successful applications of the principle of [[Self-Determination]]. A culturally distinct, geographically separated, and democratically marginalised population achieved statehood through armed struggle. This contrasts with the many failed or unrecognised separatist movements elsewhere.

## Theoretical Significance
- **[[Humanitarian Intervention]]** — Was India's motivation humanitarian or strategic?
- **[[Self-Determination]]** — When does a group have the right to form its own state?
- **Genocide studies** — The case of unrecognised genocide
- **R2P** — Anticipates the responsibility to protect debate by 30 years
