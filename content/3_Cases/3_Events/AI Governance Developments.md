# AI Governance Developments

## Overview
The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies — particularly large language models, generative AI, and autonomous systems — has generated a new frontier of global governance, with states and international organisations scrambling to establish regulatory frameworks before the technology outpaces the capacity for oversight. The AI governance landscape reflects the broader tensions in IR between innovation and security, between national sovereignty and international coordination, and between liberal democratic and authoritarian models of technology governance (Calzati, 2023; Roberts et al., 2021).

## Key Themes
- **G7 Hiroshima AI Process** — the G7's 2023 Hiroshima AI Process established a framework for coordinating AI governance among the world's largest democracies, though its non-binding nature limits its enforcement capacity (G7, 2023)
- **EU AI Act** — the European Union's AI Act (2024), the world's first comprehensive AI regulation, classifies AI systems by risk level and imposes transparency, accountability, and safety requirements, setting a global regulatory standard through the "Brussels effect" (Bradford, 2020; EU, 2024)
- **US Executive Order on AI** — the Biden administration's October 2023 executive order established safety standards, transparency requirements, and civil rights protections for AI systems, though its durability under subsequent administrations remains uncertain (White House, 2023)
- **UK AI Safety Summit (Bletchley Park)** — the November 2023 summit produced the "Bletchley Declaration" on frontier AI safety, establishing the UK as a convenor on AI governance, though the declaration lacks binding commitments (UK Government, 2023)
- **China's AI regulation** — China has implemented regulation of generative AI, deepfakes, and algorithmic recommendation systems, pursuing a governance model that emphasises state control and social stability over individual rights or innovation freedom (Roberts et al., 2021)
- **AI military applications** — the development of autonomous weapons systems, AI-enabled surveillance, and AI-powered cyber weapons raises fundamental questions about the laws of armed conflict, human control over lethal force, and the future of deterrence (Scharre, 2018)

## Related Theories
- [[Global Governance]] — AI governance represents a paradigmatic case of the "governance gap": technology develops faster than institutions can regulate it, creating regulatory races and jurisdictional arbitrage (Nye, 2021)
- [[International Law]] — the application of existing international law (laws of armed conflict, human rights law) to AI systems is contested, and no comprehensive AI treaty exists; debates about whether to develop AI-specific international law mirror earlier debates about nuclear and cyber governance (Schmitt, 2017)
- [[Deterrence]] — AI's military applications raise fundamental questions about whether deterrence theory, developed for nuclear weapons, can be extended to AI-enabled warfare, particularly in the domains of cyber and autonomous weapons (Scharre, 2018)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- ChatGPT launch (November 2022) — OpenAI's release of ChatGPT catalysed global attention to AI capabilities and risks, accelerating both investment and regulatory concern
- G7 Hiroshima AI Process (May 2023) — the G7 established a coordination framework for AI governance among democratic states
- UK AI Safety Summit (November 2023) — the Bletchley Declaration established international consensus on the need for AI safety, though without binding commitments (UK Government, 2023)
- EU AI Act adoption (2024) — the world's first comprehensive AI regulation set a global precedent for risk-based governance of AI systems (EU, 2024)
- AI arms race concerns (2023–present) — the development of military AI applications, including autonomous weapons and AI-enabled surveillance, has intensified debates about whether AI governance can keep pace with military innovation (Scharre, 2018)

## Key Questions
1. Can international AI governance emerge before the technology becomes ungovernable?
2. Will the "Brussels effect" (EU regulation as global standard) apply to AI, or will states pursue divergent regulatory models?
3. Does AI military application require new international law, or can existing laws of armed conflict suffice?
4. How should international law address the dual-use nature of AI (civilian and military applications)?
5. Can AI governance accommodate the strategic competition between democratic and authoritarian models of technology governance?
