# COVID-19 and Global Governance

## Overview
The COVID-19 pandemic (2019–23) constituted the most significant test of global governance structures since the founding of the United Nations, exposing the tensions between national sovereignty and collective action, between international institutional capacity and state retrenchment, and between liberal internationalism and great-power competition. The pandemic accelerated pre-existing trends toward geopolitical fragmentation while simultaneously demonstrating the indispensable need for international cooperation in managing transnational threats (Gostin & Wiley, 2016; Krause & Krause, 2022).

## Key Themes
- **WHO reform failures** — the World Health Organisation's initial response, including delayed declaration of a public health emergency of international concern (PHEIC), revealed institutional weaknesses in surveillance, reporting, and political independence (Gostin & Halabi, 2020)
- **Vaccine nationalism** — wealthy states secured bilateral vaccine contracts and restricted exports, leaving the Global South dependent on Chinese and Russian vaccine diplomacy, undermining the COVAX mechanism designed to ensure equitable distribution (Eccleston-Turner & Upton, 2021)
- **COVAX and equitable access** — the COVAX facility, co-led by WHO, Gavi, and CEPI, delivered over 1.9 billion doses but failed to meet its equitable distribution targets, exposing the structural inequalities in global health governance (Wouters et al., 2021)
- **Supply chain disruptions** — pandemic-induced disruptions to global supply chains, particularly for medical equipment and semiconductors, prompted debates about reshoring, nearshoring, and economic decoupling that accelerated pre-existing deglobalisation trends (Baldwin & Evenett, 2020)
- **Great power competition** — China's "mask diplomacy" and "vaccine diplomacy" sought to leverage pandemic response for geopolitical advantage, while the US retreat from WHO under the Trump administration created a vacuum in global health leadership (Huang, 2020)
- **Securitisation of health** — the pandemic accelerated the securitisation of public health, with states invoking emergency powers, border closures, and surveillance technologies that raised civil liberties concerns (McInnes & Lee, 2012)

## Related Theories
- [[Global Governance]] — the pandemic exposed the gap between the institutional architecture of global health governance (WHO, IHR, COVAX) and the political capacity to enforce collective action, particularly when great powers are disengaged or adversarial (Nye, 2021)
- [[International Organizations]] — the WHO's constrained authority, dependent on member-state cooperation and voluntary funding, demonstrated the structural limitations of international organisations when sovereignty claims override institutional mandates (Barnett & Finnemore, 2004)
- [[Health Governance]] — COVID-19 established pandemic preparedness and response as a central concern of international relations, elevating health security alongside traditional security paradigms (Kickbusch et al., 2017)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Wuhan lockdown (January 2020) — China's unprecedented lockdown of a city of 11 million signalled the scale of the emerging crisis and raised questions about transparency and information sharing
- WHO declaration of pandemic (March 2020) — the belated declaration reflected both the severity of the crisis and the political constraints on WHO leadership
- COVAX launch (September 2020) — the establishment of the equitable distribution mechanism represented a liberal institutionalist response to vaccine nationalism, though its implementation fell short of ambitions (Eccleston-Turner & Upton, 2021)
- US withdrawal from WHO (July 2020) — the Trump administration's withdrawal from the WHO, partially reversed by the Biden administration, demonstrated the vulnerability of international institutions to domestic political shifts
- China's zero-COVID reversal (December 2022) — China's abrupt abandonment of its zero-COVID policy after mass protests exposed the political costs of authoritarian pandemic management

## Key Questions
1. Did COVID-19 strengthen or weaken the case for global governance institutions?
2. How did vaccine nationalism affect the liberal international order's legitimacy?
3. Can pandemic preparedness be securitised without normalising authoritarian surveillance?
4. Did China's "vaccine diplomacy" achieve lasting geopolitical gains?
5. What reforms would make global health governance more effective for future pandemics?
