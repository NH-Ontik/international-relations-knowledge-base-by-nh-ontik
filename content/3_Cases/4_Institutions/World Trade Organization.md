# World Trade Organization

## Overview

The World Trade Organization (WTO), established on 1 January 1995 as the successor to the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT, 1947), is the foundational institution of the multilateral trading system. With 164 members accounting for over 98% of global trade, the WTO provides the legal and institutional framework for negotiating trade rules, monitoring compliance, and resolving disputes. It represents the crowning institutional achievement of post-war liberal internationalism—an attempt to embed free trade in a rules-based, legally binding framework.

## Key Themes

- **GATT to WTO**: GATT operated as a *de facto* organisation through rounds of tariff negotiations (the Kennedy, Tokyo, and Uruguay Rounds). The Uruguay Round (1986–1994) was transformative: it created the WTO as a formal organisation with a binding **Dispute Settlement Mechanism (DSM)** —the "crown jewel" of the system—and extended trade rules to services (GATS), intellectual property (TRIPS), and agriculture.
- **Dispute Settlement Mechanism**: The DSM is compulsory, binding, and includes an Appellate Body. It replaced GATT's power-based diplomacy with a rules-based adjudication system. Panels rule on trade disputes; the losing party must comply or face authorised retaliation. This quasi-judicial character makes the WTO unique among international economic institutions.
- **Liberal Institutionalist Logic**: The WTO lowers transaction costs for trade negotiations, provides information through Trade Policy Reviews, and facilitates reciprocity and enforcement, making it a paradigmatic case of an **International Regime** (Krasner) embedded in wider **Bretton Woods Institutions**.

## Challenges

The **Doha Development Round** (launched 2001) collapsed in 2008, failing to agree on agricultural subsidies, non-agricultural market access, and services. This deadlock has rendered the WTO incapable of generating new rules for the 21st-century economy—digital trade, e-commerce, data flows, state-owned enterprises. The **Appellate Body** has been paralysed since 2019, after the US blocked appointments under Trump, leaving the DSM without an appeals function. The **US-China trade war** (2018–present) fundamentally challenged the WTO's relevance: both powers imposed tariffs in violation of WTO commitments, exploiting ambiguities in national-security exceptions (GATT Article XXI). The rise of **plurilateralism** (RCEP, CPTPP) and **minilateral deals** reflects a fragmentation of the multilateral trading system.

## Key Questions

- Is the WTO terminally ill, or can it reform?
- Can developing countries gain a fairer deal in a reformed WTO?
- Does the US-China rivalry spell the end of rules-based trade?
- Should climate and labour standards be enforceable through trade rules?
