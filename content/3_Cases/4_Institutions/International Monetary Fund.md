# International Monetary Fund

## Overview

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) was conceived at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, in July 1944 by 44 Allied nations, alongside the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (the World Bank). It began operations in 1945 with a mandate to ensure international monetary stability—preventing competitive devaluations and balance-of-payments crises of the kind that characterised the interwar Great Depression. Today, with 190 member states, the IMF operates as the world's financial firefighter and macroeconomic surveillance authority.

## Key Themes

- **Bretton Woods System**: The IMF was designed to manage a fixed-but-adjustable exchange rate system, providing temporary financing to members facing balance-of-payments deficits. After the collapse of Bretton Woods in 1971–1973, the IMF adapted to a floating-rate world, shifting its focus to surveillance (Article IV consultations), crisis lending, and technical assistance.
- **Conditional Lending**: The IMF's core function is providing loans to members in financial distress—but these loans come with **conditionality**: fiscal austerity, monetary tightening, structural reforms (privatisation, deregulation, trade liberalisation). The theory holds that adjustment restores macroeconomic balance and market confidence. Critics argue conditionality deepens recessions and imposes social costs on the most vulnerable.
- **Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)**: An international reserve asset created in 1969 to supplement member states' official reserves. The 2021 allocation of $650 billion in SDRs—the largest in history—was a critical response to COVID-19 liquidity needs. However, SDRs are distributed by IMF quota (wealth-weighted), meaning developing countries received only a fraction of what they needed.

## Challenges

The IMF faces a **legitimacy crisis** rooted in its governance structure: voting power is weighted by economic size, giving the US an effective veto over major decisions (16.5% of votes) and Europe far more weight than its current economic heft justifies. This has been challenged from **Marxist and dependency-theory perspectives**, which see the IMF as an instrument of Northern capital disciplining the Global South—enforcing neoliberal orthodoxy to open markets for multinational corporations. **Arab Spring countries** (Egypt, Tunisia) experienced IMF-imposed austerity that exacerbated popular unrest. The IMF's **pandemic and climate response** has been slow, and its toolkit—designed for balance-of-payments crises—struggles with climate shocks, debt sustainability, and the rising role of non-Western creditors (China). Echoing the collapse of Bretton Woods, a new non-polar monetary order is emerging: central bank digital currencies (CBDCs), de-dollarisation initiatives, and bilateral swap arrangements erode the IMF's centrality.

## Key Questions

- Can the IMF reform its quota system to reflect 21st-century economic power?
- Does conditionality solve crises or deepen them?
- Is the IMF a neutral technical agency or a political instrument of Western hegemony?
- Can the IMF manage sovereign debt crises when China is the largest bilateral lender?
