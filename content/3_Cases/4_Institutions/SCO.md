# Shanghai Cooperation Organisation

## Overview

The Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) was founded in June 2001 by China, Russia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Uzbekistan, evolving from the "Shanghai Five" mechanism established in 1996 to resolve border disputes. It has since expanded to include India and Pakistan (2017), and Iran (2023), alongside observer and dialogue-partner states including Afghanistan, Mongolia, and Belarus. The SCO represents the most significant multilateral institution in Eurasia outside the Western-led architecture, and is often described (imprecisely) as a "NATO of the East".

## Key Themes

- **Security Cooperation**: The SCO's founding mission was confidence-building along the Sino-Russian and Central Asian borders, and combating the "three evils"—terrorism, separatism, and extremism. The **Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure (RATS)** in Tashkent coordinates intelligence-sharing and joint counter-terrorism exercises. "Separatism" is defined broadly enough to encompass Xinjiang and Chechnya, aligning with regime-security priorities of member states.
- **Anti-Western Orientation**: The 2005 SCO Astana Declaration called for a timeline for US Central Asian bases; member states have consistently opposed Western military intervention (Libya, Syria) and democracy-promotion norms. The SCO articulates an alternative vision of international order based on sovereignty, non-interference, and "respect for civilisational diversity"—often interpreted as a normative counterweight to Western liberal internationalism.
- **Not a Formal Alliance**: Despite speculation, the SCO is not a collective-defence alliance like NATO. It lacks a mutual-defence clause, a unified command structure, and integrated military forces. Joint exercises (Peace Mission series) are bilateral or multilateral but limited in scope. The institutional culture remains consultative rather than hierarchical, consistent with China's foreign-policy doctrine of "non-alignment".

## Challenges

Expansion to include India and Pakistan has introduced bilateral rivalry into the organisation's dynamics. India and Pakistan's mutual hostility—compounded by China and India's border tensions in Ladakh—has paralysed decision-making on many issues, particularly relating to connectivity projects (China's Belt and Road versus India's counter-initiatives) and counter-terrorism designations (each side blocks the other's preferred designees). The organisation's **effectiveness** is limited by its consensus requirement: joint statements are dilute to the lowest common denominator. Russia and China's divergent priorities—Russia pushing for a more overtly anti-NATO posture, China favouring economic and infrastructure cooperation—create strategic tension. The SCO's expansion has diluted its original Central Asian focus, and it risks becoming a **talk shop** rather than an operational security institution.

## Key Questions

- Is the SCO a genuine security community or an anti-Western club?
- Can the SCO survive India-China rivalry within its membership?
- Does the SCO represent a meaningful challenge to Western institutional hegemony?
- How does the SCO relate to overlapping institutions (CSTO, CIS, Eurasian Economic Union)?
