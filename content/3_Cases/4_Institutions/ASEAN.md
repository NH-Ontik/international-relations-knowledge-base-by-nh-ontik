# ASEAN

## Overview

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) was founded on 8 August 1967 with the Bangkok Declaration by Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, and Thailand. It has since expanded to ten members, incorporating Brunei, Vietnam, Laos, Myanmar, and Cambodia, and now encompasses a region of over 660 million people with a combined GDP exceeding $3 trillion. Unlike the EU's dense legalism and supranational delegation, ASEAN operates on a distinct philosophy known as the **ASEAN Way**.

## Key Themes

- **The ASEAN Way**: A set of informal norms centred on strict non-interference in members' internal affairs, decision-making by consensus (musyawarah and mufakat), and a preference for consultation over binding commitments. This approach prioritises regime stability and regime security over liberal-democratic values.
- **Contrast with the EU**: Where the EU pools sovereignty, ASEAN preserves it. Where the EU relies on legal enforcement, ASEAN relies on peer pressure and face-saving. The result is a "soft" regionalism—effective for confidence-building but weak for enforcement.
- **ASEAN Centrality**: The doctrine that ASEAN should drive the architecture of Asia-Pacific regional cooperation, including forums like the ASEAN Regional Forum (ARF) and the East Asia Summit (EAS).
- **The ASEAN Economic Community (AEC)**: Launched 2015, aims to create a single market and production base, but implementation is uneven and non-tariff barriers persist.

## Challenges

ASEAN's non-interference norm has been tested severely. **Myanmar's 2021 military coup** divided the bloc: some members pushed for engagement under the Five-Point Consensus, while others (notably Thailand and the Philippines under Marcos) argued for a softer line. The consensus requirement meant ASEAN could not impose meaningful sanctions, damaging its credibility internationally. The **South China Sea disputes** pit four ASEAN members (Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei) against China, but ASEAN has been unable to issue joint statements condemning Chinese assertiveness because Cambodia (backed by Beijing) blocks consensus. The bloc's **effectiveness problem**—gap between rhetoric and implementation—raises questions about whether ASEAN can remain central to regional order as great-power competition between the US and China intensifies. Internal governance issues in member states (Thailand's military governments, Cambodia's authoritarian turn) further complicate collective action.

## Key Questions

- Does the ASEAN Way promote stability or enable authoritarianism?
- Can ASEAN maintain centrality in a US-China bipolar Indo-Pacific?
- Is the ASEAN model transferable to other regions of the developing world?
- What happens to ASEAN if Myanmar's crisis becomes a permanent civil war?
