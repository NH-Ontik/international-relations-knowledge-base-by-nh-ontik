# World Bank

## Overview
The World Bank is a multilateral development finance institution created alongside the IMF at the [[Bretton Woods Institutions|Bretton Woods Conference]] (1944). Its original purpose: financing European post-war reconstruction. Its current purpose: reducing global poverty through development lending, technical assistance, and policy advice to developing countries.

## Key Institutions
The World Bank Group comprises five institutions, three primary:
- **IBRD** (International Bank for Reconstruction and Development, 1944) — lends to creditworthy middle-income and poor countries at near-market rates
- **IDA** (International Development Association, 1960) — concessional loans and grants to the world's poorest countries; replenished every three years by donor governments
- **IFC** (International Finance Corporation, 1956) — private-sector lending and investment in developing countries

Together, IBRD + IDA are commonly referred to as "the World Bank."

## Governance
- Voting power is weighted by financial contributions (not one-state-one-vote)
- The US is the largest shareholder (~16%); the President has always been an American by informal agreement (with Europe selecting the IMF managing director)
- Criticised as structurally undemocratic — reproducing the 1945 power order

## Evolution of Approach
| Era | Dominant Approach |
|-----|-------------------|
| 1950s–70s | Infrastructure lending (dams, roads, power plants) — modernisation theory |
| 1980s | Structural Adjustment Programmes (SAPs) — conditionalities requiring privatisation, deregulation, fiscal austerity |
| 1990s | Post-Washington Consensus — Poverty Reduction Strategy Papers (PRSPs), emphasis on governance |
| 2000s–present | Results-based lending, climate finance, pandemic preparedness |

## Structural Adjustment and Its Critique
SAPs required borrowing countries to:
- Cut public spending (education, health, subsidies)
- Privatise state enterprises
- Open markets to foreign competition
- Devalue currencies

**Critiques:**
- [[Marxism]]: SAPs enforced neoliberal capitalism on the global periphery; the Bank serves Western capital, not development
- [[Postcolonialism]]: Structural adjustment reproduced colonial extraction — the Global North dictates economic policy to the Global South
- Health and education access declined sharply in adjusted economies
- Conditionalities undermined democratic sovereignty — elected governments lost control of economic policy

## Contemporary Debates
- **Debt and development** — Is the Bank a lender or a debt-trap mechanism?
- **China as alternative** — The [[Belt and Road Initiative]] and AIIB challenge Western-dominated development finance
- **Climate** — The Bank's role in funding fossil fuel projects vs. green transition
- **Reform** — Calls for capital increases, governance reform, and better alignment with recipient priorities
