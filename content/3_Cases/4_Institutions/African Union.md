# African Union

## Overview

The African Union (AU) succeeded the Organisation of African Unity (OAU) in 2002, marking a fundamental shift in continental governance. The OAU (1963–2002) was founded on anti-colonial solidarity and the absolute defence of post-colonial borders and sovereignty—a principle enshrined in *uti possidetis juris*. Its Charter prohibited intervention in member states' internal affairs, and its consensus decision-making rendered it largely toothless in the face of the Rwandan genocide (1994). The AU represents a deliberate institutional break: it explicitly empowers collective intervention in member states under Article 4(h) of its Constitutive Act.

## Key Themes

- **From OAU to AU**: The transition reflected a consensus that the OAU's non-interference model had failed. The AU's Constitutive Act (2000) grants the Union the right to intervene in a member state in grave circumstances—war crimes, genocide, and crimes against humanity. This is the most advanced sovereignty-limiting provision of any regional organisation outside Europe.
- **Institutional Architecture**: The AU is modelled loosely on the EU, with an Assembly of Heads of State, an Executive Council, a Commission (based in Addis Ababa), a Pan-African Parliament, and a Peace and Security Council (PSC). The PSC authorises peace support operations and has deployed missions in Somalia (AMISOM/ATMIS), Sudan (UNAMID), and the Sahel.
- **Agenda 2063**: The AU's strategic framework for a "prosperous, integrated, and peaceful Africa" by 2063, encompassing infrastructure (roads, railways, ICT), the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA), and institutional reforms including self-financing.

## Challenges

The AU faces chronic **resource dependency**—over 60% of its budget is funded by external donors (EU, China, US), which constrains its autonomy and agenda-setting capacity. **Implementation gaps** between AU decisions and member-state compliance are wide, driven by weak bureaucratic capacity and the dominance of sovereignty norms among member states. The Peace and Security Council has struggled with the **proliferation of coups** (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Sudan, Gabon) in the 2020s, suspending members but lacking enforcement mechanisms. Conflict resolution in **Libya** and the **Sahel** remains elusive, and the AU's capacity to mediate Ethiopia's Tigray War (2020–2022) was limited by internal divisions and lack of leverage.

## Key Questions

- Can Article 4(h) be operationalised consistently, or does it remain symbolic?
- Does the AU represent a genuine post-Westphalian shift in African statehood?
- Can the AU achieve financial independence from donor states?
- Is the African Peace and Security Architecture effective without enforcement capabilities?
