# Non-Aligned Movement

The Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) is a forum of states that formally refused to align with either the US-led Western bloc or the Soviet-led Eastern bloc during the [[Cold War]]. It remains the largest political grouping outside the UN system.

## Origins: Bandung Conference (1955)

Twenty-nine Asian and African states met in Bandung, Indonesia, convened by **Sukarno**, **Nehru**, **Nasser**, **Nkrumah**, and **Tito**. The conference condemned colonialism, racial discrimination, and great-power military blocs. It marked the emergence of the Third World as a collective political force, linking [[Postcolonialism]] directly to international security.

## Panchsheel — The Five Principles

1. Mutual respect for territorial integrity and [[Sovereignty]]
2. Mutual non-aggression
3. Mutual non-interference in internal affairs
4. Equality and mutual benefit
5. Peaceful coexistence

These principles shaped [[India Foreign Policy]] and later [[Bangladesh Foreign Policy]], and remain central to many postcolonial diplomatic doctrines.

## Cold War Role

NAM gave newly independent states a platform independent of superpower pressure. However, critics argued it was rhetorical rather than substantive — members often accepted aid or arms from one bloc while claiming non-alignment. The 1979 Soviet invasion of Afghanistan fractured NAM unity.

## Post-Cold War Relevance

With bipolarity gone, NAM struggled to define a new purpose. It has repositioned around development, climate justice, UN reform, and opposition to unilateralism. Its size (120 members) makes it unwieldy, but it remains a symbol of Southern solidarity and a vehicle for challenging Northern-dominated institutions.

## Related Topics

NAM connects to [[Comparative Foreign Policy]] and [[Foreign Policy Analysis]] by illustrating how systemic constraints (bipolarity) and domestic identity (anti-colonial nationalism) interact. It also informs discussions of [[Sovereignty]] in postcolonial contexts.
