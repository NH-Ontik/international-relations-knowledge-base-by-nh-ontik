# Ethiopia-Tigray War

## Overview
The Ethiopia-Tigray War (2020–2022) was the deadliest conflict in the world during its duration, producing an estimated 300,000–500,000 deaths and widespread atrocities including mass sexual violence, forced starvation, and ethnic cleansing. The war pitted the federal government of Nobel Peace Prize laureate Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed against the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF), exposing the fragility of Ethiopia's ethnic federal system and the limits of international humanitarian intervention (Prunier, 2021; Gebregziabher, 2023).

## Key Themes
- **TPLF vs federal government** — the conflict originated in a political rupture between Abiy Ahmed's centralising agenda and the TPLF's decades-long dominance of Ethiopian politics, escalating into full-scale war in November 2020 (Prunier, 2021)
- **Eritrea involvement** — Eritrean forces, under President Isaias Afwerki, fought alongside Ethiopian federal forces against the TPLF, a historic adversary, adding a regional dimension and raising questions about Eritrean territorial ambitions (Scheenen, 2022)
- **Ethnic federalism crisis** — Ethiopia's constitution organises the state along ethnic lines, with the right of secession; the war exposed the structural tensions between ethnic self-governance and national unity (Clapham, 2017)
- **Nobel Peace Prize paradox** — Abiy Ahmed's receipt of the Nobel Peace Prize in 2019 for brokering peace with Eritrea contrasted sharply with his subsequent initiation of the deadliest conflict in the world, raising questions about the Nobel's criteria and the gap between peace rhetoric and war practice (Prunier, 2021)
- **Mass atrocities** — documented atrocities included mass executions, systematic rape as a weapon of war, deliberate starvation through aid blockades, and extrajudicial killings by all parties (Amnesty International, 2021)
- **Pretoria Agreement (2022)** — the AU-mediated ceasefire ended major combat operations but left unresolved questions about accountability, TPLF disarmament, and the status of Eritrean forces (AU, 2022)

## Related Theories
- [[Sovereignty]] — the conflict raised questions about the meaning of sovereignty in an ethnically federated state: does sovereignty reside in the federal government, the ethnic regional state, or the people? (Clapham, 2017)
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — the international community's limited response, characterised by diplomatic rhetoric without enforcement, demonstrated the constraints of humanitarian intervention when the conflict occurs outside Western strategic priority zones (Bellamy & Dunne, 2011)
- [[African Union]] — the AU's mediation role in the Pretoria Agreement demonstrated African institutional capacity for conflict resolution, but its inability to prevent or halt the conflict exposed the AU's structural limitations (Aning & Edu-Afful, 2015)
- [[Peacekeeping]] — the absence of UN or AU peacekeeping during the conflict, and the limited mandate of the eventual monitoring mechanism, highlighted the gap between peacekeeping doctrine and deployment reality (Diehl, 2019)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- TPLF attack on Northern Command (November 2020) — the TPLF's assault on the Ethiopian National Defence Force's Northern Command in Tigray triggered the federal military response and the start of the war
- Tigray forces' advance toward Addis Ababa (November 2021) — TPLF-allied forces briefly threatened the capital, triggering a state of emergency and mass mobilisation
- Pretoria Agreement (November 2022) — AU-mediated ceasefire between Ethiopian federal forces and the TPLF, brokered by former Nigerian President Olusegun Obasanjo, South African Deputy President David Mabuza, and former Kenyan President Uhuru Kenyatta
- TPLF disarmament (January 2023) — the TPLF surrendered heavy weapons as part of the Pretoria implementation, though Eritrean forces remained in Ethiopian territory

## Key Questions
1. How did the Nobel Peace Prize paradox shape international perceptions of Abiy Ahmed?
2. Is ethnic federalism a viable governance model for diverse states, or does it entrench division?
3. Why was the international community unable to prevent mass atrocities in Tigray?
4. Did the AU mediation demonstrate the viability of African-led conflict resolution?
5. Can Ethiopia rebuild national cohesion after the scale of violence and displacement?
