# Nagorno-Karabakh Conflict

## Overview
The Nagorno-Karabakh conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan, spanning from the Soviet collapse through the 2023 Azerbaijani military offensive, represents a paradigmatic case of post-Soviet territorial conflict, ethnic self-determination, and the failure of international mediation. The conflict's trajectory — from frozen conflict to brief but decisive wars — demonstrates the transformative impact of drone warfare on military balance and the erosion of Russian peacekeeping credibility in the post-Soviet space (De Waal, 2003; Cornell, 2017).

## Key Themes
- **Armenian-Azerbaijani ethnic conflict** — the conflict is rooted in competing claims to a mountainous enclave with an Armenian Christian majority within nominally Azerbaijani territory, producing displacement, ethnic cleansing, and humanitarian catastrophe on both sides (De Waal, 2003)
- **Soviet legacy** — the administrative boundaries of the Soviet era created the structural conditions for conflict, with the Nagorno-Karabakh Autonomous Oblast placed within Azerbaijan despite its Armenian majority (Cornell, 2017)
- **1994 ceasefire and frozen conflict** — the first war ended in Armenian victory and the occupation of Nagorno-Karabakh and seven surrounding Azerbaijani districts, displacing over 600,000 Azerbaijanis (Human Rights Watch, 1995)
- **Drone warfare revolution** — the 2020 Second Karabakh War demonstrated the decisive impact of Turkish-supplied Bayraktar TB2 drones and Israeli loitering munitions on the military balance, overturning decades of Armenian defensive advantage (Sharkov, 2020)
- **Russian peacekeeping failure** — Russia deployed 2,000 peacekeepers after the 2020 war, but failed to prevent Azerbaijan's 2023 offensive and the subsequent Armenian evacuation, severely damaging Russia's credibility as a regional security guarantor (Kofman, 2023)
- **Turkey's role** — Turkey's decisive military and diplomatic support for Azerbaijan represented a significant expansion of Turkish influence in the South Caucasus, challenging Russian regional hegemony (Kardaş, 2020)

## Related Theories
- [[Sovereignty]] — Azerbaijan's territorial sovereignty over Nagorno-Karabakh was internationally recognised but functionally contested, illustrating the gap between declaratory and effective sovereignty (Herbst, 2000)
- [[International Law]] — the conflict raised fundamental questions about the legal status of secession, the application of uti possidetis juris (inherited Soviet boundaries), and the tension between territorial integrity and self-determination (Hannum, 1996)
- [[Self-Determination]] — Armenia invoked the right of self-determination for the Armenian population of Nagorno-Karabakh, while Azerbaijan insisted on territorial integrity, demonstrating the irreconcilable tension between these principles in international law (Crawford, 2006)
- [[Balance of Power]] — the military balance shifted decisively after 2020 due to asymmetric technological advantages (drones, precision munitions) rather than traditional force accumulation, challenging conventional balance-of-power analysis (Sharkov, 2020)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- First Nagorno-Karabakh War (1992–94) — Armenian forces captured the enclave and surrounding districts, displacing over 600,000 Azerbaijanis and establishing the "frozen conflict" status quo
- April 2016 "Four-Day War" — brief but intense fighting signalled Azerbaijan's growing military capability and willingness to use force
- Second Karabakh War (September–November 2020) — Azerbaijan's decisive military victory, enabled by drone warfare and Turkish support, recaptured most of the occupied territories and killed over 4,000 Armenian soldiers (Sharkov, 2020)
- Russian-brokered ceasefire (November 2020) — deployment of Russian peacekeepers created a temporary stabilisation, but with inadequate provisions for long-term resolution
- Azerbaijani offensive (September 2023) — Azerbaijan's military operation dismantled the self-declared Republic of Artsakh, prompting the evacuation of over 100,000 ethnic Armenians and ending the territorial dispute through military fait accompli (Kofman, 2023)

## Key Questions
1. Does the Nagorno-Karabakh case demonstrate the ultimate primacy of military force over international law in territorial disputes?
2. How did drone technology alter the military balance, and what are the implications for future conflicts?
3. Has Russia's failure as a peacekeeper ended its role as the South Caucasus' primary security guarantor?
4. Can self-determination be reconciled with territorial integrity in cases of ethnic enclave conflicts?
5. What are the long-term implications of the Armenian displacement for regional stability?
