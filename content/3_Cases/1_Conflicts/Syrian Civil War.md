# Syrian Civil War

## Overview
The Syrian Civil War (2011–present) is the defining conflict of the post-Arab Spring era, transforming from a domestic uprising against the Assad regime into a multi-sided, internationalised proxy war involving regional and global powers. The conflict has produced over 500,000 deaths, displaced more than 12 million people, and generated the largest refugee crisis since the Second World War, while exposing the failures of international humanitarian intervention, the Responsibility to Protect doctrine, and great-power diplomacy (Yassin-Kassab & Al-Shami, 2019).

## Key Themes
- **Multi-sided conflict** — the war involved the Assad regime, a fragmented opposition (Free Syrian Army, jihadist factions), Kurdish forces (YPG/SDF), and ISIS, making it resistant to binary analytical frameworks (Holliday, 2013)
- **Russian intervention (2015)** — Russia's direct military intervention saved the Assad regime from collapse, demonstrating that great-power backing could override popular uprisings and fundamentally altering the conflict's trajectory (Galeotti, 2016)
- **Chemical weapons red line** — the 2013 Ghouta chemical attack and the subsequent US failure to enforce President Obama's "red line" became a paradigmatic case of credibility failure in international deterrence (Schmitt, 2013)
- **Refugee crisis** — the displacement of millions into Turkey, Lebanon, Jordan, and Europe generated secondary crises: European political polarisation, Lebanese sectarian strain, and Turkish strategic reorientation (Zetter, 2014)
- **Diplomatic failures** — the Geneva talks (2012–14) and the Astana process (2017–present) failed to produce political settlement, with competing international sponsors (US vs Russia/Turkey/Iran) unable to reconcile their positions (Hezbollah, 2022)
- **ISIS and the war on terror** — the rise and fall of the Islamic State (2014–19) layered a counter-terrorism dimension onto the civil war, complicating opposition dynamics and enabling Kurdish territorial consolidation (Lister, 2015)

## Related Theories
- [[Realism]] — the Syrian conflict validates realist assumptions about the primacy of power and the irrelevance of international norms when great-power interests are engaged (Walt, 2018)
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — Syria represents the most significant failure of humanitarian intervention since Rwanda; the international community's inability to prevent mass atrocity undermined the R2P framework (Bellamy & Dunne, 2011)
- [[Responsibility to Protect]] — the Syrian case exposed the structural weakness of R2P: the doctrine is enforceable only when great powers agree, and the P5 veto prevented UN-mandated action (Bass, 2015)
- [[Sovereignty]] — Assad's invocation of sovereignty against international intervention, combined with Russia's defence of that sovereignty at the Security Council, demonstrated the continued power of sovereignty as a shield even amid mass atrocity (Schedler, 2018)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Arab Spring protests (March 2011) — peaceful demonstrations against the Assad regime began in Daraa, escalating into armed conflict as regime repression intensified
- Ghouta chemical attack (August 2013) — the use of chemical weapons against civilians killed hundreds and prompted a US-Russian agreement to eliminate Syria's chemical arsenal, but did not end chemical attacks
- Russian military intervention (September 2015) — Russia's entry into the war decisively shifted the military balance in Assad's favour, preserving the regime and demonstrating Russian willingness to project force in the Middle East (Galeotti, 2016)
- Fall of Raqqa (October 2017) — the SDF's capture of ISIS's self-declared capital marked the end of the caliphate's territorial control
- Turkish incursion into northeastern Syria (2019) — Turkey's operation against Kurdish YPG forces demonstrated the conflict's capacity for new escalatory phases and the fragility of the anti-ISIS coalition

## Key Questions
1. Why did the international community fail to enforce the chemical weapons red line?
2. Did Russian intervention stabilise the region or entrench authoritarianism?
3. How has the Syrian conflict reshaped the norms of humanitarian intervention and R2P?
4. What role did the fragmentation of the opposition play in prolonging the conflict?
5. Can a political settlement be achieved when external sponsors hold incompatible objectives?
