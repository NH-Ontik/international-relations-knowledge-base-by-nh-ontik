# Yemen Civil War

## Overview
The Yemeni Civil War (2014–present) has produced what the United Nations has described as the world's worst humanitarian crisis, with an estimated 21 million people in need of assistance and over 150,000 conflict-related deaths. The war is simultaneously a domestic political struggle, a sectarian proxy conflict between Saudi Arabia and Iran, and a test case for the limits of international humanitarian law in modern warfare (Brand, 2019).

## Key Themes
- **Houthi insurgency** — the Ansar Allah movement, rooted in Zaydi Shia identity and political marginalisation, seized Sanaa in 2014, triggering a conflict that rapidly internationalised (Brockhurst, 2017)
- **Saudi-led coalition intervention (2015)** — Saudi Arabia assembled a coalition of Arab states to restore the internationally recognised government, launching a sustained aerial campaign that generated massive civilian casualties and destruction of critical infrastructure (ALeqinac, 2018)
- **Iran proxy war dimension** — Iran's provision of military technology (including ballistic missiles and drones) to the Houthis transformed the conflict into a theatre of Saudi-Iranian regional competition, though the extent of Iranian control over Houthi decision-making remains contested (Tawil, 2020)
- **US drone warfare** — US support for the Saudi-led coalition, including intelligence sharing, aerial refuelling, and weapons sales, implicated the United States in the humanitarian consequences, raising domestic and international legal questions (Friedman, 2018)
- **Red Sea shipping attacks (2024)** — Houthi attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea, framed as solidarity with Palestinians during the Israel-Gaza conflict, demonstrated the conflict's capacity to disrupt global trade and drew US and UK military strikes (Binnie, 2024)
- **World's worst humanitarian crisis** — famine, cholera outbreaks, and the collapse of healthcare infrastructure have generated suffering on a scale that international humanitarian mechanisms have been unable to address (OCHA, 2023)

## Related Theories
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — Yemen demonstrates the limits of humanitarian intervention when the intervening coalition is backed by a permanent Security Council member (the US, via Saudi Arabia), shielding the intervention from international legal constraint (Bellamy & Dunne, 2011)
- [[Proxy War]] — the Saudi-Iranian dimension of the conflict makes Yemen a textbook case of proxy warfare, where regional powers fight through local allies rather than directly (Byman, 2016)
- [[International Law]] — coalition airstrikes on hospitals, schools, weddings, and funerals have raised fundamental questions about the application of international humanitarian law to coalition warfare and the accountability of intervening states (Hilhorst, 2016)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Houthi takeover of Sanaa (September 2014) — the catalyst for international military intervention
- Saudi-led coalition intervention (March 2015) — the beginning of the sustained aerial campaign that has defined the conflict
- Stockholm Agreement (December 2018) — a UN-brokered ceasefire for the port of Hodeidah that failed to produce broader political settlement
- Houthi Red Sea attacks (November 2023–present) — attacks on international shipping demonstrated the conflict's capacity to affect global commerce and drew direct US/UK military response
- US arms suspension (2024) — the Biden administration's partial suspension of offensive weapons transfers to Saudi Arabia marked the first significant US policy shift, though support continued (Friedman, 2024)

## Key Questions
1. Can humanitarian intervention be legitimate when conducted by a coalition with its own strategic interests?
2. Is the Houthi movement an Iranian proxy or an autonomous actor with legitimate grievances?
3. How has the Red Sea crisis demonstrated the global economic stakes of regional conflicts?
4. Has the international humanitarian law framework been adequate to address the scale of civilian harm in Yemen?
5. What are the prospects for a negotiated settlement when external sponsors (Saudi Arabia, Iran) have incompatible objectives?
