# Sudan Civil War

## Overview
The Sudanese Civil War (2023–present) erupted from the collapse of a power-sharing arrangement between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) under General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) under General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo ("Hemedti"), triggering a conflict that has displaced over 10 million people and created the world's largest displacement crisis. The war is rooted in the unresolved legacies of the Darfur genocide (2003–present), the 2019 revolution that toppled Omar al-Bashir, and the structural militarisation of the Sudanese state (Verhoeven, 2023).

## Key Themes
- **RSF vs SAF** — the conflict is fundamentally a struggle between two military factions that jointly overthrew Bashir's civilian government, now fighting for exclusive control of the state; the RSF's origins in the Janjaweed militias of Darfur make it a force with a history of mass atrocity (Verhoeven, 2023)
- **Bashir's fall (2019)** — the popular revolution that ousted Omar al-Bashir after 30 years of authoritarian rule created a transitional civilian-military government that was itself overthrown in a coup in October 2021, setting the stage for the current conflict (Khalid & El-Nur, 2023)
- **Transitional government collapse** — the failure of the transitional government to achieve democratic reform was rooted in the structural power of the military and its economic empire, which neither faction was willing to surrender (Ismail & De Waal, 2023)
- **Darfur legacy** — the Darfur genocide, in which Janjaweed militias (RSF predecessors) killed hundreds of thousands and displaced millions, has re-erupted in the current conflict, with reports of ethnically targeted violence in West Darfur (Amnesty International, 2023)
- **UAE and Egypt involvement** — the UAE's support for the RSF and Egypt's backing of the SAF have transformed the conflict into a regional proxy struggle, with external arms supplies sustaining both factions (Verhoeven, 2023)
- **ICC warrants** — the International Criminal Court's existing warrants for Bashir and other Sudanese officials for Darfur atrocities have not prevented the recurrence of mass violence, raising questions about the deterrent effect of international criminal justice (ICC, 2023)

## Related Theories
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — Sudan demonstrates the international community's persistent inability to prevent or halt mass atrocities in the absence of great-power will; the parallel with Rwanda (1994) and Darfur (2003) is unmistakable (Bellamy & Dunne, 2011)
- [[Responsibility to Protect]] — the R2P doctrine's invocation during Darfur and its subsequent failure to prevent renewed conflict in 2023 exposed its structural dependence on P5 political consensus (Bass, 2015)
- [[International Criminal Court]] — Sudan's case illustrates the ICC's limitations: warrants exist for officials of the previous regime, but they have neither deterred current atrocities nor contributed to their prevention (Schabas, 2011)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- December Revolution (2018–19) — mass protests forced the removal of Omar al-Bashir after 30 years, creating a transitional government that promised democratic reform
- October 2021 coup — Burhan and Hemedti jointly overthrew the transitional civilian government, destroying the democratic transition and consolidating military rule
- Outbreak of war (April 2023) — fighting erupted in Khartoum between the SAF and RSF following disputes over security sector reform and integration of the RSF into the national army
- Fall of Khartoum (2023) — RSF forces seized control of most of the capital, including key government buildings and the presidential palace, forcing the SAF to relocate to Port Sudan
- Darfur ethnically targeted violence (2023–24) — reports of mass killings of Masalit and other non-Arab communities in West Darfur reprised the pattern of the 2003 genocide (Amnesty International, 2023)

## Key Questions
1. Can international criminal justice deter mass atrocity when the same patterns recur within a generation?
2. Why has the international community been unable to prevent the repetition of Darfur-style violence?
3. What role has UAE support for the RSF played in sustaining the conflict?
4. Is the SAF-RSF conflict a power struggle between military factions, or does it reflect deeper structural divides in Sudanese politics?
5. What are the prospects for civilian governance in a state where the military controls both security and economic resources?
