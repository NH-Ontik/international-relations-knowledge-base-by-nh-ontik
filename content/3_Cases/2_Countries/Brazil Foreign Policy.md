# Brazil Foreign Policy

## Overview
Brazil's foreign policy is characterised by the tension between its aspiration to great-power status and its identity as a leader of the Global South. Since independence, Brazil has pursued a doctrine of strategic autonomy, resisting alignment with any single great power while seeking to amplify its influence through multilateral institutions and South-South cooperation (Malamud, 2011). As the largest economy in Latin America and a founding member of BRICS, Brazil occupies a structurally ambiguous position: too large to be a middle power in the conventional sense, but insufficiently assertive or materially endowed to function as a great power (Saraiva, 2013).

## Key Themes
- **South-South diplomacy** — Brazil has consistently positioned itself as a bridge between the developed and developing worlds, leveraging its unique historical composition (European, African, Indigenous) to claim representational authority for the Global South (Muni, 2010)
- **IBSA Dialogue Forum** — the India-Brazil-South Africa trilateral (established 2003) exemplified Brazil's strategy of building cross-regional coalitions among democratic developing states as a counterweight to both Western-dominated and China-led institutions (Stuenkel, 2010)
- **BRICS membership** — Brazil's participation in BRICS reflects both its global south identity and its aspiration to reform global governance structures, though the grouping's internal heterogeneity limits its cohesion (Stuenkel, 2016)
- **Soft power strategy** — Brazil has invested in cultural diplomacy, development cooperation, and "technological diplomacy" (particularly in tropical agriculture and biofuels) to project influence beyond its material capabilities (Souza, 2014)
- **Amazon governance** — environmental stewardship of the Amazon rainforest has become a central diplomatic instrument, simultaneously a source of sovereignty claims and international pressure (Vigevani & Cepaluni, 2007)
- **Non-intervention principle** — Brazil has historically upheld the OAS principle of non-intervention in internal affairs, though this has been tested by crises in Venezuela and elsewhere (Malamud, 2011)

## Related Theories
- [[Soft Power]] — Brazil's cultural exports (samba, Carnival, football), development cooperation, and multilateral diplomacy constitute a textbook case of soft power deployment by a non-Western state (Nye, 2004)
- [[Middle Powers]] — Brazil exemplifies the "middle power" category: too large for small-state strategies, too constrained for great-power competition, relying on coalition-building and norm entrepreneurship (Cooper, 1997)
- [[Global Governance]] — Brazil's BRICS participation and G20 activism position it as a key actor in debates about reforming international institutions (Ikenberry, 2011)
- [[Non-Aligned Movement]] — Brazil's contemporary foreign policy inherits the NAM's commitment to strategic autonomy, though adapted to a unipolar and then multipolar context (Muni, 2010)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Rio Branco's foreign policy (1902–1912) — Baron of Rio Branco established Brazil's tradition of peaceful border dispute resolution and diplomatic professionalism (Campos, 2015)
- Goulart's "Independent Foreign Policy" (1961–64) — the first systematic attempt to decouple Brazilian diplomacy from US alignment, ended by the military coup (Cervo & Bueno, 2012)
- Itamar Franco's realignment (1990–95) — Brazil joined the Non-Aligned Movement and diversified partnerships, breaking from the Cold War-era US alignment (Lima & Hirst, 2006)
- Lula's Global South agenda (2003–10) — Lula da Silva dramatically expanded South-South cooperation, BRICS engagement, and Brazilian diplomatic visibility (Malamud, 2011)
- Bolsonaro's rightward turn (2019–22) — withdrawal from UN Global Compact on Migration, US alignment, and Amazon deforestation diplomacy reversed decades of South-South consensus (Friedman, 2022)

## Key Questions
1. Can Brazil simultaneously lead the Global South and seek great-power status?
2. Is BRICS a genuine alternative to Western-dominated governance or a forum without teeth?
3. How does Amazon environmental diplomacy balance sovereignty claims against international expectations?
4. Is Brazil's non-intervention principle a principled norm or a self-interested shield?
5. How do domestic political shifts (Lula vs Bolsonaro) affect foreign policy continuity?
