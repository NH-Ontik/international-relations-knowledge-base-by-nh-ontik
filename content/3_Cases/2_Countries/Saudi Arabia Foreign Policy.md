# Saudi Arabia Foreign Policy

## Overview
Saudi Arabia's foreign policy is shaped by the convergence of three structural factors: its role as custodian of Islam's two holiest sites, its position as the world's largest oil exporter, and its leadership of a Sunni Arab bloc confronting Iranian expansion. The Kingdom's foreign policy demonstrates the interplay between petro-politics, religious legitimacy, and strategic realism, making it a paradigmatic case for understanding resource-dependent states in IR (Lacey, 2009; Hertog, 2010).

## Key Themes
- **Petrodollar diplomacy** — oil revenue has been the primary instrument of Saudi foreign policy, funding alliances, development assistance, arms purchases, and regional influence since the 1970s oil shocks (Vitalis, 2007)
- **Vision 2030** — Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's economic diversification programme represents an attempt to reduce oil dependency and modernise the Saudi economy, with significant foreign policy implications (Esenbel, 2016)
- **Yemen war** — Saudi Arabia's 2015 military intervention in Yemen became the Kingdom's most costly foreign policy venture, producing humanitarian catastrophe and military quagmire (Schwarz, 2018)
- **Iran rivalry** — the Saudi-Iranian regional competition structures Middle Eastern geopolitics along sectarian lines, with proxy conflicts in Yemen, Syria, Iraq, and Lebanon (Takeyh, 2009)
- **Abraham Accords and Israel normalisation** — the 2020 normalisation agreements (with UAE, Bahrain) signalled a shift in Saudi strategic calculus, prioritising anti-Iran alignment over traditional Palestinian solidarity (Peters, 2021)
- **Custodian of Two Holy Mosques** — the religious title confers legitimacy that extends beyond material power, functioning as a soft power resource that shapes Saudi authority in the Muslim world (Commins, 2009)

## Related Theories
- [[Hegemony]] — Saudi Arabia pursues regional hegemony in the Persian Gulf and broader Middle East through a combination of religious legitimacy, economic resources, and military capability (Malamud, 2011)
- [[Soft Power]] — the Kingdom's custodianship of Mecca and Medina, combined with international development assistance and cultural investment, constitutes a distinctive form of soft power rooted in religious rather than cultural authority (Nye, 2004)
- [[International Law]] — Saudi Arabia's conduct in Yemen, including alleged coalition airstrikes on civilian targets, has raised significant international humanitarian law questions and ICC jurisdiction debates (Amnesty International, 2016)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Oil embargo (1973) — the OAPEC oil embargo demonstrated that petro-politics could reshape global geopolitics, elevating Saudi Arabia from regional to global significance (Yergin, 1991)
- Iranian Revolution (1979) — the fall of the Shah and the rise of Shia revolutionary ideology created Saudi Arabia's primary regional adversary, restructuring Middle Eastern geopolitics (Takeyh, 2009)
- Gulf War (1990–91) — Saudi decision to host US forces against Iraq created a permanent American military presence in the Kingdom, a decision with enduring legitimacy costs (Lacey, 2009)
- Khashoggi affair (2018) — the killing of journalist Jamal Khashoggi in the Saudi consulate in Istanbul generated unprecedented international condemnation and tested the limits of strategic partnership with Riyadh (Hubbard, 2018)
- Abraham Accords (2020) — UAE-Bahrain normalisation with Israel, with potential Saudi normalisation on the horizon, restructured regional alignments

## Key Questions
1. Can Saudi Arabia diversify away from oil dependency without political liberalisation?
2. Does the Iran rivalry justify Saudi military interventionism, or does it entrench sectarian conflict?
3. How does custodianship of Islam's holy sites shape Saudi foreign policy legitimacy?
4. Has Vision 2030 altered Saudi Arabia's strategic posture, or is it primarily domestic?
5. Can Saudi normalisation with Israel proceed without resolving the Palestinian question?
