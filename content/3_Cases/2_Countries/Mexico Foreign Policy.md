# Mexico Foreign Policy

## Overview
Mexico's foreign policy is fundamentally shaped by its geographic proximity to the United States, creating an asymmetrical bilateral relationship that conditions nearly every dimension of Mexican diplomacy. From the doctrine of "Estrada" (non-recognition of foreign governments) to contemporary immigration diplomacy, Mexico has navigated the tension between sovereignty assertion and economic dependency with considerable sophistication (Fernández de Castro, 2006). Mexico's foreign policy illustrates the constraints and opportunities of proximity to a superpower, making it a paradigmatic case in foreign policy analysis.

## Key Themes
- **Immigration diplomacy** — migration has become the defining axis of Mexico's bilateral relationship with the US, encompassing remittances, border enforcement, humanitarian obligations, and domestic political pressures on both sides (Fitzgerald, 2009)
- **NAFTA/USMCA as structural anchor** — the North American Free Trade Agreement (1994, renegotiated as USMCA in 2020) embedded Mexico into North American supply chains, making economic interdependence a cornerstone of the bilateral relationship (Dussel Peters, 2016)
- **Drug war and securitization** — the Mérida Initiative (2007) and Mexico's militarised response to drug trafficking organisations created a securitised bilateral relationship in which sovereignty is routinely compromised by US intelligence and enforcement involvement (Castañeda, 2011)
- **Human rights** — forced disappearances (Ayotzinapa, 2014), extrajudicial killings, and migrant rights abuses have generated sustained international scrutiny, complicating Mexico's human rights diplomacy (Moloeznik, 2015)
- **South-South cooperation** — Mexico has pursued diversified partnerships (Pacific Alliance, CELAC) to partially offset US dependency, though geographic reality limits this strategy's reach (Ferraro, 2012)

## Related Theories
- [[Complex Interdependence]] — Mexico-US relations exemplify Keohane and Nye's (1977) complex interdependence: asymmetric but deep integration across economic, security, and social channels, with no single issue dominating the agenda
- [[Sovereignty]] — Mexico's sovereignty is structurally compromised by US influence; the tension between formal sovereignty and functional dependency is a persistent theme in Mexican IR scholarship (Serrano, 2002)
- [[Soft Power]] — Mexico's cultural exports (cinema, cuisine, music, Day of the Dead) and diaspora communities constitute a significant soft power resource, partially offsetting the asymmetry of the bilateral relationship (Ferraro, 2012)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848) — Mexico lost half its territory to the United States, creating a historical memory of US imperialism that shapes contemporary sovereignty sensitivities (Hazard, 1928)
- Cárdenas' oil nationalisation (1938) — the expropriation of foreign oil assets became a foundational moment for Mexican sovereignty assertion and economic nationalism (Parella, 1988)
- Zapatista uprising (1994) — coinciding with NAFTA's implementation, the EZLN rebellion highlighted the domestic costs of economic liberalisation and globalisation (Collier, 1999)
- Ayotzinapa disappearances (2014) — the enforced disappearance of 43 students exposed state-criminal collusion and generated unprecedented international human rights pressure (Moloeznik, 2015)
- USMCA renegotiation (2018–20) — the Trump administration's renegotiation demonstrated Mexico's vulnerability to US political shifts, while Mexico's negotiating team preserved core trade provisions (Dussel Peters, 2016)

## Key Questions
1. Can Mexico pursue genuinely autonomous foreign policy given its structural dependency on the US?
2. Does NAFTA/USMCA represent economic integration or economic subordination?
3. How should Mexico balance human rights diplomacy against sovereignty concerns?
4. Is the "drug war" a security issue or a governance failure, and how does framing affect policy responses?
5. Can South-South cooperation meaningfully offset US influence in Mexican foreign policy?
