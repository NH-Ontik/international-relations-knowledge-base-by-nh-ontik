# Iran Foreign Policy

## Overview
Iran's foreign policy since the 1979 Islamic Revolution represents one of the most consequential ideological foreign policies in the modern Middle East. Blending Shia religious identity, revolutionary anti-imperialism, and Persian strategic culture, Iran has pursued a distinctive strategy of asymmetric deterrence, proxy warfare, and regional influence projection that has profoundly shaped the regional order (Takeyh, 2009; Walt, 2010). Iran's foreign policy demonstrates how ideological conviction, strategic culture, and structural constraints interact to produce a recognisable and internally coherent foreign policy posture.

## Key Themes
- **Strategic culture and the Qasem Soleimani era** — Major General Qasem Soleimani's command of the Quds Force (1998–2020) epitomised Iran's strategy of "forward defence" through proxy networks, projecting power through non-state allies rather than conventional military force (Urban, 2020)
- **Shia crescent** — the geopolitical concept of a contiguous arc of Shia-majority or Shia-influenced states from Iran through Iraq, Syria, and Lebanon to Hezbollah in Lebanon, representing both aspiration and strategic reality for Iranian regional policy (Takeyh, 2009)
- **Nuclear programme and JCPOA** — Iran's nuclear programme has been the defining issue of its international relations; the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (2015) represented a diplomatic breakthrough that was subsequently undermined by US withdrawal in 2018 (Samore, 2015)
- **Proxy network** — Iran's network of allied non-state actors (Hezbollah, Iraqi Shia militias, Houthis, Hamas) constitutes a form of asymmetric deterrence that compensates for conventional military weakness (Byman, 2016)
- **1979 Revolution as IR event** — the revolution fundamentally reoriented Iran's foreign policy from US-aligned modernisation to revolutionary anti-imperialism, and introduced political Islam as a potent force in international relations (Keddie, 2006)
- **Maximum pressure campaign** — the Trump administration's withdrawal from JCPOA and reimposition of sanctions (2018–present) sought to compel Iranian concessions through economic coercion, but deepened Iranian defiance and accelerated nuclear programme advancement (Sadjadpour, 2017)

## Related Theories
- [[Deterrence]] — Iran's nuclear programme and proxy network represent a layered deterrence strategy: nuclear capability as ultimate guarantee, proxy forces as conventional deterrence, and asymmetric warfare as punishment (Walt, 2010)
- [[Security Dilemma]] — the Saudi-Iranian rivalry and Israeli-Iranian antagonism exemplify classic security dilemma dynamics, where defensive preparations by one side are perceived as offensive by the other, generating escalatory spirals (Jervis, 1978)
- [[Nuclear Proliferation]] — Iran's nuclear programme is the most significant contemporary proliferation case, raising fundamental questions about NPT obligations, enrichment rights, and the effectiveness of sanctions (Samore, 2015)
- [[Sovereignty]] — Iran invokes sovereignty against international pressure while simultaneously supporting non-state actors that challenge the sovereignty of neighbouring states, illustrating the selective application of sovereignty norms (Takeyh, 2009)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Islamic Revolution (1979) — the overthrow of the Shah and establishment of the Islamic Republic fundamentally reoriented Iranian foreign policy and introduced revolutionary ideology into international relations (Keddie, 2006)
- Iran-Iraq War (1980–88) — the eight-year conflict with Iraq, during which Iran faced international isolation and chemical weapons attacks, shaped Iran's strategic culture of self-reliance and resistance (Hiro, 1991)
- Beirut barracks bombing (1983) — Hezbollah's attack on US and French peacekeeping forces demonstrated the effectiveness of Iranian proxy warfare, establishing a template that would be replicated for decades (Byman, 2016)
- JCPOA agreement (2015) — the most significant diplomatic achievement of the Rouhani government, offering sanctions relief in exchange for nuclear programme constraints (Samore, 2015)
- US withdrawal from JCPOA and Qasem Soleimani assassination (2018–20) — maximum pressure and the elimination of Iran's most influential military commander escalated the confrontation to its most dangerous point since 1988

## Key Questions
1. Is Iran's nuclear programme primarily a deterrent, a bargaining chip, or a genuine weapons ambition?
2. Can maximum pressure compel Iranian concessions, or does it entrench regime defiance?
3. How sustainable is Iran's proxy network strategy in the face of regional counter-mobilisation?
4. Does the 1979 Revolution continue to shape Iranian foreign policy, or has it evolved beyond its founding ideology?
5. Can diplomacy resolve the Saudi-Iranian rivalry, or is structural competition inevitable?
