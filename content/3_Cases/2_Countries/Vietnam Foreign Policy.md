# Vietnam Foreign Policy

## Overview
Vietnam's foreign policy, described by General Secretary Nguyen Phu Trong as "bamboo diplomacy" — rooted, resilient, and flexible — represents one of the most sophisticated hedging strategies in contemporary IR. Having fought wars against France, the United States, and China within a single century, Vietnam has developed a pragmatic, multi-directional foreign policy that balances between great powers while preserving communist party legitimacy and national sovereignty (Thayer, 2009; Goh, 2015).

## Key Themes
- **"Bamboo diplomacy"** — the metaphor of bamboo (strong roots, flexible branches) captures Vietnam's strategy of strategic flexibility: maintaining relationships with all major powers without formal alignment to any (Thayer, 2022)
- **Multi-directional hedging** — Vietnam simultaneously deepens strategic partnerships with the US, Russia, Japan, India, and the EU while managing its complex relationship with China, the most consequential bilateral relationship in its foreign policy (Goh, 2015)
- **South China Sea disputes** — Vietnam's territorial disputes with China in the Paracel and Spratly Islands constitute the most acute security challenge in its foreign policy, shaping its hedging calculus and driving diversification of security partnerships (Thayer, 2009)
- **Đổi Mới economic reforms** — the 1986 market-oriented reforms transformed Vietnam from a centrally planned economy to a globally integrated manufacturing hub, fundamentally altering its foreign policy conditions (Forsberg, 2017)
- **Communist party legitimacy** — the Communist Party of Vietnam's monopoly on power constrains its foreign policy options while simultaneously providing political continuity that enables long-term strategic planning (Thayer, 2009)
- **ASEAN membership** — Vietnam's 1995 accession to ASEAN was a watershed, integrating the former US adversary into a regional institutional framework and providing a multilateral platform for managing China (Acharya, 2014)

## Related Theories
- [[Sovereignty]] — Vietnam's foreign policy is overwhelmingly driven by sovereignty concerns: territorial integrity against China, regime survival against domestic and external pressures, and strategic autonomy against great-power domination (Thayer, 2009)
- [[Balance of Power]] — Vietnam's hedging strategy is a textbook case of balancing against a regional hegemon (China) through diversified partnerships rather than a formal alliance, consistent with balance-of-power theory (Walt, 1987)
- [[Complex Interdependence]] — Vietnam's deep economic integration with global supply chains (manufacturing for Apple, Samsung, Nike) creates interdependence that constrains conflict initiation while enhancing economic vulnerability (Keohane & Nye, 1977)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- First Indochina War (1946–54) — victory over France established Vietnam as an anti-colonial paradigm and shaped its sovereign self-conception (Devillers, 1952)
- Vietnam War (1955–75) — the conflict against the United States produced millions of casualties but established Vietnam's reputation for strategic resilience (Gawthorne, 2001)
- Sino-Vietnamese War (1979) — China's punitive invasion following Vietnam's intervention in Cambodia demonstrated that ideological alignment (communist solidarity) was subordinate to strategic competition, fundamentally shaping contemporary Vietnam-China relations (Zhang, 2005)
- Đổi Mới reforms (1986) — market liberalisation transformed Vietnam's economic base and created the conditions for its contemporary foreign policy flexibility (Forsberg, 2017)
- South China Sea oil rig crisis (2014) — China's placement of the Hai Yang Shi You 981 oil rig in disputed waters triggered anti-China protests in Vietnam and accelerated Vietnam's strategic diversification (Thayer, 2014)

## Key Questions
1. Can bamboo diplomacy survive intensifying US-China competition, or will Vietnam be forced to choose sides?
2. How does communist party legitimacy constrain or enable Vietnam's foreign policy flexibility?
3. Is Vietnam's hedging strategy a sustainable long-term approach, or a transitional posture?
4. How does historical memory of Chinese and American involvement shape contemporary strategic culture?
5. Can ASEAN provide sufficient collective security for Vietnam against Chinese assertiveness?
