# Pacific Islands Foreign Policy

## Overview
The Pacific Island states — comprising 14 sovereign nations across a vast maritime expanse — have developed a distinctive foreign policy paradigm centred on climate diplomacy as existential survival strategy. For low-lying atoll states such as Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands, climate change is not an environmental policy issue but an existential threat to territorial sovereignty itself, producing a form of "survival diplomacy" unique in international relations (Farbotko & Lazrus, 2012). The region has also become a site of intensifying strategic competition between the United States, China, and Taiwan, amplifying the geopolitical significance of these geographically dispersed but strategically positioned states (Fraenkel, 2016).

## Key Themes
- **Climate diplomacy as survival strategy** — Pacific Island states have reframed climate change from an environmental issue to a sovereignty and security issue, demanding international legal recognition of "climate refugees" and loss-and-displacement mechanisms (McNamara & Gibson, 2009)
- **Compact of Free Association (COFA)** — the US-Pacific compacts (with the Marshall Islands, Federated States of Micronesia, and Palau) provide US defence exclusivity and economic assistance in exchange for strategic basing rights, forming the foundation of US Pacific security architecture (Peattie, 1997)
- **Pacific Islands Forum** — the PIF serves as the primary regional institution, though its effectiveness has been tested by member withdrawals (Marshall Islands, Nauru, Kiribati) and geopolitical pressures (Herr, 2014)
- **RAMSI and regional security** — the Regional Assistance Mission to Solomon Islands (2003–2017) demonstrated Pacific capacity for collective security intervention, though with contested sovereignty implications (Dobson, 2014)
- **China-Taiwan competition** — Beijing and Taipei have competed for Pacific diplomatic recognition through aid and development assistance, converting sovereignty recognition into a transactional commodity (Fraenkel, 2016)
- **Blue Pacific narrative** — the "2050 Strategy for the Blue Pacific Continent" represents a collective attempt to assert Pacific agency against great-power competition, framing the Pacific Ocean as a shared domain requiring Pacific-led governance (Pacific Islands Forum, 2022)

## Related Theories
- [[Climate Change]] — Pacific Island states are the paradigmatic case for climate-security scholarship, demonstrating that climate change can constitute an existential threat to sovereignty and territorial integrity (Barnett & Adger, 2003)
- [[Sovereignty]] — the potential for territory to be rendered uninhabitable by sea-level rise challenges the Westphalian assumption that sovereignty requires permanent habitation and effective control of territory (McAdam, 2012)
- [[Middle Powers]] — Pacific Island states have exercised disproportionate influence through norm entrepreneurship (climate justice, ocean governance) disproportionate to their material capabilities, consistent with middle-power theory (Cooper, 1997)
- [[International Organizations]] — the PIF and its sub-regional bodies illustrate the opportunities and limitations of small-state institutional leadership in a geopolitically competitive environment (Firth, 2006)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Independence wave (1970s–80) — decolonisation created 14 sovereign states across the Pacific, establishing the political framework for contemporary regional diplomacy
- UN Law of the Sea (1982) — UNCLOS provided Pacific states with exclusive economic zones vastly larger than their land territory, creating economic and strategic leverage (Kwiatkowska, 2005)
- Pacific Islands Forum establishment (1971) — created the primary institutional vehicle for regional collective action and diplomatic coordination
- RAMSI deployment (2003) — Australia-led intervention in Solomon Islands demonstrated regional security capacity but also paternalistic dynamics (Dobson, 2014)
- China-Solomon Islands security pact (2022) — the bilateral security agreement between Beijing and Honiara triggered alarm across the region and demonstrated China's capacity to fragment Pacific institutional solidarity (Fraenkel, 2022)

## Key Questions
1. Can climate diplomacy constitute a new form of survival-based international norm?
2. Will great-power competition in the Pacific enhance or undermine Pacific Island agency?
3. How should international law address sovereignty for territories rendered uninhabitable by climate change?
4. Is the "Blue Pacific" narrative a viable grand strategy or aspirational rhetoric?
5. Can the Compact of Free Association framework survive Chinese penetration of Pacific diplomacy?
