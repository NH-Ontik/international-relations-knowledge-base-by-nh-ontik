# Indonesia Foreign Policy

## Overview
Indonesia's foreign policy doctrine of *bebas-aktif* ("free and active"), first articulated by Vice President Mohammad Hatta in 1948, prescribes non-alignment in great-power competition while maintaining active engagement in international affairs. As the world's largest archipelagic state, the fourth most populous country, and the largest Muslim-majority democracy, Indonesia occupies a structurally significant position in Southeast Asian and global politics (Leifer, 2001; Acharya, 2014). Its foreign policy illustrates the constraints and opportunities of middle-power diplomacy in a region defined by US-China competition and ASEAN institutionalism.

## Key Themes
- **"Free and active" doctrine** — non-alignment as strategic flexibility; Indonesia refuses formal alliance with any great power while maintaining partnerships with all, a doctrine tested by contemporary US-China rivalry (Hatta, 1948; Anwar, 2011)
- **ASEAN leadership** — Indonesia has been the driving force behind ASEAN's institutional development, including the Treaty of Amity and Cooperation (TAC) and the ASEAN Regional Forum, though its leadership is exercised through consensus-building rather than coercion (Acharya, 2014)
- **South China Sea disputes** — Indonesia's Natuna Islands are claimed by China within its "nine-dash line"; the dispute tests Indonesia's non-aligned posture and its willingness to challenge Chinese assertiveness (Storey, 2016)
- **Maritime fulcrum vision** — President Jokowi's (2014–19) "Global Maritime Fulcrum" (*Poros Maritim Dunia*) sought to reposition Indonesia as a maritime power bridging the Indian and Pacific Oceans (Natalegawa, 2015)
- **Democratic governance** — Indonesia's democratic transition (post-1998) enhances its soft power and credibility as a model of Muslim-majority democratic governance, distinguishing it from Gulf monarchies and authoritarian states (Mietzner, 2018)
- **G20 presidency** — Indonesia's G20 chairmanship (2022) demonstrated middle-power leadership in managing great-power tensions, navigating the Russia-Ukraine war to preserve summit communiqué consensus (Sato, 2022)

## Related Theories
- [[ASEAN]] — Indonesia's foreign policy is inseparable from ASEAN institutionalism; the two are mutually constitutive, with Indonesia providing leadership and ASEAN providing diplomatic framework (Acharya, 2014)
- [[Sovereignty]] — Indonesia's *bebas-aktif* doctrine is ultimately a sovereignty strategy, maintaining freedom of action in a structurally constrained environment (Leifer, 2001)
- [[Middle Powers]] — Indonesia is frequently cited as the paradigmatic middle power: large enough to command attention, too constrained to project great-power influence, relying on institutional and normative entrepreneurship (Cooper, 1997)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Bandung Conference (1955) — Indonesia hosted the Afro-Asian conference that laid the foundations for the Non-Aligned Movement, establishing its credentials as a leader of the postcolonial world (Kunter, 1985)
- *Bebas-aktif* doctrine (1948) — Hatta's articulation of non-alignment became the enduring foundation of Indonesian foreign policy (Hatta, 1948)
- East Timor intervention and independence (1975–99) — Indonesia's invasion and subsequent withdrawal from East Timor marked a pivotal moment in its regional reputation and human rights standing (Robinson, 1995)
- Asian Financial Crisis (1997–98) — economic collapse triggered democratic transition and reorientation of foreign policy toward multilateral engagement (Hewison, 2000)
- Natuna Islands confrontation (2016–present) — China's encroachment on Indonesia's exclusive economic zone tested the limits of *bebas-aktif* and pushed Indonesia toward more assertive maritime postures (Storey, 2016)

## Key Questions
1. Can *bebas-aktif* survive intensifying US-China competition, or will Indonesia be forced to choose?
2. Is Indonesia's ASEAN leadership a constraint or an amplifier of its foreign policy influence?
3. How does democratic governance enhance Indonesia's international standing?
4. Is the "Global Maritime Fulcrum" a viable grand strategy or aspirational rhetoric?
5. How does Indonesia's domestic Islamic politics shape its international posture?
