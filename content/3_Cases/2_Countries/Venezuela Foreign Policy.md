# Venezuela Foreign Policy

## Overview
Venezuela's foreign policy under Hugo Chávez (1999–2013) and Nicolás Maduro (2013–present) represents one of the most ideologically assertive foreign policies in the Western Hemisphere, grounded in Bolivarian revolutionary ideology, anti-imperialism, and petro-politics. Venezuela's case demonstrates how resource wealth can sustain an internationally ambitious foreign policy even as domestic governance collapses, and how great power competition (US vs China/Russia) creates strategic space for revisionist small states (Cannon, 2013).

## Key Themes
- **Petro-politics** — Venezuela's vast oil reserves (world's largest proven reserves) funded Chávez's "oil diplomacy," subsidising allies through preferential supply and Petrocaribe, effectively purchasing international support (Ramírez, 2011)
- **Chavismo and Bolivarian Revolution** — Chávez's ideology blended Bolivarian nationalism, socialist redistribution, and anti-Americanism into a coherent, if volatile, foreign policy framework that resonated across Latin America (Cannon, 2013)
- **ALBA (Bolivarian Alliance)** — ALBA was conceived as a counter-hegemonic alternative to US-led free trade agreements, emphasising solidarity, complementarity, and social welfare over market liberalisation (González, 2009)
- **US sanctions** — escalating US sanctions (2017–present) have progressively isolated Venezuela from global financial markets, contributing to economic collapse while failing to dislodge the Maduro government (Gallagher, 2021)
- **Humanitarian crisis** — hyperinflation, food and medicine shortages, and political repression have produced one of the worst displacement crises in the Western Hemisphere, with over 7 million Venezuelans abroad by 2023 (UNHCR, 2023)
- **Regional implications** — Venezuelan migration has strained neighbouring states (Colombia, Peru, Brazil) and become a defining regional security issue (Jaramillo, 2019)

## Related Theories
- [[Hegemony]] — Venezuela's foreign policy can be read as a sub-regional hegemonic challenge to US dominance in the Americas, using oil wealth and ideological mobilisation (Malamud, 2011)
- [[Sovereignty]] — the Maduro government has invoked sovereignty as a shield against international intervention while systematically violating the rights of its own citizens, illustrating the ambiguity of sovereignty as both protection and weapon (Schedler, 2018)
- [[Humanitarian Intervention]] — Venezuela raises acute questions about the limits of humanitarian intervention: the Lima Group, OAS, and UN have all failed to resolve the crisis, while the US "all options on the table" posture recalls interventionist precedents (Gallagher, 2021)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Election of Hugo Chávez (1998) — marked a continental shift toward left-wing populism and anti-American foreign policy
- Petrocaribe launch (2005) — Venezuela's oil diplomacy became a tool of regional influence, binding Caribbean and Central American states to Venezuelan energy supply (Ramírez, 2011)
- Chávez death and Maduro succession (2013) — Maduro inherited the foreign policy apparatus without Chávez's charisma or regional legitimacy, beginning a period of progressive isolation
- Opposition-led National Assembly (2015) and presidential crisis (2019) — Juan Guaidó's recognition by over 50 states created a dual-claims-of-sovereignty crisis unprecedented in the hemisphere (Schedler, 2018)
- Escalation of US sanctions (2017–present) — maximum pressure campaign restricted oil exports and financial transactions, deepening economic collapse

## Key Questions
1. Can petro-politics sustain a revolutionary foreign policy indefinitely?
2. Has US sanctions been effective policy, or have they deepened the humanitarian crisis?
3. How should the international community balance sovereignty respect against humanitarian concern in Venezuela?
4. Does Venezuela demonstrate the limits of revolutionary foreign policy without economic sustainability?
5. What role should regional organisations (OAS, CELAC) play in resolving the Venezuelan crisis?
