# Greek City-States System

## Overview
The ancient Greek interstate system (c. 800–323 BCE) constitutes one of the earliest documented examples of an anarchic multipolar order, wherein independent *poleis* (city-states) coexisted without a centralised sovereign authority. Thucydides' *History of the Peloponnesian War* remains the foundational text for analysing interstate behaviour in the absence of hierarchy, and the Greek system has served as a recurring reference point for structural realist accounts of balance-of-power dynamics (Thucydides, 431 BCE/1972; Waltz, 1979). Unlike the modern Westphalian system, sovereignty in the Greek world was understood through citizenship and civic participation rather than territorial integrity and non-interference (Hansen, 2006).

## Key Themes
- **Balance of power** as self-regulating mechanism — no single hegemon could dominate permanently; Sparta's hegemony after the Peloponnesian War proved as unstable as Athens' prior primacy (Kagan, 1969)
- **Hegemonic war** as systemic recurrence — the "Thucydides Trap" of hegemonic transitions, where rising powers (Athens) challenge established ones (Sparta), produces structural instability (Allison, 2017)
- **Diplomacy and institutional innovation** — proxeny (proto-consular relations), guest-friendship networks (*xenia*), and heraldic immunity created rudimentary international norms (Herman, 1987)
- **Alliance systems as polarity structures** — the Delian League (Athenian-led maritime alliance) and the Peloponnesian League (Spartan-led land coalition) mirrored the bipolar alliance structures later theorised in Cold War IR
- **International law precursors** — the Amphictyonic League administered shared sacred sites and enforced norms of interstate conduct, anticipating later multilateral institutionalism (Rhodes & Lewis, 2003)

## Related Theories
- [[Realism]] — Thucydides is widely regarded as the first realist theorist; his Melian Dialogue ("the strong do what they can, the weak suffer what they must") encapsulates power-centred logic (Ashley, 1986)
- [[Balance of Power]] — the Greek system is the oldest empirical case of balance-of-power dynamics; Sparta's coalition formed precisely to counterbalance Athenian expansion (Morgenthau, 1948)
- [[Anarchy]] — the absence of overarching authority among poleis prefigures the Westphalian anarchic structure assumed by neorealism (Waltz, 1979)
- [[Polarity]] — the system shifted between multipolarity (pre-5th century), bipolarity (Delian League vs Peloponnesian League), and brief unipolarity (Athenian or Spartan hegemony), demonstrating that polarity is dynamic rather than fixed (Mansbach & Taylor, 2011)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Persian Wars (490–479 BCE) — pan-Hellenic unity against external threat, demonstrating that shared identity could temporarily override anarchy
- Formation of the Delian League (478 BCE) — Athens transformed a defensive alliance into an imperial instrument, illustrating how institutions can be captured by dominant powers
- The Melian Dialogue (416 BCE) — the starkest articulation of realist logic in ancient diplomacy
- Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE) — the paradigmatic hegemonic war; Thucydides' analysis remains unmatched for insight into alliance politics, domestic politics shaping foreign policy (*demokratia* vs *oligarchia*), and the corrosive effects of protracted conflict
- Corinthian War (395–387 BCE) — demonstrated that defeating a hegemon does not produce stability; Sparta's victory over Athens immediately generated counter-hegemonic coalitions
- Rise of Macedon (338 BCE) — Philip II's conquest dissolved the system, showing that multipolar balances are vulnerable to external disruption

## Key Questions
1. Does the Greek system validate or challenge neorealist assumptions about anarchy and self-help? (Waltz 1979 vs Ruggie 1983)
2. How did shared Hellenic identity moderate anarchy — and where did it fail?
3. Is the "Thucydides Trap" a universal structural pattern or historically contingent?
4. What role did domestic regime type (democracy vs oligarchy) play in alliance formation and foreign policy?
5. Can the Amphictyonic League be considered a proto-international organisation, or was it fundamentally different from modern multilateralism?
