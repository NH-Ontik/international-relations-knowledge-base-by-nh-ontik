# Han-Tributary System

## Overview
The Chinese tributary system, formalised during the Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) and persisting in various forms until the late 19th century, represents the most durable alternative to the Westphalian state system in world history. Rather than an anarchic order of formally equal sovereign states, the tributary system structured interstate relations as an asymmetrical hierarchy centred on the Chinese emperor as the "Son of Heaven" (Fairbank, 1968). Peripheral states acknowledged Chinese cultural and political superiority through ritual tribute missions, receiving in return investiture, trade privileges, and a degree of security assurance (Zhang, 1991).

## Key Themes
- **Hierarchy vs anarchy** — the tributary system challenged the Westphalian assumption that the international system is necessarily anarchic; in East Asia, relations were structured by recognised rank and ritual obligation (Johnston, 2003)
- **Tributary trade as economic integration** — tribute missions were accompanied by extensive bilateral trade; the "tribute" was often nominal compared to the gifts reciprocated by the Chinese court, making the system economically self-sustaining (Hansen, 2012)
- **Ritual and legitimacy** — *li* (ritual propriety) served as the governing grammar of interstate relations, embedding material transactions within a normative framework of civilisational hierarchy (Chen, 2015)
- **Sinocentrism** — the system assumed a cultural hierarchy with China at the centre, a "civilisational state" model that contrasts with the territorially bounded Westphalian state (Callahan, 2008)
- **Comparison with Westphalian sovereignty** — while Westphalia posits formal equality and non-interference, the tributary system permitted a form of hierarchical sovereignty that was neither colonial nor egalitarian (Zhao, 2019)

## Related Theories
- [[Sovereignty]] — the tributary system challenges the Westphalian monopoly on sovereignty as absolute territorial authority; it proposed instead a layered, graduated sovereignty (Bartelson, 1995)
- [[Global Governance]] — the system functioned as a form of regional governance without formal institutions, relying on norm consensus and ritual practice rather than treaties (Ikenberry, 2011)
- [[Belt and Road Initiative]] — scholars such as Yan Xuetong (2019) have drawn explicit parallels between China's contemporary BRI and the tributary system's hub-and-spoke economic structure, though others dispute the analogy as anachronistic (He, 2019)
- [[Liberalism]] — liberal IR theory struggles with the tributary system because it was hierarchical yet maintained prolonged stability among participating states, complicating the liberal emphasis on equality and institutions (Johnston, 2003)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Han Dynasty tributary protocols (2nd century BCE) — formalised the "five ranks" of tributary relations and established the model for subsequent dynasties (Lewis, 2009)
- Zheng He's maritime expeditions (1405–1433) — demonstrated the Ming Dynasty's capacity to project tributary influence across the Indian Ocean, preceding European maritime expansion by decades (Levathes, 1994)
- Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689) — the Qing Dynasty's first formal treaty on equal terms with Russia, marking a partial adoption of Westphalian norms within a tributary framework (Perdue, 2005)
- Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60) — European military coercion forcibly dismantled tributary relations and imposed treaty-port sovereignty, ending the system's practical operation
- Submission of Ryukyu (1429–1879) — the Ryukyu Kingdom maintained tributary relations with both China and the Satsuma domain of Japan, illustrating the system's flexibility in accommodating overlapping hierarchies (Smits, 1999)

## Key Questions
1. Is the tributary system best understood as a form of international order or as a form of imperialism by another name? (Fairbank 1968 vs Womack 2006)
2. Does China's contemporary foreign policy — particularly the Belt and Road Initiative — represent a revival of tributary logic?
3. Why did the tributary system prove more durable than the European concert system?
4. Can hierarchical international orders be stable without coercion, or does hierarchy inherently require force?
5. How does the tributary system challenge the Eurocentric assumption that Westphalia represents the universal template for international order?
