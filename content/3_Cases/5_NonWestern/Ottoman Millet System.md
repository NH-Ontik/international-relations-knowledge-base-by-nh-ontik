# Ottoman Millet System

## Overview
The Ottoman millet system (c. 1453–1922) structured governance over a multi-religious, multi-ethnic empire by granting recognised religious communities (*millet*) substantial legal autonomy in personal law, education, and internal affairs, while reserving foreign policy, taxation, and criminal law for the central Ottoman state. Rather than the Westphalian principle of territorial sovereignty exercised over a culturally homogeneous citizenry, the millet system institutionalised pluralistic sovereignty on the basis of religious identity (Braude & Lewis, 1982). It represents one of the most enduring non-Western models of managing diversity within a single political framework.

## Key Themes
- **Pluralistic sovereignty** — the millet system distributed sovereign authority across religious communities, challenging the Westphalian monopoly of the state over all dimensions of governance (Barkey, 2008)
- **Religious communities as legal entities** — each millet operated its own courts, collected its own taxes, and administered its own communal affairs, creating a form of legal pluralism distinct from both Western secularism and theocracy (Davison, 1968)
- **Non-Western pluralism** — the system demonstrates that functional multicultural governance was achieved centuries before Western liberal multiculturalism, complicating Eurocentric narratives of progressive tolerance (Khoury & Kunt, 1997)
- **Comparison with Westphalian secular sovereignty** — where Westphalia separated church and state, the Ottoman model integrated religious authority into governance structures while maintaining inter-communal coexistence (Fortna, 2005)
- **Legacy for modern multicultural governance** — contemporary debates about minority rights, communal autonomy, and secularism in the Middle East and South Asia bear the imprint of millet-system legacies (Visser, 2012)

## Related Theories
- [[Sovereignty]] — the millet system proposed a fundamentally different model of sovereignty: not territorial and absolute, but personal and layered, a challenge to the Westphalian sovereignty concept that remains contested (Bartelson, 1995)
- [[International Law]] — the millet system functioned as an internal international law, with each community's legal autonomy resembling the sovereignty of states within a larger imperial framework (Qualls, 2011)
- [[Postcolonialism]] — postcolonial scholars have used the millet system to challenge the assumption that European models of governance represent the universal template; Ottoman governance was neither backward nor pre-modern but a sophisticated alternative (Kandiyoti, 2005)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Conquest of Constantinople (1453) — Mehmed II formalised the millet system by granting the Greek Orthodox Patriarch authority over Orthodox Christians, establishing the institutional template (Runciman, 1965)
- Gülhane Edict (1839) and Reform Edict (1856) — Ottoman Tanzimat reforms attempted to transform the millet system toward civic equality, partially adopting Westphalian norms of equal citizenship (Davison, 1968)
- Congress of Berlin (1878) — European powers used the "Christian millets" as a pretext for intervention, demonstrating how the millet system's internal logic could be exploited by external actors (Quataert, 2005)
- Abolition of the Caliphate (1924) — Atatürk's dissolution of the millet system's overarching framework forcibly imposed a Westphalian secular-nationalist model, with consequences that continue to shape Turkish and Middle Eastern politics (Zürcher, 2004)

## Key Questions
1. Can the millet system be considered a form of federalism, or is it something qualitatively different?
2. Did the millet system preserve communal identity or entrench sectarian division?
3. How does Ottoman religious pluralism compare with contemporary European secularism?
4. Did the Tanzimat reforms represent modernisation or cultural capitulation to Western norms?
5. Is the millet system relevant to contemporary debates about minority rights in the Middle East?
