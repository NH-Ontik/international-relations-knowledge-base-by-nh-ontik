# Pre-Colonial African State Systems

## Overview
Pre-colonial Africa hosted a diverse array of sophisticated state systems, trade networks, and diplomatic traditions that challenge the Eurocentric narrative that the continent lacked organised political structures before European colonisation. From the Ghana, Mali, and Songhai empires of the western Sahel to the Ethiopian highland kingdoms, Swahili city-states of the East African coast, Great Zimbabwe, and the Kongo Kingdom, African polities developed complex governance mechanisms, interstate norms, and commercial networks that predated and in some cases outlasted European colonial impositions (Vansina, 1990; Herbst, 2000). These systems demonstrate that sovereignty, diplomacy, and international order are not exclusively European inventions.

## Key Themes
- **African diplomatic traditions** — formalised diplomatic protocols, including the use of linguistic registers (e.g., Swahili diplomacy), embassy systems, and treaty-making, existed independently of European influence (Vansina, 1990)
- **Trade networks as interstate infrastructure** — trans-Saharan trade routes, Indian Ocean commercial networks, and intra-African exchange systems created economic interdependence that structured political relations among states (Austin, 2010)
- **Political philosophy as governance** — Ubuntu (*"I am because we are"*) and analogous philosophies embedded communal obligation into governance structures, offering a non-Western model of political legitimacy distinct from both social contract theory and divine right (Tutu, 1999; Metz, 2007)
- **Pre-colonial sovereignty** — African states exercised territorial control, collected taxes, maintained armies, and conducted foreign relations, meeting any reasonable definition of sovereignty prior to colonisation (Herbst, 2000)
- **Challenging Eurocentric IR** — the existence of these systems undermines the claim that IR as a discipline can be properly understood only through European historical experience (Acharya, 2014)

## Related Theories
- [[Postcolonialism]] — pre-colonial African state systems are a central reference point for postcolonial critiques of IR's Eurocentric assumptions; the "Scramble for Africa" disrupted functional political orders rather than imposing order on chaos (Said, 1978; Acharya, 2014)
- [[Sovereignty]] — African sovereignty existed before and was disrupted by colonialism; the contemporary Westphalian sovereignty of African states is a colonial inheritance, not an organic development (Herbst, 2000)
- [[International Society]] — the English School's concept of "international society" has been criticised for excluding African diplomatic communities that had their own norms, rules, and institutions (Buzan, 2004)

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Empire of Ghana (c. 300–1200 CE) — one of the earliest known West African states, controlling trans-Saharan gold and salt trade (Levtzion, 1973)
- Mali Empire and Mansa Musa's pilgrimage (1312–37) — Musa's hajj to Mecca, with its legendary display of wealth, demonstrated the empire's integration into global commercial and religious networks (Levtzion & Hopkins, 2000)
- Rise of Great Zimbabwe (c. 1100–1450) — stone-walled capital of a polity controlling gold trade with the Indian Ocean coast, demonstrating architectural and administrative sophistication (Pikirayi, 2001)
- Swahili city-states (10th–16th centuries) — Kilwa, Mombasa, and Zanzibar operated as independent maritime trading polities with diplomatic relations extending to Persia, India, and China (Horton & Middleton, 2000)
- Scramble for Africa (1884–85) — the Berlin Conference imposed arbitrary boundaries that disrupted existing state systems, creating artificial states whose instability is often misattributed to African "tribalism" (Pakenham, 1991)

## Key Questions
1. How should IR scholars account for non-European international societies?
2. Did colonialism create states in Africa, or did it destroy existing ones?
3. Can Ubuntu and analogous philosophies provide alternative foundations for international norm theory?
4. How did pre-colonial African trade networks compare with contemporaneous European ones?
5. What are the long-term consequences of the arbitrary colonial boundaries for contemporary African interstate relations?
