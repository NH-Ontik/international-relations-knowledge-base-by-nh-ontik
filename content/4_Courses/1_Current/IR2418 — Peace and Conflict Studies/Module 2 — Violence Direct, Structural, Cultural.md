# Module 2 — Violence: Direct, Structural, Cultural

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 2 — The Violence Triangle: Direct, Structural, and Cultural Violence
- **Prerequisites:** Module 1 (Galtung's foundational concepts)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define and distinguish direct, structural, and cultural violence
2. Explain how the three forms of violence interact
3. Critically evaluate Galtung's typology
4. Apply the violence triangle to real-world cases
5. Analyse the structural and cultural dimensions of violence in Bangladesh

---

## 1. Direct Violence

### 1.1 Definition and Characteristics

Direct violence is physical, visible harm with an identifiable actor, subject, and action:

| Element | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Actor** | Identifiable person, group, or state committing violence |
| **Subject** | Identifiable victim(s) who suffer physical harm |
| **Action** | Observable event — killing, assault, bombing, torture |
| **Intent** | Usually present (but can be negligent) |
| **Temporality** | Event-based — has a beginning and end |
| **Visibility** | High — causes immediate, visible suffering |

**Forms of direct violence**: War, genocide, terrorism, assassination, torture, sexual violence, corporal punishment, homicide. Peace research focuses primarily on the organised forms (war, genocide, terrorism) while acknowledging that interpersonal violence is also part of the picture.

### 1.2 Measuring Direct Violence

Peace research relies on several datasets for measuring direct violence:

- **UCDP/PRIO Armed Conflict Dataset**: The standard dataset for armed conflict. Defines conflict as "a contested incompatibility that concerns government or territory where the use of armed force between two parties results in at least 25 battle-related deaths in a calendar year."
- **Uppsala Conflict Data Program (UCDP)**: Battle deaths, one-sided violence (genocide), non-state conflict
- **SIPRI**: Military expenditure, arms transfers, arms production
- **Global Peace Index (GPI)**: Composite index measuring peace in 163 countries across 23 indicators
- **Armed Conflict Location & Event Data (ACLED)**: Real-time data on political violence globally

**Key trends** (see Module 1, §3.3): Battle deaths have declined dramatically since 1945, but civilian deaths in war have increased as a proportion of total deaths (from ~10% in WWI to ~90% in contemporary conflicts). One-sided violence (genocide, terrorism) remains significant.

### 1.3 Critiques of Direct Violence as a Focus

Peace research has been criticised for the intense focus on direct violence:

- **Direct violence bias**: Peace research focuses overwhelmingly on direct violence (war, battle deaths) because it is visible, measurable, and dramatic — but this may divert attention from the more pervasive structural forms of violence
- **State-centrism**: Peace research has historically focused on interstate war while understudying civil war, domestic violence, and community-level violence — though this has shifted since the 1990s
- **Gendering violence**: Direct violence is overwhelmingly committed by men. Peace research has been criticised for not adequately theorising this gender dimension — see §4.3 below

## 2. Structural Violence

### 2.1 Definition and Characteristics

Structural violence is violence that is built into the structure of society — the systematic ways in which social, political, and economic institutions harm people by preventing them from meeting their basic needs.

| Element | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Actor** | No single identifiable perpetrator — embedded in institutions |
| **Subject** | Groups of people (not individuals) who suffer from unequal life chances |
| **Action** | Not an event but a condition — poverty, inequality, discrimination |
| **Intent** | Not necessary — can persist without anyone intending harm |
| **Temporality** | Continuous — exists as long as structures are unchanged |
| **Visibility** | Low — appears as "the way things are" |

Galtung's classic formulation: "If people are starving when this could be avoided, then violence is committed" (1969: 171). The key is **avoidability** — violence is present when the realisation of human potential is below what could have been achieved under different structural arrangements.

### 2.2 Forms of Structural Violence

| Form | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Economic structural violence** | Unequal distribution of resources, exploitation | Global inequality; debt regimes; land dispossession |
| **Political structural violence** | Exclusion from decision-making, repression | Authoritarianism; discrimination against minorities; lack of representation |
| **Social structural violence** | Discrimination based on identity | Caste, race, gender, religion, ethnicity-based exclusion |
| **Health structural violence** | Unequal access to healthcare | Maternal mortality differentials; pandemics and inequality |
| **Educational structural violence** | Unequal access to education | Literacy gaps; school quality differentials |
| **Environmental structural violence** | Unequal exposure to environmental harm | Climate change vulnerability; pollution exposure |

### 2.3 Measuring Structural Violence

Structural violence is more difficult to measure than direct violence:

- **Life expectancy differentials**: Within Bangladesh, life expectancy differs by up to 10 years between the richest and poorest quintiles — a measure of structural violence within one society
- **Under-5 mortality**: 5x higher in the poorest countries vs the richest — avoidable deaths that are structurally determined
- **Global inequality**: The richest 10% own 76% of global wealth, the bottom 50% own 2% — a structural arrangement that prevents billions from meeting basic needs
- **Gender inequality**: GII (Gender Inequality Index), GDI (Gender Development Index) — measure structurally unequal life chances
- **The Positive Peace Index** (Module 1, §3.2) is the most systematic attempt to operationalise structural violence measurement

### 2.4 Critiques of Structural Violence

- **Conceptual stretching**: If everything that reduces human potential is "violence," the concept loses analytical precision (and the moral force of calling someone a "murderer" is diluted when poverty is also called "violence")
- **Determinism**: Structural violence can imply that individuals are victims of structures with no agency — ignoring resistance, resilience, and the ways people transform their conditions
- **Attribution problem**: If there is no perpetrator, who is responsible? Structural violence makes it hard to assign blame and therefore hard to design remedies (this links to debates about individual vs systemic responsibility in ethics)
- **Cold War context**: Galtung developed the concept partly as a critique of Western peace research's Cold War focus on nuclear weapons while ignoring Western structural violence (colonialism, global inequality) — the concept was always political

## 3. Cultural Violence

### 3.1 Definition and Characteristics

Cultural violence refers to aspects of culture that are used to legitimise direct and structural violence (Galtung 1990). It is the "symbolic sphere of our existence" that makes violence "feel right" or at least not wrong.

| Element | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Domain** | Religion, ideology, language, art, science, law, media, education |
| **Mechanism** | Moral justification — changes violence from wrong to right |
| **Persistence** | Deeply embedded — transmitted across generations |
| **Visibility** | Often invisible to those within the culture (taken for granted) |

### 3.2 How Cultural Violence Works

Galtung identifies several mechanisms:

- **Religion**: "Holy war" — violence in service of divine will (e.g., Crusades, jihadist extremism, just war theology)
- **Nationalism**: The nation is sacred — killing and dying for the nation is honourable (e.g., "For king and country", Bengali nationalism in 1971)
- **Racism/ethnocentrism**: Certain groups are subhuman or less worthy — violence against them is justified (e.g., Nazi ideology, Tutsi as "cockroaches" in Rwanda, Rohingya as "Bengalis" in Myanmar)
- **Sexism**: Patriarchy naturalises violence against women (e.g., domestic violence as "private matter", rape as "spoils of war")
- **Science/pseudo-science**: Scientific racism, eugenics, social Darwinism — violence justified by science (e.g., Nazi racial hygiene, colonial anthropology)
- **Media/dehumanisation**: Propaganda, media framing that dehumanises an enemy (e.g., Hutu Power radio in Rwanda, Myanmar military media against Rohingya)
- **Law**: Legal frameworks that legitimise violence (e.g., apartheid laws, emergency powers, colonial legal regimes)

### 3.3 The Cultural Violence Triangle

```
     Religion
        |\
        | \
        |  \
     Nationalism—Racism
```

These three forms are the most powerful and have been the most historically consequential.

### 3.4 Critiques of Cultural Violence

- **Too broad**: What isn't cultural violence? All culture can potentially legitimise violence — the concept risks being a catch-all
- **Agency problem**: If culture legitimises violence, is everyone within that culture responsible? The concept can be used to blame entire civilisations or religions
- **Static view of culture**: Galtung treats culture as a relatively fixed entity — but culture is contested, dynamic, and negotiated. The same tradition can legitimise violence AND inspire peace (e.g., Islam and *sulh*; Christianity and pacifism)
- **Western gaze**: Galtung's examples tend to be non-Western (religion, nationalism in the developing world) while underplaying Western cultural violence (liberal individualism, consumerism, "civilising mission")

## 4. How the Three Forms Interact

### 4.1 The Violence Triangle

The core of Galtung's contribution is that the three forms are not independent — they reinforce each other:

1. **Structural violence creates conditions for direct violence**: Poverty, inequality, and political exclusion create grievances that can erupt into war. Relative deprivation theory (Gurr 1970): people rebel when they perceive a gap between what they have and what they deserve.
2. **Cultural violence legitimises both**: Nationalism makes soldiers willing to kill and die. Racism makes civilians accept genocide. Patriarchy makes domestic violence a "private matter."
3. **Direct violence reinforces the other two**: War creates conditions for increased structural violence (destruction of infrastructure, economic collapse) and for cultural violence (demonisation of the enemy, national trauma).

### 4.2 Cases of the Triangle in Action

**Rwanda 1994**:
- **Direct**: Genocide — 800,000 Tutsi and moderate Hutu killed in 100 days
- **Structural**: Colonial construction of Hutu/Tutsi racial categories; post-colonial state dominated by Hutu elite; land scarcity and economic crisis; exclusion of Tutsi from political power
- **Cultural**: Hutu Power ideology; *Kangura* newspaper and RTLM radio propaganda; dehumanisation of Tutsi as *inyenzi* (cockroaches); church complicity in legitimising violence

**Bangladesh 1971**:
- **Direct**: Genocide by Pakistan army; Bengali intellectuals killed; mass rape
- **Structural**: Political and economic domination of West Pakistan over East Pakistan; Bengali exclusion from state power; language discrimination (1952 language movement); economic exploitation (jute/tea foreign exchange captured by West Pakistan)
- **Cultural**: Bengali nationalism as resistance; West Pakistani ideology of "one nation, two wings"; religious framing (Islam vs secular Bengali identity); language as cultural violence (Urdu imposed)

**Rohingya in Myanmar (2017–present)**:
- **Direct**: Military clearance operations; killings, rape, arson; 1M+ refugees
- **Structural**: Rohingya excluded from citizenship (1982 Citizenship Law); restricted movement, education, healthcare, marriage; economic marginalisation
- **Cultural**: Buddhist nationalist ideology (Ma Ba Tha); dehumanisation of Rohingya as "Bengali illegal immigrants"; hate speech on social media; Aung San Suu Kyi's silence legitimising violence

### 4.3 Gendering the Violence Triangle

Feminist peace researchers have criticised the violence triangle for being gender-blind:

- **Direct violence is gendered**: Men commit the overwhelming majority of direct violence (as soldiers, terrorists, perpetrators of genocide). Men are also the majority of direct victims of war. But women face specific forms of direct violence — sexual violence in war, domestic violence, femicide.
- **Structural violence is gendered**: Patriarchy is a structure that systematically disadvantages women — unequal political representation, unequal pay, unequal access to education and healthcare, unequal legal rights.
- **Cultural violence is gendered**: Masculine norms legitimise violence — militarised masculinity (soldier = real man), honour cultures, "motherland" imagery. Women's peace activism is devalued.

**Elise Boulding's contribution**: Boulding argued that the peace research field itself was gendered — dominated by men studying "hard" topics (war, nuclear weapons, battle deaths) while marginalising "softer" topics (everyday peace, women's peace movements, peace education).

## 5. Critiques of the Violence Triangle

### 5.1 Conceptual Critiques

- **Everything becomes violence?**: If poverty, inequality, and discrimination are "violence," becomes indistinguishable from injustice or bad outcomes. Critics argue peace research should retain the term "violence" for direct, physical harm and find other terms for structural and cultural dimensions.
- **Empirical inadequacy**: The violence triangle is a conceptual framework, not a testable theory. It is difficult to operationalise and empirically verify the relationships between the three forms.
- **Neglect of positive peace**: Galtung's framework focuses on violence (what peace is NOT) rather than on peace itself (what peace IS). This has led some peace researchers to argue the field should focus more on building positive peace rather than analysing violence.

### 5.2 Political Critiques

- **The structural violence concept is controversial**: It implies that critics of existing structures are "anti-violence" — which gives moral authority to challenges to the status quo. Conservatives reject structural violence as a political concept disguised as social science.
- **Cultural violence can be misused**: Accusing a culture of legitimising violence can be used to justify intervention ("they are violent because of their religion/culture"). This is the civilising mission in new form.

### 5.3 Bangladesh Critique

From a Bangladesh/Global South perspective, the violence triangle raises additional questions:
- **Whose structural violence?** Global structural violence (IFI conditionality, debt, climate change) is produced by the Global North but experienced in the Global South. Galtung's framework does not adequately theorise the global dimension of structural violence.
- **Cultural violence or cultural resistance?** What counts as "cultural violence" depends on perspective. Bengali nationalism in 1971 was cultural violence from West Pakistan's perspective (separatism) but cultural liberation from Bangladesh's perspective. The framework needs more attention to power: who decides what counts as cultural violence?

---

## Summary

Galtung's violence triangle distinguishes three forms of violence: direct (physical, event-based), structural (systemic, embedded in institutions), and cultural (legitimising ideologies). The three forms interact and reinforce each other — structural violence creates conditions for direct violence, cultural violence legitimises both, and direct violence reinforces the other two. The framework has been enormously influential in peace research but has also been criticised for conceptual breadth, empirical limitations, insufficient attention to gender, and political contestedness. The Bangladesh cases (1971, CHT, Rohingya) illustrate the power and limitations of the framework.
