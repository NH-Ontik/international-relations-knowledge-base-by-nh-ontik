## 1. Understanding Conflict

### 1.1 Defining Conflict

Peace research distinguishes conflict from violence: **conflict is normal, inevitable, and potentially constructive** — violence is one possible response to conflict, but not the only one.

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Conflict** | A perceived divergence of interests, goals, or values between parties |
| **Violence** | The use of force to impose one party's will on another |
| **Conflict ≠ Violence** | Conflicts can exist without violence; violence can exist without conflict |

**Key implications**: Since conflict is inevitable, the goal of peacebuilding is not to eliminate conflict but to channel it constructively — to transform the way parties pursue their incompatible goals.

### 1.2 Conflict as a Social Process

Conflict is not a static event but a dynamic social process with a life cycle:

```
Latent → Emergence → Escalation → (Hurting Stalemate) → De-escalation → Settlement/Transformation → (Recurrence or Peace)
```

Each stage requires different analytical tools and different intervention strategies.

## 2. Conflict Analysis Frameworks

### 2.1 Galtung's ABC Triangle

| Component | Focus | Question |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| **A**ttitudes | Perceptions, emotions, misperceptions, stereotypes | How do the parties see each other? |
| **B**ehaviour | Actions — cooperation, coercion, violence | What are the parties doing? |
| **C**ontradiction | Incompatible goals/values, structural issues | What is the conflict actually about? |

**How ABC interacts**:
- Contradictions (C) generate negative attitudes (A) which shape behaviour (B) — which in turn reinforces attitudes and deepens contradictions
- Intervention can target any corner: changing attitudes (dialogue, reconciliation), changing behaviour (ceasefire, sanctions), changing contradictions (institutional reform, power-sharing)

**Example — Rohingya conflict**:
- **C**: Competing claims to citizenship, land, and identity in Rakhine State + Myanmar's 1982 Citizenship Law excluding Rohingya
- **A**: Buddhist nationalist ideology (Rohingya as "Bengali illegal immigrants"), Rohingya perceptions of persecution, Aung San Suu Kyi's denial
- **B**: Military clearance operations (2017), ARSA attacks (2016–17), refugee flight (1M+), ICJ action, protests

### 2.2 Root Causes Analysis

Conflict analysis distinguishes between different levels of causation:

| Level | Definition | Example (CHT) |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| **Structural causes** | Long-term, systemic factors that create conditions for conflict | Colonial land policies, Bengali settlement, unitary state structure |
| **Proximate causes** | Immediate triggers that ignite conflict | Killing of PCJSS leader (1977 collapse of negotiation), military crackdown |
| **Underlying causes** | Deep-seated grievances and injustices | Cultural marginalisation, land dispossession, political exclusion |
| **Triggering events** | Specific events that escalate | The 1975 assassination of Sheikh Mujib and resulting political instability |

**Azar's Protracted Social Conflict (PSC) framework**: Azar (1990) argues that the most intractable conflicts arise from the intersection of four factors: (1) communal identity, (2) deprivation of basic human needs, (3) weak governance/poor state-society relations, and (4) international linkages. PSC theory is particularly relevant for understanding conflicts in the Global South.

### 2.3 Burton's Basic Human Needs Theory

John Burton (1990) argues that deep-rooted conflicts are driven by the denial of non-negotiable human needs:

| Need | Description | Denial in Conflict |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Identity** | Recognition, cultural belonging | CHT: Bengali vs indigenous identities |
| **Security** | Physical and psychological safety | Rohingya: citizenship denial = permanent insecurity |
| **Recognition** | Respect, dignity, status | 1971: Bengali language and cultural recognition denied |
| **Participation** | Voice in decisions affecting one's life | East Pakistan: political and economic exclusion |
| **Autonomy** | Control over one's own affairs | CHT: demand for self-governance |

**Key insight**: Needs are non-negotiable. Conflicts driven by unmet needs cannot be resolved through compromise (the standard negotiation approach) — they require structural change. This distinguishes needs from interests (which can be traded) and positions (which are negotiable).

### 2.4 Conflict Mapping

Conflict mapping is a practical tool for analysing a conflict:

| Dimension | Questions |
|-----------|----------|
| **Parties** | Who are the primary, secondary, and tertiary parties? Who are the spoilers? |
| **Issues** | What is the conflict actually about? (Positions, interests, needs) |
| **Dynamics** | How has the conflict evolved? What stage is it at? |
| **Context** | What is the historical, political, economic, and social context? |
| **Power** | Who has power? What kind? (Military, economic, political, moral) |
| **Relationships** | How do the parties relate? Trust, hostility, communication? |
| **Intervention points** | Where can peacebuilders most effectively intervene? |

**Practical tool**: The "onion diagram" (positions-interests-needs):
- **Positions** (outer layer) — What parties say they want. Often incompatible. "I want X."
- **Interests** (middle layer) — Why they want it. More compatible than positions seem. "I want X because Y."
- **Needs** (core) — What they must have. Universal and non-negotiable. Security, identity, recognition, participation.

## 3. Conflict Dynamics — Escalation and De-escalation

### 3.1 Conflict Escalation Models

**Glasl's Nine-Stage Escalation Model** (1997):

| Stage | Characteristic | Party Dynamic | Intervention |
|-------|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| 1. Tension | Hardening of positions | "We can work this out" | Early prevention |
| 2. Debate | Polarisation, verbal confrontation | "We're right, they're wrong" | Facilitation |
| 3. Actions not words | Acting without talking | Talk is useless, act | Mediation |
| 4. Images & coalitions | Stereotyping, seeking allies | "They're evil, we're good" | Power mediation |
| 5. Face-saving | Public commitment to positions | "We can't back down" | De-escalation pressure |
| 6. Threats | Strategic threats, ultimatums | "Do this or else" | Ceasefire |
| 7. Limited strikes | Controlled violence | "A taste of what's to come" | Humanitarian |
| 8. Fragmentation | Destruction of enemy system | "Annihilate them" | Protection |
| 9. Together into the abyss | Mutual destruction | "Better dead than surrender" | External imposition |

**Key insight**: The appropriate intervention strategy depends on the stage of escalation. Mediation works in stages 2–4; in stages 7–9, intervention must focus on containment and protection first, negotiation later.

### 3.2 Conflict De-escalation

De-escalation requires:
- **Ripeness**: A "mutually hurting stalemate" (Zartman) — when both parties perceive that continued conflict is more costly than settlement
- **Reassurance**: Credible commitments that de-escalation will not be exploited
- **Face-saving**: Mechanisms that allow parties to back down without appearing weak
- **Third-party intervention**: Mediation, guarantees, verification
- **Reversal of escalation dynamics**: Reducing enemy images, rebuilding communication, establishing trust-building measures

### 3.3 Spoilers in Peace Processes

Even when parties are ready for peace, **spoilers** (Stedman 1997) can derail the process:

| Type | Motivation | Strategy | Case |
|------|-----------|----------|------|
| **Total spoilers** | Reject peace entirely — want to destroy the process | Exclusion, isolation, defeating | Taliban in Afghanistan (pre-2020) |
| **Limited spoilers** | Have some interests in peace but want to change terms | Engagement, inducements, side-payments | CHT hardliners (opposed to accord) |
| **Greedy spoilers** | Seek to maximise gains from conflict | Economic incentives, criminal prosecution | Conflict entrepreneurs |

**Bangladesh case**: The CHT peace process faced limited spoilers from both sides — Bengali settlers who opposed devolution, and PCJSS factions who wanted more autonomy. The accord's incomplete implementation 25+ years later reflects ongoing spoiler challenges.

## 4. Key Conflict Theories

### 4.1 Greed vs Grievance

A major debate in civil war studies: are conflicts driven by **greed** (economic opportunities) or **grievance** (injustice, exclusion)?

| Theory | Argument | Key Work | Policy Implication |
|--------|----------|----------|-------------------|
| **Greed** | Civil wars are driven by economic opportunities — lootable resources (diamonds, oil, drugs) | Collier & Hoeffler 2004 | Cut off rebel financing, control resources |
| **Grievance** | Civil wars are driven by political and economic exclusion, identity-based discrimination | Gurr 1970; Stewart 2008 | Address horizontal inequalities, build inclusive institutions |
| **Synthesis** | Both matter in different stages — grievances create the conditions, greed determines feasibility | Ballentine & Sherman 2003 | Address root causes AND control resources |

**Bangladesh relevance**: Political violence in Bangladesh (election violence, hartals) is primarily grievance-driven (political exclusion, polarisation) rather than greed-driven — though criminal violence and extortion have economic dimensions.

### 4.2 Horizontal Inequalities

Frances Stewart (2008) argues that **horizontal inequalities** — inequalities between culturally defined groups (ethnic, religious, regional) — are a major cause of conflict:

- **Political HIs**: Unequal political representation, exclusion from power
- **Economic HIs**: Unequal income, assets, employment
- **Social HIs**: Unequal access to education, healthcare, housing
- **Cultural HIs**: Unequal recognition, respect, status

**Bangladesh applications**: HIs exist between Bengali and indigenous communities (CHT), between religious minorities and the Muslim majority (post-1971 discrimination against Hindus), and between regions (Dhaka vs peripheral districts).

### 4.3 Identity-Based Conflict

Constructivist and social-psychological approaches emphasise that conflicts are not "just" about material interests — they are about identity recognition:

- **Social identity theory** (Tajfel & Turner): People derive self-esteem from group membership — threats to group identity provoke strong responses
- **Ethnic conflict** (Horowitz 1985): Ethnic identities are especially conflict-prone because they are "sticky" (hard to change), based on descent, and linked to political claims
- **Instrumentalist view**: Elites manipulate identity for political purposes (Rwanda: Hutu Power as political mobilisation)
- **Constructivist view**: Identity is socially constructed — conflict shapes identity as much as identity shapes conflict

## 5. Bangladesh Conflicts — Analytical Applications

| Conflict | Structural Causes | Proximate Causes | Stage | Key Framework |
|----------|-------------------|------------------|-------|---------------|
| **Liberation War 1971** | Colonial exploitation, language discrimination, economic exclusion, unitary state | 1970 election result rejected, military crackdown March 25 | Ended (military victory) | Azar PSC, Burton needs, HI |
| **CHT conflict 1977–1997** | Land dispossession, cultural marginalisation, assimilation policies | Killing of PCJSS leader, military operations | Ended (accord 1997, partial implementation) | Azar PSC, needs, spoiler analysis |
| **Political violence (post-1990)** | Weak democratic institutions, winner-take-all politics, polarisation | Elections, hartals, confrontations | Cyclical | Glasl escalation, spoilers |
| **Rohingya crisis (2017–)** | Citizenship exclusion, Buddhist nationalism, military interests | ARSA attacks, military crackdown, refugee flight | Internationalised — crisis | ABC triangle, greed vs grievance |
| **Labour violence (RMG sector)** | Exploitative production chains, weak enforcement, union suppression | Wage disputes, factory conditions | Latent/emergent | Structural violence, HI |

## Summary

Conflict analysis is the diagnostic phase of peacebuilding — understanding the causes, dynamics, parties, and drivers of conflict before designing intervention. Key frameworks include Galtung's ABC triangle (attitudes, behaviour, contradiction), root cause analysis (structural / proximate / underlying / triggers), Burton's basic human needs theory (security, identity, recognition, participation, autonomy), conflict mapping, and escalation models (Glasl's 9 stages). The greed vs grievance debate and horizontal inequalities theory provide complementary explanations for why conflicts emerge. Bangladesh offers cases across the conflict spectrum — from military victory (1971) through peace accord (CHT) to ongoing crisis (Rohingya, political violence).
