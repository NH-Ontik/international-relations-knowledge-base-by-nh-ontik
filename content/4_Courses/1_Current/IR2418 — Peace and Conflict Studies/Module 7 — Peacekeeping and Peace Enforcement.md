# Module 7 — Peacekeeping and Peace Enforcement

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 7 — Peacekeeping and Peace Enforcement
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–5

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Distinguish between three generations of UN peacekeeping
2. Explain the key principles and challenges of contemporary peacekeeping
3. Analyse the Brahimi Report and HIPPO Report
4. Evaluate the A4P agenda and peacekeeping reform
5. Assess Bangladesh's role as a top peacekeeping contributor
6. Analyse the relationship between peacekeeping and peace enforcement (R2P)

## 1. Defining Peacekeeping

### 1.1 Peacekeeping in the Peace Spectrum

```
Conflict Prevention → Peacemaking → PEACEKEEPING → Peacebuilding → Reconciliation
                                        |
                            Monitoring ceasefire
                            Troop deployment
                            Civilian protection
```

### 1.2 The "Holy Trinity" of UN Peacekeeping

UN peacekeeping operates on three core principles (Capstone Doctrine 2008):

| Principle | Meaning | Challenge |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Consent of the parties** | Peacekeepers deployed only with host state consent | Host states can withdraw consent; consent may be fragile (Mali, South Sudan) |
| **Impartiality** | Not neutrality — peacekeepers enforce mandate impartially, not passively | Impartiality ≠ inaction; peacekeepers must protect civilians even when it means confronting a party |
| **Non-use of force except in self-defence or defence of the mandate** | Force only as last resort, proportional | Challenge mandates have eroded this — "robust peacekeeping" |

## 2. Three Generations of Peacekeeping

| Generation | Period | Characteristics | Examples | Doctrine |
|------------|--------|----------------|----------|----------|
| **First** (Traditional) | 1948–1988 | Interposition between states; light military observer missions; consent-based | UNTSO (1948), UNEF I (1956), UNFICYP (1964) | Minimum force, no Chapter VII |
| **Second** (Multidimensional) | 1989–1999 | Post-Cold War expansion; civil wars; DDR, elections, human rights, civilian components | UNTAG (Namibia 1989), UNTAC (Cambodia 1992), UNPROFOR (Bosnia 1992–95) | Integrated missions, civilian actors |
| **Third** (Robust) | 1999–present | Chapter VII mandates; civilian protection; offensive force authorised; counter-terror | MONUSCO (DRC 1999–), UNMIL (Liberia 2003–18), MINUSMA (Mali 2013–23) | FIB (Force Intervention Brigade), protection of civilians |

### 2.1 Key Negative Cases That Shaped Peacekeeping Evolution

**Rwanda 1994**: UNAMIR was deployed with a limited mandate (Chapter VI) and was unable to prevent the genocide. The UN refused to reinforce when the genocide began. The failure led directly to the **Brahimi Report** (2000).

**Srebrenica 1995**: Dutch peacekeepers were unable to protect the "safe area" from Bosnian Serb forces. 8,000 Bosnian men and boys killed. The failure demonstrated that peacekeeping without credible force posture is dangerous — and that impartiality in genocide is complicity.

**Somalia 1993 (Black Hawk Down)**: The shift from humanitarian (UNOSOM I) to peace enforcement (UNITAF/UNOSOM II) without clear political strategy led to disaster. The US withdrew after 18 soldiers were killed. This created a peacekeeping "pause" in the mid-1990s.

**Mali 2013–2023**: MINUSMA was the deadliest UN mission (300+ peacekeepers killed). It was a Chapter VII mission with a counter-terrorism mandate — but peacekeeping is not counter-terrorism. The mission ended in 2023 after the Malian junta expelled the UN.

## 3. Major Peacekeeping Reforms

### 3.1 Brahimi Report (2000)

The *Report of the Panel on UN Peace Operations* (Lakhdar Brahimi) was the most comprehensive peacekeeping reform:

| Problem | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|
| Slow deployment | Rapid deployment brigade (UN standby arrangements) |
| Weak mandates | Clear, credible, and resourced mandates |
| No force posture | "Robust rules of engagement" — peacekeepers can use force to defend mandate |
| Under-resourcing | Member states must provide adequate resources |
| No strategic direction | DPKO restructuring, integrated mission planning |
| No early warning | Better information and analysis capacity |

### 3.2 HIPPO Report (2015)

The *Report of the High-Level Independent Panel on Peace Operations* (HIPPO — José Ramos-Horta) assessed peacekeeping after 15 more years:

- **Politics must drive peacekeeping**: Military solutions cannot substitute for political processes
- **Peacekeeping is not the only tool**: Prevention, mediation, peacebuilding must be prioritised
- **Missions must be tailored**: No one-size-fits-all
- **Civilian protection must be central**: PoC is a core mandate, not an add-on
- **Human rights are not optional**: Missions that tolerate human rights abuses lose legitimacy

### 3.3 A4P (Action for Peacekeeping, 2018)

Secretary-General Guterres launched A4P in 2018, reaffirming commitment and identifying four areas for collective action:
1. **Political solutions**: Peacekeeping must support political processes, not substitute for them
2. **Women, Peace and Security**: Full implementation of WPS agenda
3. **Performance and accountability**: Improve effectiveness of peacekeeping personnel
4. **Protection of civilians**: Strengthen PoC in mandates and capabilities

## 4. Contemporary Challenges

### 4.1 Protection of Civilians (PoC)

PoC has become a central peacekeeping mandate since the 2000s. Key challenges:
- **Gap between mandate and capacity**: PoC mandates require peacekeepers to protect civilians, but they often lack the numbers, mobility, and force to do so
- **Credibility gap**: When peacekeepers fail to protect, local populations lose trust
- **Trade-offs**: Forceful protection can alienate host governments and compromise consent

### 4.2 Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA)

Peacekeeping has an unresolved SEA problem:
- Peacekeepers from multiple countries have been implicated in sexual exploitation of local populations (DRC, Central African Republic, Haiti)
- The UN has strengthened measures (vetting, training, investigation, repatriation) but enforcement remains weak
- **Bangladesh**: Bangladesh peacekeepers have a generally positive record, but Bangladesh has faced allegations (CAR 2017) and has taken steps to address them

### 4.3 Performance and Accountability

- **Troop contributing countries (TCCs)** vs UN: TCCs control their troops — the UN has limited disciplinary authority
- **Performance standards**: The A4P agenda pushes for performance metrics, but TCCs resist
- **Technology gap**: Peacekeeping missions often lack basic technology (drones, surveillance, data analysis)

### 4.4 Host State Consent Crisis

- **Mali**: The junta expelled MINUSMA in 2023 after 10 years — showing that consent can be withdrawn
- **South Sudan**: UNMISS operates under constant host-state pressure
- **The consent problem**: As peacekeeping shifts from interstate (first generation) to intrastate (second/third generation), consent from the host state is more fragile — peacekeeping often touches state sovereignty (human rights, protection of civilians, disarmament)

## 5. Peace Enforcement and R2P

### 5.1 Peacekeeping vs Peace Enforcement

| Dimension | Peacekeeping | Peace Enforcement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Consent** | Required | Not required |
| **Force** | Minimal, self-defence | Significant, offensive |
| **Mandate** | Chapter VI | Chapter VII |
| **Impartiality** | Core principle | May be compromised |
| **Example** | UNFICYP (Cyprus) | UNITAF (Somalia 1992) |

### 5.2 R2P and Peace Enforcement

R2P's "third pillar" (timely and decisive response, including military force) has been invoked only once — Libya 2011 — with catastrophic consequences for the R2P norm. The Libya experience has made the UNSC unwilling to authorise peace enforcement for human protection (see IR2417 Module 9).

## 6. Bangladesh and Peacekeeping

### 6.1 Bangladesh as a Top Troop Contributor

Bangladesh has been one of the top three TCCs since the 1990s:

| Statistic | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total deployed since 1988 | 160,000+ |
| Current deployment | ~6,500 (among top 3) |
| Female peacekeepers | ~600 (top contributor of female peacekeepers) |
| Key missions | MONUSCO (DRC), MINUSCA (CAR), UNMISS (South Sudan), UNIFIL (Lebanon) |
| Training institution | BIPSOT (Bangladesh Institute of Peace Support Operations Training, est. 1999) |

### 6.2 Why Bangladesh Contributes

- **Diplomatic**: Peacekeeping enhances Bangladesh's international standing — the single most visible contribution to the UN system
- **Economic**: UN reimbursement provides foreign exchange and income for the military — estimated $300–400M/year
- **Professional**: Peacekeeping experience improves military professionalism, training, and international exposure
- **Strategic**: Peacekeeping contributes to Bangladesh's "middle-power" diplomacy — a demonstration of responsible internationalism

### 6.3 Critiques of Bangladesh Peacekeeping

- **Commercial motive**: Critics argue Bangladesh's peacekeeping is primarily a business model — the army generates revenue, making it politically and economically powerful
- **Performance questions**: Allegations of SEA (CAR 2017), variable quality across units
- **Civilian casualties**: Bangladeshi peacekeepers have been involved in incidents (DRC) raising questions about force posture and civilian protection
- **Gender**: While Bangladesh is a leader in female peacekeeping, women still make up <10% of deployed troops

## 7. Alternatives to UN Peacekeeping

### 7.1 Regional Peacekeeping

| Organisation | Capacity | Key Missions |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **African Union** | African Standby Force (ASF), regional economic communities (ECOWAS, SADC, EAC) | AMISOM/ATMIS (Somalia, 2007–present), AFISMA (Mali, 2013) |
| **NATO** | High capacity, rapid deployment | IFOR/SFOR (Bosnia 1995–2004), KFOR (Kosovo 1999–present), ISAF (Afghanistan 2003–14) |
| **EU** | Battle Groups, civilian missions | EUFOR Althea (Bosnia 2004–present), EULEX (Kosovo 2008–present) |

### 7.2 Private Security

The growing role of private military and security companies (PMCs) in conflict zones raises questions about accountability, regulation, and the privatisation of peace and security.

---

## Summary

UN peacekeeping has evolved from interposition between states through multidimensional post-conflict missions to robust, Chapter VII missions with civilian protection mandates. Major reforms (Brahimi 2000, HIPPO 2015, A4P 2018) have addressed structural weaknesses but fundamental challenges remain: consent fragility, performance accountability, SEA, and the gap between mandate and resources. Bangladesh is a central actor — one of the top three peacekeeping contributors, with a 35+ year record of deployment. The relationship between peacekeeping and peace enforcement (R2P) remains contested, especially after the Libya experience.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Peacekeeping** — UN deployment of military/civilian personnel to monitor/implement peace
- **Capstone Doctrine** (2008) — UN peacekeeping doctrine: consent, impartiality, non-use of force
- **Brahimi Report** (2000) — Reform: robust mandates, rapid deployment, clear doctrine
- **HIPPO Report** (2015) — Reform: political primacy, tailored missions, PoC central
- **A4P** (2018) — Action for Peacekeeping: political solutions, women, performance, PoC
- **PoC** — Protection of Civilians, core mandate since 2000s
- **R2P** — Responsibility to Protect; relationship with peace enforcement contested
- **BIPSOT** — Bangladesh Institute of Peace Support Operations Training

### Key Cases
- **Rwanda 1994** — UNAMIR, peacekeeping failure, Brahimi trigger
- **Srebrenica 1995** — Safe area failure, Bosnian genocide
- **Somalia 1993** — UNOSOM/UNITAF, peace enforcement warning
- **Mali 2013–23** — MINUSMA, deadliest mission, conset withdrawal
- **DRC (MONUSCO)** — Largest current mission, FIB, longest-running
