## 1. The Founding of the United Nations

### 1.1 From League to UN: Continuity and Rupture

The UN was created in the final year of World War II, consciously learning from the League's failure while inheriting its institutional template. The key planning stages:

- **Dumbarton Oaks (Aug–Oct 1944)**: US, UK, USSR, and China produced the preliminary blueprint. The major powers agreed on a Security Council with primary responsibility for peace and security and a veto for permanent members — directly addressing the League's unanimity problem by concentrating authority in great powers.
- **Yalta (Feb 1945)**: Churchill, Roosevelt, and Stalin finalized the veto arrangement.
- **San Francisco Conference (Apr–Jun 1945)**: 51 founding states signed the UN Charter. Smaller states pushed for greater General Assembly authority but ultimately accepted the great-power-dominated Security Council model as the price of creating the organization.

The question of whether the UN was a fundamentally new project or League 2.0 remains contested. The League model was a "league of nations" based on the concept of nations in association. The UN founders consciously adopted an **organizational vocabulary** — world community, international law, human rights — that the League's Covenant did not emphasize (Wallin 2005). However, the Charter's institutional architecture (Assembly + Council with great-power primacy + Secretariat + Court) was directly inherited from the League with specific fixes: enforcement authority under Chapter VII instead of League-style economic sanctions, veto power for the P5 instead of League-style unanimity, and an Economic and Social Council absent from the Covenant (Claude 1956, *Swords into Plowshares*).

## 2. The Six Principal Organs

The UN Charter (Art. 7) establishes six principal organs:

### 2.1 General Assembly (GA)

- **Composition**: All 193 member states, each with one vote
- **Functions**: Deliberative, supervisory, financial, elective
  - Discusses any matter within the scope of the Charter
  - Approves the UN budget (assessed contributions from members)
  - Elects non-permanent Security Council members, ECOSOC members, ICJ judges; appoints the Secretary-General on SC recommendation
  - Adopts resolutions (non-binding on most matters, but carry political weight)
- **Decision-making**: Two-thirds majority on "important questions" (peace and security, budget, admission of new members); simple majority on others
- **Key limitation**: GA resolutions are generally **recommendations**, not binding law. The "Uniting for Peace" resolution (1950) allows the GA to act when the SC is paralyzed by veto, but its authority remains advisory.

### 2.2 Security Council (SC)

- **Composition**: 15 members — 5 permanent (China, France, Russia, UK, US) + 10 non-permanent elected by GA for 2-year terms
- **Primary responsibility**: Maintenance of international peace and security (UN Charter, Art. 24)
- **Powers under Chapter VII (Arts. 39–51)**:
  - Determine threats to peace, breaches of the peace, or acts of aggression
  - Impose sanctions (Art. 41)
  - Authorize the use of force (Art. 42)
  - SC decisions are **binding on all UN members** (Art. 25)
- **The Veto**: Each P5 member can veto any substantive resolution. A procedural vote requires 9/15; a substantive vote requires 9/15 including no P5 veto (abstentions do not count as vetoes).
- **Veto statistics**: The USSR/Russia has used the veto most frequently (~140 times), followed by the US (~85), UK (~30), China (~20), and France (~18). The Cold War saw heavy Soviet veto use; the post-Cold War period has seen increased US veto use on Israel-related resolutions and Russian vetoes on Syria and Ukraine.

### 2.3 Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC)

- **Composition**: 54 members elected by the GA for overlapping three-year terms
- **Functions**: Policy coordination, policy review, and recommendations on economic, social, and environmental issues
- **Role in UN system**: Central mechanism for coordinating the work of the specialized agencies and subsidiary bodies; oversees functional commissions (e.g., Commission on the Status of Women, Commission on Crime Prevention)
- **Limitation**: ECOSOC has limited enforcement power; its effectiveness depends on the willingness of member states and agencies to coordinate

### 2.4 Trusteeship Council

- **History**: Established to oversee the transition of Trust Territories (colonial territories placed under UN administration) to self-government or independence
- **Current status**: Suspended operations on 1 November 1994 when Palau, the last Trust Territory, became independent
- **Significance**: The Trusteeship system represented the post-WWII compromise between continued colonial rule and immediate independence; it provided international supervision of decolonization. However, TWAIL scholars (Anghie 2005) argue it legitimized and extended colonial administration under international authority.

### 2.5 International Court of Justice (ICJ)

- **Composition**: 15 judges elected by the GA and SC for nine-year terms
- **Jurisdiction**:
  - **Contentious cases**: Legal disputes between states that have accepted the Court's jurisdiction (either by declaration, treaty clause, or special agreement). Only **states** may be parties — individuals, NGOs, and IOs cannot bring cases.
  - **Advisory opinions**: Legal questions referred by authorized UN organs and specialized agencies (non-binding but authoritative)
- **Enforcement**: The ICJ has no enforcement mechanism; compliance depends on state consent or SC enforcement.
- **Key cases**: *Nicaragua v. USA* (1986), *Bosnia v. Serbia* (Genocide Convention, 2007), *Gambia v. Myanmar* (Rohingya/Rapha Convention, ongoing), *Ukraine v. Russia* (Genocide Convention, 2022), *South Africa v. Israel* (Genocide Convention, 2024)

### 2.6 Secretariat

- **Head**: The Secretary-General (chief administrative officer), appointed by the GA on SC recommendation for a five-year renewable term
- **Staff**: ~40,000 international civil servants recruited globally, organized into departments and offices
- **Functions**: Carries out the day-to-day work of the UN, services other principal organs, administers programmes and policies
- **Key departments**: Department of Peace Operations (DPO), Department of Political and Peacebuilding Affairs (DPPA), Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA), Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR), and many more
- **Secretary-General (2026)**: António Guterres (Portugal, second term runs through 2027)

The Secretariat embodies the Weberian bureaucratic ideal of an impartial, professional international civil service, but it faces persistent criticism: it is dominated by nationals of Western/wealthy states in senior positions, suffers from geographical imbalance, and has been accused of bureaucratic inefficiency and lack of accountability (Barnett & Finnemore 1999).

## 3. The UN System: Specialized Agencies, Funds, and Programmes

The UN is not a single organization but a **system** of autonomous and semi-autonomous bodies, loosely coordinated through ECOSOC and the Chief Executives Board (CEB).

### 3.1 Specialized Agencies

These are independent IOs brought into relationship with the UN through negotiated agreements (UN Charter, Arts. 57, 63). They have their own membership, budgets, governance structures, and staff:

| Agency | Founded | Mandate |
|--------|---------|---------|
| [[International Labour Organization|ILO]] | 1919 | Labour rights, standards |
| [[Food and Agriculture Organization|FAO]] | 1945 | Food security, agriculture |
| [[UNESCO]] | 1945 | Education, science, culture |
| [[World Health Organization|WHO]] | 1948 | Global health |
| [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]] | 1944 (Bretton Woods) | Financial stability, lending |
| [[World Bank]] Group | 1944 (Bretton Woods) | Development finance |
| [[International Civil Aviation Organization|ICAO]] | 1947 | Aviation standards |
| [[Universal Postal Union|UPU]] | 1874 (pre-UN) | Postal coordination |
| [[International Telecommunication Union|ITU]] | 1865 (pre-UN) | Telecommunications |
| [[World Meteorological Organization|WMO]] | 1950 | Meteorology, climate |
| [[World Intellectual Property Organization|WIPO]] | 1967 | Intellectual property |

The [[Bretton Woods Institutions]] (IMF, World Bank) are distinctive: they have weighted voting (by economic size) rather than one-state-one-vote, giving the US and Europe dominant influence.

### 3.2 Funds and Programmes

These are subsidiary organs of the GA, financed through **voluntary contributions** (not assessed budgets):

- [[United Nations Development Programme|UNDP]] (1965) — Development
- [[UNICEF]] (1946) — Children
- [[United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees|UNHCR]] (1950) — Refugees
- [[World Food Programme|WFP]] (1961) — Food aid
- [[United Nations Relief and Works Agency|UNRWA]] (1949) — Palestine refugees
- [[UN Women]] (2010) — Gender equality
- [[United Nations Environment Programme|UNEP]] (1972) — Environment
- [[United Nations Human Settlements Programme|UN-Habitat]] (1978) — Urban development

### 3.3 Related Organizations

- **IAEA** (1957) — Atomic energy; reports to both the GA and SC
- **OPCW** (1997) — Chemical weapons; linked to the UN through agreement
- **WTO** (1995) — Trade; formally independent, no reporting obligation to GA

### 3.4 UN Human Rights Machinery

The UN's human rights pillar is one of three core pillars (alongside peace and security, and development), established by the Charter's Preamble, Art. 1(3), Art. 55(c), and Art. 68.

- **Human Rights Council (HRC)**: Created in 2006 (GA Res. 60/251) to replace the Commission on Human Rights (1946–2006). Composed of 47 member states elected by the GA for three-year terms. Conducts the **Universal Periodic Review (UPR)** — a peer-review mechanism assessing the human rights records of all 193 UN members — and mandates **Special Procedures** (independent experts, rapporteurs, working groups).
- **Treaty Bodies**: Ten committees monitor implementation of the nine core human rights treaties (ICCPR, ICESCR, CERD, CEDAW, CAT, CRC, CMW, CRPD, CPED). States parties submit periodic reports; committees issue Concluding Observations and General Comments.
- **Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR)**: The Secretariat department supporting the HRC and treaty bodies. Provides technical assistance, field presences, and monitoring.
- **Third Committee**: The GA committee (Social, Humanitarian & Cultural) where human rights resolutions are debated.

The HRC has been criticized for "politicization" — states using membership to shield themselves — and for disproportionate attention on Israel (the only country with a permanent agenda item). For Bangladesh, the UPR process has highlighted concerns over freedom of expression, labour rights, and minority protection; Bangladesh served on the HRC (2023–2025).

### 3.5 Peacebuilding Architecture

The Peacebuilding Commission (PBC), Peacebuilding Fund (PBF), and Peacebuilding Support Office (PBSO) were established in 2005–2006 to address countries relapsing into conflict after peacekeeping missions withdraw.

- **PBC**: An intergovernmental advisory body (31 members from GA, SC, ECOSOC, and top TCCs/donors) supporting post-conflict countries with integrated strategies and sustained attention.
- **PBF**: A multi-year trust fund financing peacebuilding activities.
- **PBSO**: The Secretariat unit supporting the PBC and managing the PBF.

The PBC has been criticized for underfunding and limited political weight. Bangladesh, as a major TCC, has a direct interest: its peacekeepers often perform stabilization and reconstruction functions under the peacebuilding mandate.

Theoretically, Roland Paris (*At War's End*, 2004) critiques UN peacebuilding as attempting to "transplant" liberal institutions into post-conflict societies, potentially destabilizing them — the "liberal peace" critique that connects to postcolonial critiques of the UN's civilizing mission.

### 3.6 International Law Commission (ILC)

The **ILC** is a subsidiary organ of the GA (established 1947, GA Res. 174(II)) responsible for the progressive development and codification of international law under Art. 13(1)(a) of the Charter. Composed of 34 members serving in their personal capacity as legal experts, the ILC has produced foundational texts including the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969), the Articles on State Responsibility (2001), and the Articles on the Responsibility of International Organizations (2011, referenced in Module 1). The ILC represents the UN's unique role in transforming customary law into treaty law — a function that connects the UN's normative authority to the broader international legal order.

### 3.7 International Criminal Justice

- **Ad Hoc Tribunals**: The ICTY (1993–2017) and ICTR (1994–2015), established by the SC under Chapter VII, set precedents on genocide, crimes against humanity, and command responsibility.
- **International Criminal Court (ICC)**: Established by the Rome Statute (1998, in force 2002). A treaty-based independent court with a relationship agreement with the UN. The SC can refer situations (e.g., Darfur 2005, Libya 2011) and defer investigations (Art. 16). The ICC is complementary to national courts.
- **SC-ICC tension**: Permanent members can refer others' situations but are themselves outside ICC jurisdiction (the US, Russia, and China are not parties). For Bangladesh, the ICC investigation into the Rohingya situation (deportation as crimes against humanity) represents a key engagement.

### 3.8 Gender and the United Nations

- **Commission on the Status of Women (CSW)**: A functional commission of ECOSOC (est. 1946); drafted CEDAW (1979).
- **CEDAW Committee**: Monitors implementation of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women. Bangladesh entered reservations to Arts. 2, 13(a), 15(4), and 16(1)(c), citing Sharia — a tension between international law and domestic religious frameworks.
- **UNSC Resolution 1325 (2000)**: Landmark Women, Peace and Security resolution. Bangladesh adopted a National Action Plan in 2020.
- **UN Women**: Established 2010, merging four prior UN entities.
- **Gender parity at the UN**: Women hold ~45% of senior posts (D-1 and above) as of 2026, with persistent criticism of underrepresentation from the Global South.

A feminist critique (Cynthia Enloe, Jacqui True) argues the UN's institutional structure — dominated by male diplomats and security-focused SC agendas — reflects patriarchal assumptions about what counts as "international peace and security."

### 3.9 Environmental Governance and the UN

- **UNEP**: Established 1972 after the Stockholm Conference; coordinates environmental activities, hosts MEA secretariats, produces Global Environmental Outlook reports. Chronically underfunded and lacks enforcement authority.
- **Major Conferences**: Stockholm (1972), Rio Earth Summit (1992, producing UNFCCC, CBD, UNCCD), Rio+20 (2012), and subsequent COPs.
- **Multilateral Environmental Agreements (MEAs)**: UNFCCC (climate), CBD (biodiversity), UNCCD (desertification), Vienna Convention/Montreal Protocol (ozone), all products of UN-led negotiations.
- **Climate Regime**: UNFCCC → Kyoto Protocol (1997) → Paris Agreement (2015) → Loss and Damage mechanism (COP28, 2023).

For Bangladesh, the UNFCCC is the primary forum for climate advocacy at the multilateral level.

## 4. UN Decision-Making and Finance

### 4.1 The UN Budget

- **Regular budget** (~$3.4 billion annually): Assessed contributions from member states based on capacity to pay (GNI); the US pays ~22%, the largest share
- **Peacekeeping budget** (~$6.5 billion annually): Assessed separately with a different scale; permanent members pay a surcharge
- **Voluntary contributions**: Funds, programmes, and specialized agencies rely on voluntary pledges; this creates donor influence over UN priorities
- **Chronic underfunding**: The UN is consistently underfunded relative to its mandates, leading to the "delivery gap" between what the UN is asked to do and what it can do

### 4.2 Decision-Making Dynamics

- **GA**: One-state-one-vote; resolutions are non-binding on most matters. The GA has become increasingly assertive on issues where the SC is deadlocked (e.g., the 2022 resolution condemning Russia's invasion of Ukraine passed 141–5; the 2024 resolution on Gaza)
- **SC**: Binding decisions produce the most consequential outcomes but are subject to P5 veto. The veto has frozen SC action on Syria (14 Russian vetoes since 2011), Ukraine (Russian veto), and Gaza (US vetoes)
- **Consensus norm**: Many UN decisions are taken by consensus rather than vote, reflecting the preference for legitimacy through broad acceptance

## 5. Theoretical Perspectives on the United Nations

The UN can be analysed through multiple theoretical lenses, each highlighting different aspects of its power dynamics, functions, and limits.

### 5.1 Realism

For realists, the UN is largely **epiphenomenal** — a reflection of great-power interests rather than an independent force. The Security Council embodies this logic: five powers granted permanent membership and veto, ensuring no UN action proceeds against their interests (Mearsheimer 1994, "The False Promise of International Institutions").

- **Evidence**: SC paralysis on Syria (14 Russian vetoes), Ukraine (Russian veto), Gaza (US vetoes) confirms the realist prediction that great powers will not permit UN action against their strategic interests.
- **Limitation**: Realism struggles to explain why states invest significant resources in the UN despite its constraints, and why the UN exercises influence on non-security matters (health, environment, norms).

### 5.2 Liberal Institutionalism / Neoliberal Institutionalism

Liberal institutionalists see the UN as facilitating cooperation by reducing transaction costs, providing information, creating focal points, and building trust through repeated interaction (Keohane 1984, *After Hegemony*).

- **Evidence**: Specialized agencies (WHO, ILO, ICAO) coordinate technical cooperation no single state could manage. The **Principal-Agent** model (Hawkins et al. 2006) frames UN agencies as "agents" that states (principals) delegate to, but that develop autonomous interests (Barnett & Finnemore 1999).
- **Limitation**: Overestimates the extent to which formal institutional frameworks constrain great powers.

### 5.3 Constructivism

Constructivists focus on the UN as a **norm entrepreneur** that shapes what states consider legitimate (Finnemore 1996, *National Interests in International Society*).

- **Evidence**: The UN was instrumental in creating the norm of decolonization (1960 Declaration), the human rights regime, R2P (2005 World Summit), and the SDGs. The Secretary-General exercises moral authority beyond formal Charter powers.
- **Limitation**: Can overstate norm influence — great powers violate norms with impunity when interests dictate.

### 5.4 English School

The English School frames the UN as the **central institution of international society** (Bull 1977, *The Anarchical Society*). The Charter defines the rules (sovereignty, non-intervention, peaceful settlement); its organs maintain diplomatic interaction.

- The tension between **order** (SC's great-power management) and **justice** (GA resolutions on human rights, development, self-determination) is a central theme. SC reform is a contemporary manifestation of the order–justice tension.
- **Limitation**: Eurocentric in origin; under-theorizes the UN's role in maintaining structural inequality.

### 5.5 Feminist Approaches

Feminist IR scholars critique the UN as shaped by **patriarchal assumptions** about security, peace, and authority (True 2012; Enloe 1996).

- **Evidence**: The SC's definition of "peace and security" historically excluded gender-based violence. Resolution 1325 (2000) was a landmark but implementation remains limited. The Secretariat has faced criticism for underrepresentation of women in senior posts from the Global South. CSW and CEDAW remain marginalized within the UN system.
- **Limitation**: Feminist critiques are often marginalized within mainstream IO scholarship, limiting policy influence.

### 5.6 Postcolonial / TWAIL Approaches

Postcolonial scholars and TWAIL theorists critique the UN as legitimizing **colonial hierarchies** while claiming universality (Anghie 2005; Chimni 2006; Grovogui 1996).

- **Evidence**: The P5 reproduces the 1945 great-power hierarchy, excluding Africa and Latin America from permanent membership. The Trusteeship system functioned as international administration over colonized peoples (Anghie 2005). Bretton Woods Institutions impose conditionalities the Global South had little role in designing. Sovereignty (Art. 2(1)) protects postcolonial states but also enables authoritarian governments.
- The call for SC reform is, from this perspective, a demand for **decolonizing** the UN's governance structure.
- **Limitation**: Risks treating the UN as merely an instrument of domination, underestimating the spaces for agency that the UN creates for the Global South (G77, NAM, human rights advocacy).

## 6. Security Council Reform: The Central Debate

### 6.1 Why Reform?

The SC's composition reflects 1945, not 2026:
- **Africa** (54 UN members) has no permanent seat
- **Latin America** (33 members) has no permanent seat
- **Asia-Pacific** has only China as a permanent member
- **Europe** is overrepresented (France, UK, plus Russia if counted as European)
- Three of the P5 are European states

The veto system has been criticized for enabling paralysis on major crises — Syria, Ukraine, Gaza. The Pact for the Future (2024, Action 39–40) committed member states to developing a "consolidated model" for SC reform.

### 6.2 Major Reform Coalitions

| Coalition | Position | Proposed Model |
|-----------|----------|----------------|
| **G4** (Brazil, Germany, India, Japan) | Expand in both permanent and non-permanent categories | 25–26 members: +6 permanent (2 Africa, 2 Asia-Pacific, 1 LAC, 1 WEOG) + additional non-permanent; new permanent members would not exercise veto until a 15-year review (G4 April 2026 statement) |
| **African Group (Ezulwini Consensus)** | At least 2 permanent African seats with full veto; total SC expansion to 26 | In line with the Common African Position (CAP); insists on "equality of prerogatives" — new permanent members must have same rights as existing ones |
| **Uniting for Consensus (UfC)** / Coffee Club | Expand only non-permanent seats; oppose adding permanent members | Led by Italy, Pakistan, South Korea, Canada, Turkey, Argentina; propose longer-term renewable non-permanent seats (e.g., 3–5 year terms) |
| **L.69 Group** | Large developing-country coalition supporting expansion in both categories | Overlaps with G4 and African Group positions; includes Small Island Developing States (SIDS) |
| **Fixed Regional Seats proposal** (Co-Chairs, 2025–26) | Permanent seats allocated on regional basis; elected by GA for longer renewable terms | A "bridging proposal" meant to reconcile differing positions; has no official state sponsor; criticized by G4 as functionally equivalent to non-permanent expansion (G4 Statement, February 2026) |

### 6.3 State of Play (June 2026)

The Intergovernmental Negotiations (IGN) have been ongoing since 2009 — 17 years without a breakthrough. The 80th Session (2025–26) has seen:

- The 10 June 2026 Revised Co-Chairs Elements Paper, which acknowledged: "The issues of the veto and categories of membership, in particular permanency, continue to generate the greatest divergences among Member States"
- G4 count of >110 members supporting both-category expansion (beyond the 97 majority threshold)
- African Group submission of its reform model (April 2026)
- Continued UfC resistance to new permanent seats
- Repeated G4 calls for "text-based negotiations" rather than continued thematic debate

The fundamental impasse remains: the P5 must approve any Charter amendment (Art. 108 requires ratification by all P5), but expansion in the permanent category reduces the relative power of existing permanent members.

## 7. The UN's Effectiveness and Legitimacy

### 7.1 Strengths

- **Universality**: 193 members — the only truly universal organization
- **Norm-setting**: UN conferences and treaties define global standards (human rights, environment, disarmament, development goals)
- **Operational capacity**: Peacekeeping, humanitarian relief, development programmes reach millions
- **Legitimacy**: UN authorization confers legitimacy on international action (the "UN seal of approval")
- **Agenda-setting**: The UN defines problems as global issues (climate change, sustainable development, pandemic response)

### 7.2 Weaknesses

- **SC paralysis**: The veto enables great powers to block action on crises they are involved in
- **Implementation gap**: The UN can pass resolutions but often cannot enforce them without great-power support
- **Bureaucratic inefficiency**: Fragmentation, duplication, lack of coordination across the system
- **Funding shortfalls**: Assessed budgets are small relative to mandates; voluntary funding creates donor leverage
- **Democratic deficit**: Power in the SC and Bretton Woods Institutions is concentrated among a few states

### 7.3 Bangladesh and the United Nations

Bangladesh's relationship with the UN illustrates both the promises and limitations of multilateralism:

- **Peacekeeping**: Bangladesh is consistently among the top three troop- and police-contributing countries (TCCs), with over 7,000 peacekeepers deployed as of 2026. This gives Bangladesh operational influence but no decision-making power — it implements missions authorized by a Security Council where it has no permanent voice. Bangladesh's status as a leading TCC is driven by financial incentives (UN reimbursement, salary supplements for peacekeepers), diplomatic prestige, and professional military training opportunities.
- **Labour Rights and the ILO**: The 2013 Rana Plaza collapse (1,134 killed) became a defining Bangladesh-ILO engagement. The ILO coordinated the Bangladesh Accord on Fire and Building Safety (a legally binding agreement between global brands and trade unions), led labour law reforms, and continues to monitor workplace safety. The ILO's role illustrates how a UN specialized agency can exercise influence through standard-setting, technical assistance, and leverage with global supply chains — but also shows the limits: enforcement depends on buyer pressure and domestic political will.
- **SDGs and Development**: Bangladesh's MDG and SDG progress has been heavily supported by UN agencies ([[United Nations Development Programme|UNDP]], [[UNICEF]], [[World Food Programme|WFP]]).
- **Human Rights Engagement**: Bangladesh has served on the Human Rights Council (2023–2025). Its UPR cycles have highlighted concerns over freedom of expression, labour rights, minority protection, and the state of emergency provisions. The tension between Bangladesh's peacekeeping contributions and its domestic human rights record is a recurring theme in UN human rights forums.
- **Rohingya Crisis**: Bangladesh hosts over 1 million Rohingya refugees and has worked extensively with [[United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees|UNHCR]], the International Organization for Migration ([[International Organization for Migration|IOM]]), [[World Food Programme|WFP]], and OCHA. The UN Independent International Fact-Finding Mission on Myanmar (2018) documented genocide and crimes against humanity. Bangladesh supported The Gambia's case under the Genocide Convention at the ICJ (*Gambia v. Myanmar*) — a prime example of a small state using the ICJ to pursue accountability where the SC was paralyzed. The crisis illustrates both the UN's normative capacity (investigation, legal framework) and its operational limits (no enforcement against a P5-aligned state).
- **Climate Advocacy**: Bangladesh chairs the Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF) and actively engages with [[United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change|UNFCCC]], pushing for the Loss and Damage mechanism (operationalized at COP28 in 2023).
- **Diplomatic Strategy**: Bangladesh operates at the UN through the **Group of 77 (G77)** and the **Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)**, amplifying its voice on development, climate finance, and UN reform issues.
- **SC Reform**: Bangladesh supports the African position on SC expansion and has advocated for greater representation of the Asia-Pacific region.

---

## Summary

The United Nations System comprises six principal organs (GA, SC, ECOSOC, ICJ, Trusteeship Council, Secretariat), 15+ specialized agencies, and numerous funds, programmes, and related organizations. The Security Council — with its P5 veto — is the most powerful organ but also the most contested, as its 1945 composition no longer reflects contemporary geopolitical realities. Reform efforts (G4, African Group, UfC, L69) remain deadlocked after 17 years of negotiations. For Bangladesh, the UN is simultaneously a vital partner (peacekeeping, development, humanitarian relief, climate advocacy) and a frustrating institution (exclusion from SC permanent membership, donor-driven agenda, limited voice in Bretton Woods).

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **UN Charter** — The founding treaty of the UN (signed 26 June 1945, entered into force 24 October 1945); defines the purposes, principles, organs, and procedures of the UN
- **P5** — The five permanent members of the Security Council (China, France, Russia, UK, US), each holding veto power
- **Veto** — The power of any P5 member to block a substantive SC resolution; Charter Art. 27(3) requires "concurring votes" of permanent members; abstention has been treated as non-veto by custom since the 1950s
- **Chapter VII** — UN Charter provisions (Arts. 39–51) authorizing the SC to impose sanctions and authorize force to maintain or restore peace; SC decisions under Ch. VII are binding on all UN members
- **Chapter VI** — UN Charter provisions (Arts. 33–38) on pacific settlement of disputes; SC recommendations under Ch. VI are non-binding (contrast with Ch. VII)
- **Peacekeeping** — UN operations deploying military/police personnel with host-state consent to monitor ceasefires and support peace processes (not explicitly in the Charter; developed as a practice between Ch. VI and Ch. VII)
- **Uniting for Peace** — 1950 GA resolution (GA Res. 377(V)) allowing the GA to act when the SC is paralyzed by veto; authority remains advisory
- **Human Rights Council (HRC)** — 47-member intergovernmental body (replacing the Commission on Human Rights in 2006) conducting the Universal Periodic Review and mandating Special Procedures
- **Universal Periodic Review (UPR)** — Peer-review mechanism assessing the human rights records of all 193 UN members
- **CEDAW** — Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women (1979); monitored by the CEDAW Committee
- **Responsibility to Protect (R2P)** — UN principle adopted at the 2005 World Summit: each state has the primary responsibility to protect its population from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity; the international community has a responsibility to assist and, if the state manifestly fails, to take collective action through the SC
- **International Criminal Court (ICC)** — Permanent treaty-based court (Rome Statute 1998, in force 2002) prosecuting genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression; complementary to national jurisdictions
- **Assessed Contributions** — Mandatory payments from member states based on capacity to pay, funding the regular and peacekeeping budgets
- **Ezulwini Consensus / Common African Position (CAP)** — African Union's demand for at least 2 permanent African SC seats with full veto
- **Pact for the Future** — 2024 Summit of the Future outcome document (GA Res. 79/1, Actions 39–40) committing to a consolidated model for SC reform
- **Intergovernmental Negotiations (IGN)** — The member-state-led process for SC reform, ongoing since 2009

3. "Critically assess the role of the veto in the Security Council. Does it protect international peace or enable paralysis?"
4. "What is Bangladesh's relationship with the United Nations? Analyse both the benefits and the limitations of this relationship."
5. "Compare the League of Nations and the United Nations as institutional designs. What specific problems did the UN's founders address?"

8. "Analyse the UN's role in international human rights. How effective are the Human Rights Council and the UPR mechanism?"
