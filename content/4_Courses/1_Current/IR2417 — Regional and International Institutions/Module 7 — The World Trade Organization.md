## 1. From GATT to WTO: The Evolution of the Multilateral Trading System

### 1.1 The GATT System (1947–1994)

The **General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT)** was created in 1947 as a temporary arrangement while the **International Trade Organization (ITO)** — the third Bretton Woods institution — awaited ratification. The ITO's Havana Charter (1948) was never ratified (primarily because the US Congress refused to approve it), leaving GATT as the de facto multilateral trade agreement for 47 years.

GATT was not an organisation but a treaty with a small secretariat. It functioned through a series of **trade rounds**:

| Round | Year | Participants | Scope |
|-------|------|-------------|-------|
| Geneva | 1947 | 23 | Tariff reductions |
| Annecy | 1949 | 33 | Tariff reductions |
| Torquay | 1951 | 34 | Tariff reductions |
| Geneva | 1956 | 22 | Tariff reductions |
| Dillon Round | 1960–61 | 26 | Tariff reductions |
| Kennedy Round | 1964–67 | 62 | Anti-dumping, tariff cuts |
| Tokyo Round | 1973–79 | 102 | Non-tariff barriers, codes |
| **Uruguay Round** | **1986–94** | **123** | **WTO creation, services (GATS), intellectual property (TRIPS), agriculture, textiles** |

**GATT's core principles**:
1. **Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) Principle** (Art. I): Any trade advantage granted to one member must be granted to all members. Non-discrimination is the foundation of the multilateral trading system.
2. **National Treatment** (Art. III): Imported goods must be treated no less favourably than domestically produced goods once they have entered the market.
3. **Tariff bindings**: Members commit not to raise tariffs above agreed bound rates. Bindings provide predictability and security for traders and investors.
4. **Prohibition of quantitative restrictions** (Art. XI): Import quotas are generally prohibited (with exceptions for balance-of-payments, agriculture, and national security).

**GATT's achievements and limitations**: GATT successfully reduced average industrial tariffs from ~40% (1947) to ~5% (1994), contributing to the postwar expansion of world trade. But its weaknesses became increasingly apparent: no coverage of services, intellectual property, or agriculture; weak dispute settlement (requiring consensus to adopt panel reports — meaning the losing party could block adoption); and the proliferation of "grey area" measures (voluntary export restraints, orderly marketing arrangements).

### 1.2 The Uruguay Round and the Creation of the WTO (1986–1994)

The Uruguay Round (1986–1994) was the most ambitious trade negotiation in history, producing:
- The **Marrakesh Agreement Establishing the World Trade Organization** (signed April 1994, effective January 1995).
- The WTO replaced GATT as a **permanent organisation** with binding dispute settlement and a comprehensive legal framework.

**Key Uruguay Round outcomes**:
- **WTO as an organisation**: Single undertaking (members must accept all WTO agreements — no GATT-style "à la carte" participation). Regular ministerial conferences. Secretariat in Geneva.
- **GATS** (General Agreement on Trade in Services): First multilateral framework for services trade.
- **TRIPS** (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights): Minimum standards for IP protection — the most controversial WTO agreement.
- **Agreement on Agriculture (AoA)**: Began the process of agricultural trade liberalisation (with significant loopholes).
- **Agreement on Textiles and Clothing (ATC)** : Phased out the Multi-Fibre Arrangement (MFA) quotas on textiles and clothing over 10 years — critically important for Bangladesh.
- **Dispute Settlement Understanding (DSU)** : Binding dispute settlement with a standing Appellate Body.

**The "Grand Bargain"**: The Uruguay Round was possible because of a North-South bargain. Developing countries accepted new commitments on services (GATS) and intellectual property (TRIPS) in exchange for: (1) the phase-out of MFA quotas in textiles and clothing, (2) agricultural trade liberalisation by developed countries, and (3) strengthened dispute settlement that would give them legal recourse against powerful trading partners. In practice, many developing countries argue that the bargain was one-sided: they implemented costly TRIPS obligations while developed countries delayed agricultural liberalisation and textile quotas were only phased out at the very end of the transition period.

## 2. WTO Structure and Functions

### 2.1 Institutional Structure

- **Ministerial Conference**: Supreme decision-making body, meets at least every two years. Composed of all members' trade ministers.
- **General Council**: Geneva-based body managing day-to-day operations. Meets as the **Dispute Settlement Body (DSB)** and the **Trade Policy Review Body (TPRB)** .
- **Councils**: Goods Council (for GATT), Services Council (GATS), TRIPS Council.
- **Committees**: Covering trade and development, balance-of-payments, budget, and specific agreements.
- **Secretariat**: Headed by the Director-General (currently **Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala**, Nigeria; first African and first woman to hold the position; term 2021–2025, re-elected 2025–2029). The Secretariat has ~625 staff — remarkably small for an organisation governing global trade.
- **Decision-making**: Consensus is the norm (WTO Art. IX). Formal voting (one member, one vote) exists but is almost never used. The consensus requirement has become a major obstacle to progress — any single member can block a decision.

### 2.2 The Dispute Settlement Mechanism (DSM)

The WTO Dispute Settlement Understanding (DSU) is often called the "jewel in the crown" of the multilateral trading system. It provides a **binding, rules-based system** for resolving trade disputes:

1. **Consultations**: Members attempt to resolve disputes bilaterally (60-day period).
2. **Panel establishment**: If consultations fail, the complainant requests a panel. Panel establishment is automatic (unless the DSB decides by consensus *not* to establish — "negative consensus").
3. **Panel ruling**: A three-member panel issues a ruling. The panel report is adopted by the DSB automatically (negative consensus again — unless every member agrees to reject it).
4. **Appellate Body**: Either party can appeal to the seven-member Appellate Body (AB). The AB hears appeals on issues of law and legal interpretation. Its rulings are final and binding.
5. **Implementation**: The losing party must bring its measure into compliance. If it fails to do so, the winning party may be authorised to retaliate (suspension of concessions).
6. **Retaliation**: The "last resort" — authorisation to impose trade sanctions on the losing party. Retaliation is supposed to be in the same sector, but cross-sector retaliation is permitted in some circumstances.

**The Appellate Body crisis**: The AB was effectively destroyed between 2019 and 2020. The United States (under the Trump administration) blocked the reappointment of AB members and refused to allow new appointments, citing concerns about "judicial overreach" (AB exceeding its mandate, creating new law through rulings). By December 2019, the AB lacked the minimum three members needed to hear appeals. As of 2026, the AB remains **non-functional**. The Biden administration maintained the blockage.

**Consequences of the AB crisis**:
- Any WTO member can "appeal to the void" — filing an appeal that cannot be heard, so the panel ruling cannot be adopted.
- As of 2026, over 30 disputes are in "appeals limbo."
- The **Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement (MPIA)** — a plurilateral alternative — has been joined by ~25 WTO members (including the EU, China, Canada, Australia, Brazil) but not the US.
- The crisis threatens the entire dispute settlement system: without binding dispute resolution, the WTO is reduced to a negotiating forum — precisely the weakness that the GATT system had before 1995.

### 2.3 Trade Policy Review Mechanism (TPRM)

The TPRM conducts periodic reviews of each member's trade and trade-related policies. It is a **surveillance** function (analogous to the IMF's Article IV). Reviews are conducted with varying frequency: the largest four trading entities (EU, US, China, Japan) every two years; the next 16 every four years; others every six years; least-developed countries less frequently.

## 3. The Doha Round and the Crisis of Multilateral Trade Negotiations

### 3.1 The Doha Development Agenda (2001–Present)

The Doha Round (launched November 2001, Doha, Qatar) was supposed to be the "development round" — placing developing-country interests at the centre of trade negotiations. The agenda included:
- **Agriculture**: Deeper liberalisation, reduction of developed-country agricultural subsidies.
- **Non-Agricultural Market Access (NAMA)** : Industrial tariff reductions.
- **Services**: Greater market access commitments.
- **Trade facilitation**: Streamlining customs procedures.
- **TRIPS and public health**: Clarifying that TRIPS does not prevent countries from protecting public health (the Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health, 2001).
- **Special and Differential Treatment (SDT)** : Preferential treatment for developing countries.

**Why the Doha Round failed**:
1. **Agriculture impasse**: Developing countries (led by India, Brazil, and the African Group) demanded deep cuts to developed-country agricultural subsidies and tariff protections. The US and EU resisted. The EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) and US farm subsidies were politically untouchable.
2. **NAMA/SDG divide**: Developed countries demanded deeper industrial tariff cuts from large emerging economies (China, India, Brazil) as the price for agricultural liberalisation. Emerging economies refused.
3. **Consensus decision-making**: With 164 members, any single country (or coalition) could block progress. The "single undertaking" meant nothing was agreed until everything was agreed.
4. **The rise of emerging economies**: The 2008 financial crisis shifted the global economic balance. The US and EU no longer had the leverage to drive the agenda, while China, India, and Brazil had the power to resist but lacked the incentive to make the concessions needed for a deal.
5. **The 2008 "collapse"** : The July 2008 ministerial meeting in Geneva came close to a deal but collapsed over a disagreement between the US and India on the Special Safeguard Mechanism (SSM) for agriculture — a mechanism to allow developing countries to raise tariffs temporarily in response to import surges.
6. **Abandonment**: By the 2015 Nairobi Ministerial, WTO members formally acknowledged that the Doha Round was stalled. The 2017 Buenos Aires Ministerial produced no significant outcomes. The 2024 Abu Dhabi Ministerial (MC13) similarly failed to reach agreement on key Doha issues.

**What survived**: A few fragments of the Doha Round were salvaged as **"deliverables"** :
- **Trade Facilitation Agreement (TFA)** (2013, in force 2017): Streamlining customs procedures — the first multilateral trade agreement concluded since 1995.
- **Nairobi Package (2015)** : Elimination of agricultural export subsidies, expansion of the Information Technology Agreement (ITA).
- **Fisheries Subsidies Agreement (2022, MC12)** : Prohibition of subsidies for illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing — a significant environmental achievement after 20+ years of negotiation.
- **MC12 (2022) outcomes**: Agreement on fisheries subsidies (Part I), TRIPS waiver for COVID-19 vaccines (a compromise after 18 months of negotiation), and a Ministerial Declaration on the WTO's response to the pandemic.

### 3.2 The TRIPS Waiver Debate

The COVID-19 pandemic generated a historic debate over intellectual property and access to medicines:

- **The proposal**: India and South Africa (October 2020) proposed a temporary waiver of certain TRIPS obligations for COVID-19 vaccines, diagnostics, and therapeutics — arguing that patent monopolies were constraining vaccine production and access.
- **The opposition**: Pharmaceutical companies, Germany, the UK, and Switzerland opposed the waiver, arguing that patents were not the binding constraint (the real constraints were manufacturing capacity, supply chains, and know-how).
- **The MC12 compromise (June 2022)** : After 18 months of negotiation, WTO members agreed to a narrow TRIPS waiver *only* for COVID-19 vaccines (not therapeutics or diagnostics), with significant limitations (only for "eligible developing countries," voluntary, requiring "necessity" demonstration). The waiver was extended to diagnostics and therapeutics in 2024.
- **Assessment**: The TRIPS waiver debate revealed deep North-South divisions on IP and public health. The compromise was widely criticised as too little, too late (by the time the waiver was agreed, vaccine production capacity had expanded anyway through voluntary licensing and technology transfer). The episode demonstrated that the WTO can respond to crises — but only slowly and minimally.

## 4. The WTO and Regional Trade Agreements

### 4.1 The Proliferation of RTAs

One of the most significant challenges to the multilateral trading system is the exponential growth of **Regional Trade Agreements (RTAs)** :

- GATT/WTO members have notified over 600 RTAs (as of 2026), of which ~350 are in force.
- Major RTAs include: EU (single market), USMCA (replacing NAFTA), CPTPP, RCEP, AfCFTA, Mercosur.
- Almost every WTO member is party to at least one RTA; the typical member belongs to 5–10.

**The "spaghetti bowl" problem** (Bhagwati 1995): Overlapping and inconsistent RTA rules (rules of origin, standards, dispute settlement) increase transaction costs for traders and undermine the MFN principle. Businesses face a different trade regime depending on the origin and destination of their goods — the opposite of the MFN principle's simplicity.

### 4.2 RTAs: Building Blocks or Stumbling Blocks?

The debate over whether RTAs complement or undermine multilateralism is one of the central questions in trade governance:

**"Building blocks" argument** (liberal perspective):
- RTAs can "ratchet up" liberalisation beyond what is possible at the multilateral level (e.g., services liberalisation in the CPTPP, digital trade rules in the USMCA).
- RTAs can serve as "laboratories" for new rules that can later be multilateralised.
- RTAs maintain momentum for trade liberalisation when the multilateral system is stalled.

**"Stumbling blocks" argument** (critical perspective):
- RTAs divert trade from more efficient non-members to less efficient members (trade diversion).
- RTAs fragment the global trading system into competing blocs (US-led vs China-led).
- RTAs can weaken support for the WTO — members invest negotiating capital in RTAs rather than the multilateral system.
- The "competitive liberalisation" dynamic pressures countries to join RTAs for fear of being excluded from markets — not because RTAs are economically optimal.

**The current architecture**: The global trading system is increasingly organised around competing mega-RTAs. The **CPTPP** (Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership, led by Japan, excluding the US after Trump's 2017 withdrawal) and the **RCEP** (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership, led by China, including ASEAN+5) represent two competing models of trade governance — one with strong labour and environmental standards (CPTPP), one with weaker standards (RCEP). The US has promoted the **Indo-Pacific Economic Framework (IPEF)** as a non-tariff alternative. This competition risks the fragmentation of global trade into US-allied and China-allied spheres.

## 5. Theoretical Perspectives on the WTO

### 5.1 Liberal Institutionalism

The WTO is often presented as the exemplary liberal institution — a rules-based system that reduces transaction costs, provides information, facilitates dispute resolution, and enables reciprocity (Keohane 1984; Bagwell & Staiger 2002):
- **Reciprocity and enforcement**: The WTO allows members to negotiate reciprocal trade liberalisation and enforce commitments through binding dispute settlement. This solves the "prisoner's dilemma" of trade policy: without enforcement, each country has an incentive to protect, leading to sub-optimal outcomes for all.
- **The WTO as a commitment device**: Governments use WTO commitments to "tie their own hands" against domestic protectionist pressures.
- **Limitation**: The liberal institutionalist account struggles to explain the stagnation of multilateral negotiations and the persistence of protectionism in wealthy countries.

### 5.2 Realism

Realists see the WTO as a reflection of US power and interests:
- **The WTO as US hegemony**: The GATT/WTO system was created and maintained by the United States as a public good for the liberal international economic order. The US provided market access, absorbed adjustment costs, and enforced the rules.
- **The crisis of the WTO as the crisis of US hegemony**: The US blockage of the Appellate Body, the Trump trade war, and the US retreat from multilateralism all reflect a declining hegemon that no longer sees the WTO as serving its interests.
- **Strategic trade theory**: Realists argue that states engage in trade not for mutual gain but for relative power advantages. The WTO constrains the trade policy tools that states could use for strategic advantage (but powerful states violate WTO rules when they can).
- **Limitation**: Realism underestimates the WTO's autonomous effect on state behaviour — even powerful states mostly comply with WTO rulings.

### 5.3 Constructivism

Constructivists analyse the WTO as a **normative order** and a **site of legal discourse**:
- **The WTO as a legal system**: The DSU has created a quasi-judicial system with a legal community (WTO lawyers, panelists, AB members) that shares a set of norms, practices, and interpretive methods. This community shapes the content of WTO law through its rulings.
- **The development norm**: The concept of "Special and Differential Treatment" for developing countries is a norm that has evolved over time — from GATT's "Part IV" (1965) through the Enabling Clause (1979) to the Doha Development Agenda (2001) — reflecting changing ideas about development and fairness in the trading system.
- **The AB's interpretive practices**: The AB developed a distinctive approach to treaty interpretation (using the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties, considering public international law, developing concepts like "evolutionary interpretation"). This "juridification" of trade governance is a constructivist triumph — but it also generated the backlash (US criticism of AB overreach) that destroyed the AB.

### 5.4 Critical and Postcolonial Perspectives

Critical IPE scholars analyse the WTO as an institution that structures global inequality:
- **TRIPS and the knowledge commons**: The TRIPS Agreement globalised Northern intellectual property standards, imposing significant costs on developing countries (higher medicine prices, restricted access to knowledge and technology). The TRIPS waiver debate revealed the fundamental North-South conflict embedded in the WTO.
- **The "development deficit"**: The Doha Round was supposed to address the development deficit in WTO rules, but it failed. The WTO's rules disproportionately benefit developed countries (strong IP protection, services liberalisation) while the commitments that would benefit developing countries (agricultural liberalisation, textile quota removal) have been delayed or blocked.
- **The WTO and global commodity chains**: The WTO's rules govern trade in a way that reinforces the position of developing countries as commodity exporters and low-wage manufacturers, while protecting the "value-added" activities (design, branding, marketing) concentrated in developed countries.
- **The "dual movement" in trade governance**: The rise of civil society opposition to the WTO (the 1999 "Battle of Seattle," the 2003 Cancún Ministerial collapse) can be understood as a Polanyian "counter-movement" against the disembedding of markets from social regulation. The WTO's evolution — the inclusion of environmental and health exceptions in WTO law, the greater openness to civil society — is a response to this pressure.

## 6. Bangladesh and the WTO

### 6.1 The RMG Sector and Market Access

Bangladesh's relationship with the WTO is defined by its **ready-made garment (RMG) sector**, which accounts for ~85% of exports:

- **The MFA phase-out (1995–2005)** : The Uruguay Round Agreement on Textiles and Clothing (ATC) phased out the Multi-Fibre Arrangement (MFA) quota system over ten years. Bangladesh was a major beneficiary — its RMG exports grew from $6 billion (2004) to $25 billion (2014) and ~$55 billion (2025), as quotas no longer constrained its exports.
- **The "China+1" dynamic**: After the MFA phase-out, China's share of global textile exports grew rapidly, but Bangladesh maintained its competitive position through lower labour costs and preferential market access from the EU ("Everything But Arms" — EBA) and other developed countries.
- **LDC preferences**: As an LDC, Bangladesh benefits from preferential market access under: (1) the EU's Everything But Arms (EBA) initiative (duty-free, quota-free access for all products except arms), (2) similar schemes from other developed countries (US GSP, but Bangladesh has been excluded from US GSP since 2013 following the Rana Plaza disaster and labour rights concerns), (3) the WTO's LDC waiver for services.

### 6.2 LDC Graduation and Its Trade Implications

Bangladesh's scheduled graduation from LDC status (2026) will have profound consequences for its trade regime:

- **Loss of EU EBA preferences**: Post-graduation, Bangladesh will lose duty-free, quota-free access to the EU market. It will fall back to the EU's **Generalised Scheme of Preferences (GSP+)**, which grants preferential access conditional on implementing 27 international conventions (human rights, labour rights, environment, governance). Bangladesh is not yet a GSP+ beneficiary.
- **Trade preference erosion**: Bangladesh's key competitors (Vietnam, Cambodia, Myanmar) will continue to benefit from preferential access that Bangladesh no longer enjoys — reducing Bangladesh's competitive advantage in the RMG sector.
- **Adjustment strategy**: Bangladesh is pursuing free trade agreements with major partners (the FTA with China is under negotiation; the Bangladesh-India FTA has been discussed; Bangladesh is also seeking FTA negotiations with the EU post-graduation).
- **Structural transformation challenge**: LDC graduation means Bangladesh must transition from a preference-dependent export model to one based on competitiveness, diversification (moving beyond RMG to electronics, pharmaceuticals, IT services), and productivity growth. The WTO's role in facilitating this transition (through trade facilitation, capacity building, and transition periods) is critical.

### 6.3 Bangladesh's WTO Engagement

- Bangladesh has been an active WTO member, advocating for LDC interests — particularly in the Doha Round (demanding agricultural liberalisation, cotton subsidy removal, and special safeguards for LDCs).
- Bangladesh has supported the TRIPS waiver for public health.
- Bangladesh participates in the **Joint Statement Initiatives (JSIs)** — plurilateral negotiations on e-commerce, investment facilitation, and MSMEs — which have emerged as an alternative to stalled multilateral negotiations.
- Bangladesh has been involved in WTO dispute settlement: it has been a complainant (e.g., against India on anti-dumping) and a respondent (the US filed a complaint against Bangladesh's cigarette tax regime in 2020).

## 7. Contemporary Challenges

### 7.1 The Appellate Body Crisis and WTO Reform

The WTO's most immediate existential challenge is the non-functioning of the Appellate Body. Reform proposals include:
- **Full restoration**: Reappointing AB members and addressing US concerns (currently blocked by the US).
- **The MPIA**: Maintaining a plurilateral alternative for willing members.
- **Political agreement**: Reducing the role of binding dispute settlement in favour of political negotiation — returning the WTO towards a GATT-style system.
- **The reform agenda**: The 2024 MC13 mandated a "reform process" for the dispute settlement system. Progress has been slow. The US position (that the AB cannot be restored until "systemic concerns" are addressed) remains the principal obstacle.

### 7.2 E-commerce and Digital Trade

The WTO has struggled to develop rules for the digital economy:
- **The E-Commerce Moratorium**: Since 1998, WTO members have agreed not to impose customs duties on electronic transmissions. The moratorium is extended at each Ministerial Conference. Some developing countries (India, South Africa, Indonesia) have opposed renewal, arguing that it deprives them of tariff revenue and benefits developed countries with large digital service exports.
- **The Joint Statement Initiative on E-Commerce**: A plurilateral negotiation among ~90 WTO members (including the EU, US, China, Japan — but not India or South Africa) to establish rules for digital trade: data flows, data localisation, source code protection, digital taxation. The JSI reflects the broader shift from multilateral to plurilateral negotiations in the WTO.
- **Bangladesh and digital trade**: Bangladesh has a stake in the outcome — as a growing exporter of IT services (freelancing, software) and as a potential beneficiary of digital trade rules that lower barriers for services trade.

### 7.3 Trade and Climate Change

The WTO is a central arena for the intersection of trade and climate policy:
- **Carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM)** : The EU's CBAM (phased in from 2023, fully operational 2026) imposes a carbon price on imports of carbon-intensive products (steel, cement, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity). Developing countries (including Bangladesh, India, China) argue that CBAM is discriminatory and violates WTO principles (MFN, national treatment). The WTO has not yet ruled on CBAM's legality.
- **Environmental goods liberalisation**: The stalled Environmental Goods Agreement (EGA) negotiations sought to reduce tariffs on environmental goods. The plurilateral **Climate Club** (proposed by Germany's Lindner, supported by the G7) is an alternative approach.
- **Fisheries subsidies**: The 2022 Fisheries Subsidies Agreement (prohibiting IUU fishing subsidies) is the WTO's first environment-focused multilateral agreement — an important precedent.
- **Fossil fuel subsidy reform**: Trade and climate advocates push for WTO disciplines on fossil fuel subsidies (globally over $7 trillion annually, including externalities, per IMF 2023).

## Summary

The WTO is the institution of global trade governance — successor to the GATT (1947) and product of the Uruguay Round (1994). It provides a rules-based framework for trade liberalisation (MFN, national treatment, bound tariffs), binding dispute settlement (the DSU), and trade policy surveillance. The WTO faces multiple existential crises: the paralysis of the Doha Round, the destruction of the Appellate Body, the proliferation of RTAs that fragment the trading system, and the challenge of adapting to the digital economy and climate imperatives. For Bangladesh, the WTO has been central to the RMG-led export boom, but LDC graduation (2026) threatens to reverse the trade preferences that have sustained it. The WTO's future will be shaped by US-China strategic competition, the tension between multilateralism and minilateralism (JSIs, mega-RTAs), and the organisation's ability to integrate climate and digital governance into its rules.
