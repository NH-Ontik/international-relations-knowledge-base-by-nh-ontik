# Module 1 — Defining International Organizations: Concepts, Typology, and Historical Evolution

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2417 — Regional and International Institutions
- **Module:** 1 — What are International Organizations? (Definitions, Typology, History)
- **Weight:** Foundational — concepts used across all subsequent modules

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define international organizations using the four-element ILC framework and distinguish IOs from related phenomena (conferences, alliances, federations)
2. Classify IOs by type (IGO/NGO), scope, function, authority, and membership using the UIA typology
3. Trace the historical evolution of IOs from 19th-century administrative unions through the League of Nations to the contemporary UN system
4. Explain why sovereign states create IOs using at least three theoretical traditions (realist, neoliberal institutionalist, constructivist) and critically evaluate their explanatory power
5. Identify postcolonial and critical perspectives on IOs, including their relevance to Bangladesh's engagement with international institutions

## 1. Defining International Organizations

### 1.1 The Fitzmaurice/ILC Definition

The most authoritative legal definition comes from Sir Gerald Fitzmaurice, Rapporteur of the [[International Law]] Commission (1956), who defined an IO as "a collectivity of States established by treaty, with a constitution and common organs, having a personality distinct from that of its member-States, and being a subject of international law with treaty-making capacity" (UNYBILC 1956, vol. II, 108).

This yields **four constitutive elements** generally accepted in international law:
1. **Formal basis** — a treaty (multilateral agreement)
2. **Membership** — states (and possibly other IOs)
3. **Institutional structure** — own organs distinct from member states
4. **International legal personality** — capacity to enter treaties, own property, bring claims

The last element was accepted only progressively. For decades, it was not assumed that legal capacity was inherent in the concept of an IO. The eternal tension in IO life is between **member states seeking control** and **the IO seeking autonomy** to perform its functions effectively (Dehousse, cited in OUP EPIL).

### 1.2 The UIA/ECOSOC Operational Definition

The [[United Nations|UN]] Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC) Resolution 288(X) of 27 February 1950 provided the operational distinction used by the Union of International Associations in its *Yearbook of International Organizations*:

> "Any international organization which is not established by intergovernmental agreement shall be considered as a non-governmental organization for the purpose of these arrangements."

In practice, an organization is deemed **intergovernmental** if established by signature of an agreement creating obligations between governments. An organization declaring itself **non-governmental** is accepted as such by the editors (UIA *Yearbook*, 2015).

### 1.3 Distinguishing IOs from Related Phenomena

An IO is **not** (OPIL definition):
- A **conference** of states — conferences meet and dissolve without permanent organs
- An **international organ** like the ICJ — IOs have state membership in a plenary assembly
- An **alliance** — alliances rest on normative duties and consultation without institutional structure
- A **federation of states** — federations claim statehood with constitutional authority over domestic legislation; IOs are not "super-states"

The distinction from confederations is particularly subtle. IOs were historically not distinguished from confederations; the modern term "international organization" stresses **international functions** (cooperation, commonweal) rather than the **state-related functions** (security, defence) of traditional confederations.

## 2. Typology of International Organizations

### 2.1 The IGO-NGO Distinction

The fundamental distinction in IO studies is between IGOs and NGOs (Hurd 2018; Karns & Mingst 2015; UIA):

| Type | Definition | Examples | Legal Basis |
|------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Intergovernmental (IGO)** | State members, created by intergovernmental treaty | [[United Nations|UN]], [[NATO]], [[European Union|EU]], [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]] | Treaty |
| **Non-Governmental (NGO)** | Non-state members, non-profit civil society | Amnesty International, [[MSF]], BRAC, Greenpeace | Domestic law (typically) |

Note: Some frameworks add **Multinational Enterprises (MNEs)** as a third category (for-profit cross-border actors such as Shell or Google), but mainstream IO scholarship does not classify them as international organizations — they lack treaty basis, public-purpose mandate, and the non-profit character that defines IOs. MNEs are more accurately analysed as **private global governance actors** (Ruggie 2011; Cutler, Haufler & Porter 1999) rather than IOs proper.

### 2.2 The UIA Hierarchical Typology (15 Types)

The *Yearbook of International Organizations* assigns each body to one of 15 types (A through O), using a hierarchical system:

- **A-F (Conventional IOs)**: Federations of IOs (A), Universal membership (B), Intercontinental (C), Regionally defined (D), Emanations from other bodies (E), Foundations/special forms (F)
- **G (IO-adjacent)**: Internationally-oriented national organizations
- **H-O (Boundary cases)**: Inactive/dissolved bodies (H), Recently reported (I), Proposed/unconfirmed (J), Religious orders (K), Conference series (S), Multilateral treaties (T), Passive bodies (U), Subsidiary/internal (N)

Each can be further qualified by 13 second-level codes (e.g., `g` = intergovernmental, `e` = commercial enterprise, `b` = bilateral). The full typology captures the spectrum from formal IGOs to entities that are only marginally "international organizations."

### 2.3 Other Dimensions of Classification

**By geographic scope:**
- **Universal/Global** — open to all states (UN, [[World Trade Organization|WTO]], [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]])
- **Regional** — limited to a geographic area ([[African Union|AU]], [[ASEAN]], [[European Union|EU]], [[SAARC]])
- **Intercontinental** — spanning multiple regions but not universal (OECD, OSCE)

**By function:**
- **General-purpose** — multiple issue areas (UN, EU, AU)
- **Specialized** — single issue area ([WHO], [[International Labour Organization|ILO]], [[Universal Postal Union|UPU]], [[World Intellectual Property Organization|WIPO]])

**By authority:**
- **Intergovernmental** — states retain sovereign control, decisions require consent
- **Supranational** — authority above states; binding decisions by majority vote; direct effect on citizens (the [[European Union|EU]] is the most developed example, but the [[African Union|AU]] Peace and Security Council can authorize intervention without member consent under Article 4(h) of its Constitutive Act, and the [[International Criminal Court|ICC]] exercises supranational criminal jurisdiction over nationals of non-party states in certain situations)

**By membership:**
- **Open** — any qualifying state may join (UN)
- **Closed** — restricted by geography, culture, or criteria ([[NATO]], EU, [[African Union|AU]])

## 3. Historical Evolution of International Organizations

### 3.1 Antecedents: The 19th Century

Before the 19th century, there were no IOs in the modern sense. The environment for IO creation required: (a) a world of many states, (b) perceived interdependence, and (c) awareness that ad hoc conferences could not manage continuous technical coordination (OUP EPIL).

**First administrative unions (the "public international unions"):**
- **International Telegraph Union (ITU)** — 1865, to standardize telegraphy across borders
- **General Postal Union (later UPU)** — 1874, to unify postal systems
- **International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM)** — 1875

**River commissions:**
- **Central Commission for Navigation on the Rhine** — 1815 (modernized 1868)
- **European Danube Commission** — 1856

These were **non-political, technical** bodies — the "functional" precursors to the specialized agencies of the UN system. They were created because industrialization, railways, telegraphy, and trade created **material interdependencies** that required continuous administration beyond what conferences could provide.

### 3.2 The League of Nations (1919–1946)

The League was the **first universal political IO** — the "first move to institutions" in world order governance (Tsagourias 2020, *White Rose Research*). It represented a rupture with the pre-institutional world order.

**Key features:**
- Founded by the Treaty of Versailles (1919), Covenant drafted solely by Allied victors
- **Institutionalized collective security** — Article 10: "to respect and preserve against external aggression the territorial integrity and existing political independence of all Members"
- **Organs**: Assembly (all members), Council (great powers + rotating), Secretariat, Permanent Court of International Justice
- **Specialized bodies**: ILO (1919), Health Organization, Mandates Commission, Committee on Intellectual Cooperation

**Why it failed:**
- **US non-membership** — Senate rejected the Versailles Treaty; the absence of the emerging hegemon fatally weakened the League (US Office of the Historian)
- **Unanimity voting** — any member could block action
- **No enforcement capacity** — no standing military; economic sanctions could be evaded
- **Manchurian Incident (1931)** — League condemned Japanese aggression but took no effective action; set a precedent for impunity (PDXScholar thesis)
- **Abyssinia Crisis (1935)** — League sanctions against Italy were partial and ineffective
- **Great Power disengagement** — Germany (1933), Japan (1933), Italy (1937) withdrew

**Legacy:**
Despite its failure, the League established the **template** for modern IOs: assembly + council + secretariat. It introduced **collective security**, **multilateralism**, **sovereign equality**, **self-determination**, and **codification of international law** as governance institutions. "The LoN should not be viewed as an end in itself but as a stepping stone to building our contemporary world order, constituting its protoplasmic ancestor" (Tsagourias 2020). Final assembly: 18 April 1946 — by which time the UN had already been founded.

### 3.3 The United Nations System (1945–Present)

**Founding:**
- Dumbarton Oaks (1944) — preliminary blueprint by US, UK, USSR, China
- San Francisco Conference (April–June 1945) — 51 founding members signed the UN Charter
- A fundamentally new project, not merely League 2.0: the founders consciously moved from a "league of nations" (based on the *nation* concept) to an **organizational model** emphasizing world community, law, and human rights (Wallin 2005, *University of Jyväskylä*)

**Principal organs:**
- [[United Nations|General Assembly]] — deliberative; all 193 members; one state, one vote
- [[United Nations|Security Council]] — primary responsibility for peace and security; 5 permanent + 10 elected
- Secretariat — international civil service led by the Secretary-General
- [[International Law|International Court of Justice]] — judicial arm
- ECOSOC — coordinating economic and social work
- Trusteeship Council (now inactive)

**Specialized Agencies (the "UN Family"):**
The UN system expanded through a network of specialized agencies with individual membership, budgets, and governance — what Tsagourias calls the "network of institutions":
- **[[Bretton Woods Institutions|Bretton Woods]]**: [[International Monetary Fund|IMF]], [[World Bank|World Bank Group]] (1944)
- **[[World Trade Organization|GATT/WTO]]**: General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (1947) → WTO (1995)
- **Specialized agencies**: [[WHO]] (1948), [[UNESCO]] (1945), [[International Labour Organization|ILO]] (1919, absorbed 1946), [[Food and Agriculture Organization|FAO]] (1945), [[United Nations Development Programme|UNDP]] (1965), [[United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees|UNHCR]] (1950), and 10+ more
- **Programmes and funds**: [[United Nations Development Programme|UNDP]], [[UNICEF]] (1946), [[World Food Programme|WFP]] (1961)

**Quantitative growth:**
- 1909: 37 IOs (following UIA methodology)
- 1945: ~80 IOs
- 2024: 350+ IGOs, 6,000+ NGOs (UIA *Yearbook*)
- This proliferation reflects the institutionalization of nearly every issue-area in international relations

**Methodological caveat:** IO counts vary significantly by dataset. The Correlates of War (CoW) IGO dataset counts ~250 IGOs in 2005, while the UIA counts ~350+, because they use different inclusion criteria (Pevehouse, Nordstrom & Warnke 2004). Students should be aware that "how many IOs there are" depends on definitional choices, not just empirical observation.

### 3.4 Why Do States Create IOs? Theoretical Explanations

| Theoretical Perspective | Core Explanation | Key Scholars | Key Text |
|------------------------|-----------------|--------------|----------|
| [[Realism]] | IOs are instruments of powerful states; reflect power distribution | Mearsheimer (1994/95) | "The False Promise of International Institutions" |
| [[Liberalism\|Neoliberal Institutionalism]] | IOs reduce transaction costs, facilitate cooperation, provide information | Keohane (1984) | *After Hegemony* |
| [[Constructivism]] | IOs as norm-setters and identity-shapers; "teachers of norms" | Finnemore (1993), Barnett & Finnemore (1999) | "The Politics, Power, and Pathologies of IOs" |
| **Principal-Agent Theory** | States (principals) delegate to IOs (agents) but face shirking, slippage, and autonomy problems | Hawkins, Lake, Nielson & Tierney (2006) | "Delegation and Agency in International Organizations" |
| Rational Design | IOs are designed to solve specific cooperation problems | Koremenos, Lipson & Snidal (2001) | "The Rational Design of International Institutions" |
| Legalization | "Hard" vs "soft" law shapes institutional design choices | Abbott & Snidal (2000) | "Hard and Soft Law in International Governance" |
| Functional/Supply-Side | States act through formal IOs for centralized, independent, and delegatory benefits | Abbott & Snidal (1998) | "Why States Act Through Formal International Organizations" |
| [[Critical Theory\|Marxist/Coxian]] | IOs legitimize capitalist world order; mechanisms of hegemony | Cox (1981); Gill (1995) | "Social Forces, States and World Orders" |
| Postcolonial/TWAIL | IOs extend Northern legal-ordering; institutionalize colonial hierarchies | Anghie (2005); Chimni (2006) | *Imperialism, Sovereignty and the Making of International Law* |
| Feminist | IOs reproduce gender hierarchies; marginalize women's concerns in security/economics | True (2012); Shepherd (2010) | *The Political Economy of Violence against Women* |

The **dominant puzzle** in IO theory: Why do sovereign states create autonomous organizations that constrain them? The answers range from "they don't — IOs are tools" (realism) to "IOs develop autonomy and pathologies of their own" (Barnett & Finnemore 1999).

### 3.5 Critical and Postcolonial Perspectives

The theoretical table above reflects mainstream "Northern" IR scholarship. Critical traditions offer fundamentally different answers:

**Third World Approaches to International Law (TWAIL):**
Scholars such as Anghie (2005) argue that IOs are not neutral governance platforms but vehicles for extending Northern legal-ordering into the postcolonial world. The League of Nations Mandate System, for instance, institutionalized colonial administration under international supervision rather than challenging it. The IMF's weighted voting and the UN Security Council's permanent membership reproduce 1945 power hierarchies in perpetuity.

**Coxian/Neo-Gramscian Perspective:**
Robert Cox (1981) contended that IOs are mechanisms of hegemony — they stabilize the dominant global economic order by making it appear natural and inevitable. The [[Bretton Woods Institutions]], in this view, embed neoliberal economic principles into the institutional fabric of global governance, constraining developing countries' policy space.

**Feminist IO Critique:**
Feminist scholars argue that IOs reproduce gender hierarchies — through male-dominated leadership, gender-blind policy frameworks, and the marginalization of women's rights within security and economic institutions (True 2012; Shepherd 2010).

**Bandung and the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM):**
The Bandung Conference (1955) and the subsequent NAM represent the first coordinated attempt by newly independent states (including Bangladesh as an independence movement observing) to reshape IO norms. They pushed decolonization onto the UN agenda, challenged institutionalized hierarchy, and argued for economic sovereignty — directly contesting the Northern-dominated IO order described in sections 3.1–3.3.

**Relevance to the Bangladesh context:**
These critical perspectives are not abstract — they speak directly to Bangladesh's experience. Bangladesh is a leading UN peacekeeping contributor yet has no permanent voice in the Security Council that authorizes missions. IMF conditional lending programmes have shaped Bangladesh's economic policy for decades. BRAC, the world's largest NGO by employees, navigates a regulatory environment shaped by the tension between IO norms (NGO freedom) and state sovereignty. SAARC's paralysis due to India-Pakistan rivalry has led Bangladesh to pursue sub-regional alternatives (BIMSTEC, BBIN), illustrating how weak states navigate institutional landscapes not of their making.

### 3.6 Informal and Hybrid IOs

Not all important IOs fit the Fitzmaurice definition. **Informal IOs** such as the G7, G20, and BRICS lack treaties, permanent secretariats, and formal legal personality but exercise significant governance functions. Their rise — particularly BRICS (2009) — represents a Global South challenge to Western-dominated formal IOs. **Hybrid organizations** (The Global Fund, GAVI, ICANN) blend state and non-state participation, public and private funding, and formal and informal governance. These "public-private partnerships" are increasingly important in global health, internet governance, and development finance, yet they do not fit neatly into the IGO/NGO distinction.

## Summary

International Organizations are defined by four elements: treaty basis, state membership, institutional structure, and legal personality. They are classified primarily as IGOs or NGOs, with further sorting by scope (universal/regional), function (general/specialized), authority (intergovernmental/supranational), and membership (open/closed). The historical trajectory runs from 19th-century technical unions → League of Nations → UN system. However, this narrative must be read critically: the "universal" institutions emerged alongside and within colonial hierarchies, and postcolonial/TWAIL scholarship argues they continue to reproduce Northern dominance. Theoretical traditions from realism through constructivism to critical and postcolonial approaches offer competing accounts of why sovereign states create — and sometimes resist — IOs. Bangladesh's own experience in UN peacekeeping, IMF lending, SAARC/BIMSTEC, and NGO governance makes these debates immediate, not abstract.

### Key Definitions
- **International Organization (IO)** — A collectivity of states established by treaty, with constitution, common organs, legal personality distinct from member states (Fitzmaurice/ILC)
- **Intergovernmental Organization (IGO)** — IO whose members are states, created by intergovernmental agreement (ECOSOC Res. 288/X)
- **Non-Governmental Organization (NGO)** — IO not established by intergovernmental agreement; private, non-profit actors
- **Supranational Organization** — IO with authority above states; binding decisions by majority; direct effect (strongest example: EU)
- **Collective Security** — System where aggression by any state is met by collective response from all others (League Covenant, UN Charter Ch. VII)
- **Legal Personality** — Capacity of an IO to enter treaties, own property, bring international claims
- **Spillover** — Neo-functionalist concept: integration in one sector creates pressures for integration in related sectors (Haas)
- **Principal-Agent Problem** — Tension between states delegating authority to IOs and IOs pursuing autonomous agendas that diverge from members' preferences (Hawkins et al. 2006)
- **Informal IO** — Institution without treaty basis or permanent secretariat that nonetheless exercises governance functions (G7, G20, BRICS)

### Essay Framework (Question 4 Sample)
- **Thesis:** While realists argue that IOs merely reflect state power, both neoliberal institutionalist and constructivist approaches demonstrate that IOs acquire meaningful autonomy and independently shape state behaviour, making each theoretical lens necessary for a complete picture.
- **Evidence:**
  - Realism: Mearsheimer's "False Promise" — SC veto, IO paralysis in Ukraine
  - Neoliberalism: Keohane — regime theory, monitoring, iterated cooperation
  - Constructivism: Finnemore — UNESCO as "teacher" of science policy norms; Barnett & Finnemore — UNHCR's bureaucratic autonomy
- **Counter-arguments:** IOs may appear independent but powerful states can defund or bypass them (US withdrawal from UNESCO, UNRWA funding cuts); IO autonomy varies significantly by issue-area.
