## 1. From Humanitarian Intervention to R2P

### 1.1 The 1990s: Humanitarian Intervention Without Consensus

The 1990s produced a paradox: the end of the Cold War enabled unprecedented UN peace operations, but the Security Council was paralysed in the worst genocides:

- **The Gulf War (1991)**: UN-authorised (SC Res. 678) coalition to expel Iraq from Kuwait — but this was collective self-defence, not humanitarian intervention.
- **Northern Iraq (1991)**: After Saddam Hussein's crackdown on Kurds, the US, UK, and France established a "safe haven" and no-fly zone in northern Iraq without UN authorisation. Post facto humanitarian cover (SC Res. 688 "demanded" that Iraq end repression). This was the first case of intervention justified as humanitarian without explicit SC authorisation.
- **Somalia (1992–1993)** : SC Res. 794 authorised "all necessary means" to establish a secure environment for humanitarian relief — the first time the UN authorised a peace enforcement operation for humanitarian purposes without host-state consent. The operation ended disastrously (the "Black Hawk Down" incident).
- **Rwanda (1994)** : The UN had 2,500 peacekeepers in Rwanda when the genocide began. The Security Council **reduced** the force to 270. Approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus were killed in 100 days. The failure of the international community to prevent the Rwandan genocide is the foundational trauma of the R2P project.
- **Kosovo (1999)** : NATO's 78-day air campaign against Serbia — to stop ethnic cleansing in Kosovo — had no UN Security Council authorisation (Russia and China would have vetoed). NATO justified the intervention as "humanitarian." The intervention was legally controversial but politically decisive. The **Independent International Commission on Kosovo** (2000) concluded the intervention was "illegal but legitimate."

### 1.2 The ICISS and the Responsibility to Protect (2001)

The Rwandan genocide and Kosovo intervention produced Kofi Annan's challenge to the UN: "If humanitarian intervention is an unacceptable assault on sovereignty, how should we respond to a Rwanda, to a Srebrenica — to gross and systematic violations of human rights?" In response, Canada established the **International Commission on Intervention and State Sovereignty (ICISS)** , which published *The Responsibility to Protect* (2001).

**ICISS conceptual innovation**: Recasting the debate from a "right to intervene" to a **responsibility to protect**:
1. **Responsibility to prevent**: Address root causes of conflict (most important pillar).
2. **Responsibility to react**: Respond to situations of compelling human need with appropriate measures (sanctions, pressure, military intervention as last resort).
3. **Responsibility to rebuild**: Provide full post-intervention assistance for recovery and reconstruction.

**Threshold criteria for military intervention**: Just cause (large-scale loss of life or ethnic cleansing), right intention, last resort, proportional means, reasonable prospects, right authority (UNSC authorisation).

### 1.3 The 2005 World Summit: R2P Endorsed

At the 2005 World Summit, UN member states unanimously endorsed R2P (paras. 138–139 of the Outcome Document). But the Summit **narrowed** the ICISS formulation:

| ICISS (2001) | World Summit (2005) |
|-------------|-------------------|
| "Just cause" = large-scale loss of life or ethnic cleansing | Genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, crimes against humanity |
| Responsibility to prevent, react, rebuild | Three pillars (see below) |
| SC authorisation preferred; GA or regional authorisation possible if SC fails | Only SC authorisation for military action |
| No explicit exclusion of permanent members | "Intends to commit" — no formal commitment |

**The three pillars** (2005 World Summit, as elaborated by the UN Secretary-General 2009):
1. **Pillar I**: The state has the primary responsibility to protect its population from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity.
2. **Pillar II**: The international community has a responsibility to assist states in fulfilling this responsibility (capacity building, early warning).
3. **Pillar III**: When a state "manifestly fails" to protect its population, the international community must take collective action "in a timely and decisive manner" through the UN Charter (Ch. VI, VII, VIII), with SC authorisation for military measures.

## 2. R2P in Practice

### 2.1 The Libya Case (2011): The "Successful" Intervention That Destroyed R2P

The Libya intervention is R2P's defining case — and its undoing:

**The intervention**: In February 2011, the Gaddafi regime violently repressed protests. SC Res. 1970 imposed sanctions, an arms embargo, and referred Libya to the ICC. When Gaddafi threatened Benghazi ("we will hunt you down door by door"), the Arab League called for a no-fly zone. SC Res. 1973 (March 17, 2011) authorised "all necessary measures" to protect civilians. A NATO-led coalition implemented the resolution.

**The implementation failure**: What began as a civilian-protection mandate became **regime change**. NATO's air campaign provided close air support to rebel forces, General Gaddafi was captured and killed (October 2011), and Libya collapsed into a failed state with two rival governments and civil war.

**The R2P backlash**:
- **Russia and China**: Felt betrayed by NATO's interpretation of the civilian-protection mandate. They argue that "R2P" was used as a cover for regime change. This directly shaped their subsequent positions on Syria (where they vetoed resolutions that could lead to "another Libya").
- **The BRICS bloc**: Brazil proposed "Responsibility While Protecting" (RWP, 2011) — requiring intervening forces to be subject to monitoring and accountability. India and South Africa joined in criticising the Libya implementation.
- **African states**: The Libya intervention was seen in Africa as "regime change by the West" against an African leader. It poisoned the R2P debate on the continent.
- **Scholarly consensus**: Most IR scholars agree that the Libya intervention "saved" civilians in Benghazi in the short term but destroyed R2P's political viability for the foreseeable future. The "R2P backlash" is the central theme of post-2011 R2P scholarship.

### 2.2 Syria (2011–Present): The Failure That R2P Could Not Prevent

Syria is the case that proves R2P's limits. The Assad regime's repression of protests escalated into a civil war (500,000+ dead, 13 million displaced). The Security Council was paralysed: Russia and China vetoed resolutions that would have condemned the regime or imposed sanctions. The UN's inability to respond reflected the post-Libya consensus among P5 members that no "R2P" language should be used.

The Syrian case demonstrates: (1) R2P is only as strong as the Security Council — when P5 interests conflict, R2P cannot be invoked; (2) the Libya backlash made the P5 (especially Russia, China, and even the US) reluctant to invoke R2P; (3) R2P's "credibility gap" — the international community is willing to use force selectively (Libya) but not consistently (Syria).

### 2.3 Other Cases

- **Côte d'Ivoire (2011)**: UN peacekeepers (UNOCI) used force against forces loyal to Laurent Gbagbo after he refused to cede power. SC Res. 1975 invoked R2P language. The operation succeeded — but was possible because France and the UN already had forces on the ground.
- **Kenya (2008)**: The first case where R2P's Pillar II (prevention) was invoked. The UN and international community supported mediation (the Annan-led Kenya National Dialogue) that ended post-election violence. Often cited as an R2P success — the humanitarian crisis was prevented before it reached threshold levels.
- **Myanmar/Rohingya (2017–present)**: The Myanmar military's "clearance operations" against the Rohingya (2017) produced over 700,000 refugees in Bangladesh. The Security Council was divided (China protected Myanmar). The AU's non-interference prevented regional action. R2P was invoked rhetorically but produced no meaningful response. The ICJ's provisional measures (Gambia v. Myanmar, 2020) and the ICC's investigation are the primary institutional responses — legal, not military.

## 3. R2P and Regional IOs

- **The AU's Art. 4(h)**: The AU Constitutive Act gives the Union the right to intervene in a member state for genocide, war crimes, and crimes against humanity — the most far-reaching regional R2P framework. The AU has suspended member states after coups (consistent practice) but has not used Art. 4(h) to authorise military intervention against a member state.
- **NATO and R2P**: NATO has been the primary military instrument for R2P enforcement (Bosnia 1995, Kosovo 1999, Libya 2011). But NATO's use of force under R2P mandates has generated controversy — particularly the Libya "regime change" critique. NATO has no formal R2P policy; decisions are taken by the NAC on a case-by-case basis.
- **ASEAN and R2P**: ASEAN has the weakest R2P engagement. The ASEAN Way's non-interference norm is incompatible with R2P. ASEAN's Intergovernmental Commission on Human Rights (AICHR) has limited mandate. The Myanmar/Rohingya case demonstrates ASEAN's inability to operationalise R2P.

## 4. Theoretical Perspectives

| Theory | View of R2P | Assessment |
|--------|------------|------------|
| **Realism** | R2P is a rhetorical cover for great-power interests. Interventions occur when powerful states have strategic interests (Libya); they do not occur when interests are absent (Syria) or when rivals block (Myanmar). | Powerful states do what they can; weak states suffer what they must. |
| **Liberal Institutionalism** | R2P is a emerging norm that constrains state behaviour and creates expectations of collective action. The norm has not been eliminated by the backlash — it has been contested and is evolving. | Norms matter, but they are weaker than material interests. |
| **Constructivism** | R2P represents a fundamental shift in the meaning of sovereignty — from "sovereignty as control" to "sovereignty as responsibility." The norm is being contested, but it cannot be uninvented. | Sovereignty norms are socially constructed and can be reconstructed. |
| **Critical/Postcolonial** | R2P is a neo-colonial discourse — "humanitarian" justifications for Northern military intervention in the Global South. The selective application (Muslim countries like Libya, not oil-rich autocracies like Saudi Arabia) reveals racialised double standards. | R2P is the global North's mechanism to discipline the global South. |
| **Feminist** | R2P pays insufficient attention to gendered violence. Feminist scholars argue for "feminist R2P" that centres women's experiences of conflict and includes gendered forms of harm (sexual violence, forced marriage, reproductive coercion). | R2P must be broadened and democratised. |

## Summary

R2P transformed the debate about humanitarian intervention by shifting the focus from a "right to intervene" to a "responsibility to protect." Unanimously endorsed at the 2005 World Summit, R2P was operationalised in Libya (2011) — a case that destroyed its political viability as NATO's civilian-protection mandate became regime change. The subsequent paralysis in Syria and Myanmar demonstrates that R2P is only as strong as the Security Council's willingness to act. R2P is not dead — it continues to shape humanitarian discourse and conflict prevention (Kenya), but it has lost the mobilising power it had between 2001 and 2011. For Bangladesh, the Rohingya case illustrates the gap between R2P rhetoric and institutional action.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Humanitarian Intervention** — Use of military force for humanitarian purposes without host-state consent
- **Responsibility to Protect (R2P)** — 2005 UN norm: states have a responsibility to protect populations from mass atrocities
- **ICISS** — International Commission on Intervention and State Sovereignty (2001)
- **Three Pillars of R2P** — State responsibility, international assistance, collective action
- **R2P Backlash** — Post-Libya loss of political consensus on R2P
- **Art. 4(h) AU Constitutive Act** — Regional R2P framework; right to intervene for genocide, war crimes, CAH
- **Responsibility While Protecting (RWP)** — 2011 Brazilian proposal to constrain intervention mandates

### Key Thinkers
- **Gareth Evans / Mohamed Sahnoun** — Co-chairs of ICISS (2001)
- **Kofi Annan** — UN Secretary-General; challenged the UN to resolve the intervention-sovereignty dilemma
- **Ramesh Thakur** — Leading R2P scholar; ICISS member
- **Alex Bellamy** — Leading R2P scholar; tracks R2P implementation globally
- **Aidan Hehir** — Critical R2P scholar; argues R2P is "hollow" without enforcement
- **Anne Orford** — Critical legal analysis of R2P; humanitarian intervention and international law
- **Mahmood Mamdani** — Postcolonial critic of R2P as "saviour politics"
