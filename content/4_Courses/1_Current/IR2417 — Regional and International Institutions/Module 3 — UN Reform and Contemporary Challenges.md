# Module 3 — UN Reform and Contemporary Challenges

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2417 — Regional and International Institutions
- **Module:** 3 — The United Nations: Reform, Financing, and Contemporary Challenges
- **Prerequisites:** Module 1 (IO definition, typology, League → UN) and Module 2 (UN system structure, principal organs, SC reform)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Explain the major UN reform efforts since the 1990s, including management reform, UNDS repositioning, and the UN80 Initiative
2. Analyse the UN financing crisis and its implications for institutional legitimacy and effectiveness
3. Evaluate the UN's response to contemporary global challenges (pandemics, climate change, digital governance, terrorism, migration, peacekeeping, disarmament)
4. Assess the relationship between the UN and regional organizations under Chapter VIII of the Charter
5. Critically evaluate reform proposals through six theoretical lenses: realism, liberal institutionalism, constructivism, English School, feminist approaches, and postcolonial/TWAIL
6. Analyse Bangladesh's stakes in and contributions to UN reform debates, using the Rohingya crisis as a case study

## 1. Why Reform the UN?

### 1.1 Theorising UN Reform

The UN reform debate can be understood through the theoretical frameworks established in Module 2. Each perspective diagnoses a different cause of UN dysfunction and prescribes a different solution:

- **Realism**: Reform is largely irrelevant because the UN reflects great-power interests. The SC veto, the financing crisis, and the paralysis on Syria/Ukraine/Gaza are not bugs — they are features of a great-power system. Reform that threatens P5 interests will be blocked (Art. 108). A realist would predict that the UN80 Initiative's structural proposals (UNDP-UNOPS merger, UNAIDS sunset) will be approved only when they align with donor-state interests. **Implication**: meaningful reform is impossible without a change in the distribution of power.

- **Liberal Institutionalism**: Reform is a problem of institutional design. The UN can be made more effective through better rules, clearer mandates, reformed financing, and strengthened coordination (the UN80 Initiative's workstream approach). The Prisoner's Dilemma logic of collective action can be overcome through institutional learning — demonstrated by the July 2026 budget reform (fixing the credit-return paradox). **Implication**: incremental, technically sound reform is feasible and should be pursued.

- **Constructivism**: The UN's normative environment evolves through practice, persuasion, and learning. Reform happens not through grand bargains but through the gradual acceptance of new norms — the evolution from sovereignty to R2P, from state security to human security, from MDGs to SDGs. The 20-year campaign for the Loss and Damage Fund illustrates how developing countries used UN processes to construct a new norm of climate justice. **Implication**: reform is slow but real; the UN's normative influence should not be underestimated.

- **English School**: Reform represents the tension between **pluralism** (the SC's veto-based order protecting state sovereignty) and **solidarism** (the push for human rights, R2P, and collective enforcement). The UN is the central institution of international society (Bull 1977); its reform debates reveal whether international society is becoming more solidarist or remaining pluralist. The failure to implement R2P in Syria suggests pluralism remains dominant. **Implication**: reform debates are a window into the character of international society itself.

- **Feminist Approaches**: Reform must address the patriarchal structures embedded in the UN's institutional design. The SC remains dominated by male diplomats and masculine security framings; gender-based violence is under-prioritised in peace and security mandates; UN Women is chronically underfunded compared to the peace and security pillar; and peacekeeping scandals involving sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA) expose the gender power dynamics within the system (Cohn 2008; True 2012; Enloe 1996). **Implication**: genuine reform requires feminist institutional design, not just gender mainstreaming.

- **Postcolonial / TWAIL**: The UN's architecture is irredeemably shaped by colonial hierarchies — the SC's P5 composition, the weighted voting at Bretton Woods, the Trusteeship system's civilising mission discourse, and the sovereignty doctrine that protects postcolonial states while also enabling authoritarian governments (Anghie 2005; Chimni 2006). Reform that does not address these structural hierarchies is cosmetic. The SC reform impasse, the failure of the Pandemic Treaty to ensure equitable vaccine access, and the inadequacy of the Loss and Damage Fund all reflect this deeper structural inequality. **Implication**: meaningful reform requires decolonising the UN.

The remainder of this module evaluates specific reform efforts through these theoretical lenses.

### 1.2 Why Reform Now?

The UN has been in a state of near-permanent reform since the end of the Cold War. The gap between what the UN is asked to do and what it can actually deliver — the "delivery gap" — has widened as global challenges have multiplied and intensified.

Three structural tensions drive reform demands:
- **Legitimacy vs Effectiveness**: The UN's universal membership gives it unique legitimacy, but its decision-making structures (particularly the SC) produce paralysis on major crises
- **Mandate vs Resources**: Member states assign the UN ever-expanding mandates (peacekeeping, development, human rights, health, climate) but are unwilling to provide commensurate funding
- **1945 Architecture vs 2026 Realities**: The UN's institutional design reflects the postwar settlement; it has struggled to adapt to decolonization, globalization, the rise of new powers, and transnational threats

## 2. Management and Structural Reform

### 2.1 The Annan Era Reforms (1997–2006)

Kofi Annan (SG 1997–2006) initiated the most ambitious reforms since the UN's founding:

- **1997 reform**: Created the Deputy Secretary-General position, consolidated departments, established the UN Development Group (UNDG), and introduced programme budgeting
- **2005 World Summit**: Endorsed the Responsibility to Protect (R2P), created the Peacebuilding Commission (PBC) and Human Rights Council (HRC), and committed to management reform
- **Secretariat restructuring**: Streamlined the Department of Peacekeeping Operations (DPKO) and created the Department of Field Support (DFS), the Department of Political Affairs (DPA), and the Counter-Terrorism Committee

The Annan reforms were ambitious but partial. The 2005 Outcome Document was significantly weaker than Annan's original proposals (*In Larger Freedom*, 2005) due to opposition from the US (on development funding) and developing countries (on R2P and SC expansion).

### 2.2 The 2017 Reform: Peace, Development, and Management

António Guterres launched three parallel reforms in 2017:

- **Peace and Security Reform**: Restructured the peace and security pillar, creating the Department of Political and Peacebuilding Affairs (DPPA, merging DPA and the Peacebuilding Support Office) and the Department of Peace Operations (DPO, replacing DPKO). Aimed to shift from "reactive crisis management" to "preventive action."
- **Development System Reform (UNDS Repositioning)**: The most far-reaching reform of the UN development system. Mandated by GA Res. 72/279 (2018), it sought to reposition the UN development system around the **UN Sustainable Development Cooperation Framework** as "the most important instrument for the planning and implementation of UN development activities in each country." A **reinvigorated Resident Coordinator (RC) system** was at its core — RCs now report directly to the UN Secretary-General rather than through UNDP. The aim was to produce more coherent, accountable, and effective country-level support for the 2030 Agenda.
- **Management Reform**: Decentralized decision-making, simplified rules, established a new Management Strategy, Policy and Compliance Department, and shifted to a "global service delivery model."

A 2025 system-wide evaluation found that while the repositioning had produced **important improvements** (more coherent analysis and planning, widespread appreciation for the RC system, increased non-resident entity participation), the **gap between strategic intent and operational reality remained significant**. Cooperation Frameworks had not yet become the most important planning instrument, and UNCTs had not significantly reconfigured around CF priorities (UN SWEO 2025, *System-Wide Evaluation on Progress Towards a New Generation of UNCTs*).

### 2.3 The UN80 Initiative (2025–2026)

The **UN80 Initiative**, launched by Guterres in March 2025 as the UN marked its 80th anniversary, is the most comprehensive reform effort since 1945. It brings previous reforms under a single framework with three workstreams:

- **Workstream 1 (Efficiency)**: Optimizing the Secretariat. A 21% reduction in staff positions in 2026, new common administrative platforms, relocation of ~230 posts from high-cost to lower-cost locations (part of 2,300 positions system-wide). Virtually all proposals approved by the GA in December 2025.
- **Workstream 2 (Mandate Review)**: Addressing gaps in how mandates are created, delivered, and reviewed. An Informal Ad Hoc Working Group (co-chaired by Jamaica and New Zealand) advanced proposals; GA Res. 80/251 established a new basis for mandate discipline.
- **Workstream 3 (Structural Change)**: "Shifting Paradigms: United to Deliver" (September 2025) proposed:
  - Peace and security: DPPA and DPO reorganization, consolidated peacebuilding support, networked peace operations
  - Humanitarian: New Humanitarian Compact to simplify coordination and integrate supply chains
  - Sustainable development: Merger assessment for UNDP and UNOPS; reconfigured UN country teams; reset of regional setups; **UNAIDS sunset by end of 2026**; single entry point for macroeconomic analysis (DESA, UNCTAD, Regional Commissions)
  - Human rights: Establishment of a Human Rights Group to align UN system efforts
  - Cross-pillar: **Regional Integrated Platforms** — single entry points for peace, development, and human rights expertise at the regional level
  - Shared services: Unified Services Roadmap, global shared services for supply chain, ICT, payroll, finance, HR, and ERP systems

The UN80 Initiative represents a paradigm shift from a "fragmented system of projectized support" toward integrated, cross-pillar delivery. As Guterres stated, "If the UN were created today, against the backdrop of a global development emergency, there is no doubt its development arm would be designed differently" (UN80 Progress Report, 2025).

## 3. The UN Financing Crisis

### 3.1 The Assessed Contribution System

The UN is funded through three streams:
- **Regular budget** (~$3.4B for 2026): Assessed contributions based on capacity to pay (GNI). The US pays ~22%; the next largest are China (~15%), Japan (~8%), Germany (~6%), and the UK (~4%).
- **Peacekeeping budget** (~$6.5B): Assessed separately with a different scale; P5 members pay a surcharge.
- **Voluntary contributions**: Funds, programmes, and specialized agencies rely on voluntary pledges, creating donor leverage over UN priorities.

### 3.2 The Liquidity Crisis (2025–2026)

By mid-2026, the UN faced its most severe financial crisis in decades:
- **Record arrears**: Outstanding assessments reached $2.8 billion (including $1.57B in arrears from 2025 — the lowest collection rate in seven years).
- **US non-payment**: The US owed $2.04B to the regular budget and $2.25B to peacekeeping (as of April 2026). It did not pay its regular budget contribution for 2025 and offered only 30% of its expected peacekeeping funding.
- **China arrears**: China owed $429M regular + $870M peacekeeping.
- **UN cash reserves**: Only sufficient to meet obligations through mid-August 2026. The UN controller warned of "imminent financial collapse" (Guterres letter, January 2026).
- **Peacekeeping reductions**: A 15% reduction in peacekeeping spending was implemented due to unpaid assessments.
- **Credit return paradox**: Under existing financial rules, the UN was required to return unspent funds as credits to members — even when underspending resulted from never having received those funds. This Kafkaesque cycle forced the UN to "give back approximately $1.3 billion in 2027 across regular budget and peacekeeping operations that we could not spend because we did not collect" (UN Controller, May 2026).

### 3.3 The July 2026 Budget Reform

On 1 July 2026, the GA approved a four-year trial of new budget rules: the UN will only return unspent assessed contributions **when those funds have been received in cash**. This change, described as "ending a 75-year-old rule," will save $1.5B over 12 months. Guterres welcomed the decision, noting the previous system had created a "Kafkaesque cycle" of refunding money that never arrived.

Despite the reform, the UN remains dependent on overdue payments from the US and China. The UN's proposed regular budget for 2026 is $3.4B, down ~7% from the previous year.

### 3.4 Implications

The financing crisis has profound implications for UN legitimacy:
- It exposes the **gap between member-state rhetoric and commitment** — states pass ambitious mandates but refuse to pay for them
- It creates **donor leverage**: the growing share of voluntary compared to assessed funding means donors (especially the US and EU) can shape UN priorities through earmarked contributions
- It undermines the UN's claim to **universal and impartial service** when major powers can withhold funding to extract political concessions

A **realist** interpretation: the financing crisis demonstrates that the UN remains dependent on great-power support; the US and other major contributors use arrears as leverage. A **liberal institutionalist** interpretation: this is a collective-action problem that requires better institutional design (the 2026 budget reform as an example of institutional learning). A **postcolonial** interpretation: weighted voting at the Bretton Woods Institutions and the US's disproportionate share of the UN budget give Western powers structural control over an organization that claims to be universal.

## 4. The UN and Global Governance Challenges

### 4.1 Global Health: COVID-19 and the WHO

The COVID-19 pandemic was the most severe test of the UN system since its founding, exposing deep structural weaknesses:

- **WHO** was at the centre of the global response but was criticized for initially deferring to China, for the slow declaration of a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC), and for political constraints on its investigative capacity.
- The **ACT-Accelerator** and **COVAX** mechanisms (coordinated by WHO, Gavi, and CEPI) aimed to ensure equitable vaccine access but failed dramatically: by mid-2021, high-income countries had vaccinated at rates 30x higher than low-income countries, despite COVAX's design.
- The pandemic prompted calls for **WHO reform**: strengthening the International Health Regulations (IHR), negotiating a **Pandemic Treaty** (WHO CA+), and enhancing WHO's independence from member-state political pressure. As of 2026, the Pandemic Treaty negotiations remained incomplete, revealing fundamental disagreements between developing countries (concerned about access to medicines and pathogen sovereignty) and developed countries (prioritizing surveillance and transparency).

### 4.2 Climate Governance

The UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) process represents the UN's most sustained attempt at multilateral environmental governance:

- **Paris Agreement (2015)**: Achieved near-universal participation through a "bottom-up" architecture of Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). However, the aggregate NDCs are insufficient to limit warming to 1.5°C — a "pledge and review" system with no enforcement mechanism.
- **Loss and Damage**: At COP28 (2023), the Loss and Damage Fund was operationalized after 30 years of developing-country demand. The fund was initially hosted by the World Bank (a concession to developed countries, criticized by the Global South), and pledges remain far below estimated needs (~$700M against projected $400B+ annually by 2030).
- **UNEP** coordinates environmental activities but lacks enforcement authority and is chronically underfunded.
- **Postcolonial analysis**: The climate regime illustrates the North-South divide in UN governance. Historical responsibility for emissions lies overwhelmingly with industrialised countries, but developing countries bear the worst impacts. The UNFCCC principle of **Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capabilities (CBDR-RC)** is the site of continuous struggle — developed countries resist quantified responsibility for historical emissions, while developing countries insist that climate justice requires reparative action.

### 4.3 Digital Governance and AI

The UN has increasingly engaged with digital governance:

- **Internet Governance Forum (IGF)**: Established 2006; a multi-stakeholder forum but with limited decision-making power.
- **Global Digital Compact** (adopted at the Summit of the Future, 2024): Commits member states to digital inclusion, data governance, and AI oversight. Includes commitments to connect the remaining 2.6 billion people offline.
- **UN Secretary-General's AI Advisory Body** (2023–2024): Proposed a **Global AI Governance Architecture** including a lightweight UN-level AI governance entity, a scientific panel, and an AI knowledge exchange.
- The main tension in digital governance is between **multi-stakeholder governance** (favoured by tech companies, Western states, and civil society) and **intergovernmental control** (favoured by China, Russia, and many developing countries concerned about digital sovereignty).

### 4.4 Counter-Terrorism

The UN's counter-terrorism architecture has expanded significantly since 9/11:
- **Counter-Terrorism Committee (CTC)**: Established by SC Res. 1373 (2001); mandated all states to criminalize terrorist financing and report on counter-terrorism measures.
- **CTED**: The CTC's Executive Directorate provides expert assessments of member-state implementation.
- **UN Office of Counter-Terrorism (UNOCT)**: Created in 2017; coordinates the UN's counter-terrorism efforts across 38 entities.
- **Critique**: Counter-terrorism has been used to justify human rights restrictions. Postcolonial scholars argue that the UN's counter-terrorism agenda disproportionately targets Muslim-majority countries and has been instrumentalized to suppress legitimate opposition and civil society (Anghie, *The TWAIL of Counter-Terrorism*).

### 4.5 Peacekeeping Reform

Peacekeeping is the UN's most visible operational activity, and it has been the subject of sustained reform efforts:

- **Action for Peacekeeping (A4P)**: Launched in 2018 by Guterres; endorsed by 156 member states. Based on a "Declaration of Shared Commitments" covering political solutions, women peace and security, protection of civilians, safety and security, performance, and accountability. The **A4P+** implementation strategy (2021–2023) focused on translating commitments into field-level change.
- **Sexual Exploitation and Abuse (SEA)**: The crisis of peacekeeper-perpetrated SEA has damaged the UN's legitimacy and spurred institutional reform: the **Circle of Leadership** (chaired by the SG), the **Victims' Rights Advocate** (established 2017), the **Trust Fund for Victims**, and strengthened vetting and reporting. A feminist analysis (Cohn 2008; True 2012) argues that the SEA crisis reflects the hyper-masculine culture of peacekeeping and insufficient female representation — which is why increasing the proportion of female peacekeepers (the Uniformed Gender Parity Strategy) is itself a reform priority.
- **Technology and peacekeeping**: UAVs, digital situational awareness, and AI-powered early warning systems are transforming peacekeeping. The **UN Peacekeeping Intelligence Policy** (2019, updated 2023) formalised the use of intelligence in missions, raising ethical and legal debates about surveillance and the UN's impartiality.
- **Peacekeeping transitions**: The drawdown of MINUSMA (Mali, 2023), the transition from MONUSCO (DRC, ongoing), and the closure of UNAMID (Darfur, 2020) have raised critical questions about how the UN exits — the risk of "peacekeeping syndrome" in which missions depart without addressing root causes.
- **Stabilisation mandates**: The shift from traditional peacekeeping (consent, impartiality, minimum use of force) to **stabilisation** (proactive force, support to host states against armed groups) has been controversial. Missions like MONUSCO's Force Intervention Brigade (FIB) and MINUSMA received robust mandates that critics argue blurred the lines between peacekeeping and counter-insurgency.

### 4.6 Migration Governance

The UN's role in migration governance has expanded significantly:

- **Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (GCM, 2018)**: The first intergovernmental agreement covering all dimensions of migration. Non-binding but establishes a comprehensive framework (23 objectives) covering data, driver addressing, legal pathways, border management, smuggling, trafficking, and return. Adopted by the GA (152-5-12), but the US, Hungary, Poland, and others opposed, reflecting the deep politicisation of migration.
- **Global Compact on Refugees (2018)**: Parallel framework for refugee protection and responsibility-sharing; complements the 1951 Refugee Convention.
- **IOM's integration into the UN system**: The International Organization for Migration became a **related organization of the UN in 2016** (following the 2016 Agreement), moving from an independent, non-treaty body to full UN system membership. This institutional reform reflected the growing centrality of migration to UN governance.
- **UNHCR's expanding mandate**: From refugee protection to climate displacement, mixed migration flows, and internal displacement. The **High-Level Panel on Internal Displacement** (2019–2021) led to the SG's **Action Agenda on Internal Displacement** (2022).
- **UN Network on Migration**: Established to coordinate GCM implementation across 40+ UN entities.

The migration governance debate reveals North-South tensions similar to those on climate and health: developing countries host the majority of the world's refugees and migrants but have the least influence on UN governance structures. The GCM's non-binding nature reflects developed-country resistance to any legal obligation on migration.

### 4.7 Disarmament and Arms Control

The UN was founded to "save succeeding generations from the scourge of war," making disarmament central to its mandate:

- **UN Office for Disarmament Affairs (UNODA)**: Supports multilateral disarmament efforts, including the NPT review process, the Conference on Disarmament, and the BWC and CWC.
- **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)**: The NPT review process is in crisis. The 2022 Review Conference ended without a consensus outcome; the 2026 Review Conference faces fundamental disagreements between nuclear-weapon states (resisting disarmament commitments) and non-nuclear states (insisting on NPT Art. VI implementation). The **Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (TPNW, 2017)** emerged from frustration with NPT paralysis but has been rejected by all nuclear-armed states.
- **Arms Trade Treaty (ATT, 2013)**: Regulates the international conventional arms trade. The US withdrew in 2019; implementation remains weak.
- **Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)**: The CCW Group of Governmental Experts on LAWS ("killer robots") has debated pre-emptive regulation since 2014 without reaching agreement on a legally binding instrument.
- **Cybersecurity**: The UN Group of Governmental Experts (GGE) and Open-Ended Working Group (OEWG) have developed norms of responsible state behaviour in cyberspace (11 norms, 2015 and 2021 reports), but implementation and enforcement remain voluntary.
- **Postcolonial analysis**: Disarmament governance reflects the hierarchy of the international system — nuclear-weapon states are exclusively P5 members (plus non-NPT states India, Israel, Pakistan, and North Korea); the TPNW emerged from a coalition of non-nuclear states precisely because the NPT system was perceived as perpetuating nuclear apartheid. The UN's failure to advance disarmament since the end of the Cold War is arguably its greatest unfulfilled promise.

## 5. The UN and Regional Arrangements (Chapter VIII)

The UN Charter (Arts. 52–54) provides for **regional arrangements** in peace and security. This relationship has evolved significantly, particularly with the African Union.

### 5.1 The Legal Framework

- **Art. 52**: Encourages regional arrangements for pacific settlement of disputes; provides that the SC "shall encourage the development of pacific settlement of local disputes through such regional arrangements."
- **Art. 53(1)**: The SC "shall, where appropriate, utilize such regional arrangements or agencies for enforcement action under its authority." Crucially, "no enforcement action shall be taken under regional arrangements or by regional agencies without the authorization of the Security Council."
- **Art. 54**: The SC "shall at all times be kept fully informed of activities undertaken or in contemplation under regional arrangements or by regional agencies for the maintenance of international peace and security."

### 5.2 The UN-AU Partnership

The UN maintains more extensive cooperation with the AU than with any other regional organization. Since 2007, the SC and the AU Peace and Security Council (PSC) hold annual joint consultative meetings.

**SC Resolution 2719 (2023)** was a landmark: it created a framework for **predictable and sustainable funding of AU-led Peace Support Operations (PSOs) from UN assessed contributions** — responding to a long-standing African demand. Key elements:
- Joint planning and authorization by the UNSC and AU PSC
- UN support provided in line with Human Rights Due Diligence Policy
- Financing through a support office model
- Burdensharing between the UN and AU

The UN-AU Joint Roadmap (endorsed October 2024) focused on four workstreams: joint planning and decision-making, mission support, financing and budgeting, and compliance/protection of civilians. As of mid-2026, the first operational application of Res. 2719 was being considered for the AU Support and Stabilization Mission in Somalia (AUSSOM).

### 5.3 Regionalisation of Peace Operations

A broader trend is the **regionalisation of peace operations**, driven by:
- Host-state resistance to UN peacekeeping (e.g., Mali, DRC)
- Great-power divisions in the SC making UN authorization difficult
- The AU's claim to "African solutions to African problems"
- The increasing role of sub-regional organisations (ECOWAS, SADC, EAC) in deploying forces

This trend is theoretically significant: it represents a **shift from centralized UN authority to networked multilateralism** (a term Guterres has used), but also risks fragmentation and inconsistent human rights standards across regional arrangements. The UN80 Initiative's Regional Integrated Platforms aim to mitigate this by concentrating UN capacities from all pillars at the regional level.

## 6. Charter Reform and the Future of the UN

### 6.1 Charter Amendment Procedures

The UN Charter is famously difficult to amend:
- **Art. 108**: Amendments require a two-thirds GA vote and ratification by **two-thirds of all member states**, including **all P5 members**.
- This gives every P5 member a de facto veto over any Charter amendment. The difficulty of amendment is why the Charter has been amended only five times since 1945 (the most significant being the expansion of the SC from 11 to 15 in 1965).
- Most UN reform therefore happens through **institutional practice and GA resolutions** rather than formal Charter amendment — the shift in the abstention custom, the Uniting for Peace resolution, the creation of the Human Rights Council (which replaced the Commission on Human Rights without amending the Charter).

### 6.2 Our Common Agenda (2021)

Guterres's **Our Common Agenda** report (September 2021) was the most ambitious forward-looking UN vision since the Millennium Declaration. Proposals included:
- **Emergency Platform**: A convening mechanism for complex global crises
- **Repurposed Trusteeship Council**: To serve as a deliberative forum for future generations
- **Futures Lab**: Strategic foresight capacity
- **Declaration on Future Generations** (adopted at the Summit of the Future, 2024)
- **UN Special Envoy for Future Generations**
- **World Social Summit** (held in 2025)
- Reforms to the **international financial architecture**
- A **New Agenda for Peace** (issued 2023) proposing reform of peace operations, strengthening prevention, and addressing the relationship between the UN and regional organizations

### 6.3 The Pact for the Future (2024)

The **Pact for the Future** (GA Res. 79/1, September 2024) was the outcome of the Summit of the Future. It commits member states to:
- A consolidated model for SC reform (Actions 39–40)
- Strengthening the Human Rights Council
- Implementing the Global Digital Compact
- A Declaration on Future Generations
- Reform of the international financial architecture
- Accelerating SDG implementation

The Pact has been criticized as too general and lacking concrete implementation timelines, but it represents the most recent political consensus on the UN's direction.

### 6.4 Is Reform Possible?

Scholars are deeply divided:
- **Reform optimists** (Weiss, Guterres) argue that incremental reform is possible and that the UN80 Initiative demonstrates the system's capacity for self-correction
- **Reform pessimists** (Glennon, Mearsheimer) argue that the UN's fundamental structure — great-power primacy in the SC, Westphalian sovereignty, the one-state-one-vote GA — cannot be reformed without changing the underlying distribution of power in world politics
- **Postcolonial scholars** (Anghie, Chimni) argue that the UN's architecture is irredeemably shaped by colonial hierarchies; meaningful reform requires not tinkering but fundamental decolonization of international institutions
- **Constructivists** (Finnemore, Ruggie) see reform as possible but slow — the UN's normative framework evolves through practice, persuasion, and learning, not grand bargains

## 7. Bangladesh and UN Reform

Bangladesh has multiple stakes in UN reform debates:

- **Peacekeeping and SC Reform**: As a consistently top-three TCC (over 7,000 peacekeepers), Bangladesh exemplifies the structural injustice of the current UN: it supplies the troops but has no permanent voice in the Council that authorises missions. Bangladesh supports the African position and has advocated for greater Asia-Pacific representation in the SC. Prominent Bangladeshi peacekeepers include **Lt. Gen. Abul Manzoor** (first Force Commander of UNEFME, 1957) — illustrating Bangladesh's long peacekeeping tradition even before independence.
- **The Rohingya Crisis**: The most consequential case study of Bangladesh's engagement with the UN system. Since August 2017, when Myanmar's military campaign forced over 740,000 Rohingya into Bangladesh (now ~1 million total), Bangladesh has worked with multiple UN organs simultaneously:
  - **UNHCR**: Manages refugee registration, shelter, and protection in Cox's Bazar camps alongside the Bangladesh government, but repatriation efforts under the 2018 Bangladesh-Myanmar-UNHCR Memorandum of Understanding have failed entirely due to Myanmar's non-cooperation.
  - **UN Fact-Finding Mission**: The Independent International Fact-Finding Mission on Myanmar (2018, mandated by the HRC) documented "genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes" — but the UN could not act because China and Russia vetoed SC resolutions on Myanmar.
  - **ICJ**: Bangladesh supported The Gambia's case under the Genocide Convention (*Gambia v. Myanmar*), a landmark use of the ICJ where the SC was paralysed.
  - **ICC**: The ICC Prosecutor opened an investigation into deportation as a crime against humanity, given Bangladesh's ICC party status (Myanmar is not a party, so jurisdiction is contested).
  - The crisis exposes the "UN paradox": the normative machinery (HRC investigation, ICJ case, ICC investigation) works, but the enforcement machinery (SC action, repatriation) fails. UNHCR cannot compel Myanmar; it can only manage the consequences. For Bangladesh, the UN is simultaneously indispensable (humanitarian relief, legal recourse) and profoundly limited (no capacity to resolve the root cause).
- **Financing Crisis**: As a net recipient of UN development programmes, Bangladesh is harmed by the financing crisis. Voluntary funding shortfalls disproportionately affect the UN's development pillar (UNDP, UNICEF, WFP). Bangladesh's forthcoming **LDC graduation** (as of 2026, Bangladesh is in the final stages of LDC graduation) will also change its relationship with UN development finance — it will lose access to certain concessional resources and must navigate a post-graduation transition strategy with UN support.
- **Climate Governance**: Bangladesh chairs the Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF) and the V20 group of climate-vulnerable finance ministers. The operationalization of the Loss and Damage Fund at COP28 was a key diplomatic victory for Bangladesh and other CVF members. Bangladesh's advocacy at UNFCCC illustrates how small states leverage UN processes to advance interests despite structural disadvantages.
- **Human Rights**: Bangladesh's UPR cycles have highlighted concerns over freedom of expression, labour rights, minority protection, and civil society space. Its HRC membership (2023–2025) was a platform for engagement. The tension between Bangladesh's peacekeeping image ("UN's leading TCC") and its domestic human rights record is a recurring theme in UN human rights debates. **Ambassador Rabab Fatima** served as Bangladesh's Permanent Representative to the UN and as UN High Representative for the Least Developed Countries, Landlocked Developing Countries, and Small Island Developing States (UN-OHRLLS) — representing the professional calibre of Bangladesh's diplomatic corps at the UN.
- **Global Health**: COVID-19 exposed Bangladesh's vulnerability in global health governance. The inequitable distribution of vaccines through COVAX — where wealthy countries secured early doses while Bangladesh waited — reinforced arguments for WHO reform and a Pandemic Treaty that ensures equitable access to medicines. The proposed **TRIPS waiver** at the WTO (supported by Bangladesh and other developing countries) became a North-South flashpoint in pandemic governance.
- **Digital Governance**: Bangladesh's "Digital Bangladesh" strategy aligns with the Global Digital Compact's commitments to digital inclusion and connectivity.

## Summary

The UN faces a paradox: it is more needed than ever (climate, pandemics, AI, conflict in a multipolar world) but less able to act decisively due to structural constraints, financing crises, and great-power divisions. The UN80 Initiative represents the most ambitious reform effort in UN history, but its success depends on member-state willingness to fund mandates, accept institutional consolidation, and — in the case of SC reform — reduce their own power. Peacekeeping reform, migration governance, and disarmament present distinct institutional challenges, each reflecting deeper North-South tensions and power asymmetries. For Bangladesh, the Rohingya crisis encapsulates the entire UN paradox — the UN's normative machinery works (HRC investigation, ICJ case, ICC investigation), but its enforcement machinery fails (SC paralysis, no repatriation). The UN remains indispensable, but its limitations are increasingly apparent.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **UNDS Repositioning** — 2018 reform mandating Cooperation Frameworks as the central planning instrument for UN country teams; reinvigorated the Resident Coordinator system
- **UN80 Initiative** — System-wide reform launched March 2025 with three workstreams (efficiency, mandate review, structural change)
- **QCPR (Quadrennial Comprehensive Policy Review)** — The GA's four-year review of UN development system operational activities
- **Resident Coordinator (RC) System** — The UN's highest-ranking representative in each country; since 2018, RCs report directly to the UN Secretary-General
- **Cooperation Framework** — The UN's strategic planning instrument for country-level development activities (replacing the UNDAF)
- **Chapter VIII** — UN Charter provisions (Arts. 52–54) governing the relationship between the UN and regional arrangements
- **SC Resolution 2719 (2023)** — Framework for UN assessed contribution funding of AU-led peace support operations
- **A4P (Action for Peacekeeping)** — 2018 UN peacekeeping reform initiative endorsed by 156 member states
- **SEA (Sexual Exploitation and Abuse)** — Peacekeeper-perpetrated abuse that has driven major UN institutional reform
- **Our Common Agenda** — 2021 Guterres report proposing 12 commitments for UN reform
- **Pact for the Future** — 2024 Summit of the Future outcome document
- **GCM (Global Compact for Migration)** — 2018 first intergovernmental agreement covering all dimensions of migration; non-binding
- **TPNW (Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons)** — 2017 treaty prohibiting nuclear weapons; rejected by all nuclear-armed states
- **Liquidity Crisis** — The UN's inability to meet financial obligations due to member-state arrears
- **Credit Return Paradox** — The rule requiring the UN to return unspent funds to members even when underspending was caused by non-payment
- **Loss and Damage Fund** — Approved at COP28 (2023) to compensate climate-vulnerable countries for irreparable harm from climate change
- **Global Digital Compact** — 2024 agreement on digital inclusion, data governance, and AI oversight
- **CBDR-RC** — Common But Differentiated Responsibilities and Respective Capabilities (UNFCCC principle)
