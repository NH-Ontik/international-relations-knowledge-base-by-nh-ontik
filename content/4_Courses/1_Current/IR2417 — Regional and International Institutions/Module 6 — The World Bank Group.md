# Module 6 — The World Bank Group

## Course Relevance

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Describe the structure and functions of the five World Bank Group institutions
2. Explain the evolution of the World Bank's mission from postwar reconstruction to poverty reduction to climate action

## 1. Origins and Evolution

### 1.1 Bretton Woods and the IBRD

The International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD) was created alongside the IMF at the **Bretton Woods Conference (1944)** . While the IMF was designed for short-term balance-of-payments support, the IBRD was created to finance long-term reconstruction and development:

- **Original purpose**: To provide capital for the reconstruction of war-devastated economies (especially Europe) and to supplement private capital for development in "poorer and backward areas."
- **Early operations**: The IBRD's first loans (1947–1950s) went to Western European reconstruction (France, Netherlands, Denmark, Luxembourg) — a function soon taken over by the Marshall Plan (1948). The Bank then shifted its focus to "development" in the Global South.
- **The "Bank" model**: Unlike the IMF (which lends from a pool of member contributions), the World Bank raises most of its funds from international capital markets by issuing bonds. Its lending rate is set just above its borrowing cost (a small spread to cover administrative costs and build reserves). This gives the Bank a distinctive financial model: it is a **development cooperative owned by governments but funded by capital markets**.

### 1.2 The Five Institutions of the World Bank Group

The World Bank Group (WBG) comprises five institutions:

| Institution | Founded | Function | FY2025 Lending |
|-------------|---------|----------|----------------|
| **IBRD** (International Bank for Reconstruction and Development) | 1944 | Loans to middle-income and creditworthy lower-income countries at near-market rates | ~$40.9 billion |
| **IDA** (International Development Association) | 1960 | Grants and zero/low-interest loans to the world's poorest countries | ~$30.8 billion |
| **IFC** (International Finance Corporation) | 1956 | Private-sector investment and advisory services | ~$8.1 billion (climate) |
| **MIGA** (Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency) | 1988 | Political risk insurance and credit guarantees for private investors | ~$3.5 billion (climate) |
| **ICSID** (International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes) | 1966 | Dispute settlement between foreign investors and states | Administrative |

**IBRD and IDA** are the core lending arms. The IBRD lends at market-related rates (currently ~4–5%) with a 3–5 year grace period and 15–20 year maturity. IDA provides grants and credits at zero or very low interest (0% interest, 10-year grace, 30–40 year maturity). IDA is replenished every three years through donor pledges — the **IDA21 replenishment** (2025–2028) raised $23.7 billion in donor commitments, leveraging to ~$100 billion in total financing through capital market issuance.

### 1.3 Mission Evolution

The World Bank's mission has expanded dramatically since 1944:

1. **Reconstruction (1940s–1950s)**: Postwar reconstruction of Europe and Japan.
2. **Development lending (1960s–1970s)**: Under Robert McNamara (President 1968–1981), the Bank shifted to poverty reduction, expanding lending for agriculture, education, health, and population control — the "basic needs" approach.
3. **Structural adjustment (1980s–1990s)**: Under the influence of the "Washington Consensus," the Bank (with the IMF) required borrowing countries to implement neoliberal reforms (privatisation, trade liberalisation, deregulation) as conditions for loans. This period generated intense controversy and the most sustained criticism of the Bank.
4. **Post-Washington Consensus (2000s)**: The Bank adopted the **Millennium Development Goals** (2000–2015), the **Poverty Reduction Strategy** approach (PRSPs, 1999), and emphasised "good governance," institutional reform, and country ownership.
5. **Sustainable development and climate (2015–2023)**: The Bank integrated the **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** and significantly expanded climate finance.
6. **Evolution Roadmap under Ajay Banga (2023–present)**: The Bank's vision was updated to "creating a world free of poverty **on a livable planet**." The evolution includes expanded climate finance, crisis preparedness, a new "One WBG" approach, and Global Challenge Programs covering eight areas: climate change, fragility and conflict, pandemics, energy access, food security, water security, digitalisation, and biodiversity. The G20 Capital Adequacy Framework review created up to $50 billion in additional lending capacity over ten years.

## 2. Governance

### 2.1 Voting Power

Like the IMF, the World Bank uses **weighted voting**. Each member has basic votes plus additional votes proportional to their shareholding (based on economic weight, contributions, and subscriptions). The IBRD voting shares (as of 2026):

| Country | Voting Share (%) |
|---------|-----------------|
| United States | 16.09 |
| Japan | 6.93 |
| China | 5.79 |
| Germany | 4.12 |
| France | 3.78 |
| United Kingdom | 3.78 |
| India | 2.97 |
| Russia | 2.76 |
| Saudi Arabia | 2.69 |
| Brazil | 1.88 |

**Key governance features**:
- The US holds a **de facto veto** over major decisions (amendments to the Articles require an 85% supermajority; the US holds 16.09%).
- Europe collectively holds ~30% of votes — significantly over-represented relative to economic weight.
- The President of the World Bank is **always an American** (by convention since 1944; President Ajay Banga, Indian-born US citizen, term 2023–2028). This US monopoly mirrors the European monopoly on the IMF MD position.
- Developing countries are systematically under-represented. China is the world's second-largest economy but holds less than 6% of votes.

### 2.2 The Board of Executive Directors

The World Bank's Board of 25 Executive Directors functions similarly to the IMF's Executive Board. Five are appointed by the largest shareholders (US, Japan, Germany, UK, France); 20 are elected by constituencies of the remaining members. The Board approves all loans, guarantees, and policies. In practice, the Board operates by consensus — formal votes are rare, and the largest shareholders exercise influence through informal channels.

### 2.3 Governance Reform Debates

Why is governance reform so difficult?
- **The US veto**: The US Congress has consistently blocked governance reforms that would reduce US voting share. Any reform requires US legislative approval (the US must appropriate funds for capital increases), giving Congress veto power over governance change.
- **European overrepresentation**: Europe resists reform because it would lose voting share. The EU collectively holds ~30% but contributes less than 15% of IBRD capital.
- **The 2010 reform**: The 2010 Voice Reform increased developing-country voting share by 3.13 percentage points — a modest adjustment that did not fundamentally change the governance structure.
- **China's challenge**: China has used its creation of the AIIB and NDB to pressure the World Bank for reform. China's argument — that the Bank's governance reflects the economic order of 1944, not 2026 — is difficult to refute.

## 3. Core Functions and Instruments

### 3.1 The Project Cycle

The World Bank's traditional instrument is **project lending** — financing specific infrastructure, agricultural, education, or health projects. The project cycle includes:
- **Identification**: The Bank identifies potential projects through Country Partnership Frameworks (CPFs).
- **Preparation**: The borrower prepares the project with Bank support (feasibility studies, environmental and social assessments).
- **Appraisal**: The Bank evaluates the project's technical, economic, financial, environmental, and social viability.
- **Negotiation and approval**: The loan agreement is negotiated and submitted to the Board.
- **Implementation and supervision**: The borrower implements; the Bank supervises through field missions and reporting.
- **Evaluation**: The Independent Evaluation Group (IEG) assesses completed projects.

**Policy-based lending** (Development Policy Operations, DPOs) provides budget support in exchange for policy reforms (analogous to IMF conditionality). **Program-for-Results (PforR)** links disbursements to achieved results.

### 3.2 IDA and the Poorest Countries

IDA is the World Bank's concessional window for the world's 74 poorest countries. Key features:
- **Replenishment cycle**: Donors pledge contributions every three years (IDA21: $23.7 billion in pledges, ~$100 billion total financing).
- **Allocation**: IDA resources are allocated based on country performance (the Country Policy and Institutional Assessment — CPIA) and need (per capita income). This creates a "performance-based allocation" system that rewards good governance.
- **Crisis instruments**: The IDA Crisis Response Window (CRW) provides rapid financing for natural disasters, health emergencies, and economic shocks.
- **Private sector window**: The IDA Private Sector Window (PSW) leverages IFC and MIGA instruments to mobilise private investment in IDA countries.

### 3.3 IFC and the Private Sector

The IFC is the world's largest development institution focused exclusively on the private sector. It:
- Provides loans, equity investments, and guarantees to private companies in developing countries.
- Offers advisory services to improve the business environment.
- Mobilises third-party capital through syndications and structured finance.
- Targets climate finance ($8.1 billion in FY2025) and gender-lens investing.

### 3.4 MIGA and Political Risk

MIGA provides political risk insurance to foreign investors against:
- Currency inconvertibility and transfer restriction
- Expropriation
- War and civil disturbance
- Breach of contract
- Non-honouring of sovereign financial obligations

MIGA's guarantees help overcome the "trust deficit" that discourages private investment in fragile and conflict-affected states.

### 3.5 The Human Capital Project (2018)

The Human Capital Project (HCP) was launched under President Jim Yong Kim to address the global human capital crisis:
- **Human Capital Index (HCI)** : Measures the amount of human capital a child born today can expect to achieve by age 18, based on health (survival, stunting) and education (years of schooling, learning outcomes). A score of 0.56 means a child born in a typical low-income country will achieve only 56% of her potential productivity.
- **Human Capital Project+ (2026)** : Expanded the HCI to track human capital from birth to age 65, including labour-market outcomes. Current deficits cost low- and middle-income countries 51% of future labour earnings.
- 97 countries participate in the Human Capital Network.
- **Critique**: The HCP has been criticised for treating human capital as a production input (instrumentalising education and health) rather than as a right, and for promoting a narrow, standardised approach to education and health policy.

## 4. The Structural Adjustment Controversy

### 4.1 The SAP Era (1980–1999)

The 1980s debt crisis transformed the World Bank from a project-lending institution into a driver of policy reform. **Structural Adjustment Loans (SALs)** and **Sectoral Adjustment Loans (SECALs)** required borrowing countries to adopt sweeping policy reforms as a condition for lending:

- **Trade liberalisation**: Removing import restrictions, reducing tariffs.
- **Privatisation**: Selling state-owned enterprises.
- **Agricultural reform**: Eliminating subsidies, liberalising commodity prices.
- **Financial liberalisation**: Deregulating interest rates, removing credit controls.
- **Public sector reform**: Reducing public employment, cutting subsidies.

**The Wapenhans Report (1992)** : A leaked internal Bank report documenting systematic project failure — 37.5% of completed projects had "unsatisfactory" outcomes. The report attributed failures to a "approval culture" in which staff were rewarded for lending volume, not development results.

### 4.2 The Social and Environmental Costs

The SAP era generated sustained criticism:

- **Social costs**: Adjustment programmes reduced public spending on health, education, and social protection — with measurable negative effects on child mortality, school enrolment, and poverty (Cornia, Jolly & Stewart 1987, *Adjustment with a Human Face*).
- **Environmental costs**: Structural adjustment promoted resource extraction and agricultural export expansion (logging, mining, cash crops) without adequate environmental safeguards, contributing to deforestation and land degradation (Shandra et al. 2011).
- **Deforestation evidence**: Research by Shandra et al. (2011) found that World Bank structural adjustment lending was statistically associated with increased deforestation in borrowing countries — as export-oriented agriculture and resource extraction expanded at the expense of forest cover.
- **Democratic erosion**: By conditioning loans on policy reforms, the Bank bypassed democratic decision-making in borrowing countries. SAPs were negotiated between Bank technocrats and finance ministry officials, with little parliamentary or civil society input.

### 4.3 The Reform Agenda (1999–2015)

The failures of structural adjustment produced reforms:

- **Poverty Reduction Strategy Papers (PRSPs)** : Replacing SAPs with country-owned poverty strategies (1999). In practice, PRSPs were still heavily influenced by Bank/IMF policy preferences.
- **Comprehensive Development Framework (CDF)** : Wolfensohn's attempt to integrate social, structural, and institutional dimensions into Bank programmes.
- **Environmental and Social Safeguards**: The Bank developed safeguard policies (environmental assessment, resettlement, indigenous peoples) to mitigate project harms. These were consolidated into the **Environmental and Social Framework (ESF)** in 2018.
- **Debt relief**: The Bank participated in the HIPC Initiative and MDRI, cancelling substantial debt for the poorest countries.

### 4.4 The Evolution Roadmap and the "Livable Planet" Shift

Under President Ajay Banga (2023–present), the World Bank has undergone its most significant strategic shift since the 1990s:

- **New vision**: "Creating a world free of poverty on a livable planet" — the addition of "livable planet" represents a fundamental expansion of the Bank's mission into climate and environmental sustainability.
- **Climate finance**: The Bank achieved 45% climate co-benefits in its lending in FY2025 — up from ~35% in 2020. Cumulative climate finance (2021–2025) reached $190.3 billion. The Bank is now the largest multilateral climate financier for developing countries.
- **Crisis preparedness**: The Bank created a Crisis Preparedness and Response Toolkit, including a Global Crisis Response Facility.
- **Capital Adequacy Framework (CAF) reforms**: Working with G20, the Bank implemented reforms to stretch its balance sheet, creating up to $50 billion in additional lending capacity over ten years without new capital injections.
- **Global Challenge Programs**: Issue-specific programs (climate, food security, pandemics, energy access) designed to mobilise concessional resources and private capital.
- **Critique of the evolution**: Critics argue that the "livable planet" expansion dilutes the Bank's core poverty mandate, subjects the Bank to mission creep, and risks subordinating development to Northern climate priorities. Developing countries worry that climate conditionality will replace structural adjustment conditionality.

## 5. The World Bank and Developing Countries

### 5.1 The China Story

China's relationship with the World Bank illustrates the Bank's evolution and its limits:

- **Major borrower (1980–2007)**: China became the World Bank's largest borrower, receiving $64.4 billion across 434 projects (1980–2020). Bank-financed projects contributed to China's infrastructure, poverty reduction, and institutional development.
- **Graduation from IDA (2000)** : China "graduated" from IDA and became an IBRD-only borrower — a milestone in China's development trajectory.
- **From borrower to donor (2007–present)**: China began contributing to IDA in 2007 and is now the fifth-largest IDA donor. China is the third-largest IBRD shareholder (5.79% of votes).
- **Phase-out**: Under the 2023 Country Partnership Framework, the Bank and China agreed to **phase out IBRD lending to China by 2031** — reflecting China's reduced need for World Bank capital and evolving identity as a net contributor.
- **The institutional competitor**: China's creation of the AIIB (2015) and NDB (2014) was partly a response to frustration with World Bank governance. The AIIB and NDB offer an alternative institutional model (lower governance standards, no environmental or social conditionality, China-determined lending priorities) that competes with the World Bank's development model.

### 5.2 Bangladesh and the World Bank

Bangladesh has one of the World Bank's largest country programmes:

- **Active portfolio**: ~$47.1 billion in total commitments across 309 projects (as of February 2026). The IBRD has $3.3 billion in 84 projects; MIGA has $1.38 billion in eight projects.
- **Major sectors**: Infrastructure (transport, energy), human development (education, health), rural development, water and sanitation, and disaster risk management.
- **IDA dependency**: Bangladesh is one of IDA's largest borrowers — with significant concessional financing for poverty reduction, infrastructure, and human capital.
- **LDC graduation implications**: Bangladesh's scheduled graduation from LDC status (2026) will affect its access to IDA resources. As an LDC graduate, Bangladesh will transition to IBRD-only lending (harder terms) — a process the Bank is managing through a "graduation dialogue."

**The Padma Bridge controversy**: The World Bank's most high-profile engagement with Bangladesh involves the cancelled Padma Bridge loan:
- In 2011, the World Bank committed **$1.2 billion** in IDA financing for the Padma Multipurpose Bridge — Bangladesh's largest infrastructure project.
- In June 2012, the Bank **cancelled the loan** citing corruption concerns, alleging that a Canadian engineering firm had conspired to bribe Bangladeshi officials. Bangladesh's then-Communications Minister (now Prime Minister) Syeda Sheikh Hasina's government rejected the allegations.
- **Bangladesh's response**: The government decided to **self-finance the bridge** entirely from domestic resources ($3.9 billion total cost). The Padma Bridge was completed in June 2022 — the longest bridge in the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta and a symbol of national infrastructural sovereignty.
- **Broader implications**: The Padma Bridge case illustrates: (1) the tension between the World Bank's anti-corruption mandate and development lending, (2) the limits of donor leverage when a country has sufficient domestic resources, and (3) the growing assertiveness of "rising" aid recipients who reject donor conditionality. It also damaged Bank-Bangladesh trust, though the relationship has since recovered.
- **Post-Padma relationship**: After the cancellation, the World Bank reallocated the Padma funds to human development projects (education, health). The broader country programme continued. Bangladesh returned to the Bank for financing other major infrastructure (the Rooppur Nuclear Power Plant is financed by Russia, not the World Bank; the metro rail in Dhaka involved the Bank and JICA alongside bilateral partners).

### 5.3 The World Bank and the Global South: Critical Assessment

A balanced assessment requires acknowledging both achievements and failures:

**Achievements**:
- The World Bank has financed infrastructure that has improved the lives of millions (roads, electricity, water supply).
- IDA has provided essential concessional resources to the world's poorest countries — resources they would not have obtained from capital markets.
- The Bank's Human Capital Project and health financing have contributed to global health improvements (the Bank is a major funder of the Global Fund, GAVI, and pandemic preparedness).
- The Bank's knowledge and data functions (the World Development Report, Doing Business indicators, statistical databases) are globally influential.

**Failures and criticisms**:
- Structural adjustment's social and environmental costs remain the deepest stain on the Bank's record.
- The Bank's governance structure (US dominance, European overrepresentation) is increasingly anachronistic.
- Project failures continue: large dams displace populations (Narmada, Sardar Sarovar in India — over 140,000 displaced), resettlement programmes often fail to restore livelihoods.
- The Bank's environmental and social safeguards, while improved under the ESF (2018), are criticised as "Northern-imposed" conditionality by rising powers.
- The Bank's knowledge functions have been criticised for promoting a single development model (the "Washington Consensus") and for being insufficiently open to alternative development approaches.
- The Doing Business indicators (discontinued in 2021 after data irregularities) raised broader questions about the Bank's influence over domestic policy and the rigour of its research.

## 6. Theoretical Perspectives on the World Bank

### 6.1 Realism

For realists, the World Bank is an instrument of US-led liberal hegemony:

- **US control**: The US Presidency convention, the weighted voting system (US veto), and the location in Washington, DC, all reflect US dominance. The Bank's policies have generally aligned with US foreign policy objectives (supporting Cold War allies, opening markets for US corporations, promoting neoliberal orthodoxy).
- **Containment function**: During the Cold War, the Bank's lending was strategically directed: allies (South Korea, Turkey, Pakistan) received large programmes; states that deviated from US preferences (Cuba, North Korea, Sandinista Nicaragua) were excluded or penalised.
- **Limitation**: Realism struggles to explain the Bank's genuine development mission and the many cases where the Bank has resisted US pressure (e.g., continuing lending to countries the US has sanctioned).

### 6.2 Liberal Institutionalism

Liberal institutionalists (Keohane 1984; also the "functionalist" tradition in development studies) emphasise the Bank's role in solving market failures and collective action problems:

- **Correcting market failures**: Private capital markets underinvest in long-term infrastructure, health, and education in poor countries because the returns are uncertain and the time horizons long. The World Bank fills this gap by providing long-term financing with concessional terms.
- **Providing global public goods**: The Bank's climate finance, pandemic preparedness, and biodiversity programmes address global externalities that individual countries have no incentive to provide.
- **Knowledge as a public good**: The Bank's research, data collection, and policy analysis reduce information asymmetries and improve development policy globally.
- **Limitation**: Liberal institutionalism assumes that Bank policies are technocratically optimal and underestimates the political interests that shape Bank behaviour.

### 6.3 Constructivism

Constructivists (Barnett & Finnemore 2004; Weaver 2008; Park & Vetterlein 2010) analyse the Bank as a **normative actor** and a **bureaucratic organisation**:

- **The Bank as a "missionary" organisation**: The Bank is driven by a strong internal culture — a belief in development, economic growth, and the Bank's own expertise. This culture shapes what the Bank does and how it defines problems (Barnett & Finnemore's "authority of expertise").
- **Norm diffusion**: The Bank diffuses norms and models of "good development" — from structural adjustment (neoliberal orthodoxy) to poverty reduction (PRSPs) to climate action (the "livable planet" vision). The Bank is a norm entrepreneur that shapes what governments consider appropriate development policy.
- **Bureaucratic pathologies**: Weaver (2008, *Hypocrisy Trap*) argues that the World Bank is caught between its stated goals (sustainable development, participation) and its bureaucratic incentives (lending volume, staff rewards for disbursement). This creates a "hypocrisy trap" in which the Bank publicly commits to ambitious goals but fails to implement them because doing so would disrupt core bureaucratic routines.
- **The "development discourse"**: Post-structuralist scholars (Escobar 1995, *Encountering Development*) argue that the World Bank is central to the "development discourse" — a system of knowledge and power that constructs the Global South as "underdeveloped" and in need of Northern intervention. The Bank's categories (GDP growth, poverty lines, Human Capital Index) define what development means and what policies are considered legitimate.

### 6.4 Critical and Postcolonial Perspectives

Critical and postcolonial scholars (Cox 1981; Pahuja 2011; Taylor 2014) analyse the World Bank as an institution of **global capitalist governance**:

- **The Bank and neoliberal hegemony**: The World Bank (alongside the IMF and WTO) constitutes the "embedded neoliberalism" of the post-Cold War global order — locking in market-oriented policies through loan conditionality and policy advice.
- **The rule of law and development**: Pahuja (2011, *Decolonising International Law*) argues that the Bank's rule-of-law promotion and good governance agenda extends Northern legal norms into postcolonial states, foreclosing alternative development paths.
- **The Bank as a site of resistance**: The Bank has been a target of sustained civil society mobilisation — from the "Fifty Years Is Enough" campaign (1994) to protests against adjustment lending, dam projects, and extractive industry financing. The adoption of the ESF, the creation of the Inspection Panel (1993), and the shift away from adjustment lending are partly responses to this pressure.
- **The "new" development banks as counter-hegemonic**: The AIIB and NDB represent an alternative development model — one that explicitly rejects the "post-Washington Consensus" norms of governance conditionality and environmental/social safeguards. Whether this is genuinely "counter-hegemonic" or a different form of capitalist development (state-led, China-centred) is debated.

### 6.5 Feminist Perspectives

Feminist scholars (Elson 1991; Bedford 2009; Griffin 2009) analyse the gendered effects of World Bank policies:

- **Gender and structural adjustment**: Bank austerity disproportionately affects women (cuts in public services, reduced health and education spending, increased unpaid care work).
- **The Bank's "gender mainstreaming"**: The Bank has adopted gender equality as a corporate priority (the Gender Strategy 2016–2023, the Gender Tag for IFC investments). Feminist critics argue that this "gender mainstreaming" is superficial — it uses gender equality as a productivity-enhancing instrument ("smart economics") rather than addressing structural gender inequality.
- **The Bank and the "household"**: Bedford (2009) argues that Bank programmes aimed at changing gender relations (e.g., conditional cash transfers that give payments to mothers) reproduce rather than challenge gender roles.

## 7. Contemporary Challenges

### 7.1 Climate Finance and the Evolution Roadmap

The Bank's most consequential contemporary shift is its expansion into climate finance:
- Climate finance reached $50.8 billion in FY2025 (45% of total lending).
- The Bank is the largest multilateral climate financier for developing countries.
- The evolution raises fundamental questions: Can the World Bank simultaneously be a poverty-reduction institution and a climate-finance institution? Will climate conditionality replicate the problems of structural adjustment (Northern policy preferences imposed on Southern borrowers)? Is the Bank adequately staffed and structured for climate work?

### 7.2 The Rise of New Development Banks

The World Bank faces institutional competition for the first time since 1944:

- **AIIB (Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, 2016)** : 109 members, $100 billion capital base. Focused on infrastructure in Asia. China is the largest shareholder (26.6%). The AIIB has been lending $3–5 billion annually. It operates with a leaner bureaucracy and weaker environmental and social standards than the World Bank.
- **NDB (New Development Bank, BRICS, 2014)** : Founders: Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa. Expanded membership includes Bangladesh, Egypt, UAE. Lending ~$3 billion annually. Focused on infrastructure and sustainable development.
- **Is competition real?** The AIIB and NDB are still much smaller than the World Bank (combined annual lending ~$8 billion vs the WBG's ~$80+ billion). They co-finance frequently with the Bank. But their existence has created "competitive pressure" for governance reform — the World Bank's evolution roadmap is partly a response to the perception that it must modernise to stay relevant.

### 7.3 The Inspection Panel and Accountability

The **World Bank Inspection Panel** (created 1993) was a landmark innovation in IO accountability:
- It allows affected people to file complaints alleging that Bank-financed projects have violated the Bank's own policies (environmental, social, resettlement).
- The Panel investigates and reports to the Board.
- It has been called the "first independent accountability mechanism" at any international institution.
- **Limitations**: The Panel cannot suspend or cancel projects; it can only recommend corrective action. Borrowing governments often resist Panel findings. The Panel's effectiveness depends on Board willingness to follow through on its recommendations.

## Summary

The World Bank Group is the world's largest development institution — a complex of five institutions providing loans, grants, guarantees, technical assistance, and knowledge services to developing countries. Created at Bretton Woods for postwar reconstruction, its mission has expanded through six eras: reconstruction (1940s–1950s), development lending (1960s–1970s), structural adjustment (1980s–1990s), post-Washington Consensus (2000s), sustainable development (2015–2023), and the current "Evolution Roadmap" under Ajay Banga, which expands the Bank's mission to include climate action, crisis preparedness, and global public goods. The Bank's weighted voting system (US veto, European overrepresentation) is increasingly contested by emerging economies, who have created alternative institutions (AIIB, NDB) as hedges against World Bank dominance. For Bangladesh, the World Bank remains the largest development partner despite the Padma Bridge cancellation — illustrating both the Bank's enduring relevance and the growing assertiveness of borrower countries. The Bank's future will be shaped by: the climate-development trade-off, competition from new development banks, governance reform pressure, and the challenge of maintaining relevance in a multipolar development finance landscape.

### Quick Revision Checklist
**Institutional Structure**
- [ ] Five WBG institutions: IBRD, IDA, IFC, MIGA, ICSID
- [ ] IBRD: market-based lending; IDA: concessional, 3-year replenishment cycle
- [ ] IFC: private sector; MIGA: political risk insurance; ICSID: dispute resolution
- [ ] Governance: weighted voting, US veto (16.09%), President always American
- [ ] US dominance, European overrepresentation, developing-country underrepresentation

**Mission Evolution**
- [ ] Six eras: reconstruction → development lending → SAP → post-WC → SDGs → Evolution Roadmap
- [ ] McNamara: poverty reduction (1968–1981)
- [ ] Structural Adjustment (1980s–1990s): Washington Consensus, social/environmental costs
- [ ] Post-WC reforms: PRSPs (1999), ESF (2018), Inspection Panel (1993)
- [ ] Evolution Roadmap (2023): "livable planet", climate finance, Global Challenge Programs

**Structural Adjustment Debate**
- [ ] Social costs: Cornia, Jolly & Stewart (1987)
- [ ] Environmental costs: deforestation (Shandra 2011)
- [ ] Wapenhans Report (1992): 37.5% project failure rate, "approval culture"
- [ ] PRSPs, CDF, streamlined conditionality as response

**Bangladesh Case**
- [ ] Largest IDA borrower; $47.1B portfolio
- [ ] Padma Bridge (2012): $1.2B cancelled over corruption; Bangladesh self-financed
- [ ] LDC graduation: transition from IDA to IBRD
- [ ] Post-Padma recovery of Bank-Bangladesh relationship

**Theories**
- [ ] Realism: instrument of US hegemony, Cold War lending patterns
- [ ] Liberal institutionalism: correcting market failures, global public goods
- [ ] Constructivism: normative actor, bureaucratic culture, "hypocrisy trap" (Weaver)
- [ ] Critical/postcolonial: global capitalist governance, development discourse (Escobar)
- [ ] Feminist: gender-blind adjustment, "smart economics" critique

**Contemporary Challenges**
- [ ] Climate finance: $50.8B (FY2025), 45% of lending; climate-development tension
- [ ] AIIB and NDB: institutional competition, governance reform pressure
- [ ] Inspection Panel: accountability innovation but limited enforcement
- [ ] Governance reform: US veto, Europe's share, China's demand
- [ ] China phase-out by 2031; China as donor and competitor
