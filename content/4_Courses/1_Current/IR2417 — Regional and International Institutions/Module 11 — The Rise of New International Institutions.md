# Module 11 — The Rise of New International Institutions

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2417 — Regional and International Institutions
- **Module:** 11 — The Rise of New International Institutions: BRICS, AIIB, G20, Minilateralism
- **Prerequisites:** All previous modules — this is a synthesis module

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Explain the rise of new institutions as a challenge to the post-1945 institutional order
2. Analyse the BRICS+ expansion and its significance for global governance
3. Compare the AIIB and NDB as institutional alternatives to the Bretton Woods system
4. Evaluate the G20's role in global economic governance
5. Assess the phenomenon of minilateralism (CVF, Quad, AUKUS)
6. Apply IR theories to the fragmentation of the global institutional architecture
7. Analyse Bangladesh's engagement with new institutions

## 1. The Challenge to the Post-1945 Order

The global institutional architecture created after 1945 (UN, Bretton Woods) is facing the most serious challenge to its legitimacy since the founding of the UN. The sources of challenge:

- **Power shift**: The institutions reflect the power distribution of 1945 (US, Western Europe), not 2026 (China's rise, emerging economies, Africa's growing demographic weight).
- **Interest divergence**: The Western-led liberal international order (embedded liberalism, democracy promotion, human rights conditionality) does not reflect the interests of non-Western powers, who prefer sovereignty-centred, non-interventionist, development-focused institutions.
- **Institutional proliferation**: Rather than reforming existing institutions, rising powers have created **new institutions** that compete with or parallel the existing order. This "institutional proliferation" (Morse & Keohane 2014) or "institutional layering" is a defining feature of contemporary global governance.

**Theoretical framing**: The fragmentation of the institutional order challenges liberal institutionalist expectations (that institutions are efficient solutions to cooperation problems). Realists see it as the inevitable consequence of power transition. Constructivists analyse how institutional choices reflect different normative orders (Western liberal vs sovereignty-centred vs China-led). Each theory generates distinct predictions:
- **Liberal institutionalism**: Predicts institutional complementarity — new institutions will converge toward the most efficient, legitimate designs. Competition will produce a "race to the top" (Kahler 2016). The persistence of the old institutions despite new entry confirms this: the WBG and IMF still dominate.
- **Realism**: Predicts geopolitical competition — new institutions are vehicles for rising powers to challenge the existing order. The AIIB and BRICS+ are Chinese instruments for increasing influence. The existing order will resist or adapt (Mearsheimer).
- **Constructivism**: Predicts normative contestation — new institutions reflect different underlying norms (sovereignty-centric vs rules-based vs developmental). Institutional design choices (AIIB's non-conditionality, NDB's equal capital structure) are not just functional but express competing worldviews.
- **Critical theory/neo-Gramscian**: Predicts that new institutions may reproduce the same capitalist logic — BRICS elites are integrated into global capitalism, and their institutions serve transnational capitalist interests rather than representing an alternative to them (Bond & Garcia 2015; Roberts 2016).

## 2. BRICS and BRICS+

### 2.1 Origins and Evolution

BRICS originated as an investment bank acronym (Goldman Sachs' "BRIC," 2001) and became a political grouping:

- **First BRIC summit** (Yekaterinburg, 2009): Russia, China, India, Brazil. Focused on reforming global financial institutions, criticising US monetary policy.
- **South Africa joins** (2010): BRICS became BRICS.
- **The New Development Bank (NDB)** (2014): $100 billion capital base. Authorised capital: $50 billion initially, with equal shares among the five founders. First president: K. V. Kamath (India); current president: Dilma Rousseff (Brazil). Focused on infrastructure and sustainable development.
- **Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA)** (2015): $100 billion pool of reserves for balance-of-payments support — a BRICS alternative to the IMF.

### 2.2 BRICS Expansion (2024)

At the 2023 Johannesburg Summit, BRICS agreed to invite six new members (effective January 2024): **Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Argentina** (Argentina's President Milei declined the invitation in December 2023, but new members joined). BRICS+ now represents:
- ~45% of the world's population
- ~36% of global GDP (PPP)
- Significant energy suppliers (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran, Russia)
- China and India as the world's two most populous countries

**Significance**: The expansion deepens BRICS's claim to represent the "Global South." But it also complicates decision-making: BRICS+ includes countries with conflicting interests (Saudi Arabia/Iran; India/China) and reduces the cohesion that made the original BRICS effective as a negotiating bloc.

**The "Global South" as contested category**: BRICS+ claims to represent the Global South, but the category itself is contested:
- **India-China rivalry**: India and China are locked in a border dispute (ongoing since 2020 Galwan clashes), compete for leadership of the developing world, and have opposed each other's initiatives within BRICS (India blocked China's proposals for expanding the NDB).
- **Saudi-Iran rivalry**: The 2023 China-brokered Saudi-Iran rapprochement reduced but did not eliminate their regional competition (Yemen, Syria, Iraq).
- **Middle-power anxieties**: Brazil, South Africa, and Egypt face pressure from greater powers without gaining proportional influence.
- **Different models of development**: China's state-led capitalism, India's democratic capitalism, Brazil's social-developmentalism, South Africa's redistributive model — these are not converging.
This means BRICS+ is not a natural community of interests but a coalition of convenience. Its members share grievances against the Western-led order but disagree on what to build in its place.

### 2.3 Is BRICS a Counter-Hegemonic Project?

Scholars debate whether BRICS is: (1) a genuine alternative to Western-dominated institutions, (2) a symbolic challenge without substantive institutional capacity, or (3) a Chinese-led vehicle for expanding Beijing's influence:

- **BRICS as alternative**: The NDB and CRA provide alternative financial instruments. The expansion diversifies membership. BRICS advocates for reforming the UN, IMF, and World Bank.
- **BRICS as symbolic**: The NDB's lending (~$3 billion/year) is trivial compared to the World Bank (~$80 billion/year). The CRA has never been activated. BRICS has no secretariat, no binding agreements, and no enforcement mechanism.
- **BRICS as Chinese instrument**: China is the dominant economy in BRICS (China's GDP is larger than all other BRICS combined). China uses BRICS to advance its agenda (de-dollarisation, alternative payment systems) without making concessions on the issues that matter to other members (India's UNSC seat, Brazil's WTO concerns, South Africa's African agenda).

## 3. The AIIB and NDB: Competition or Complementarity?

### 3.1 The Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB)

- Founded 2016 on China's initiative. 109 members as of 2026. $100 billion capital base (China 26.6%, India 7.6%, Russia 6.0%).
- **Governance**: China has a blocking minority (30% threshold for major decisions; China's 26.6% is less than a veto — but China's influence as the host, largest shareholder, and source of the bank's AAA rating is dominant).
- **Lending**: $3–5 billion annually (small relative to the ADB or World Bank). Focused on infrastructure in Asia. AIIB co-finances with the World Bank and ADB in many projects.
- **Standards**: The AIIB's Environmental and Social Framework (ESF) is weaker than the World Bank's — fewer requirements, less consultation, no independent accountability mechanism comparable to the World Bank Inspection Panel.

### 3.2 Competition or Complementarity?

| Argument | Competition | Complementarity |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| Governance | AIIB/NDB challenge Western dominance | AIIB/NDB fill gaps the WB cannot address (infrastructure gap) |
| Standards | Weaker standards = race to the bottom | AIIB/NDB co-finance with WB — no race to the bottom |
| Lending volume | Combined lending <15% of WB | Additional resources = more development finance |
| Motivation | Created out of frustration with WB reform | "Plan B" for countries that need more infrastructure finance |

**Assessment**: The most accurate characterisation is **"competitive complementarity"** — the new banks compete with the Bretton Woods institutions (on governance norms, lending terms, and standards) but also complement them (co-financing, filling gaps, putting pressure on the WBG to reform). The WBG's Evolution Roadmap (Banga reforms) is partly a response to competitive pressure.

### 3.3 China's Broader Institutional Statecraft

The AIIB is only one element of China's emerging institutional ecosystem. China is building a parallel system of institutions that provides alternatives across governance domains:

- **Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)** — The largest infrastructure investment programme in history (over $1 trillion committed across 150+ countries). Not an IO in the formal sense, but a Chinese-led governance framework with its own rules, financing mechanisms, and dispute resolution.
- **Silk Road Fund** — $40 billion equity fund for BRI projects.
- **Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB)** — $100 billion multilateral development bank.
- **New Development Bank (NDB)** — BRICS development bank.
- **Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA)** — BRICS alternative to the IMF.
- **Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO)** — Security institution (Module 8).
- **Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP)** — World's largest FTA by GDP (Module 4).
- **FOCAC (Forum on China-Africa Cooperation)** — Institutionalised Africa engagement.
- **China-CELAC Forum** — Latin America engagement.
- **China-Arab States Cooperation Forum** — Middle East engagement.
- **Asian Dialogue** — Broader Asian cooperation forum.
- **Proposed expansion at specialised agencies**: China has contested leadership of the WHO (re-election of Dr Tedros), UNESCO (Chinese nationals in senior positions), FAO (China's Qu Dongyu as Director-General, 2019–), ITU, and WIPO. This represents a strategy of influence across both new and existing institutions.

**The question of parallelism**: Is China building a genuinely separate institutional order, or is it working within the existing order while creating complementary alternatives? The current evidence suggests a **dual-track strategy**: China works within the existing order (increasing its vote share at the IMF, holding leadership positions at UN specialised agencies) while also building alternative institutions (AIIB, NDB, BRI) that reduce dependence on the Western-led system. This is not a binary choice (reform vs replace) but a layered strategy.

### 3.4 AIIB Governance: Formal Rules and Informal Power

The module's description of AIIB governance as "no veto" (China 26.6% < 30% threshold) captures the formal rules but understates Chinese influence:
- **Presidency**: The AIIB president has always been Chinese (Jin Liqun 2016–2026, senior Chinese official). The president controls the agenda, staffing, and project pipeline.
- **Headquarters**: Located in Beijing, giving China host-country advantages (visas, logistics, intelligence, cultural influence on staff).
- **Staff composition**: A disproportionate share of senior management is Chinese nationals.
- **AAA rating**: China's sovereign guarantee supports the AIIB's AAA credit rating — a structural dependence that limits other members' willingness to challenge China.
- **Voting groups**: China's 26.6% is a blocking minority in practice because no coalition of other members can realistically counter China plus the developing-country majority.

**Broader lesson**: Institutional power is not only about formal voting rules. Informal power — agenda control, staff composition, host-country advantages, funding dependence — can be equally important.

## 4. The G20 and Global Economic Governance

### 4.1 The Rise of the G20

The G20 emerged from the 2008 global financial crisis. The G20 leaders' summits (replacing the G7/G8 as the primary forum for economic coordination) were a significant shift in global economic governance: the G20 includes the G7 + major emerging economies (China, India, Brazil, Russia, South Africa, Indonesia, Mexico, Turkey, etc.).

- **2008–2009**: The G20's "golden age" — coordinated stimulus (the 2009 London Summit produced a $1.1 trillion package), financial regulation reform (Basel III), and IMF reform.
- **2010–2020**: The G20's effectiveness declined as US-China tensions rose and the consensus on economic policy fragmented.
- **2020–present**: The G20 played a role in coordinating the COVID-19 response (debt moratorium, Common Framework) and the Russian invasion of Ukraine (2022 Bali Summit declaration — an achievement to maintain consensus despite the Ukraine divide). India hosted the G20 in 2023 (the New Delhi Declaration); Brazil in 2024; South Africa in 2025.

### 4.2 The G7's Revival as a Competing Club

While the G20 was designed to replace the G7/G8 as the premier forum for international economic cooperation, the G7 has been revived — especially after Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine:

- **G7 as "the West":** The G7 now functions as the coordinating forum for Western economic strategy: sanctions against Russia, technology controls on China, climate club, infrastructure partnerships (the G7's Partnership for Global Infrastructure and Investment — PGII — is a direct competitor to China's BRI).
- **G7 formalisation:** Under Germany's 2022 presidency and Japan's 2023 presidency, the G7 formalised new commitments (climate club, supply chain resilience, critical minerals partnership) that go beyond the G20 framework.
- **Dual-track governance:** The coexistence of the G7 and G20 creates a two-track system. For Western powers, the G7 is for substantive coordination; the G20 is for dialogue with emerging powers without conceding Western control. For rising powers, this dual track confirms that the Western-led order has not genuinely opened to new voices.

**Theoretical implication**: The persistence of the G7 alongside the G20 contradicts liberal institutionalist expectations (that broader membership increases legitimacy and effectiveness). It confirms realist predictions: powerful states will not cede control of governance forums unless forced to, and they maintain exclusive clubs for substantive coordination while using inclusive forums for legitimation.

### 4.3 The G20's Legitimacy Problems

- **Unequal representation**: The G20 is a self-selected group of "systemically important" economies. It excludes 170+ countries that are not members. African representation is minimal (South Africa is the only African G20 member; the African Union became a permanent member in 2024).
- **No decision-making authority**: The G20 is an informal forum (Sherpas, ministers, leaders). It has no secretariat, no treaty, no binding decisions. Its effectiveness depends on the political will of member states — which is declining.
- **The G7's persistence**: Despite the G20's rise, the G7 continues to meet and coordinate. The 2022 G7 (Germany) formalised new commitments (infrastructure partnership, sanctions coordination, climate club) outside the G20 framework. This dual-track system (G7 for Western coordination, G20 for broader dialogue) reflects the tension between inclusive and exclusive global governance.

## 5. Minilateralism

Minilateralism — small-group cooperation among the states most relevant to a specific problem — is an increasingly prominent governance form (Naim 2009; Keohane & Nye 2016; Bremberg 2020):

**Examples**:
- **QUAD** (Quadrilateral Security Dialogue): US, Japan, Australia, India. Security coordination in the Indo-Pacific. Loosely institutionalised (no secretariat, no treaty). Focused on maritime security, supply chain resilience, technology.
- **AUKUS** (US, UK, Australia, 2021): Nuclear-powered submarine technology sharing. A minilateral pact that generated a major alliance crisis (the French recall of ambassadors after the cancellation of the submarine contract with Australia).
- **CVF/V20** (Climate Vulnerable Forum, Vulnerable 20): 68 climate-vulnerable countries. Bangladesh has chaired the CVF and V20.
- **Alliance of Sahel States (AES)** : Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger (2023). Breakaway minilateral from ECOWAS.
- **IPEF** (Indo-Pacific Economic Framework): US-led economic cooperation in the Indo-Pacific (no tariff reductions, focus on supply chains, digital trade, climate).

**Theoretical assessment of minilateralism**:
- **Pragmatic case**: Minilateralism is more efficient than multilateralism — you can reach agreement among the few states that matter for a given problem (Naim 2009).
- **Critical case**: Minilateralism fragments the institutional order, privileges powerful states, and undermines the multilateral principle of equal participation. It can be captured by great powers (QUAD/AUKUS as US-led containment of China; the G7 as an exclusionary club for the West). It excludes most states from decision-making while decisions affect everyone. AES as a minilateral brekaway illustrates how minilateralism can undermine regional integration.
- **Constituent case**: Minilateralism can be a "building block" for multilateralism — testing new norms that can later be generalised (the CVF/V20's advocacy for loss and damage paved the way for the 2023 Loss and Damage Fund; the G20's 2009 financial regulation coordination built toward Basel III).

## 6. Bangladesh and New Institutions

- **AIIB membership**: Bangladesh is a founding member of the AIIB and has received financing for energy and infrastructure projects.
- **NDB membership**: Bangladesh joined the NDB in 2021.
- **G20 engagement**: Bangladesh is not a G20 member but has been invited to G20 summits as a "guest" (Indonesia 2022, India 2023, Brazil 2024).
- **CVF/V20 leadership**: Bangladesh chairs the CVF and V20 — its most influential minilateral platform.
- **BCIM**: The stalled Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar corridor illustrates the limits of minilateralism in South Asia.
- **Bangladesh's strategic diversification**: Across all dimensions (bilateral, regional, multilateral, minilateral), Bangladesh pursues a strategy of institutional diversification — hedging bets, maintaining flexibility, and avoiding dependence on any single institution or partner.

## 7. Africa and Institutional Proliferation

Africa is both a participant in and a site of institutional proliferation. The continent's institutional landscape illustrates the opportunities and risks of the fragmented order:

- **Overlapping memberships**: African countries hold membership in multiple, often competing institutional frameworks: the AU (continental), the AfCFTA (trade), RECs (ECOWAS, SADC, EAC, IGAD), BRICS+ (South Africa, Ethiopia, Egypt), AIIB, the Commonwealth, Francophonie. This creates institutional congestion — "spaghetti bowl" regionalism.
- **AU G20 membership**: The African Union became a permanent G20 member in 2024 — a significant gain in global governance representation.
- **AIIB membership**: 23 African countries are AIIB members — the bank's expansion into Africa and its energy/infrastructure lending.
- **China-Africa finance**: China has lent over $170 billion to African countries through bilateral and institutional channels (AIIB, NDB, BRI). This has been criticised as "debt-trap diplomacy" — though recent research shows most debt distress is driven by commercial creditors and COVID-19, not Chinese lending (Brautigam 2023).
- **The Africa capacity dilemma**: African states face a structural challenge — they lack the diplomatic capacity to effectively participate in all institutional forums. This creates a "participation deficit" even where formal membership is open.

## 8. Post-COVID Debt and the Institutional Response

The sovereign debt crisis facing many developing countries is a critical test of both old and new institutions:

- **The problem**: By 2024, 60% of low-income countries were in or at high risk of debt distress (IMF). The pandemic, global inflation, and rising interest rates created a debt crisis concentrated in Africa and South Asia.
- **Existing mechanisms**: The Paris Club (creditor coordination), the IMF's Debt Sustainability Framework, the World Bank's IDA. These have proven inadequate — slow, fragmented, and unable to address the new challenge of China as a major bilateral creditor.
- **G20 Common Framework**: Launched in 2020, intended to provide a multilateral debt restructuring mechanism. Results have been poor: only Chad, Ethiopia, and Zambia applied (2021); Chad completed its restructuring in 2023, Zambia in 2024, but Ethiopia stalled. The Common Framework has been criticised as too slow, too demanding (requiring IMF programme), and unable to compel China's participation.
- **BRICS CRA**: The Contingent Reserve Arrangement has never been activated — a significant gap in the BRICS institutional offering.
- **New institutions' response**: The NDB has not addressed sovereign debt restructuring. The AIIB has no crisis lending facility. The institutional response to the debt crisis has come overwhelmingly from the IMF (emergency lending) and the World Bank (IDA20 replenishment).
- **Bangladesh**: Bangladesh's debt is sustainable (public debt ~38% of GDP as of 2025), but the global debt crisis affects its export markets and creates systemic risk through the international financial system.

**Implication**: The post-COVID debt crisis reveals that new institutions (BRICS, AIIB, NDB) have not yet developed the capacity to address systemic financial crises. The existing order (IMF, World Bank) still dominates crisis response — for all its governance problems.

## Summary

The global institutional architecture is fragmenting. New institutions (BRICS+, AIIB, NDB) challenge the post-1945 order without replacing it. The G20 — the most promising reform of global economic governance — has declined in effectiveness. Minilateralism proliferates as an alternative to slow-moving multilateralism. For Bangladesh, new institutions offer additional financing sources (AIIB, NDB) and diplomatic platforms (CVF). The overall picture is one of "institutional layering" — new institutions added to old ones without the old ones being reformed — producing a more complex but not necessarily more effective governance system.
