## 1. Global Climate Governance

### 1.1 The Institutional Architecture

Climate governance is a **regime complex** (Keohane & Victor 2011) — a fragmented set of overlapping institutions rather than a single integrated regime:

- **UNFCCC** (1992): The framework convention. Universal membership (198 parties). Annual Conference of the Parties (COP). The UNFCCC's core principle: **common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities (CBDR-RC)** — developed countries bear primary responsibility for historical emissions.
- **Kyoto Protocol** (1997, in force 2005): Legally binding emission reduction targets for developed countries (Annex I). Exempted developing countries (including China, India, Brazil). The US never ratified. Canada withdrew. Kyoto's first commitment period (2008–2012) had limited impact.
- **Paris Agreement** (2015, in force 2016): A fundamental shift in architecture. **Bottom-up** nationally determined contributions (NDCs) rather than top-down targets. **"Holding the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the increase to 1.5°C."** Ratcheting mechanism: NDCs must be updated every five years with increasing ambition. Transparency framework (biennial reporting, review). The Paris Agreement succeeded where Kyoto failed by accommodating all major emitters (US, China, India, EU all participate). The US withdrew under Trump (2017–2021) and rejoined under Biden; future US participation remains vulnerable.
- **COP outcomes**: The **Glasgow Climate Pact** (COP26, 2021) "phasedown" of coal power (weakened from "phase-out" by India/China). The **Loss and Damage Fund** (COP27, 2022) — established to compensate vulnerable countries for climate impacts — operationalised at COP28 (2023). The **Global Stocktake** (COP28, 2023) — the first assessment of collective progress — found the world "not on track."

### 1.2 The Enforcement Problem

Climate governance is characterised by a massive **enforcement gap**. The Paris Agreement has no binding enforcement mechanism — it relies on transparency, peer pressure, and the "ratchet mechanism." Countries are not legally required to meet their NDCs. As of 2026, global emissions continue to rise; the 1.5°C target is almost certainly out of reach; current policies are on track for ~2.7°C of warming by 2100.

### 1.3 Bangladesh: Climate Vulnerability and Diplomacy

- Bangladesh is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries (sea-level rise, cyclones, floods, salinity intrusion) despite contributing <0.5% of global emissions.
- Bangladesh leads the **Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF)** and **V20** (Vulnerable 20 Group of Finance Ministers) — minilateral platforms that amplify the voice of climate-vulnerable countries.
- Bangladesh has used the **Loss and Damage Fund** negotiations to advocate for predictable, grant-based finance. Bangladesh's **Mujib Climate Prosperity Plan** (2021) reframes climate from "threat to opportunity" — shifting from vulnerability to resilience and green growth.
- Domestic climate governance: the Bangladesh Climate Change Strategy and Action Plan (BCCSAP, 2009), the Bangladesh Delta Plan 2100, and the National Adaptation Plan (NAP).

## 2. Global Health Governance

### 2.1 The World Health Organization (WHO)

The WHO (1948) is the UN specialised agency for health. Governance: World Health Assembly (all 194 members, one vote per state); Executive Board (34 technically qualified members); Director-General (Dr Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, Ethiopia, term 2017–2027).

**Core functions**: Setting international health standards (International Health Regulations — IHR, 2005), coordinating pandemic response, providing technical assistance, managing the Global Polio Eradication Initiative.

### 2.2 The COVID-19 Pandemic and Institutional Failure

COVID-19 exposed catastrophic weaknesses in global health governance:

- **The IHR failure**: The 2005 IHR require states to develop core capacities for disease surveillance and response, and to report public health emergencies. Most states were non-compliant before COVID-19. The IHR have no enforcement mechanism. China's delay in reporting the outbreak and the failure of the IHR's "early warning" system demonstrate the limits of a state-centric reporting system.
- **The WHO's limitations**: The WHO was criticised for (1) deferring to China too long before declaring a PHEIC (January 30, 2020), (2) lacking the authority to enforce compliance, and (3) being underfunded (the WHO's budget is ~$6 billion/year — less than a medium-sized hospital system — and heavily dependent on voluntary contributions from wealthy states and foundations).
- **COVAX failure**: COVAX (the global vaccine equity mechanism) aimed to deliver 2 billion vaccine doses to developing countries by the end of 2021. It delivered ~300 million. The failure reflected: (1) vaccine hoarding by wealthy countries, (2) export controls, (3) insufficient financing, and (4) the absence of a mechanism to compel technology transfer or waive patents.
- **The WHO's future**: Reform proposals include (1) a **Pandemic Treaty** (a legally binding convention on pandemic preparedness and response — negotiations ongoing 2021–2026, delayed by political disagreements); (2) increased assessed contributions to reduce donor dependency; (3) strengthened IHR compliance mechanisms; and (4) an independent pandemic surveillance and alert system.

### 2.3 Bangladesh and Global Health

- Bangladesh has one of the strongest community-health-worker systems (over 50,000 community health workers), a legacy of BRAC's health programmes and government investment.
- COVID-19 response: Bangladesh managed the pandemic without a complete health system collapse but faced severe economic impacts (RMG sector disruption, remittance decline).
- Bangladesh chairs the **Global Alliance for Vaccines and Immunization (GAVI)** Board (2023–2025) and has been a major advocate for vaccine equity.
- Bangladesh's pharmaceutical sector (the world's third-largest generic medicine producer, after India and China) gives it a stake in the TRIPS waiver debate and local vaccine manufacturing capacity.

## 3. Digital Governance

### 3.1 The Institutional Gap

Digital governance lacks the institutional architecture of climate or health governance. There is no "World Digital Organization." Instead, governance is fragmented across:

- **The Internet Governance Forum (IGF)** : Multi-stakeholder dialogue (governments, civil society, private sector, technical community). No binding authority.
- **UN processes**: The UN Secretary-General's **Digital Cooperation** roadmap, the **Global Digital Compact** (adopted 2024 as part of the Pact for the Future), and the UN's **AI Advisory Body**.
- **The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN)** : Technical coordination of domain names and IP addresses. Privatised (not intergovernmental) — a unique governance model that Western countries defend and Russia/China criticise.
- **The EU's regulatory influence**: Through the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2018) and the AI Act (2024), the EU exercises "the Brussels Effect" — setting global standards for digital governance without an international treaty.

### 3.2 The AI Governance Challenge

The rapid development of generative AI (2022–present) has outpaced governance frameworks:
- No binding international treaty on AI safety, transparency, or accountability.
- The **Bletchley Declaration** (UK AI Safety Summit, November 2023): 28 countries agreed on a shared understanding of AI risks — but no binding commitments.
- The EU AI Act (2024): Risk-based regulation (prohibited, high-risk, limited-risk, minimal-risk). The first comprehensive AI legislation in the world.
- The **Seoul Summit** (May 2024) and **Paris Summit** (February 2025): Continued the governance conversation but produced no institutional architecture.
- Debate between: (1) "AI safety" framing (existential risk, alignment — Western/Global North), (2) "AI development" framing (closing the digital divide, capacity building — Global South), and (3) "AI sovereignty" framing (ensuring national control over AI — China, Russia, India).

### 3.3 Bangladesh and Digital Governance

- Bangladesh's **Digital Bangladesh** vision (2009–present) has expanded internet access and digitised government services (a2i programme). The country has a growing IT/ITeS sector.
- Bangladesh participates in the Global Digital Compact and the UN's AI discussions. Its priorities: closing the digital divide, ensuring affordable internet access, and protecting domestic digital economies.

## Summary

Global governance across climate, health, and digital issue-areas is characterised by institutional fragmentation, enforcement gaps, and North-South tensions. Climate governance has the strongest institutional framework (UNFCCC, Paris Agreement) but the weakest enforcement. Health governance was catastrophically tested by COVID-19 and found wanting — the IHR, the WHO, and COVAX all failed. Digital governance has no institutional centre — it is a patchwork of multi-stakeholder forums, EU regulatory power, and ad hoc initiatives. For Bangladesh, each issue-area presents both vulnerabilities (climate, health) and opportunities (digital).

### Key Definitions
- **UNFCCC** — Framework convention on climate change (1992)
- **Paris Agreement** — 2015 bottom-up climate architecture (NDCs, ratchet mechanism, transparency)
- **CBDR-RC** — Common but differentiated responsibilities (foundational climate justice principle)
- **Loss and Damage Fund** — COP27 mechanism for climate impact compensation
- **NDC** — Nationally Determined Contribution (Paris Agreement commitments)
- **WHO** — World Health Organization (1948)
- **IHR (2005)** — International Health Regulations
- **COVAX** — Global vaccine equity mechanism (largely failed)
- **Pandemic Treaty** — Proposed legally binding convention on pandemic preparedness
- **Global Digital Compact** — 2024 UN framework for digital cooperation
- **EU AI Act** — First comprehensive AI regulation (2024)
- **Bletchley Declaration** — 2023 statement of AI risks (non-binding)
