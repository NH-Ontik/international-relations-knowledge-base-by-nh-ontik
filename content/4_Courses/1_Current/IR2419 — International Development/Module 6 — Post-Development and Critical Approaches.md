# Module 6 — Post-Development and Critical Approaches

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2419 — International Development
- **Module:** 6 — Post-Development and Critical Approaches

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:

4. Critically evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of post-development
5. Apply post-development thinking to Bangladesh

## 1. The Post-Development Critique

### 1.1 What is Post-Development?

Post-development is a radical critique that emerged in the 1990s, arguing that **"development" is not a solution but a problem**:

| Central Claim | Explanation |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Development is a discourse** | Not a neutral concept — it is a way of thinking that divides the world into "developed" and "underdeveloped" |
| **Development is a Western invention** | The idea was created by the West to assert dominance over the rest |
| **Development has failed** | After 50+ years, the "developing world" is still "developing" — the benefits went to elites, the costs to the poor and the environment |
| **The "underdeveloped" were fine before** | Before "development," most people had adequate livelihoods — development destroyed them |
| **We need alternatives TO development** | Not "alternative development" (different ways to pursue development) but alternatives that reject the development framework entirely |

### 1.2 Key Texts

- **Escobar, A. (1995). *Encountering Development: The Making and Unmaking of the Third World* — The foundational post-development text. Escobar traces how "development" was constructed as a discourse after Truman's 1949 speech.
- **Sachs, W. (ed.) (1992). *The Development Dictionary: A Guide to Knowledge as Power* — Contributors show how key development terms (poverty, progress, standard of living, market) are Western constructs.
- **Rahnema, M. with Bawtree, V. (eds.) (1997). *The Post-Development Reader* — A comprehensive collection critiquing development.

## 2. Post-Development Theory

### 2.1 Arturo Escobar: Development as Discourse

Escobar applies Foucault's concept of discourse to development:
- **Development is a regime of truth** — it determines what can be said, thought, and done
- **The "Third World" was invented** through development discourse — a diverse set of societies was homogenised as "underdeveloped"
- **Experts, institutions, and knowledge** — the World Bank, UNDP, development economists — produce and manage this discourse
- **Resistance is possible** — social movements, local knowledge, and grassroots alternatives can resist the development discourse

### 2.2 Development as "A Project of Western Modernity"

Post-development theorists argue that development is inseparable from Western modernity:
- Development imposes Western values — science, progress, individualism, consumerism
- It destroys local knowledge, cultures, and livelihoods
- It creates a "needs" industry — development experts define needs and then sell the solutions
- The "standard of living" is a Western concept — it imposes one materialist vision of the good life

### 2.3 Wolfgang Sachs: The Development Dictionary

Sachs and his contributors deconstruct key development terms:

| Term | Deconstruction |
|------|----------------|
| **Poverty** | Before development, people were not "poor" — they had sufficient livelihoods; "poverty" was invented to justify intervention |
| **Progress** | A 19th-century Western concept imposed as universal — implies linear, material, technological advancement |
| **Standard of Living** | Reduces human well-being to consumption — ignores spirituality, community, ecological harmony |
| **Market** | Presented as natural and universal — actually a specific Western institution expanding through colonialism and neoliberal policy |
| **Environment** | A concept that separates humans from nature — enables environmental management as a technical problem |

## 3. Postcolonial Critiques

Postcolonial theory shares ground with post-development but has distinct intellectual roots and emphases.

### 3.1 Key Thinkers

| Thinker | Key Work | Contribution |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **Edward Said** | *Orientalism* (1978) | The West constructs the "Orient" as backward, exotic, inferior — making domination appear natural |
| **Gayatri Spivak** | "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988) | The marginalised cannot speak within Western knowledge systems — even when they try, they are reinterpreted |
| **Dipesh Chakrabarty** | *Provincializing Europe* (2000) | European thought is always treated as universal — we need to provincialise it, see it as one tradition among many |

### 3.2 The Subaltern Studies Project

A group of South Asian historians (Ranajit Guha, Partha Chatterjee, Gayatri Spivak) challenged elite historiography:
- **Elite nationalism** (Gandhi, Nehru, Congress) dominates Indian history
- **Subaltern perspectives** — peasants, workers, lower castes, women — have been erased
- Development history is written from the perspective of the "developmental state" — not from the perspective of those displaced, dispossessed, or marginalised by development

### 3.3 Postcolonial Development Critique

| Postcolonial Insight | Implication for Development |
|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Western knowledge is universalised | Development projects impose Western frameworks as "scientific" and "expert" |
| Colonial hierarchies persist | The Global North still claims authority over the Global South |
| "Modernity" was imposed, not chosen | Developing countries were forced into the modern state system, capitalism, and development |
| The "developed" West defines itself against the "backward" Rest | Development discourse reinforces this binary |

## 4. De-Growth and Post-Capitalism

### 4.1 The De-Growth Movement

De-growth (décroissance) emerged as a critique of growth-centric development:

| Claim | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Growth is impossible** | Infinite growth on a finite planet is physically impossible |
| **Growth is harmful** | Growth-driven development destroys ecosystems, increases inequality, and reduces well-being |
| **Rich countries must de-grow** | The Global North consumes far more than its fair share — must reduce consumption |
| **De-growth is not recession** | Planned, equitable, democratic reduction in throughput — not economic collapse |
| **Well-being without growth** | Happiness, community, health, and meaning do not require growth |

### 4.2 Key De-Growth Thinkers

| Thinker | Contribution |
|---------|--------------|
| **Serge Latouche** | *Farewell to Growth* (2007) — A society of de-growth is possible; we need to de-colonise our imagination |
| **Tim Jackson** | *Prosperity Without Growth* (2009) — Ecological macroeconomics: well-being on a finite planet |
| **Kate Raworth** | *Doughnut Economics* (2017) — The safe and just space for humanity: between social foundation and ecological ceiling |
| **Jason Hickel** | *Less is More* (2020) — Capitalism and industrialism are the problem; de-growth is the solution |

### 4.3 Doughnut Economics

Kate Raworth's model synthesises many critical approaches:

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **The social foundation** | Minimum standards for well-being (food, water, health, education, gender equality, etc.) — from the SDGs |
| **The ecological ceiling** | Planetary boundaries — climate change, biodiversity loss, freshwater, etc. (Rockström et al.) |
| **The doughnut** | The space between foundation and ceiling — the safe and just space for humanity |
| **Regenerative and distributive** | The economy should be regenerative by design (circular) and distributive by design (redistributive) |

## 5. Beyond Development: Practical Alternatives

### 5.1 Sumak Kawsay (Buen Vivir)

Derived from Indigenous Andean (Quechua) cosmovision:
- **Living well**, not living better — harmony with community and nature, not accumulation
- **Nature as subject** — Pachamama (Earth Mother) has rights; nature is not a resource to be exploited
- **Incorporated into Ecuador's (2008) and Bolivia's (2009) constitutions**
- **Critique**: Has been co-opted by governments pursuing extractive development under the banner of "Buen Vivir"

### 5.2 Ubuntu

Southern African philosophy:
- **"I am because we are"** — personhood is constituted through relationships
- Emphasises community, solidarity, care
- Challenges development's individualism and consumerism

### 5.3 Swadeshi and Gandhian Development

India's tradition of local self-reliance:
- **Gandhi**: "Earth provides enough to satisfy every man's needs, but not every man's greed"
- **Khadi** (hand-spun cloth) as resistance to industrial capitalism
- **Sarvodaya** (welfare for all) — development from below
- **Critique**: Impractical for a globalised economy; romanticises rural life

## 6. Critiques of Post-Development

### 6.1 Internal Critiques

| Critique | Response |
|----------|----------|
| **Romanticises the pre-development past** | Escobar later acknowledged this — traditional societies were not utopias |
| **Ignores real gains** | Development has brought longer lives, lower child mortality, literacy, and access to technology — for billions of people |
| **No practical alternative** | What does "post-development" look like in practice? De-growth cannot feed 9 billion people at current technology levels |
| **Anti-poor** | The poor want development — they want electricity, clean water, schools, hospitals, and the chance to consume |
| **Essentialises the West** | The West is treated as a monolithic evil — ignores Western movements for social justice, environmentalism, and decolonisation |

### 6.2 Post-Development and Bangladesh

| Post-Development Critique | Bangladesh Application |
|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Development destroys local livelihoods | Readymade garments created jobs but also disrupted agriculture, small-scale manufacturing |
| Development creates dependency | Bangladesh is heavily dependent on foreign buyers, credit rating agencies, and aid |
| Development imposes Western values | The RMG industry imposes global standards, labour discipline, and consumerist aspirations |
| Development benefits elites | The benefits of Bangladesh's growth have been unequally shared |
| But... | Most Bangladeshis would not reject "development" — they want better lives, and they associate this with economic growth, education, and modernity |

## Summary

Post-development offers the most radical critique in this module sequence: development is not a neutral process of improvement but a Western discourse that constructs "underdevelopment" and imposes a particular vision of modernity. Escobar's discourse analysis, the postcolonial tradition (Said, Spivak, Chakrabarty), and the de-growth movement (Latouche, Jackson, Raworth) provide different angles on this critique. While post-development exposes the hidden assumptions and power dynamics of development, it is criticised for romanticising the past, ignoring real gains, and offering few practical alternatives. For Bangladesh, the tension is acute — the country has benefited enormously from "development," yet many of the critiques (inequality, dependency, environmental destruction) are clearly valid.

- **Post-Development** — Rejection of the entire development framework as a Western discourse of domination
- **Discourse** — Foucault/Escobar: a system of knowledge that determines what can be said and thought
