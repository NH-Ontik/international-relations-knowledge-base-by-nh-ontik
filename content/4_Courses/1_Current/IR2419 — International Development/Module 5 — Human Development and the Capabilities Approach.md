## 1. The Capabilities Approach

### 1.1 Origins

Amartya Sen developed the capabilities approach as an alternative to:
- **Utilitarianism**: Development as maximising total utility — ignores distribution and rights
- **Income/wealth approaches**: Development as GDP growth — ignores what people can actually do
- **Basic needs**: Focus on physical goods (food, water, shelter) but ignores agency and freedom

### 1.2 Core Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Functioning** | What a person actually does or is — "beings and doings" | Being well-nourished, being literate, participating in politics |
| **Capability** | The *freedom* to achieve various functionings — the set of options available | Having the ability to be well-nourished (even if one chooses to fast) |
| **Agency** | The ability to act on one's own goals and values — not just to be well-off but to be a causal agent | A woman deciding whether to work, not just being employed |
| **Conversion factors** | What determines whether a resource becomes a functioning | A bicycle enables mobility only if the person can ride (personal) and roads exist (social) |

### 1.3 Key Distinction: Resources vs Capabilities

The capabilities approach shifts focus from **what people have** (income, goods) to **what people can do and be** (capabilities). This matters because:
- Two people with the same income can have very different capabilities (one is healthy, one is disabled; one lives in a well-governed city, one in a remote village)
- Income is a means, not an end — development should be about expanding human freedom
- Poverty is capability deprivation, not just low income — and capability deprivation can persist even with income growth

## 2. The Human Development Index

### 2.1 Origins

The **Human Development Index (HDI)** was created by Mahbub ul Haq and Amartya Sen, first published in the UNDP's *Human Development Report* (1990). It was designed to refocus development debate on people rather than GNP.

### 2.2 Components

| Dimension | Indicator | Weight | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| **Health** | Life expectancy at birth | 1/3 | Longer life is a universal capability |
| **Education** | Mean years + Expected years of schooling | 1/3 | Knowledge is essential for freedom |
| **Income** | GNI per capita (PPP, log) | 1/3 | Means to achieve other capabilities |

**Calculation**: Each dimension is normalised (0–1), then the HDI is the geometric mean of the three dimension indices.

### 2.3 Critiques of HDI

| Critique | Counter-argument |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Reductive** — only 3 dimensions, loses Sen's richer vision | UNDP acknowledges this; IHDI and other indices supplement HDI |
| **Income still dominates** — GNI per capita correlates with HDI | But HDI adjusts for diminishing returns (log of income) and education/longevity are not purely income-driven |
| **National average hides inequality** — ignores within-country distribution | IHDI (Inequality-adjusted HDI) addresses this |
| **No environmental dimension** | The "Anthropocene HDI" is a proposed alternative |
| **Quality issues** — years of schooling ≠ quality of education | A valid critique; the SDGs added learning outcomes |

### 2.4 Other Human Development Indices

| Index | What It Measures | Added Value |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| **IHDI** | HDI discounted by inequality | Shows human development loss from inequality |
| **GII** | Gender Inequality Index | Reproductive health, empowerment, labour market |
| **MPI** | Multidimensional Poverty Index | Deprivations in health, education, living standards — not just income |
| **GDI** | Gender Development Index | Ratio of female to male HDI |

## 3. Martha Nussbaum's Capabilities List

Martha Nussbaum (philosopher, feminist) offered a more prescriptive version of the capabilities approach:
- **Sen**: Capabilities are the space for evaluation — he refuses to specify a definitive list
- **Nussbaum**: A list is necessary for justice and political action — we need to know which capabilities matter

**Nussbaum's Ten Central Capabilities**:
1. **Life** — To live a normal lifespan
2. **Bodily Health** — Good health, nutrition, shelter
3. **Bodily Integrity** — Freedom of movement, security from assault
4. **Senses, Imagination, Thought** — Education, expression, religion
5. **Emotions** — To love, grieve, experience
6. **Practical Reason** — To plan one's own life
7. **Affiliation** — To live with others, to participate, dignity, non-discrimination
8. **Other Species** — To live with nature
9. **Play** — To enjoy recreation
10. **Control over One's Environment** — Political participation, property rights

**Critique of the list approach**:
- **Paternalistic** — Who decides which capabilities matter?
- **Cultural imperialism** — Western scholar lists universal capabilities
- **Incomplete** — Why ten? Why not more?
- **Political** — A list that all governments must guarantee reduces political deliberation

## 4. Capabilities and Development Policy

### 4.1 From HDI to SDGs

The capabilities approach directly influenced the **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** :
- The MDGs (2000–2015) were more basic-needs oriented (halve poverty, reduce child mortality)
- The SDGs (2015–2030) reflect capabilities thinking — broader, more comprehensive, focused on human flourishing

### 4.2 Budgeting for Human Development

The capabilities approach implies:
- **Budget analysis**: Not just how much is spent, but what capabilities are being expanded
- **Multidimensional poverty targeting**: Programmes should address multiple deprivations simultaneously
- **Agency**: Beneficiaries should be participants, not passive recipients

### 4.3 Bangladesh and Human Development

**Achievements**:
| Indicator | 1990 | 2025 | Change | Remark |
|-----------|------|------|--------|--------|
| HDI value | 0.39 | 0.67 | +72% | Fastest improvement in the world since 1990 |
| Life expectancy | 59 | 75 | +16 years | Remarkable for a low-income country |
| Mean years schooling | 2.8 | 7.6 | +4.8 | Rapid expansion in female education |
| GNI per capita (PPP) | $1,044 | $8,700 | ×8+ | Consistent growth |
| MPI headcount | 83% (1990) | ~25% | −58pp | Dramatic poverty reduction |

**Key insight**: Bangladesh's HDI improvement has outpaced its income growth — confirming Sen's argument that human development and income growth can, and should, go together.

**Remaining challenges**:
- Inequality-adjusted HDI is significantly lower (IHDI ~0.53 vs HDI 0.67)
- Education quality lags behind quantity
- Gender inequality persists despite improvements
- Environmental sustainability

## 5. Critiques of the Capabilities Approach

### 5.1 Theoretical Critiques

| Critique | Argument | Sen's Response |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| **Individualism** | Too focused on individual capabilities — ignores social structures, groups, class | Individual capabilities require social conditions — he does not ignore structure |
| **Vagueness** | "Capabilities" are not clearly defined — too broad to be operational | This is a strength — it is a framework, not a fixed formula |
| **No guidance on trade-offs** | When capabilities conflict (e.g., liberty vs security), the approach gives no guidance | Practical reason and public deliberation should decide |
| **Western bias** | Individual freedom and choice are Western values | Freedom is universal — all cultures value capabilities (though they may emphasise different ones) |

### 5.2 Political Economy Critiques

- **The capabilities approach says little about power** — who decides which capabilities matter? How do the powerful constrain the capabilities of the weak?
- **It ignores capitalism** — the market system may systematically undermine certain capabilities (worker exploitation, environmental destruction)
- **It is compatible with neoliberalism** — The World Bank and IMF can accept "human development" while promoting structural adjustment; the capabilities approach may not challenge the fundamental structure of the global economy
- **It is vague about the state** — The state must provide capabilities, but the approach does not specify the state's role or limits

## 6. The Capabilities Approach Today

The capabilities approach remains one of the most influential alternatives to income-based development:
- **UNDP** uses it as its framework
- **SDGs** reflect its influence
- **Multidimensional poverty measurement** is now standard
- **Academic development studies** use the capabilities approach extensively
- **Informing national policy**: Bhutan's Gross National Happiness, Colombia's happiness index, UK's national wellbeing indicators

---

## Summary

The capabilities approach, developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum, redefines development as the expansion of human freedoms — what people can do and be — rather than the growth of income or utility. The HDI operationalised this vision, redirecting development thinking from GDP to people. While powerful, the approach is criticised for its individualism, vagueness, and compatibility with neoliberal economics. Bangladesh's remarkable HDI improvement — outpacing income growth — confirms Sen's central insight. The approach continues to shape development practice through the SDGs, multidimensional poverty measurement, and human development reporting.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Functionings** — What a person actually does or is ("beings and doings")
- **Capabilities** — The freedom or opportunity to achieve various functionings
- **Agency** — The ability to pursue one's own goals and values
- **Conversion factors** — Personal, social, and environmental factors affecting whether a resource becomes a functioning
- **HDI** — Composite measure: health, education, income
- **IHDI** — HDI discounted by inequality
- **MPI** — Multidimensional Poverty Index — non-income deprivations
- **Capability Deprivation** — Poverty as lack of freedom, not just low income

### Key Thinkers
- **Amartya Sen** — Capabilities approach, *Development as Freedom* (1999)
- **Martha Nussbaum** — List of ten central capabilities
- **Mahbub ul Haq** — Creator of HDI, *Reflections on Human Development* (1995)
- **Sabina Alkire** — Multidimensional poverty measurement (OPHI, MPI)
