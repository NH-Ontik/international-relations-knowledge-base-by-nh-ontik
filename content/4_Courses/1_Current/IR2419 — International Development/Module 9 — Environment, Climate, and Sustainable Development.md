By the end of this module, students will be able to:

2. Explain the relationship between development and environmental degradation
3. Analyse the climate-development nexus
4. Critically evaluate green growth and degrowth as responses
5. Assess Bangladesh's climate vulnerability and adaptation

## 1. Sustainable Development

### 1.1 Origins

Sustainable development emerged from:
- **UN Conference on the Human Environment** (Stockholm, 1972) — first global environmental summit
- **Brundtland Commission** (1987) — *Our Common Future*
- **UN Conference on Environment and Development (Earth Summit)** (Rio, 1992) — Agenda 21, UNFCCC, CBD
- **MDGs** (2000) — Goal 7: environmental sustainability
- **SDGs** (2015) — 17 goals integrating social, economic, and environmental dimensions

### 1.2 The Brundtland Definition

"Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

| Dimension | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Intergenerational equity** | Future generations have equal claim to resources |
| **Intragenerational equity** | Current inequalities must be addressed |
| **Needs** | The essential needs of the world's poor should be prioritised |
| **Limits** | Technology and social organisation impose limits on the environment's ability to meet needs |

### 1.3 Three Pillars

Sustainable development is conventionally understood as three interdependent pillars:

| Pillar | Focus | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **Economic** | Growth, efficiency, prosperity | GDP growth, productivity, jobs |
| **Social** | Equity, inclusion, participation | Poverty reduction, education, health |
| **Environmental** | Ecological integrity, resource conservation | Biodiversity, climate, pollution |

**Critique**: The three-pillar model assumes harmony — in practice, the three dimensions often conflict (e.g., economic growth vs carbon reduction). Power determines which pillar wins.

## 2. Development and Environmental Degradation

### 2.1 The Environmental Kuznets Curve

The Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) hypothesises an inverted-U relationship: environmental degradation rises with income at low levels but falls at higher income levels.

| Stage | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| **Low income** | Environmental degradation increases (industrialisation, resource extraction) |
| **Middle income** | Peak — worst environmental damage |
| **High income** | Degradation decreases (service economy, regulation, technology, shifting dirty production abroad) |

**Critique**:
- The EKC does not hold for all pollutants (CO₂ emissions do not decline at high income)
- The decline in rich countries is partly because they **exported** dirty production to developing countries (pollution haven hypothesis)
- Even if the EKC holds, the level of damage at the peak may be catastrophic
- The time to turn the corner may be too long — irreversible damage occurs first

### 2.2 The Tragedy of the Commons

Garrett Hardin (1968) argued that common resources (atmosphere, oceans, forests) are overused because each individual benefits from exploitation while the costs are shared:

| Resource | Development Impact | Solution |
|----------|-------------------|----------|
| Forests | Deforestation for agriculture, timber | Sustainable forestry, REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) |
| Fisheries | Overfishing from industrial fleets | Exclusive economic zones, quotas |
| Atmosphere | CO₂ from industrialisation | Carbon pricing, Paris Agreement |
| Water | Pollution, depletion | Integrated water resource management |

**Critique**: Ostrom (1990) showed that communities can sustainably manage common resources through local institutions — the "tragedy" is not inevitable.

## 3. Climate Change and Development

### 3.1 The Climate-Development Nexus

Climate change affects every dimension of development:

| Development Sector | Climate Impact | Countries Most Affected |
|-------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Agriculture** | Changing rainfall, heat stress, floods, droughts | Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia |
| **Health** | Heat-related mortality, vector-borne diseases (malaria, dengue), malnutrition | Low-income tropical countries |
| **Water** | Glacial melt, saltwater intrusion, variable rainfall | Small islands, coastal and mountain regions |
| **Infrastructure** | Floods, storms, sea-level rise | Coastal cities (Dhaka, Mumbai, Shanghai, Lagos) |
| **Migration** | Climate-induced displacement (slow and rapid onset) | Sahel, South Asia, Pacific Islands |
| **Conflict** | Resource scarcity, food prices, displacement | Weak states (Sahel, Horn of Africa) |

### 3.2 Climate Justice

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Historical responsibility** | The Global North produced the majority of cumulative CO₂ emissions — they caused the problem |
| **Disproportionate impact** | The Global South suffers most from climate change despite contributing least |
| **Common but Differentiated Responsibilities (CBDR)** | All countries have a responsibility to act, but developed countries should do more |
| **Loss and Damage** | Beyond adaptation — some climate impacts are irreversible and cannot be adapted to |
| **Green Climate Fund** | $100 billion/year pledge (not yet met) — rich countries pay for mitigation and adaptation in poor countries |

### 3.3 The Climate-Development Dilemma

| Argument | For | Against |
|----------|-----|---------|
| **Developing countries must grow** | Growth reduces poverty — they cannot be asked to sacrifice development for climate | Growth based on fossil fuels will make climate change catastrophic |
| **Green growth is possible** | Renewable energy, efficiency, and innovation can decouple growth from emissions | No country has achieved absolute decoupling at the rate required |
| **Developing countries have a right to develop** | Historical emissions are the North's responsibility — the South should not be penalised | But if every country follows the fossil fuel path, the planet warms by 3°C+ |
| **Rich countries must lead** | The North has the resources and historical responsibility | The North has failed to meet climate finance pledges |

## 4. Responses

### 4.1 Green Growth

Green growth claims that economic growth and environmental sustainability can be reconciled:

| Strategy | Mechanism | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Renewable energy** | Solar, wind, hydro replace fossil fuels | Bangladesh: 4 GW of renewable capacity planned |
| **Energy efficiency** | Less energy for same output | LED lighting, efficient appliances |
| **Circular economy** | Reduce, reuse, recycle | EPR (extended producer responsibility), waste-to-energy |
| **Green technology** | Innovation drives cleaner production | Electric vehicles, green hydrogen |
| **Natural capital accounting** | Include environmental costs in GDP | SEEA (System of Environmental-Economic Accounting) |

**Critique**:
- Green growth assumes **absolute decoupling** — but global resource use is still rising
- Green technology is expensive — developing countries may not afford it
- Green growth does not challenge consumerism — it is "greening" capitalism, not transforming it

### 4.2 Degrowth

Degrowth argues that green growth is an oxymoron — rich countries must reduce consumption and production:

| Argument | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Infinite growth on a finite planet is impossible** | Thermodynamics — you cannot have perpetual material growth |
| **Decoupling is a myth** | Relative decoupling (less CO₂ per unit of GDP) is real but absolute decoupling (reducing total CO₂ while growing) has not happened at scale |
| **Rich countries consume too much** | The richest 10% produce ~50% of emissions — they must reduce |
| **Well-being does not require growth** | Beyond a threshold (~$20,000 GDP/capita), well-being is not correlated with income |
| **Degrowth is democratic planning** | Not recession — planned reduction in throughput, redistribution, shorter working hours |

**Critique**:
- Degrowth is politically impossible — no electorate votes for recession
- Degrowth is a Northern luxury — the poor need growth to escape poverty
- Renewable energy may enable decoupling eventually
- Degrowth does not specify a viable political strategy

### 4.3 Planetary Boundaries

Rockström et al. (2009) identified **nine planetary boundaries**:

| Boundary | Status | Risk |
|----------|--------|------|
| Climate change | Exceeded (safe zone: <350 ppm, current: ~420 ppm) | Catastrophic |
| Biosphere integrity | Exceeded (extinction rate 100–1000× natural) | Ecosystem collapse |
| Land-system change | Exceeded (forests converted to agriculture) | Biodiversity loss |
| Biogeochemical flows (N, P) | Exceeded (fertiliser runoff) | Dead zones, water pollution |
| Freshwater use | Approaching boundary | Water stress |
| Ocean acidification | Approaching boundary | Marine ecosystem collapse |
| Atmospheric aerosol loading | Within boundary | Localised air pollution |
| Stratospheric ozone | Within boundary (recovering) | UV radiation (Montreal Protocol success) |
| Novel entities (pollution, plastics) | Exceeded (poorly quantified) | Unknown |

## 5. Bangladesh: Climate and Environment

### 5.1 Vulnerability

Bangladesh is one of the most climate-vulnerable countries in the world:

| Climate Risk | Impact |
|-------------|--------|
| **Sea-level rise** | 10–30 cm by 2050 — 17% of land area could be lost, 20 million people displaced |
| **Cyclones** | Increasing intensity — Cyclone Sidr (2007) killed 3,400; Cyclone Amphan (2020) $13 billion damage |
| **Floods** | 20–25% of the country is flooded annually — climate change increases variability |
| **Heat stress** | Rising temperatures reduce labour productivity and increase mortality |
| **Salinity** | Saltwater intrusion in coastal aquifers — drinking water, agriculture affected |
| **Riverbank erosion** | Millions displaced annually (Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna delta is dynamic) |

### 5.2 Adaptation

Bangladesh has become a global leader in climate adaptation:

| Adaptation | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Cyclone Preparedness Programme** | 78,000 volunteers, 4,000+ cyclone shelters — dramatically reduced cyclone mortality |
| **Embankments and polders** | Protects coastal agriculture from tidal flooding |
| **Climate-resilient crops** | Salt-tolerant rice varieties (e.g., BRRI dhan 47, 53) |
| **Disaster management** | Standing Orders on Disasters, disaster management committees at all levels |
| **Micro-insurance** | Weather-indexed crop insurance for small farmers |
| **Community-based adaptation** | Local adaptation plans, homestead gardening, floating agriculture |

### 5.3 Mitigation

Bangladesh's mitigation contribution is small (0.5% of global emissions) but the country has committed to:
- **Renewable energy**: Target of 40% by 2041 (solar, wind, hydropower from Nepal-Bhutan)
- **Energy efficiency**: Efficient brick kilns, solar irrigation pumps, LED lighting
- **Mujib Climate Prosperity Plan**: Shifts from vulnerability to resilience to prosperity

## Summary

Sustainable development attempts to reconcile economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection — but these three pillars often conflict in practice. Climate change is the defining challenge: it disproportionately affects developing countries (who have contributed least to the problem) while creating a dilemma between growth and decarbonisation. Responses range from green growth (technology-driven decoupling) to degrowth (planned reduction in consumption). Bangladesh exemplifies the tensions: one of the most climate-vulnerable countries, a leader in adaptation, a minor emitter — but still pursuing rapid, fossil-fuel-dependent growth.

### Key Definitions
- **Sustainable development** — Meeting present needs without compromising future generations
- **Intergenerational equity** — Fairness across generations
- **Environmental Kuznets Curve** — Inverted-U relationship between income and environmental degradation
- **CBDR** — Common but Differentiated Responsibilities
- **Loss and Damage** — Irreversible climate impacts beyond adaptation
- **Green growth** — Decoupling economic growth from environmental degradation
- **Degrowth** — Planned reduction in throughput in rich countries
- **Planetary boundaries** — Nine Earth-system limits
- **Adaptation** — Adjusting to actual or expected climate impacts
- **Mitigation** — Reducing greenhouse gas emissions
