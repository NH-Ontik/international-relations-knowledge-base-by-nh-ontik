## 1. The Modernization Paradigm

### 1.1 Historical Context

Modernization theory emerged in the early Cold War period (1950s–1960s):
- The US needed a development model to counter communist influence in the newly decolonising world
- It drew on classical sociology (Durkheim, Weber — transition from traditional to modern society)
- It reflected the optimistic belief that all societies follow a linear path from "backwardness" to "modernity"
- Key institutional home: Harvard, MIT, US government, World Bank

### 1.2 Core Assumptions

| Assumption | Meaning | Implication |
|------------|---------|-------------|
| **Linear progress** | All societies pass through the same stages | Developing countries can follow the Western path |
| **Traditional vs modern** | A binary opposition — traditional is obstacle, modern is goal | Development requires replacing traditional values |
| **Endogenous change** | Development is primarily internal (domestic factors, not external exploitation) | Blaming colonialism is an excuse |
| **Diffusion** | Modern values, technology, and institutions spread from developed to developing | Contact with the West is beneficial |
| **Evolutionary** | Development is natural and inevitable — some societies are just "behind" | Given time and help, all societies will develop |

## 2. Rostow's Five Stages of Economic Growth

W. W. Rostow's *The Stages of Economic Growth: A Non-Communist Manifesto* (1960) is the iconic modernization text:

| Stage | Description | Historical Example | Duration |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|----------|
| **1. Traditional Society** | Limited technology, subsistence agriculture, pre-Newtonian science, family/clan-based social structure | Pre-colonial societies; pre-1700 Europe | Centuries |
| **2. Preconditions for Take-Off** | New ideas, investment in infrastructure, emergence of entrepreneurial class, modern education | Early industrial Europe (1700s); post-colonial states (1950s–60s) | 50–100 years |
| **3. Take-Off** | Rapid industrialisation, sustained growth, investment >10% of national income, new political and social institutions | Britain (1783–1802), Japan (1880s–1900), Russia (1890s–1914) | 20–30 years |
| **4. Drive to Maturity** | Economy diversifies, technology spreads to all sectors, new industries emerge | US (1900–1920s), Western Europe (1950s–60s), Japan (1950s–70s) | 40–60 years |
| **5. Age of High Mass Consumption** | Consumer society, service sector dominant, welfare state, mass production of durable goods | US (1920s–), Western Europe (1960s–), Japan (1980s–) | Ongoing |

**Political implications**: Rostow explicitly framed this as an alternative to Marx's stages and to communist development — the "non-communist manifesto."

### 2.1 Critiques of Rostow's Stages

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Eurocentric** | The stages are based on Western European history — assumes the Western path is universal |
| **No colonialism** | Ignores how colonialism shaped the starting point of developing countries — they did not start with "traditional society" but with a society that had been exploited and distorted |
| **Linear and deterministic** | Development does not follow one path — East Asian success differed from Latin American experience |
| **Take-off is vague** | The concept of "take-off" is theoretically weak — what actually causes the transition? |
| **No international dimension** | Treats development as internal — ignores how the global economy constrains developing countries |

## 3. The Social Psychology of Modernization

Modernization theory is not just economic — it is also about changing values and attitudes:

**Inkeles & Smith's "Becoming Modern" (1974)** : Identified characteristics of "modern" individuals:
- Open to new experiences
- Independent from traditional authority
- Belief in science and technology
- Ambition and achievement orientation
- Punctuality and planning
- Active participation in public life

**Lerner's "The Passing of Traditional Society" (1958)** : Argued that modernization requires empathy — the ability to imagine oneself in a different role. Traditional societies lacked this; exposure to media (especially radio and film) created it.

**Critique**: These approaches assume traditional values are obstacles — but many "traditional" values (community, solidarity, religious faith) may support development rather than hinder it. They also impose Western cultural norms.

## 4. Modernization and Development Policy

Modernization theory directly shaped development policy in the 1950s–60s:

| Policy | Theory | Practice |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Import-substituting industrialization (ISI)** | Industrialisation requires state support for infant industries | Latin America, South Asia — mixed results |
| **Big Push / Balanced Growth** | Large-scale investment is needed to break out of poverty trap | World Bank projects, development planning |
| **Foreign aid for infrastructure** | Building preconditions for take-off | US aid, World Bank lending |
| **Education and training** | Creating modern individuals and skills | UNESCO literacy campaigns, technical training |
| **Population control** | High fertility is a barrier to modernization | Family planning programmes (Bangladesh success story) |

## 5. Modernization in Practice: Bangladesh

### 5.1 Did Bangladesh "Modernize"?

| Modernization Indicator | 1971 | 2026 | Modern? |
|------------------------|------|------|---------|
| **Industrialisation** | Agrarian (80% agriculture) | Industrialising (RMG 15% GDP) | In transition |
| **Urbanisation** | 8% urban | 40% urban | In transition |
| **Literacy** | ~25% | ~78% | Yes |
| **Fertility** | 7.0 children/woman | 2.0 | Yes (demographic transition) |
| **Mass media** | Radio only | Universal mobile, widespread internet | Yes |
| **Traditional authority** | Feudal zamindari, religious leadership | Reduced but persistent | Partial |

### 5.2 Modernization Critique of Bangladesh

From a modernization perspective, Bangladesh's remaining challenges include:
- **Incomplete structural transformation**: Still dependent on low-wage RMG — has not moved to higher-value production
- **Traditional political culture**: Patrimonial politics, patronage, weak institutions
- **Social conservatism**: Gender inequality persists despite economic gains; religious conservatism

### 5.3 Bangladesh as Modernization Success?

Modernization theorists would point to Bangladesh's growth (6%+ per year), demographic transition, education expansion, and urbanisation as confirmation that the theory works. Critics would argue:
- Not all "traditional" values were obstacles (community-based microfinance, family networks)
- Bangladesh's success owes more to specific historical and structural factors (RMG access to US/EU markets) than to a general "modernization" process
- Bangladesh's development has been highly unequal and environmentally unsustainable — "modern" does not mean "good"

## 6. Critiques and Legacy

### 6.1 Summary of Critiques

| Critique | Theorists |
|----------|-----------|
| **Ethnocentric/Eurocentric** | Frank, Escobar, Said |
| **Ignores colonialism/exploitation** | Frank, Rodney, Fanon |
| **Assumes one path** | Gerschenkron, Sen |
| **Traditional vs modern false binary** | Gupta, Chakrabarty |
| **Linear and deterministic** | All critics |
| **Cold War instrument** | Latham |

### 6.2 Why Modernization Theory Still Matters

Despite its critiques, modernization theory remains influential:
- **National development plans**: Many developing countries still pursue the "catch-up" model
- **World Bank/IMF thinking**: Modernization assumptions underpin structural adjustment and institutional reform
- **The BRICS model**: BRICS development strategies (state-led industrialisation, export-oriented growth) are essentially modernization in different form
- **SDGs**: The SDG framework implicitly assumes linear progress toward development goals

---

## Summary

Modernization theory was the dominant development paradigm from the 1950s–1970s, tracing its assumptions to classical sociology and Cold War geopolitics. Rostow's stages of growth provided an influential but deeply flawed linear model. The theory shaped development policy through ISI, the Big Push, foreign aid, and population control. Critiques exposed its Eurocentrism, disregard for colonialism, false traditional/modern binary, and its function as Cold War ideology. Despite these critiques, modernization assumptions persist in development policy and in the implicit belief that development means "becoming like the West."

### Key Definitions
- **Modernization Theory** — Development as linear transition from traditional to modern society
- **Rostow's Stages** — Traditional, preconditions, take-off, drive to maturity, high mass consumption
- **Take-Off** — The critical stage when industrialisation and sustained growth begin
- **Big Push** — Massive state-led investment to break out of poverty
- **Diffusion** — Spread of modern values/technology from developed to developing
- **Traditional vs Modern** — Binary distinction at the heart of modernization theory
