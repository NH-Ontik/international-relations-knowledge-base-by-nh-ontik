# Module 1 — What is Development? Contested Concepts

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2419 — International Development
- **Module:** 1 — What is Development? Contested Concepts
- **Prerequisites:** None

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Explain why development is a contested concept
2. Compare economic, human, and alternative definitions of development
3. Critically evaluate GDP and HDI as development measures
4. Trace the evolution of development thinking
5. Analyse Bangladesh's development trajectory through different lenses

---

## 1. Why Development Is Contested

### 1.1 The Central Question

Development is one of the most contested concepts in the social sciences because it involves fundamental questions about the good society:

| Question | Competing Answers |
|----------|------------------|
| What is the goal of development? | Economic growth? Human well-being? Freedom? Sustainability? |
| Who decides what development means? | States? Markets? Communities? International institutions? |
| Who benefits from development? | The poor? The elite? The West? Everyone? |
| How do we measure development? | GDP? HDI? Happiness? Freedom? |

**The contested nature is not a weakness** — it reflects the fact that development is about values, power, and choices, not just technical questions of growth.

### 1.2 Competing Definitions

| Definition | Focus | Key Proponents |
|------------|-------|---------------|
| **Economic growth** | Increase in GDP/GNI per capita | Rostow, Lewis, classical economists |
| **Modernization** | Transition from traditional to modern society | Rostow, Inkeles, Lerner |
| **Human development** | Expanding people's freedoms and capabilities | Sen, ul Haq, UNDP |
| **Basic needs** | Meeting minimum requirements for a dignified life | ILO, Streeten, Hicks |
| **Sustainable development** | Meeting present needs without compromising future generations | Brundtland, WCED, UN |
| **Rights-based development** | Development as the realisation of human rights | UN, Sen, Pogge |
| **Post-development** | Rejection of Western-defined development | Escobar, Sachs, Rahnema |

## 2. The Evolution of Development Thinking

| Era | Dominant Paradigm | Key Actors | Key Events |
|-----|-------------------|------------|------------|
| **1940s–50s: Reconstruction** | Modernization, state-led growth | US, World Bank, UN | Marshall Plan, Point Four, UN specialized agencies |
| **1960s–70s: Dependency critique** | Dependency, basic needs, redistribution | Global South, UNCTAD, ILO | NIEO, Brandt Report, dependency theory |
| **1980s: Neoliberal turn** | Washington Consensus, structural adjustment | IMF, World Bank, US/UK | SAPs, debt crisis, lost decade for Latin America |
| **1990s: Human development** | Capabilities, human development, MDGs | UNDP, Sen, ul Haq | Human Development Report (1990), HDI |
| **2000s: MDGs/SDGs** | Poverty reduction, goals framework | UN, donors, developing states | MDGs (2000), SDGs (2015) |
| **2010s–present: Crisis and contestation** | Climate, inequality, pandemic, de-growth | Multiple, fragmented | COVID-19, climate crisis, BRICS+, SDG backsliding |

## 3. Measuring Development

### 3.1 GDP and Its Limitations

| Measure | What It Captures | What It Misses |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| **GDP per capita** | Market value of goods and services per person | Inequality, informal economy, non-market work, environmental degradation, well-being, happiness |
| **GNP/GNI** | Income of residents (including abroad) | Same limitations as GDP |

**Critiques of GDP**:
- **Does not capture distribution**: GDP can rise while most people get poorer (Bangladesh: GDP growth 6%+ per year but inequality has risen — Gini index from 0.32 in 2000 to 0.48 in 2022)
- **Ignores unpaid work**: Women's domestic labour, care work, subsistence farming — disproportionately women's work
- **Destruction counts as growth**: An oil spill increases GDP (clean-up costs) — but welfare has clearly declined
- **No environmental accounting**: Depletion of natural resources is not deducted

### 3.2 The Human Development Index (HDI)

Created by Mahbub ul Haq and Amartya Sen (1990) as an alternative to GDP:

| Dimension | Indicator | Weight |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Health** | Life expectancy at birth | 1/3 |
| **Education** | Expected years + mean years of schooling | 1/3 |
| **Income** | GNI per capita (PPP, log) | 1/3 |

**Bangladesh's HDI progress**:
- 1990: 0.386 (low) — among the world's poorest countries
- 2022: 0.661 (medium) — the fastest HDI improvement of any country since 1990
- Life expectancy: 59 years (1990) → 74 years (2022)
- Expected schooling: 5 years → 12 years
- GNI per capita: $1,160 → $7,420 (PPP)

**Critiques of HDI**:
- **No inequality adjustment**: HDI does not capture distribution within the country (IHDI — Inequality-adjusted HDI — addresses this but is less used)
- **No environmental dimension**: HDI does not measure sustainability
- **No human rights/political freedom**: A country can score high on HDI but be authoritarian
- **Crude indicators**: Life expectancy captures health systems but not morbidity (quality of life); years of schooling captures quantity not quality

### 3.3 Alternative Measures

| Index | What It Measures | Creator |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)** | Poverty across health, education, living standards | OPHI, UNDP |
| **Gini Coefficient** | Income/wealth inequality | — |
| **Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI)** | GDP adjusted for environmental and social costs | Daly, Cobb |
| **Happy Planet Index** | Well-being per unit of environmental impact | NEF |
| **World Happiness Report** | Subjective well-being, trust, social support | Gallup, UN |
| **Gender Inequality Index (GII)** | Gender disparities in reproductive health, empowerment, labour | UNDP |

## 4. Development as a Western Discourse

### 4.1 The "Invention" of Development

Development as a concept and practice was "invented" in the post-1945 period:
- **Truman's 1949 inaugural address**: Used the term "underdeveloped areas" for the first time — creating a new category of states
- **The Cold War context**: Development was a geopolitical project — winning hearts and minds against communism
- **The modernisation assumption**: Development was defined as becoming like "us" (the West) — a linear path from traditional to modern, from poor to rich

### 4.2 Postcolonial Critique

Escobar's *Encountering Development* (1995) argues development is a discourse that:
- **Constructs "the Third World"** as poor, backward, traditional — in need of intervention by the West
- **Depoliticises poverty**: Poverty is treated as a technical problem (experts, plans, projects) rather than a political problem (power, exploitation, colonialism)
- **Legitimises Western intervention**: Experts, institutions, and technologies from the North are deployed to "develop" the South — a continuation of the colonial relationship

## 5. Bangladesh Development: A Case Study

### 5.1 The Bangladesh Development Paradox

Bangladesh presents a paradox for development studies:

| Indicator | 1971 (Independence) | Today (2026) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| GDP per capita | ~$100 | ~$2,800 |
| Life expectancy | 47 years | 74 years |
| Fertility rate | 7.0 | 2.0 |
| Primary school enrolment | ~60% | ~98% |
| Poverty rate | ~80% | ~12% (extreme) |
| RMG exports | $0 | ~$50 billion/year |

**Key question**: How did a country with "hopeless" prospects (Henry Kissinger's "basket case") achieve such rapid development?

### 5.2 Explanations

| Perspective | Explanation | Evidence |
|-------------|-------------|----------|
| **Neoliberal** | Trade liberalisation, export-oriented growth, private sector | RMG sector success, FDI growth |
| **State-led** | State investment in health, education, family planning, infrastructure | Massively expanded public health system, female education |
| **Bottom-up/NGO** | BRAC, Grameen Bank, microfinance, women's empowerment | BRAC as world's largest NGO, microfinance revolution |
| **Social** | Women's empowerment, fertility decline, education | Family planning success, gender parity in primary education |
| **Structural** | Low wages, global supply chains, preferential trade access | RMG sector built on cheap labour, EU EBA |
| **Critical** | Development has increased inequality, environmental degradation, and labour exploitation | Rising Gini, RMG labour rights violations, Dhaka pollution |

### 5.3 Lessons for Development Theory

- Bangladesh challenges dependency theory (a poor, peripheral economy has experienced rapid growth)
- Bangladesh supports human development theory (deliberate investment in health, education, family planning)
- Bangladesh illustrates the importance of context-specific factors (RMG as the "right" industry at the "right" time)
- Bangladesh raises questions about development quality (is RMG-led growth sustainable? Has it benefited women?)

## Summary

Development is a contested concept — definitions range from economic growth through human development to post-development rejection. The evolution of development thinking reflects broader historical shifts: post-war optimism, dependency critique, neoliberal turn, human development paradigm, and contemporary fragmentation. How we measure development (GDP, HDI, MPI) shapes what we value. Bangladesh — from "basket case" to lower-middle-income country — is one of the most interesting development stories of the last 50 years, challenging some theories while confirming others.
