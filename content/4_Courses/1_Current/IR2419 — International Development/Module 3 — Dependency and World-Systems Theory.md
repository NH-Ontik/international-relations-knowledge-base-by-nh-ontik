## 1. The Dependency Critique

### 1.1 Historical Context

Dependency theory emerged in the 1960s–70s as a direct challenge to modernization theory:
- **Latin American origins**: Linked to ECLAC (UN Economic Commission for Latin America), Raúl Prebisch, and the "structuralist" school
- **Intellectual roots**: Marxism, Lenin's theory of imperialism, nationalism in the Global South
- **Historical context**: The failure of ISI in many countries, the debt crisis, and growing frustration with the US-dominated global system

### 1.2 Core Argument

**Dependency is a condition in which the economies of one group of countries are conditioned by the development and expansion of others** (Dos Santos 1970).

| Claim | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Development and underdevelopment are two sides of the same process** | The wealth of the core is produced by the exploitation of the periphery |
| **Underdevelopment is not a stage — it is a condition produced by capitalism** | Developing countries are not "behind" — they have been actively underdeveloped |
| **The global system creates dependency** | Trade, investment, and technology transfer maintain unequal relationships |
| **Development in the periphery is "dependent development"** | Growth is possible but is constrained, distorted, and externally oriented |

## 2. Major Contributors

### 2.1 Prebisch-Singer Thesis

Raúl Prebisch (ECLAC) and Hans Singer (UN) independently identified a structural problem in global trade:

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Secular decline in terms of trade** | Over time, the price of primary commodities (raw materials, agricultural goods) falls relative to manufactured goods |
| **Why it happens** | Primary product demand grows slowly (Engel's Law); technological progress in manufacturing benefits the producer; primary commodity markets are competitive, manufacturing markets are oligopolistic |
| **Implication** | Developing countries (primary exporters) must export more to buy the same amount of manufactured goods — a structural drain |

**Solution**: Import-Substituting Industrialization (ISI) — protect domestic industry, produce manufactured goods at home, reduce dependence on core-country imports.

### 2.2 Andre Gunder Frank

Frank is the most influential dependency theorist. His key arguments:

| Concept | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Development of underdevelopment** | The metropolis (core) exploits the satellite (periphery) — underdevelopment is actively created by capitalism |
| **No "dual society"** | The idea of a traditional sector separate from the modern economy is false — the "traditional" sector is integrated into and exploited by global capitalism |
| **The satellite becomes more underdeveloped when ties to the metropolis are strongest** | Latin America was most underdeveloped during periods of closest integration into the world economy (colonial era, 19th century free trade) |
| **Potential for development when ties are weakest** | Periods of relative autonomy (wars, crises, protectionism) were periods of growth |

### 2.3 Cardoso and Faletto

Fernando Henrique Cardoso and Enzo Faletto's *Dependency and Development in Latin America* (1969) offered a more nuanced view:
- **"Dependent development" is possible**: Some peripheral economies (Brazil, Mexico) experienced industrialisation — but it was structurally constrained
- **Internal class dynamics matter**: Local elites ally with international capital — development benefits them, not the population
- **Associational development**: Development through alliance with foreign capital, not against it

### 2.4 Immanuel Wallerstein — World-Systems Theory

Wallerstein's *The Modern World-System* (1974–1989) extended dependency theory into a comprehensive historical-sociological framework:

| Zone | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Core** | Strong states, diversified economies, capital-intensive, high wages | Western Europe, US, Japan |
| **Semi-periphery** | Mix of core and periphery characteristics — exploited by core, exploits periphery | China, India, Brazil, Mexico |
| **Periphery** | Weak states, monoculture, labour-intensive, low wages, raw material exports | Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Latin America, South Asia |

**Key claims**:
- The world-system has existed since the "long 16th century" (1450–1640)
- Capitalism is inherently a world-system, not a national phenomenon
- States rise and fall within the system (hegemonic cycles — Dutch, British, US)
- Change is possible within the system (semi-periphery states can rise) but the capitalist world-system itself persists

## 3. Policy Implications

| Policy | Theory | Practice |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Import-Substituting Industrialization (ISI)** | Reduce dependence by manufacturing locally behind tariff barriers | Widely adopted in Latin America, India, Bangladesh (1950s–80s) |
| **South-South cooperation** | Reduce dependence on core-country markets | NIEO (1974), UNCTAD, G77 |
| **National control of resources** | Prevent foreign exploitation | Nationalisation of oil (1970s), mining |
| **Economic nationalism** | Develop domestic industrial capacity | Various — mixed results |

## 4. Critiques of Dependency Theory

### 4.1 Theoretical Critiques

| Critique | Argument | Counter-argument |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Deterministic** | Periphery has no agency — all development is impossible | Cardoso's "dependent development" allows for partial agency |
| **Cannot explain East Asia** | South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong developed from periphery to core — dependency theory predicted this was impossible | East Asia's success was exceptional — Cold War geopolitical context, US support, unique conditions |
| **Ignores internal factors** | Blames external exploitation for everything — ignores domestic politics, corruption, bad policies | Internal factors matter, but they operate within structural constraints |
| **Underdeveloped theory of the state** | Treats the state as a tool of capital — cannot explain state-led development | Cardoso's analysis incorporated state autonomy |
| **Monocausal** | One explanation for all underdevelopment — ignores diversity of developing country experience | A general theory will inevitably generalise |

### 4.2 Empirical Challenges

- **East Asian NICs**: South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Hong Kong industrialised and converged with core incomes between 1960 and 2000 — contradicting dependency predictions
- **China**: The world's largest developing country has grown at 9%+ for 40 years — not by delinking but by integrating into global capitalism on its own terms
- **Bangladesh**: Growth has been export-oriented (RMG), integrated into global supply chains — a type of integration that dependency theory would predict to be exploitative but has produced significant poverty reduction

## 5. Dependency and Bangladesh

### 5.1 Bangladesh as a Peripheral Economy

| Dependency Indicator | Bangladesh Situation |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Export concentration** | 84% of exports = RMG — extreme concentration in one low-value sector |
| **Terms of trade** | RMG prices have declined over time; import costs (machinery, oil) have risen |
| **Foreign ownership** | RMG is mostly Bangladeshi-owned but dependent on foreign buyers (Walmart, H&M, Zara, Inditex) |
| **Debt** | Moderate (public debt ~38% of GDP) but rising |
| **Aid dependence** | Declining — aid is down to ~2% of GNI, but was critical in early decades |
| **Technology dependence** | RMG is low-tech; Bangladesh imports most machinery, technology, and capital goods |

### 5.2 Between Dependency and Dependent Development

Bangladesh illustrates the complexity of applying dependency theory:
- **For dependency**: Extreme export concentration, declining terms of trade in RMG, buyer power of foreign corporations, low value-added
- **Against dependency**: Rapid poverty reduction, rising wages, domestic ownership of RMG, successful diversification into ships-breaking, IT, pharma
- **Dependent development**: Growth is real but structurally constrained — Bangladesh's "development" is shaped by the needs of global capital (low-wage production for Western consumers)

## 6. World-Systems and Contemporary Globalization

### 6.1 Has the World-System Changed?

| Change | Implication for World-Systems Theory |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| **Rise of China** | China has moved from periphery through semi-periphery to near-core — confirming the possibility of mobility within the system |
| **Global value chains** | Production is fragmented across countries — the core/periphery boundary is blurred within supply chains |
| **Financialization** | The core increasingly dominates through finance (Wall Street, City of London) rather than production |
| **BRICS+** | Semi-periphery states are building alternative institutions — challenge to core dominance |

### 6.2 Relevance Today

Dependency and world-systems theory remain relevant for understanding:
- **Global inequality**: The core-periphery income gap persists (~50:1 ratio between richest and poorest countries)
- **Unequal exchange**: Developing countries export low-value goods and import high-value goods — the structural imbalance continues
- **Debt**: The debt crisis reflects core-periphery financial relationships (IMF, World Bank, private creditors)
- **Climate**: Climate change is produced by core-country emissions but experienced most severely in the periphery

## Summary

Dependency theory emerged as a powerful critique of modernization theory, arguing that underdevelopment is produced by capitalism, not a stage before development. Frank's "development of underdevelopment," Prebisch-Singer's declining terms of trade, and Wallerstein's world-systems theory offered competing but related frameworks. Critiques — especially the East Asian experience — challenged the theory's determinism and empirical predictions. Bangladesh's experience is ambiguous: rapid growth within global capitalism contradicts pure dependency predictions, but the structural constraints (export concentration, declining terms of trade, foreign buyer power) confirm the theory's insights about peripheral vulnerability.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Dependency** — Conditioning of peripheral economies by core economies
- **Prebisch-Singer Thesis** — Secular decline in terms of trade for primary producers
- **Development of Underdevelopment** — Frank: underdevelopment is actively produced by capitalism
- **ISI** — Import-Substituting Industrialization — policy response to dependency
- **World-System** — Wallerstein: capitalist world-economy with core, semi-periphery, periphery
- **Core/Periphery/Semi-Periphery** — Three zones of the world-system
- **Terms of Trade** — Ratio of export prices to import prices

### Key Thinkers
- **Raúl Prebisch** — Terms of trade decline, ECLAC, structuralism
- **Hans Singer** — Co-developer of Prebisch-Singer thesis
- **Andre Gunder Frank** — Development of underdevelopment

- **Immanuel Wallerstein** — World-systems theory
