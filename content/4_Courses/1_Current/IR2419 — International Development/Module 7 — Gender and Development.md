## 1. Evolution of Gender in Development Thinking

### 1.1 Women in Development (WID)

WID emerged in the 1970s, driven by:
- Ester Boserup's *Woman's Role in Economic Development* (1970) — showed that women were marginalised by modernisation
- Liberal feminism in the West — equal rights, equal opportunities
- Critique of the male bias in development policy

| WID Claim | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Women have been left out of development | Modernisation projects went to men — women lost status and access |
| Women need to be integrated into development | Give women education, credit, training, jobs |
| The problem is exclusion | If women are included, they will benefit equally |
| Focus on practical needs | Income, credit, technology, access |

**Critiques of WID**:
- Does not question the development model itself — just wants women included
- Treats women as a homogeneous category — ignores class, race, ethnicity
- Assumes integration into a male-defined system is the solution
- Instrumentalises women — women are included because they are "good for development" (efficient workers, better loan repayments)

### 1.2 Women and Development (WAD)

WAD emerged from Marxist and dependency traditions:
- Focuses on the *relationship* between women and development processes, not just integration
- Argues that women have always been part of development — but on unequal, exploitative terms
- Blames capitalism and colonialism for women's subordination
- Criticises WID for not challenging the structures that produce inequality

**Critiques of WAD**:
- Still focuses on women in isolation — does not analyse *gender relations*
- Assumes that class is more fundamental than gender — women's oppression is secondary to capitalist exploitation
- Too structural — leaves little space for agency

### 1.3 Gender and Development (GAD)

GAD emerged in the 1980s, influenced by feminist theory:
- **Shifts from "women" to "gender"** — gender is a social relation, not a category of people
- **Analyses power** — gender relations are power relations; development reinforces or challenges them
- **Men and masculinity** — development also changes men's roles; gender analysis includes men
- **Strategic gender interests** — not just practical needs (income) but strategic interests (decision-making power, legal equality, freedom from violence)

| GAD Concept | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| **Practical gender needs** | Immediate material needs (water, income, health) |
| **Strategic gender interests** | Transforming gender relations (legal reform, political participation, ending violence) |
| **Gender mainstreaming** | Not a separate programme but integrating gender analysis into all policies |
| **Empowerment** | Women gaining power to control their own lives — not just being "integrated" |

## 2. Feminist Critiques of Development

### 2.1 Liberal Feminism

Focuses on **equal rights, equal opportunities, and legal reform**:
- Development discriminates against women — equal access to education, credit, land, jobs is the solution
- Microfinance, girls' education, and political quotas are liberal feminist strategies
- **Strength**: Achievable, measurable, works within existing systems
- **Weakness**: Does not challenge structural inequality — can be co-opted by neoliberal development

### 2.2 Socialist/Marxist Feminism

Focuses on **capitalism and patriarchy as interlocking systems**:
- Women's unpaid labour (domestic work, care) subsidises capitalism
- Development exploits women as cheap labour — RMG, domestic work, informal sector
- **Strength**: Connects gender to economic structure
- **Weakness**: Economic determinism — reduces gender to class

### 2.3 Postcolonial/Third World Feminism

Focuses on **race, colonialism, and the diversity of women's experience**:
- Western feminism cannot speak for all women (Mohanty, *Under Western Eyes*, 1984)
- Third World women are not a homogeneous category — class, caste, ethnicity, and religion intersect with gender
- Development interventions from the North often impose Western feminist assumptions
- **Strength**: Makes visible the diversity of women's experiences
- **Weakness**: Can slide into cultural relativism — justifying patriarchal practices as "cultural"

### 2.4 Intersectionality

Kimberlé Crenshaw's concept (1989) applied to development:
- Gender, class, race, ethnicity, caste, disability, religion — these are not separate systems of oppression
- They *intersect*, producing unique experiences of advantage or disadvantage
- Development policy that only addresses one dimension (e.g., "women's empowerment") may miss or even worsen other forms of inequality

## 4. Gender and Development in Practice

### 4.1 Microfinance

Microfinance is the most famous (and controversial) gender and development intervention:
| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| Reaches women excluded from formal banking | High interest rates (sometimes >30%) |
| Increases household income and consumption | Loans often controlled by husbands |
| Improves women's decision-making within the household | Adds to women's debt burden and stress |
| Bangladesh's Grameen Bank = global model | Little evidence of transformative empowerment |

### 4.2 Girls' Education

Education is consistently the most effective gender intervention (and one of the most effective development interventions overall):
- Bangladesh achieved near-parity in primary and secondary enrolment
- Female secondary school stipend programme (since 1994) — conditional cash transfer
- Results: later marriage, lower fertility, higher earnings, better child health

### 4.3 Women in the RMG Sector

| Empowerment dimension | RMG Effect |
|----------------------|------------|
| Income | Women earn wages — unprecedented economic independence |
| Household decision-making | Women have more say in household spending |
| Delayed marriage | Women marry later — RMG work delays marriage |
| Social networks | Women develop workplace friendships and networks |
| BUT — Working conditions | Long hours, low pay (though rising), harassment |
| BUT — Gendered division of labour | Women are 80% of RMG workers but mostly in low-skill, low-pay roles |
| BUT — Temporary/ precarious | Many women leave RMG after marriage — not career progression |

## 5. Critiques of Gender and Development

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Instrumentalism** | Women are targeted for development because they are "efficient" — not because gender equality is valuable in itself |
| **Neoliberal co-optation** | "Women's empowerment" has been absorbed by the World Bank and corporations — microfinance as poverty reduction without structural change |
| **Essentialism** | Women are still treated as a homogeneous group — not differentiated by class, ethnicity, age, location |
| **Focus on productive role** | Development focuses on women as workers — ignores reproductive labour (care, domestic work) |
| **De-politicisation** | Gender mainstreaming has become a technical exercise (checklists, gender audits) rather than a political struggle |

## 6. Bangladesh and Gender

### 6.1 The Bangladesh Paradox

| Against expectations | Explanation |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Bangladesh is a poor, Muslim-majority, conservative society | Yet it has achieved remarkable progress in gender indicators |
| Female employment = RMG | Extreme concentration in one sector — vulnerable and precarious |
| But: gender inequality persists | Labour force participation gap, wage gap, political representation gap, violence against women |

### 6.2 Explaining Bangladesh's Gender Progress

| Factor | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| NGO movement | BRAC, Grameen Bank, and self-driven organisations created entry points for women (credit, education, health) |
| RMG sector | Mass employment for women — 4 million jobs, mostly women |
| Female education policy | Secondary stipend programme dramatically increased girls' education |
| Demographic transition | Lower fertility = fewer children = more investment per child |
| Social change | Women's aspirations have changed — but social norms have been slower to shift |

## Summary

Gender and development thinking has evolved from WID (integrate women) through WAD (structural critique) to GAD (transform gender relations). Feminist critiques from liberal, socialist, and postcolonial perspectives enrich the analysis. In practice, microfinance, girls' education, and RMG employment have been key gender interventions in Bangladesh — each with significant achievements and equally significant limitations. The core tension remains: can "development" as currently practiced (neoliberal, growth-oriented, market-based) genuinely promote gender equality, or does it reproduce patriarchal structures in new forms?
