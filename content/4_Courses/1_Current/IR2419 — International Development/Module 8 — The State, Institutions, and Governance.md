# Module 8 — The State, Institutions, and Governance

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2419 — International Development
- **Module:** 8 — The State, Institutions, and Governance
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–7

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Explain different theoretical perspectives on the state's role in development
2. Define the "developmental state" and its features
3. Critically evaluate good governance and institutional reform
4. Analyse the relationship between state capacity and development outcomes
5. Assess Bangladesh's state capacity and developmental performance

## 1. Theoretical Perspectives on the State

### 1.1 The State in Modernization Theory

Modernization theory saw the state as:
- **Agent of transformation** — the state builds infrastructure, invests in education, creates the conditions for modernisation
- **Neutral and rational** — the state acts in the national interest
- **Catching up** — the state helps overcome traditional obstacles

### 1.2 The State in Dependency Theory

Dependency theory saw the state as:
- **Instrument of the bourgeoisie** — the state serves the interests of the core and the local elite
- **Comprador** — the state manages the dependent relationship with the core
- **Repressive** — when the poor resist, the state represses

### 1.3 The Neoliberal State

Neoliberalism (Module 4) saw the state as:
- **Minimal** — the state should provide security, property rights, and basic public goods
- **Inefficient** — the state is prone to rent-seeking and corruption
- **Market facilitator** — create the conditions for private sector growth, then get out of the way
- **"New Public Management"** — run the state like a business (performance targets, outsourcing, PPPs)

### 1.4 The Developmental State

The developmental state model was identified in East Asia (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore):

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Strong, autonomous state** | The state is insulated from private interests — can make long-term plans |
| **Elite bureaucracy** | Highly competent, meritocratic civil service (Japan's MITI, South Korea's EPB) |
| **State-led industrial policy** | The state "governs the market" — picks winners, coordinates investment |
| **Embedded autonomy** | Paradox: the state is autonomous from society but deeply connected to business (Peter Evans) |
| **Performance legitimacy** | The state's legitimacy depends on delivering economic growth |
| **Authoritarian or semi-authoritarian** | Democracy is limited — state autonomy requires limiting political competition |

## 2. The Developmental State Debate

### 2.1 Key Works

| Thinker | Work | Argument |
|---------|------|----------|
| **Chalmers Johnson** | *MITI and the Japanese Miracle* (1982) | Identified the developmental state in Japan — a "plan-rational" state |
| **Alice Amsden** | *Asia's Next Giant* (1989) | South Korea "got the prices wrong" — state intervention drove industrialisation |
| **Robert Wade** | *Governing the Market* (1990) | Taiwan: the state governed the market, not just let it work |
| **Peter Evans** | *Embedded Autonomy* (1995) | The state must be autonomous AND connected — the paradox of the developmental state |

### 2.2 Was There a "East Asian Miracle"?

The World Bank (1993) acknowledged East Asia's success but attributed it to:
- Sound macroeconomic policy (neoliberal)
- High savings and investment
- Export orientation
- Education

**Critique**: The World Bank downplayed the role of active state intervention — industrial policy, selective protection, directed credit, and state-owned banks were central to East Asia's growth.

### 2.3 Can the Developmental State Be Replicated?

| Factor | Contested? |
|--------|------------|
| Historical timing | East Asia industrialised during the Cold War — unique geopolitical conditions (US support for strategic allies, access to American markets) |
| Bureaucratic capacity | Can developing countries build competent, autonomous bureaucracies from scratch? |
| WTO constraints | Industrial policy is now more constrained by WTO rules (subsidies, local content requirements) |
| Democracy | East Asian developmental states were authoritarian — can democratic states do the same? |
| China | China is a developmental state — but it is also a unique case (large, authoritarian, late-industrialising) |

## 3. Good Governance

### 3.1 The Good Governance Agenda

The World Bank's 1989 study of Sub-Saharan Africa concluded that the problem was a "crisis of governance." This launched the "good governance" agenda, which became central to the Post-Washington Consensus.

| Governance Component | Meaning |
|----------------------|---------|
| **Rule of law** | Predictable, enforceable legal framework |
| **Accountability** | Leaders answerable to citizens |
| **Transparency** | Open government, access to information |
| **Participation** | Civil society involvement in decision-making |
| **Anti-corruption** | Strong oversight, enforcement, and sanctions |
| **Public sector reform** | Efficient, professional civil service |
| **Judicial independence** | Courts enforce contracts and check government power |

### 3.2 Critiques of the Good Governance Agenda

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Neoliberal agenda in disguise** | "Good governance" is a way of imposing Western institutional models on developing countries |
| **De-politicisation** | Governance is treated as a technical problem (rules, procedures, audits) — ignores politics and power |
| **Ahistorical** | Western countries had terrible governance during their industrialisation — corruption, patronage, slavery |
| **Conditionality failure** | Governance reforms imposed through aid conditionality rarely work — ownership matters |
| **Instrumentalisation** | Good governance is promoted because it is "good for growth" — not because it is valuable in itself |

### 3.3 Measuring Governance

The **Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI)** (Kaufmann, Kraay, Mastruzzi) — six dimensions:
1. Voice and accountability
2. Political stability
3. Government effectiveness
4. Regulatory quality
5. Rule of law
6. Control of corruption

**Critique**: These indicators are subjective (perception-based), biased toward Western models, and correlate with income — but it is unclear whether good governance causes growth or growth enables good governance.

## 4. State Capacity

### 4.1 What is State Capacity?

| Dimension | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Extractive capacity** | Ability to tax | Tax-to-GDP ratio |
| **Administrative capacity** | Ability to implement policy | Bureaucratic quality |
| **Coercive capacity** | Monopoly on violence | Police, military effectiveness |
| **Legitimacy** | Citizen belief in the state's right to rule | Trust in government |
| **Service delivery** | Ability to provide public goods | Health, education, infrastructure |

### 4.2 Why State Capacity Matters

Countries with stronger state capacity:
- Grow faster
- Respond better to crises (pandemics, natural disasters, economic shocks)
- Have lower poverty and inequality
- Are more democratic (or at least more stable)

### 4.3 Weak State Capacity

Weak or "fragile" states face overlapping challenges:
- Inability to tax reduces revenue → reduces capacity → reduces trust → reduces tax compliance
- Weak rule of law discourages investment → reduces growth → reduces tax base
- Poor service delivery reduces legitimacy → reduces cooperation → reduces security

## 5. Bangladesh: The State and Development

### 5.1 State Capacity in Bangladesh

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **Extractive capacity** | Low — tax-to-GDP ratio ~8% (one of the lowest in the world; India ~18%, Thailand ~16%) |
| **Administrative capacity** | Mixed — competent in some areas (RMG, health), weak in others (education quality, infrastructure) |
| **Service delivery** | Improving — significant gains in health, education, poverty reduction — but quality lags |
| **Legitimacy** | Moderate — trust is reasonable, but political polarisation is extreme |
| **Corruption** | Persistent — Bangladesh ranks ~147/180 in CPI — but corruption has not prevented growth |

### 5.2 Is Bangladesh a Developmental State?

| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| The state has actively promoted RMG (EPZs, bonded warehouses, trade diplomacy) | The bureaucracy is not elite, meritocratic, or autonomous |
| Infrastructure investment has increased (Padma Bridge, power, metro) | Corruption is pervasive — rent-seeking, political interference |
| Social policy (education stipends, social safety nets) has been effective | Political partisanship undermines institutional autonomy |
| The state is stable despite poverty, natural disasters, and political conflict | The state is not "developmental" in the East Asian sense — it is more a "patronage state" with developmental outcomes |

### 5.3 The Puzzle of Bangladesh

Bangladesh's development success (rapid growth, poverty reduction, HDI improvement) is puzzling because it occurred **despite weak state capacity**:
- Low tax collection
- Moderate-to-high corruption
- Weak rule of law
- Political instability

This has been called the "Bangladesh paradox." Possible explanations:
- **NGOs filled the gap**: BRAC, Grameen, and others provided services the state could not
- **Export-led growth bypassed the state**: RMG is private-sector driven — the state did not need to build industrial capacity
- **Demographic dividend**: Falling fertility created a favourable dependency ratio
- **Resilience**: Bangladesh society is highly adaptive — informal networks substitute for weak formal institutions

## 6. Contemporary Debates

### 6.1 Governance vs Growth

Does good governance cause growth, or does growth cause good governance? The empirical evidence is inconclusive:
- China grew rapidly with poor governance indicators
- Bangladesh grew with weak governance
- Many countries with good governance (Jamaica, Philippines) grew slowly
- But long-term development may require governance improvements (middle-income trap)

### 6.2 The State and the SDGs

The SDGs require states to deliver across 17 goals — requiring massive strengthening of state capacity in most developing countries:
- Universal health coverage requires a strong health system
- Quality education requires an effective school system
- Climate action requires regulatory capacity

### 6.3 Digital Governance

New technologies offer opportunities:
- Bangladesh's Digital Bangladesh (a2i) programme: digital birth registration, online land records, mobile government services
- India's Aadhaar: biometric ID enabling direct benefit transfers — reducing leakage
- But risks: surveillance, exclusion of the poor (digital divide), privacy

---

## Summary

The state is central to development — but theoretical perspectives on the state vary widely. The developmental state model (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan) demonstrated the potential of strong, autonomous, interventionist states — but it may be difficult to replicate today. The good governance agenda promoted Western institutional models through aid conditionality — with limited success. Bangladesh's development success with a weak state (low tax, corruption, patronage) presents a puzzle: growth and poverty reduction occurred despite, not because of, state capacity. This suggests that development can be driven by non-state actors (NGOs, private sector) and informal institutions — but long-term sustainability may require stronger state capacity.

### Key Definitions
- **Developmental state** — Strong, autonomous state that actively drives industrialisation
- **Embedded autonomy** — Evans: state is autonomous from society but deeply connected to business
- **Good governance** — Rule of law, accountability, transparency, participation, anti-corruption
- **State capacity** — Ability to tax, implement policy, maintain order, delivery services
- **Fragile state** — Weak state capacity across multiple dimensions
- **Extractive capacity** — The state's ability to collect revenue
- **Performance legitimacy** — Legitimacy based on delivering economic growth
