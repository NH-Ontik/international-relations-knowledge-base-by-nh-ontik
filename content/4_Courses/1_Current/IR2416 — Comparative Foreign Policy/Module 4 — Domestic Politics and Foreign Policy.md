## 1. Regime Type and Foreign Policy

### 1.1 Democracies and Foreign Policy

**Liberal/Democratic peace**: The most robust finding in CFP — democracies rarely (almost never) fight each other.

| Explanation | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| **Institutional constraints** | Elections, legislatures, judicial review limit war-making | Democratic leaders are removed if they lose costly wars |
| **Normative constraints** | Democracies resolve disputes peacefully at home, apply this abroad | Democracies share norms of compromise |
| **Audience costs** | Leaders who back down lose domestic credibility — so threats are credible | Democracies win the wars they start (selection effect) |

Democratic foreign policy characteristics:
- **More transparent** — public debate, media scrutiny, legislative oversight
- **More consistent** — institutional memory, less personalised, less erratic
- **More cooperative** — multilateralism, alliances, international law
- **More constrained** — slower decision-making, multiple veto points, coalition governments

### 1.2 Autocracies and Foreign Policy

Autocracies have fewer domestic constraints but face different pressures:

| Characteristic | Mechanism | Example |
|----------------|-----------|---------|
| **Personalised** | Leader's preferences directly translate into policy | Putin decides on Ukraine |
| **Less transparent** | Secrecy, no legislative oversight | China's South China Sea strategy |
| **More risk-acceptant** | No electoral penalty for losing wars | Saddam invaded Kuwait (1990) |
| **Regime survival driving** | Foreign policy is shaped by leader's fear of overthrow | Assad's alliance with Iran and Russia |
| **More erratic** | No institutional checks, sudden shifts | North Korea's alternating threats and negotiations |

### 1.3 Hybrid Regimes

Most regimes are neither fully democratic nor fully autocratic:
- **Competitive authoritarianism** — elections exist but are unfair (Russia under Putin, Bangladesh under Hasina before 2024)
- **Electoral authoritarianism** — elections are window-dressing for authoritarian control
- Foreign policy in hybrid regimes is unpredictable: domestic legitimacy depends on nationalist foreign policy, but institutionalisation limits extreme shifts

## 2. Public Opinion

### 2.1 The Almond-Lippmann Consensus

Early Cold War scholars (Almond, Lippmann) argued that public opinion was:
- **Unstable** — the public has no consistent foreign policy views
- **Incoherent** — opinions are not structured by ideology
- **Irrelevant** — elites lead; the public follows

This consensus dominated until the 1990s.

### 2.2 The Revisionist View

Later research showed that public opinion:
- **Is stable** at the aggregate level — collective opinion changes slowly and rationally
- **Is structured** — the public has coherent foreign policy orientations (cooperative vs assertive; internationalist vs isolationist)
- **Does constrain leaders** — leaders anticipate public reaction (the "electoral connection")
- **Reverberates** — leaders can shape opinion, but only within limits

### 2.3 Comparative Public Opinion

| Country | Public Opinion Characteristic | Foreign Policy Effect |
|---------|------------------------------|----------------------|
| **US** | Polarised by party; enduring internationalist majority | Military intervention constrained by casualties (Somalia 1993 effect) |
| **India** | Nationalist public opinion; anti-China, anti-Pakistan | Government must appear "strong" on territorial issues |
| **Japan** | Pacifist public opinion — Article 9 legacy | Government constrained on military alliances and overseas deployment |
| **Bangladesh** | Public opinion on India is a major domestic issue; China is viewed positively | Government must balance India (sceptical public) with China (positive view) |
| **Russia** | Nationalist, anti-Western; war support enforced by repression | Putin's war is domestically popular; dissent is crushed |

## 3. Interest Groups and Foreign Policy

### 3.1 Economic Interests

| Group | Mechanism | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Export-oriented industries** | Lobby for free trade and trade agreements | Walmart and textile companies shape Bangladesh's trade preferences |
| **Import-competing industries** | Lobby for protection | US steel industry lobbies for tariffs |
| **Multinational corporations** | Influence home and host government policy | Oil companies and US Middle East policy |
| **Agriculture** | Agricultural subsidies are a major point of trade negotiation | EU's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) |

### 3.2 Diaspora and Ethnic Lobbies

| Diaspora | Effect | Example |
|----------|--------|---------|
| **Jewish-American lobby** (AIPAC) | US strong support for Israel | Consistent US veto of UN resolutions critical of Israel |
| **Indian diaspora** | Stronger US-India relationship | US-India civil nuclear deal (2008) |
| **Cuban-American lobby** | US embargo on Cuba | Maintained long after it was strategically useful |
| **Bangladeshi diaspora** | Remittances = 5% of GDP; influence on Bangladesh's labour migration policy | Government protects migrant workers' interests |

### 3.3 Military-Industrial Complex

Eisenhower's (1961) warning — the military, defence industry, and politicians form an interlocking constituency for military spending:
- More relevant for great powers (US, Russia, China)
- Defence contractors lobby for weapons systems
- Military leaders advise on foreign policy
- Comparative: the military's role in foreign policy varies enormously — in Pakistan the military practically controls foreign policy; in Bangladesh the military is professional and depoliticised

## 4. The Media and Foreign Policy

### 4.1 The CNN Effect

The hypothesis that 24-hour news coverage forces governments to respond to humanitarian crises:
- **Strong version**: Media drives policy — governments act because TV shows suffering (Somalia 1992)
- **Weak version**: Media sets the agenda — governments must respond, but responses vary

### 4.2 Comparative Media Systems

| System | Foreign Policy Implications |
|--------|----------------------------|
| **Free media (US, UK, India)** | Media scrutinises government, shapes public opinion, sometimes drives policy |
| **Captured media (Russia, China)** | Media is a tool of foreign policy — frames issues for domestic and international audiences |
| **Hybrid (Bangladesh)** | Growing private media, strong state influence, social media increasingly important |

## 5. Parliaments and Foreign Policy

### 5.1 Variation in Legislative Power

| Parliament Type | Foreign Policy Role | Example |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------|
| **Strong (US Congress)** | Treaty ratification (2/3 Senate), war declaration, budget approval, oversight hearings | Congress limits presidential discretion |
| **Moderate (UK, India)** | Debate and oversight but limited constitutional power | India's Parliament debates foreign policy but rarely constrains it |
| **Weak (Bangladesh)** | Parliament is a rubber stamp — PM dominates foreign policy completely | No significant parliamentary constraint |

### 5.2 Coalition Governments

| Feature | Effect |
|---------|--------|
| **Multiple parties** | Foreign policy must satisfy coalition partners — more moderate, more consistent |
| **Junior coalition partners** | May extract foreign policy concessions for specific issues |
| **Coalition breakdown risk** | Foreign policy cannot be too divisive |

## 6. Bangladesh: Domestic Politics and Foreign Policy

### 6.1 Key Domestic Constraints

| Constraint | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| **India factor** | Domestic politics is dominated by the India question — opposition parties criticise the government for being too pro-India or insufficiently assertive |
| **China factor** | China is popular domestically (infrastructure, investment, no visa issues) — this constrains the government's ability to balance China |
| **Public opinion** | Nationalist, religious, and pro-China sentiment limits compromises with India |
| **Opposition** | The BNP criticises the Awami League's foreign policy as pro-India |
| **Elections** | Foreign policy becomes partisan — especially India policy — during election cycles |
| **Remittance dependence** | Migrant workers in the Gulf shape policy toward Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, and Malaysia |

### 6.2 Changing Domestic Landscape

| Period | Domestic Dynamic |
|--------|-----------------|
| **1972–1975** (Sheikh Mujib) | Parliamentary democracy — India-friendly, secular, pro-Soviet |
| **1975–1981** (Zia) | Military regime — anti-India, pro-China, Islamic identity |
| **1982–1990** (Ershad) | Military regime — pragmatic, balanced India and China |
| **1991–2006** (Parliamentary) | Competitive democracy — India policy is the main FP battleground |
| **2009–2024** (Hasina) | Dominant party — Hasina concentrated foreign policy authority; pro-India, economic diplomacy |

## Summary

Domestic politics is central to comparative foreign policy. Regime type (democracy, autocracy, hybrid) shapes how foreign policy is made and what constraints operate. Public opinion, interest groups, and the media vary in influence across countries. Legislatures, coalition governments, and elections create different constraint patterns. Bangladesh's foreign policy is deeply shaped by domestic politics — especially the India-China dynamic, public opinion, and the personality-driven decision-making of the Prime Minister.

### Key Definitions
- **Democratic peace** — Democracies rarely fight each other
- **Audience costs** — Leaders pay a domestic cost for backing down internationally
- **Almond-Lippmann consensus** — Public opinion is unstable, incoherent, irrelevant
- **CNN effect** — Media coverage drives government response to crises
- **Coalition constraint** — Multiple parties in government moderate foreign policy
- **Regime survival** — Foreign policy is shaped by the leader's need to stay in power
