# Module 7 — Middle Powers and Regional Powers

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2416 — Comparative Foreign Policy
- **Module:** 7 — Middle Powers and Regional Powers
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–6

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define "middle power" and identify criteria
2. Compare middle power foreign policy strategies
3. Analyse Brazil, India, and Turkey as middle powers
4. Evaluate the role of middle powers in global governance
5. Distinguish middle powers from great and small powers

---

## 1. What is a Middle Power?

### 1.1 Defining Middle Powers

The concept is contested — there is no single agreed definition:

| Approach | Criterion | Examples |
|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Positional** | Ranked by GDP, military, population | 10th–30th in aggregate power |
| **Functional** | Capacity to act in specific areas | Australia on trade; Norway on mediation; South Korea on digital governance |
| **Behavioural** | Propensity for niche diplomacy, multilateralism, bridge-building | Canada, Sweden, Brazil |

### 1.2 Key Characteristics

| Characteristic | Meaning |
|----------------|---------|
| **Not a great power** | Cannot impose preferences unilaterally on global scale |
| **Not a small power** | Has significant regional influence and some global reach |
| **Niche diplomacy** | Specialises in specific issue areas (peacekeeping, climate, trade, health) |
| **Multilateralism** | Prefers working through institutions (UN, WTO, G20) |
| **Bridge-building** | Mediates between great powers and small states |
| **Regional leadership** | Often the dominant power in its region, challenged by neighbours |

---

## 2. Middle Power Foreign Policy Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Niche diplomacy** | Focusing on specific issues where the state can make a difference | Norway on peace mediation (Oslo Accords, Sri Lanka, Colombia) |
| **Coalition-building** | Forming coalitions with other like-minded states | MIKTA (Mexico, Indonesia, Korea, Turkey, Australia) |
| **Institutional activism** | Using international institutions to advance interests | Canada on landmines (Ottawa Treaty 1997) |
| **Hedging** | Maintaining relationships with multiple great powers without choosing sides | India between US and Russia; Turkey between Russia and NATO |
| **Soft power / role model** | Projecting an attractive model of development | South Korea's cultural wave; Costa Rica's environmentalism |
| **Regional leadership** | Dominating a region to secure influence | Brazil in South America; South Africa in Southern Africa |

## 3. Case Studies

### 3.1 India

| Dimension | Indian Foreign Policy |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Position** | Rising middle power / emerging great power — 3rd largest GDP (PPP), nuclear power, 1.4 billion people |
| **Primary identity** | Strategic autonomy — non-aligned legacy, but increasingly multi-aligned |
| **Key relationships** | US (QUAD, defence partnership), Russia (historical, energy, arms), China (rivalry, border disputes, economic interdependence) |
| **Region** | Dominant power in South Asia; contested by China (Pakistan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Indian Ocean) |
| **Strategy** | Hedging between US and Russia; competing with China; leading the Global South (G20 presidency 2023) |
| **Rosenau variables** | Systemic (rising power, China threat); societal (Hindu nationalism under Modi); idiosyncratic (Modi's personal diplomacy) |

### 3.2 Brazil

| Dimension | Brazilian Foreign Policy |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Position** | Largest South American economy; regional hegemon; rising middle power |
| **Primary identity** | Autonomous, South-South cooperation; Lusophone leadership |
| **Key relationships** | US (economic but distant), China (largest trading partner), Latin America |
| **Strategy** | South-South diplomacy, BRICS, environmental leadership (Amazon), WTO activism |
| **Challenges** | Economic volatility, domestic political instability, competition with China in South America |

### 3.3 Turkey

| Dimension | Turkish Foreign Policy |
|-----------|-----------------------|
| **Position** | Regional power (Middle East, Balkans, Caucasus, Central Asia); NATO member |
| **Primary identity** | Ottoman heritage, Islamic world leadership, NATO ally, Eurasian bridge |
| **Key relationships** | Russia (energy, Syria, Ukraine mediation), US (NATO but tensions), EU (candidate but stalled), Middle East (activist) |
| **Strategy** | Assertive neo-Ottomanism under Erdoğan; military intervention in Syria, Libya, Nagorno-Karabakh; drone diplomacy |
| **Rosenau variables** | Idiosyncratic (Erdoğan's personal ambition); systemic (geopolitical location); societal (nationalism, religion) |

### 3.4 South Korea

| Dimension | South Korean Foreign Policy |
|-----------|-----------------------------|
| **Position** | 12th largest economy; global technology power; middle power in Asia |
| **Primary identity** | Global pivot state between US and China; cultural power (Hallyu) |
| **Strategy** | Alliance with US, economic interdependence with China, cultural diplomacy, middle power activism |
| **Contribution** | Green growth, digital governance, development assistance (KOICA), peacekeeping |

## 4. Middle Powers and Global Governance

### 4.1 G20

Middle powers are central to the G20:
- 19 countries + EU — middle powers are the majority
- Bloc formation within G20 (BRICS, MIKTA, ASEAN)
- Coalitions shift by issue

### 4.2 BRICS

BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) + expanded (2024: Iran, UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, Indonesia):
- Originally a middle power coalition
- Increasingly seen as a counterweight to Western dominance
- New Development Bank (NDB) as alternative to Western-led financial institutions
- Tensions within: China dominates; India and China are rivals; Russia is isolated

### 4.3 MIKTA

MIKTA (Mexico, Indonesia, Korea, Turkey, Australia):
- Cross-regional middle power coalition
- Not formal or institutionalised
- Focused on specific issues (trade, climate, UN reform)
- Example of niche diplomacy

## 5. Middle Powers and Bangladesh

### 5.1 Is Bangladesh a Middle Power?

| For | Against |
|-----|---------|
| 8th largest population | Low GDP per capita |
| Fastest-growing economy in South Asia | Still lower-middle income |
| Significant diplomacy (Rohingya, climate, UN peacekeeping) | Limited military power |
| LDC graduation shows development success | Limited structural power in global institutions |
| Active in regional organisations (SAARC, BIMSTEC) | Regional rival India dominates South Asia |

### 5.2 Bangladesh's Aspirations

Bangladesh does not yet qualify as a middle power by most definitions, but it:
- Pursues **niche diplomacy** (climate adaptation, peacekeeping, RMG governance)
- Seeks **middle power status** as a goal — "Sonar Bangla" vision
- Builds **coalitions** (Climate Vulnerable Forum, CVF; LDC group)
- **Hedges** between India, China, and the US

---

## Summary

Middle powers occupy a distinctive position: not great powers but not small ones. Their strategies include niche diplomacy, multilateral activism, coalition-building, hedging, and regional leadership. India, Brazil, Turkey, and South Korea illustrate different middle power approaches. The G20, BRICS, and MIKTA provide institutional vehicles for middle power influence. Bangladesh is emerging as a potential middle power — its rapid growth and activist diplomacy position it for a more influential role.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Middle power** — A state with significant regional influence and some global reach, but not a great power
- **Niche diplomacy** — Specialising in specific issue areas to maximise influence
- **Hedging** — Maintaining options with multiple great powers
- **Strategic autonomy** — Avoiding permanent alignment with any great power
- **MIKTA** — Cross-regional middle power coalition
- **BRICS** — Major emerging powers grouping
