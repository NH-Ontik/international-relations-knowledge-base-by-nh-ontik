# Module 10 — Contemporary Issues and the Future of CFP

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2416 — Comparative Foreign Policy
- **Module:** 10 — Contemporary Issues and the Future of CFP
- **Prerequisites:** All modules

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Analyse the impact of new technologies on foreign policy
2. Evaluate how climate change reshapes foreign policy
3. Assess the effect of rising populism on foreign policy
4. Synthesise the main debates in CFP
5. Identify future directions for comparative research

---

## 1. Digital Diplomacy

### 1.1 What is Digital Diplomacy?

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Diplomatic communication** | Social media as a tool for public diplomacy, messaging, and signalling |
| **Cyber foreign policy** | Cybersecurity, cyber conflict, norms of responsible state behaviour in cyberspace |
| **Digital economy** | Data governance, digital trade, platform regulation, technology standards |
| **Digital authoritarianism** | States using digital technology for surveillance, control, and coercion at home and abroad |

### 1.2 Comparative Digital Diplomacy

| State | Digital Diplomacy Strategy | Key Feature |
|-------|---------------------------|-------------|
| **US** | Promotes open internet, digital trade, but also sanctions and technology controls | Technology competition with China (semiconductors, AI, 5G) |
| **China** | Digital Silk Road, surveillance technology exports, data sovereignty | Promotes "Internet sovereignty" — state control of digital space; exports surveillance tech |
| **Russia** | Information warfare, disinformation, cyber operations | Weaponising digital space as part of hybrid warfare |
| **India** | Digital public infrastructure (UPI, Aadhaar), data sovereignty | Digital India as a development model and geopolitical tool |
| **Bangladesh** | Digital Bangladesh programme — e-governance, digital services | Modest but growing digital diplomacy; uses social media extensively (PM, MFA) |

### 1.3 Twitter/X Diplomacy

Social media has transformed diplomatic communication:
- **Leaders bypass traditional channels** — Trump, Modi, Erdoğan use Twitter/X to announce policy
- **Diplomatic signalling** — a tweet can be a threat, an offer, or a policy statement
- **Reach and speed** — messages reach millions instantly, cannot be retracted
- **Crisis management** — social media can escalate crises (communication is public, not private)
- **Comparison**: Democracies use social media openly; autocracies use it for propaganda and information control

## 2. Climate Change and Foreign Policy

### 2.1 Climate as a Foreign Policy Issue

Climate change is increasingly central to foreign policy across all state types:

| Foreign Policy Domain | Climate Impact |
|----------------------|----------------|
| **Security** | Climate-induced migration, resource conflict, food and water security |
| **Trade** | Carbon border adjustments, green technology competition, fossil fuel phase-out |
| **Development** | Climate finance, loss and damage, green transition support |
| **Diplomacy** | Climate summits (COPs), Paris Agreement, climate clubs |
| **Alliances** | Climate cooperation is a new axis of alignment (High Ambition Coalition, Climate Vulnerable Forum) |

### 2.2 Comparative Climate Foreign Policy

| State | Climate Strategy | Foreign Policy Implications |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------------|
| **EU** | Climate leader — Green Deal, carbon pricing, ambitious targets | Sets standards that become global norms (carbon border tax) |
| **China** | Largest emitter but also largest investor in renewables | Dual identity: developing country negotiating position + green technology superpower |
| **US** | Fluctuating — Obama (in), Trump (out), Biden (back in), political polarisation | US leadership cannot be relied upon — creates uncertainty for climate diplomacy |
| **Small island states** | Climate survival is the existential foreign policy priority | Aggressive climate diplomacy, litigation, norm entrepreneurship |
| **Bangladesh** | Climate vulnerability drives foreign policy — CVF chair, adaptation leadership | Climate diplomacy is a core pillar of foreign policy; seeks climate finance and technology |

## 3. Populism and Foreign Policy

### 3.1 The Populist Turn

Since the 2000s, populism has reshaped foreign policy in multiple countries:

| Aspect | Populist Foreign Policy Feature | Example |
|--------|--------------------------------|---------|
| **Anti-globalism** | Rejection of trade agreements, multilateralism, and international institutions | Trump (US), Brexit (UK) |
| **Nationalism** | "America First," "India First," national sovereignty above all | Modi (India), Erdoğan (Turkey) |
| **Personalism** | Leader's personality and messaging dominate; institutional processes bypassed | Orban (Hungary), Bolsonaro (Brazil) |
| **Anti-elite** | Attacks on "globalist elites," foreign policy experts, and the diplomatic establishment | Trump's attacks on the State Department |
| **Delegitimising institutions** | Attacking media, courts, and civil society — removing domestic constraints on FP | Orban, PiS (Poland) |

### 3.2 Comparative Populist Foreign Policy

| Leader | Populist Style | Foreign Policy Effect |
|--------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Trump** | "America First" — trade wars, NATO criticism, transactional diplomacy | Weakened alliances, disrupted global governance, catalysed China's rise |
| **Modi** | Hindu nationalist — strong on Pakistan, diaspora diplomacy, global stage | Assertive on Kashmir, skillfully balances US and Russia |
| **Erdoğan** | Neo-Ottoman — assertive in Middle East, Eastern Mediterranean, and beyond | Military interventions in Syria, Libya, Nagorno-Karabakh; mediates Ukraine-Russia |
| **Orban** | Illiberal — Eurosceptic, anti-immigration | Blocks EU decisions, maintains ties with Russia and China |

### 3.3 Implications for CFP

Populism challenges the assumptions of mainstream CFP:
- **Institutions no longer constrain** — populist leaders bypass foreign ministries, parliaments, and traditional processes
- **Rhetoric matters more** — social media, dramatic announcements, and norm-breaking replace quiet diplomacy
- **Continuity is not guaranteed** — populist leaders can reverse decades of policy (Trump's Iran deal withdrawal)
- **CFP must adapt**: Rosenau's idiosyncratic variable is more important than ever; the "open vs closed" distinction needs updating for hybrid populist regimes

## 4. Great Power Competition and the Emerging Order

### 4.1 US-China Rivalry

| Domain | Competition | Implication |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Trade and technology** | Tariffs, export controls (semiconductors, AI), tech decoupling | Fragmentation of the global economy; countries must choose sides or hedge |
| **Military** | South China Sea, Taiwan, Indo-Pacific alliances | Increased risk of conflict; small states pressured to choose |
| **Ideology** | Democracy vs. authoritarian capitalism | Competition for influence in the Global South |
| **Institutions** | China creates parallel institutions (AIIB, BRI) while US defends existing ones (IMF, World Bank) | Institutional competition creates options for developing countries |

### 4.2 The Multipolar Moment

| Feature | Implication for CFP |
|---------|---------------------|
| Multiple poles (US, China, Russia, India, EU) | More complexity, more coalition options, more unpredictability |
| Declining US hegemony | The "unipolar moment" is over — structural constraint changes for all states |
| Rising middle powers | More actors with independent agency |
| Regionalisation | Regional dynamics become more important than global ones |

## 5. The Future of CFP

### 5.1 Where CFP Is Going

| Direction | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Decolonising CFP** | Centring Global South perspectives, developing new frameworks |
| **Digital diplomacy** | Social media, cyber power, and technology as foreign policy tools |
| **Climate foreign policy** | Climate as a central rather than peripheral issue |
| **Non-state actors** | Cities, corporations, NGOs, and diaspora as foreign policy actors |
| **Behavioural IR** | Integrating psychology and behavioural economics more deeply |
| **Computational methods** | Large-scale data analysis, text-as-data, network analysis |

### 5.2 Enduring Questions

Despite new issues, the core questions of CFP remain:
1. What explains foreign policy? (Rosenau)
2. At what level of analysis should we explain it? (Singer)
3. How does domestic politics relate to international behaviour? (Putnam)
4. Are leaders or structures more important? (Levels of analysis)
5. Is our knowledge universal or specific to the West? (Decolonising)

### 5.3 CFP and Bangladesh

| Future Direction | Bangladesh Relevance |
|------------------|---------------------|
| **Decolonising CFP** | Bangladesh's postcolonial identity is central — studying its FP requires frameworks beyond mainstream CFP |
| **Digital diplomacy** | Bangladesh is active on social media; Digital Bangladesh is a foreign policy tool |
| **Climate foreign policy** | Climate is existential for Bangladesh — the CVF and adaptation leadership are cases for comparative study |
| **Great power hedging** | Bangladesh's strategy of balancing India, China, and the US is a test case for hedging theory |

---

## Summary

Contemporary CFP must grapple with new issues — digital diplomacy, climate change, populism, and the shifting great power order — while engaging with enduring theoretical questions about levels of analysis, leader vs structure, and the West's dominance of knowledge production. Populism challenges the assumption that institutions constrain leaders; climate change is reshaping the substance of foreign policy; and the US-China rivalry creates a new structural context for all states. For Bangladesh, this is a moment of opportunity (hedging, climate leadership) and risk (being caught between great powers). The future of CFP lies in decolonisation, methodological pluralism, and engagement with new issues.
