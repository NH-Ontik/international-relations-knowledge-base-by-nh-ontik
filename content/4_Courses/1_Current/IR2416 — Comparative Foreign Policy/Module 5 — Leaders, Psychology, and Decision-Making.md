# Module 5 — Leaders, Psychology, and Decision-Making

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Analyse how leader psychology shapes foreign policy
2. Apply operational code analysis across leaders
3. Explain prospect theory and its implications for risk-taking
4. Evaluate group decision-making dynamics
5. Compare leader effects across different political systems

## 1. Leaders Matter

### 1.1 The Return of the Leader

For much of IR history, leaders were treated as unimportant:
- **Realism**: The system determines behaviour — leaders are interchangeable
- **Structuralism**: Domestic structures and economic systems determine policy
- **Behaviouralism**: Quantitative analysis aggregates individual decisions into patterns

The end of the Cold War revived interest in leaders:
- Why did Gorbachev choose to end the Cold War peacefully? Structural accounts claimed the USSR was in decline, but history is full of declining powers that chose war
- Why did different leaders respond to the same structural pressures differently?
- Personality, beliefs, and cognitive style systematically shape foreign policy

### 1.2 When Leaders Matter Most

| Condition | Leader Effect |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Concentrated power** | Autocracies, personalist regimes — leader preferences directly translate into policy |
| **Low institutionalisation** | Weak bureaucracies, new states — fewer constraints on leaders |
| **Crisis** | Time pressure, stress, surprise — institutional processes are bypassed |
| **Novel situations** | No precedent or routine — leader must interpret the situation |
| **High-stakes decisions** | War, alliance, regime survival — leader directly engaged |

## 2. Operational Code

### 2.1 Origins

Nathan Leites (1951) studied the *Bolshevik operational code* — the beliefs that guided Soviet leaders. Alexander George (1969) systematised the concept, which has become the dominant framework for leader belief analysis.

### 2.2 The Five Questions

The operational code has two parts — philosophical (how the world is) and instrumental (how to act):

**Philosophical Beliefs**:
| Question | Meaning | Example (Putin) |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| P-1 | What is the nature of politics? Conflict or harmony? | Conflict (the West seeks to destroy Russia) |
| P-2 | What are the prospects for achieving one's fundamental values? Optimistic or pessimistic? | Pessimistic (Russia is under existential threat) |
| P-3 | Is the future predictable? | Predictable (historical forces, NATO expansion) |
| P-4 | How much control do leaders have? | High control (strong leader can shape destiny) |
| P-5 | What is the role of chance? | Low (history is deterministic) |

**Instrumental Beliefs**:
| Question | Meaning | Example (Putin) |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| I-1 | What is the best approach for achieving goals? | Decisive action, military force |
| I-2 | How should goals be pursued? Timing and tactics? | Seize opportunities; create faits accomplis |
| I-3 | How is risk calculated? | Accept high risk for high stakes |
| I-4 | What is the role of different means? | Force is primary; diplomacy is secondary |
| I-5 | What is the best way to manage conflict? | Overwhelming force to end it quickly |

### 2.3 Comparative Operational Codes

| Leader | P-1 (Nature of Politics) | I-1 (Best Approach) | Foreign Policy Implication |
|--------|-------------------------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| **Vladimir Putin** | Conflict | Decisive military action | Assertive, risk-acceptant |
| **Xi Jinping** | Conflict over time | Strategic patience, long-term planning | Assertive but incremental |
| **Joe Biden** | Cooperation possible | Multilateral diplomacy | Alliance-based, cautious |
| **Narendra Modi** | Conflict mixed with cooperation | Nationalist assertiveness | Strong on Pakistan, pragmatic on US/Russia |
| **Sheikh Hasina** | Cooperation (economic focus) | Diplomacy and economic engagement | Pro-India, pragmatic, avoids conflict |

## 3. Prospect Theory

### 3.1 The Theory

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky (1979) developed prospect theory, which challenges rational choice:

| Principle | Meaning | Foreign Policy Implication |
|-----------|---------|---------------------------|
| **Reference dependence** | Outcomes are evaluated as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms | Putin sees NATO expansion as a loss of Russia's sphere of influence |
| **Loss aversion** | Losses hurt more than equivalent gains please (2:1 ratio) | States are more sensitive to territorial loss than territorial gain |
| **Diminishing sensitivity** | The difference between 0 and 100 is more significant than between 1000 and 1100 | Small losses near the reference point matter more than large losses far from it |
| **Risk acceptance in domain of loss** | When facing losses, decision-makers accept high risks to recover them | A state that has lost territory is more likely to take military risks |
| **Risk aversion in domain of gain** | When winning, decision-makers become conservative | A state that has achieved its goals is reluctant to take further risks |

### 3.2 Applied Comparatively

| Leader | Reference Point | Domain | Predicted Behaviour | Actual Behaviour |
|--------|----------------|--------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Putin (2022)** | Russia as great power with sphere of influence | Loss (NATO expansion, Ukraine moving West) | Risk-acceptant | Invaded Ukraine |
| **Xi Jinping** | China's "century of humiliation" + return to greatness | Gain (rising, approaching peak) | Risk-averse for core goal (Taiwan) but risk-acceptant for other issues | Assertive but avoided Taiwan invasion |
| **Khrushchev (1962)** | Soviet strategic inferiority | Loss | Risk-acceptant | Placed missiles in Cuba |
| **Hasina** | Bangladesh as rising economic power | Gain (GDP growth, LDC graduation) | Risk-averse | Avoids confrontation, focuses on economic diplomacy |

## 4. Group Decision-Making

### 4.1 Groupthink

Irving Janis (1972) identified groupthink — a mode of thinking in cohesive groups that prioritises consensus over critical evaluation.

| Symptom | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Illusion of invulnerability | Bay of Pigs (1961) — Kennedy's advisors believed they could not fail |
| Collective rationalisation | Ignoring warnings that the invasion would fail |
| Belief in inherent morality | "We are the good guys" |
| Stereotyping the out-group | Enemy is evil, stupid, or weak |
| Self-censorship | Members do not raise doubts |
| Illusion of unanimity | Silence is interpreted as agreement |
| Direct pressure on dissenters | "You are not a team player" |
| Mindguards | Members protect the leader from dissenting information |

**Comparative**: Groupthink is more likely in:
- Cohesive groups (long-serving advisors, shared worldview)
- Insulated groups (no external input)
- Directive leadership (leader signals preferred outcome)
- High-stress situations

### 4.2 Polythink and Group Conflict

Juliet Kaarbo identified the opposite problem — **polythink**: fragmented groups with conflicting preferences:
- More common in coalition governments
- Produces disjointed, inconsistent policy
- Different members pursue different agendas simultaneously

### 4.3 Comparative Group Decision Structures

| System | Decision Structure | Implications |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|
| **US presidential** | National Security Council, multiple agencies, congressional input | Fragmented, competitive, multiple veto points |
| **Chinese collective** | Politburo Standing Committee (7 members), Xi dominant | Consensus within CCP, but Xi dominates |
| **Russian personalised** | Putin's inner circle (Siloviki — security services) | Highly personalised, insulated |
| **Bangladesh parliamentary** | PM dominates; foreign ministry implements | Highly centralised, Hasina makes key decisions |

## 5. Cognitive Biases

| Bias | Description | Comparative Example |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Anchoring** | Over-relying on initial information | First impressions of an adversary persist despite new evidence |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking evidence that confirms prior beliefs | Ignoring intelligence that contradicts policy |
| **Wishful thinking** | Believing something because you want it to be true | Saddam believed the US would not invade (2003) |
| **Hindsight bias** | "I knew it all along" | After the Soviet collapse, everyone claimed they predicted it |
| **Fundamental attribution error** | Attributing others' behaviour to their character, our behaviour to our situation | "Their aggression = evil; our aggression = self-defence" |
| **Overconfidence** | Overestimating the probability of success | Almost all leaders who start wars expect to win |

## 6. Comparative Leader Analysis: Bangladesh

### 6.1 Sheikh Hasina's Operating Code

| Belief | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| **P-1: Nature of politics** | Cooperation is possible through economic engagement — pragmatism over ideology |
| **P-2: Prospects for values** | Optimistic — Bangladesh can achieve middle-income status through economic diplomacy |
| **I-1: Best approach** | Diplomacy, economic integration, multilateralism |
| **I-2: Timing** | Patient, long-term (Rohingya repatriation, trade agreements) |
| **Risk orientation** | Risk-averse — prioritises stability and continuity |

### 6.2 How Hasina Has Shaped Bangladesh's FP

- **Personalised diplomacy**: Hasina has established direct personal relationships with Xi Jinping, Modi, and US leaders — foreign policy is conducted at the leader level
- **Centralised decision-making**: Critical decisions (Rohingya crisis response, border agreements) are made by the PM, not the foreign ministry
- **Continuity**: Despite domestic political turbulence, Hasina's foreign policy has been remarkably consistent since 2009

## Summary

Leaders matter in foreign policy — more than structural IR theories acknowledge. Their beliefs (operational code), risk psychology (prospect theory), and group dynamics (groupthink, polythink) systematically shape decisions. The comparative analysis of leaders enables us to explain variation in foreign policy behaviour across states and over time. In Bangladesh, Sheikh Hasina's personal dominance, pragmatic outlook, and risk-averse orientation have produced a strikingly consistent foreign policy despite a volatile domestic and regional environment.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Operational code** — A leader's fundamental political beliefs (philosophical + instrumental)
- **Prospect theory** — Decisions depend on reference point, loss aversion, and domain (gains vs losses)
- **Groupthink** — Consensus-seeking that overrides critical evaluation
- **Polythink** — Fragmented groups with conflicting preferences
- **Cognitive bias** — Systematic error in information processing
- **Loss aversion** — Losses hurt ~2× more than gains please
- **Domain of loss** — Situation where the decision-maker faces losses relative to their reference point
