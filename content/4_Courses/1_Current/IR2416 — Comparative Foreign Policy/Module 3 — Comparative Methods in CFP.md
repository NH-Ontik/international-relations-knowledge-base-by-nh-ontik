# Module 3 — Comparative Methods in Foreign Policy

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2416 — Comparative Foreign Policy
- **Module:** 3 — Comparative Methods and Research Design
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–2

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Identify and evaluate different comparative methods
2. Apply Mill's methods of agreement and difference
3. Choose appropriate case selection strategies
4. Analyse the trade-off between depth and generalisability
5. Critically assess the use of comparison in CFP

---

## 1. The Logic of Comparison

### 1.1 Why Compare?

Comparison is central to CFP because:
- **We cannot experiment** — we cannot randomly assign foreign policies to states
- **We must control for alternative explanations** — comparison imitates experimental logic
- **We seek generalisations** — comparison reveals patterns across cases
- **We test theories** — comparison determines which explanations hold in which conditions

### 1.2 The Fundamental Problem

John Stuart Mill identified the core challenge: in comparative social science, we can never hold all other variables constant. Unlike a chemistry experiment where we can control temperature, pressure, and reagents, in CFP every country is unique.

## 2. Mill's Methods

### 2.1 Method of Agreement

| Logic | If cases with different characteristics share the same outcome, the common factor may be the cause |
|-------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Case | Factor A | Factor B | Factor C | Outcome |
|------|----------|----------|----------|---------|
| India | Yes | No | Yes | Joins QUAD |
| Japan | No | Yes | Yes | Joins QUAD |
| Australia | Yes | Yes | Yes | Joins QUAD |

THE COMMON FACTOR: C (perception of China threat) — may explain why all three joined QUAD despite different domestic structures.

**Weakness**: We can never be sure the common factor is the cause — there may be other common factors we have not considered.

### 2.2 Method of Difference

| Logic | If similar cases have different outcomes, the differing factor may be the cause |
|-------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Case | Factor A | Factor B | Factor C | Outcome |
|------|----------|----------|----------|---------|
| Sweden | Yes | Yes | No | Neutral |
| Norway | Yes | Yes | Yes | NATO member |

THE DIFFERING FACTOR: C (NATO membership) — Norway borders Russia, Sweden does not.

**Weakness**: Finding genuinely similar cases is difficult — Sweden and Norway are similar but not identical.

### 2.3 Joint Method

Combines both: compare cases with the same outcome but different characteristics (agreement) AND cases with different outcomes but similar characteristics (difference). This is the strongest comparative design.

## 3. Case Selection Strategies

### 3.1 Most Similar Systems Design (MSSD)

| Strategy | Select cases that are similar on many variables but differ on the outcome |
|----------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Example | Cases | Controlled Similarities | Difference |
|---------|-------|------------------------|------------|
| Iran nuclear deal stance | Saudi Arabia vs. Qatar | Both Gulf monarchies, oil exporters, US allies, Sunni | Saudi opposed deal; Qatar supported it |
| Why compare these? | Controls for region, religion, regime type, economic structure | | Isolates the causal factor |

**Used when**: We have cases in the same region, regime type, or historical context.

### 3.2 Most Different Systems Design (MDSD)

| Strategy | Select cases that are different on many variables but share the same outcome |
|----------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Example | Cases | Differences | Same Outcome |
|---------|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Recognition of Palestine | Sweden, India, South Africa | Different regions, regime types, economic levels, alliance patterns | All recognised Palestine |

**Used when**: We want to identify a universal causal factor that operates across diverse contexts.

### 3.3 Crucial Case Studies

| Type | Logic | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Most likely case** | If the theory fails here, it fails everywhere | Testing democratic peace theory on US-UK (most likely — both democracies, allies, shared culture) |
| **Least likely case** | If the theory holds here, it holds everywhere | Testing democratic peace theory on India-Pakistan (least likely — both democracies, conflict, partition) |
| **Deviant case** | The case the theory cannot explain | China's rise challenges balance-of-power theory — China should be contained but is deeply integrated |

## 4. Quantitative Methods in CFP

### 4.1 Event Data

The CREON project pioneered quantitative event data:
- Foreign policy actions are coded into categories (conflictual, cooperative, diplomatic, economic)
- These are analysed statistically to identify patterns
- Variables from Rosenau's framework are operationalised and tested

**Strengths**:
- Large-N analysis enables generalisation
- Replicable and transparent
- Can test multiple variables simultaneously

**Weaknesses**:
- Reliability problems (what counts as a "cooperative" action?)
- Loss of context and meaning
- Event data sets are expensive and time-consuming
- Events data cannot capture inaction (the dog that did not bark)

### 4.2 Methods

| Method | Description | Use in CFP |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Regression analysis** | Tests relationships between variables | Does democracy reduce conflict? |
| **Time-series analysis** | Analyses change over time | Has China's FP become more assertive? |
| **Factor analysis** | Groups variables into underlying dimensions | Identifying dimensions of FP |
| **QCA (Qualitative Comparative Analysis)** | Boolean algebra to identify necessary/sufficient conditions | What combinations produce military intervention? |

## 5. Qualitative Methods in CFP

### 5.1 Process Tracing

Process tracing is the most common qualitative method in CFP:
- **What it does**: Traces the causal mechanism linking an independent variable to an outcome
- **How it works**: Examines evidence (documents, interviews, archives) to see whether the hypothesised mechanism operated
- **Strength**: Can test causal claims that quantitative methods cannot
- **Weakness**: Case-specific — generalisability is not automatic

| Evidence Type | What It Shows |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Smoking gun** | If present, confirms the hypothesis; if absent, does not disprove it |
| **Hoop test** | Must pass to remain plausible; passing does not confirm |
| **Straw in the wind** | Provides suggestive but not definitive evidence |
| **Doubly decisive** | Both confirms the hypothesis and rules out alternatives |

### 5.2 Comparative Case Studies

| Design | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Controlled comparison** | Two similar cases with different outcomes | Why did India (non-signatory) and Bangladesh (signatory) take different positions on the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty? |
| **Paired comparison** | Two cases carefully selected to isolate one difference | Comparing US (invaded Iraq) and UK (followed US) — alliance dynamics |
| **Diachronic comparison** | Same country over time | India's FP: non-aligned (1947–1991) vs. multi-aligned (1991–present) |

## 6. Challenges in Comparative Foreign Policy

| Challenge | Description | Mitigation |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Concept stretching** | Applying the same concept (e.g., "democracy") to very different cases loses meaning | Use precise, operationalised definitions |
| **Selection bias** | Choosing cases based on the dependent variable (selecting on the outcome) | Select on independent variables |
| **Equifinality** | Many different paths can produce the same outcome | QCA, process tracing |
| **Complex causality** | Multiple factors interact in non-additive ways | Case studies, interaction terms |
| **Taxonomy vs explanation** | Describing differences (foreign policy of India = X, of China = Y) is not explaining them | Explicit theoretical frameworks |

---

## 7. Bangladesh as a Comparative Case

Bangladesh illustrates the challenges and opportunities:

| Method | Bangladesh Application |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Single case study** | In-depth analysis of Bangladesh's foreign policy — rich but limited generalisability |
| **Comparative (MSSD)** | Compare Bangladesh and Sri Lanka (both South Asian, small, developing, post-conflict) — isolates their different foreign policy orientations |
| **Comparative (MDSD)** | Compare Bangladesh and Vietnam (different regions, different regime types, different war experiences) — both are rising export economies in contested geostrategic spaces |
| **Process tracing** | Trace how domestic politics (India/China card) shapes specific decisions (border management, trade, energy imports) |

---

## Summary

Comparative methods in CFP range from Mill's methods (agreement, difference) through case selection strategies (MSSD, MDSD, crucial cases) to quantitative (event data, regression) and qualitative (process tracing, controlled comparison) approaches. Each method has strengths and weaknesses — the key is matching method to research question. All comparative methods face challenges of concept stretching, selection bias, and equifinality. Bangladesh offers a rich comparative case for small state foreign policy.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Method of Agreement** — Common factor across different cases with same outcome
- **Method of Difference** — Differing factor across similar cases with different outcomes
- **MSSD** — Most Similar Systems Design
- **MDSD** — Most Different Systems Design
- **Process tracing** — Tracing causal mechanisms within a case
- **Event data** — Quantitative coding of foreign policy actions
- **Equifinality** — Multiple paths to same outcome
- **QCA** — Qualitative Comparative Analysis
