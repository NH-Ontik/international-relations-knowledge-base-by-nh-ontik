# Module 9 — Decolonising Comparative Foreign Policy

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2416 — Comparative Foreign Policy
- **Module:** 9 — Decolonising CFP: Critical and Postcolonial Approaches
- **Prerequisites:** All modules

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Critically evaluate the Eurocentrism of mainstream CFP
2. Explain postcolonial critiques of FPA/CFP
3. Analyse non-Western foreign policy traditions
4. Apply decolonising approaches to comparative analysis
5. Assess the implications for studying Bangladesh's foreign policy

## 1. The Eurocentrism of CFP

### 1.1 The Problem

Mainstream CFP is built on assumptions and cases from Western, industrialised states:

| Eurocentric Feature | Explanation |
|---------------------|-------------|
| **Western cases are the norm** | Most CFP theory is based on US, UK, and European foreign policy |
| **Non-Western cases are "other"** | Developing countries are studied as exceptions — they either fit the model (which they rarely do) or are found lacking |
| **Western concepts are universalised** | "Democracy," "bureaucracy," "rationality," "national interest" — all have specific Western meanings but are applied universally |
| **The state is treated as given** | CFP assumes the Western model of the state — ignores how post-colonial states were constructed by colonialism |
| **The international system is treated as neutral** | CFP ignores how the international system itself was built by and for Western powers |

### 1.2 Rosenau's Framework as Eurocentric

Rosenau's pre-theories illustrate the problem:

| Rosenau Category | Western Assumption | Postcolonial Critique |
|------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **"Developed" vs "underdeveloped"** | Linear development spectrum | These categories reflect colonial hierarchy — countries are not "behind," they have been actively underdeveloped |
| **"Open" vs "closed"** | Polity type as binary | Many postcolonial states are hybrid — nominally democratic but with authoritarian features, and this hybridity is a product of colonial institutional design |
| **Idiosyncratic variable in developing states** | Leaders matter more where institutions are weak | This can be read as "developing countries are irrational/personalistic" — ignores the colonial origins of weak institutions |
| **Systemic variable** | Treated as neutral structure | The system was created by colonialism and imperialism — it is not a neutral constraint |

## 2. Postcolonial Critiques of FPA/CFP

### 2.1 The Blind Spots of Mainstream CFP

| Blind Spot | Postcolonial Critique | Theorist |
|------------|----------------------|----------|
| **Colonialism** | The colonial experience fundamentally shapes postcolonial foreign policy — alliance choices, institutional forms, and identity | Grovogui, Nkiwane |
| **Race** | Race and racial hierarchy shape the international system and foreign policy — ignored by mainstream CFP | Vitalis, Muppidi |
| **The subaltern** | Whose foreign policy is studied? Elites speak for the state; subaltern perspectives (minorities, border communities, victims of foreign policy) are erased | Spivak |
| **Westphalian myth** | The assumption that all states are sovereign equals ignores massive power asymmetries and the fact that many states never had genuine sovereignty | Anghie |
| **Historical amnesia** | CFP focuses on the present — forgets that the current system was built through violence, slavery, and colonisation | Hobson |

### 2.2 Postcolonial FPA Scholars

| Scholar | Contribution |
|---------|--------------|
| **Siba Grovogui** | African state sovereignty is partial and conditional — post-colonial states do not have the same sovereignty as Western states |
| **Randolph Persaud** | FPA marginalises the Global South as "data" rather than as source of theory |
| **Himadeep Muppidi** | The postcolonial state's foreign policy is shaped by the tension between colonial inheritance and the desire for autonomy |
| **John Hobson** | *The Eurocentric Conception of World Politics* (2012) — IR theory is built on racial and civilisational hierarchy |
| **Caroline Thomas** | Third World foreign policy is fundamentally different because of structural inequality and dependence |

## 3. Global South Foreign Policy

### 3.1 Distinctive Features

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Postcolonial identity** | Foreign policy is partly about asserting independence and overcoming the colonial past |
| **Anti-colonial solidarity** | Support for decolonisation, national liberation movements, and South-South cooperation |
| **Non-alignment** | Not aligning with either Cold War bloc — rejecting European great power competition |
| **North-South divide** | Foreign policy is shaped by the structural inequality between North and South |
| **Developmental foreign policy** | Foreign policy is subordinated to development goals — economic diplomacy is central |
| **Regime security** | Foreign policy serves regime survival — great powers are courted for economic and political support |

### 3.2 Non-Alignment

The Non-Aligned Movement (NAM, founded 1961 in Belgrade) was a foundational expression of postcolonial foreign policy:

| NAM Principle | Meaning |
|---------------|---------|
| **Anti-colonialism** | Support for decolonisation and self-determination |
| **Anti-racism** | Opposition to apartheid and racial discrimination |
| **Peaceful coexistence** | Rejection of bloc politics and Cold War competition |
| **Non-interference** | Sovereign equality and territorial integrity |
| **Economic cooperation** | South-South cooperation as alternative to dependence on North |

**Critique**: NAM declined after the Cold War. Critics argue it was never genuinely non-aligned — many members were aligned with one bloc or the other. But the spirit of NAM continues in G77, BRICS, and the "Global South" identity.

### 3.3 Bandung Spirit

The 1955 Asian-African Conference (Bandung, Indonesia) was a landmark:
- 29 newly independent Asian and African states
- Launched the Afro-Asian movement
- "Bandung Spirit" — solidarity against colonialism, peaceful coexistence, economic cooperation
- Precursor to NAM, G77, China's "Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence"

## 4. Non-Western Foreign Policy Traditions

### 4.1 Panchsheel (Five Principles)

| Principle | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| 1. Mutual respect for sovereignty and territorial integrity |
| 2. Mutual non-aggression |
| 3. Mutual non-interference in internal affairs |
| 4. Equality and mutual benefit |
| 5. Peaceful coexistence |

Agreed between China and India (1954), signed by Myanmar, Indonesia, and others. Central to Asian diplomatic norms. Underpins China's claim to "peaceful development" — though critics argue China violates these principles in practice (South China Sea, Tibet, Xinjiang).

### 4.2 Asian Values

A 1990s debate (Singapore, Malaysia, China):
- Argued that "Asian values" (community, authority, harmony) differ from Western values (individualism, democracy, human rights)
- Used to justify authoritarian governance and limit human rights criticism
- **Critique** : "Asian values" is an elite discourse — used by authoritarian governments to defend against democracy and human rights; essentialises "Asia" as if all Asian countries share the same values

### 4.3 Ubuntu in Foreign Policy

Southern Africa's *Ubuntu* philosophy ("I am because we are"):
- Emphasises interconnectedness, humanity, and collective responsibility
- South Africa's post-apartheid foreign policy under Mandela drew on Ubuntu
- Used to justify multilateralism, peacekeeping, and conflict resolution
- **Critique** : Ubuntu has been used more rhetorically than operationally — South Africa's foreign policy under Mbeki and Zuma was less Ubuntu-inspired

## 5. Studying the Global South

### 5.1 Methodological Implications

| Mainstream Practice | Decolonised Alternative |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| Apply Western theory to non-Western cases | Develop theory FROM non-Western cases |
| Search for generalisable laws | Emphasise context, history, and particularity |
| Treat the state as a rational actor | Understand the state as a product of specific historical forces |
| Use quantitative data for comparison | Use historical and interpretive methods |
| Assume separation of foreign and domestic | Recognise that foreign and domestic are deeply entangled |
| Ignore colonial history | Centre colonialism in the analysis |

### 5.2 Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | Explanation | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Reverse orientalism** | Romanticising the "non-Western" as superior | Claiming Asian states are inherently more peaceful or community-oriented |
| **Nativism** | Only insiders can study their own states | Implies non-Bangladeshi scholars cannot study Bangladesh |
| **Essentialism** | All postcolonial states are the same | India's foreign policy is not the same as Ghana's |
| **Dismissing all Western theory** | Throwing out useful concepts because they are Western | Marxism, realism, and liberalism have all been used productively by Global South scholars |

## 6. Decolonising Bangladesh's Foreign Policy Analysis

### 6.1 Questions a Postcolonial CFP Would Ask

| Mainstream Question | Postcolonial Question |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| What explains Bangladesh's foreign policy? | How did colonialism and partition shape the possibilities of Bangladeshi foreign policy? |
| Is Bangladesh hedging between India and China? | How does Bangladesh assert sovereignty in a region dominated by India and China — two former colonised states that are now great powers? |
| What is Bangladesh's role in multilateral institutions? | How does Bangladesh navigate institutions created by and for Western powers (UN, IMF, World Bank)? |
| Does Sheikh Hasina's personality shape foreign policy? | How does postcolonial state formation and the 1971 war of independence frame the identity within which leaders operate? |

### 6.2 The 1971 War and Postcolonial Identity

Bangladesh's foreign policy cannot be understood without its postcolonial identity:
- Born from a war of independence against Pakistan — a postcolonial state that became an internal coloniser
- India's critical role in 1971 shapes the love-hate relationship with India
- The secular vs. religious identity debate (Awami League secular vs. BNP/Jamaat religious) directly shapes foreign policy orientation
- The trauma of 1971 (genocide, displacement) remains central to national identity

---

## Summary

Mainstream CFP is Eurocentric — built on Western assumptions, concepts, and cases, then applied universally. Postcolonial critiques expose the blind spots: colonialism, race, subaltern perspectives, and structural inequality. Understanding Global South foreign policy requires centring colonial history, recognising non-alignment and the Bandung tradition, and developing theory FROM the South. For Bangladesh, a postcolonial CFP would centre the 1971 war of independence, the colonial and partition legacy, the complex relationship with India, and the challenge of asserting sovereignty in an unequal world.
