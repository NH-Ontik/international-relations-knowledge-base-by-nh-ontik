# Module 8 — Small States and Developing Country Foreign Policy

## 1. Defining Small States

### 1.1 The Problem of Definition

There is no universally accepted definition:

| Approach | Criterion | Who Is "Small"? |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Population** | Usually <10–15 million | Bhutan, Fiji, Estonia, Qatar |
| **Territory** | Small land area | Singapore, Bahrain, Maldives |
| **Economic size** | Small GDP, undiversified economy | Most Pacific Islands, Caribbean states |
| **Military capacity** | Cannot independently defend territory | Most small states |
| **Vulnerability** | Susceptible to external shocks | All of the above |
| **Perception/self-identification** | Elites see their state as small | Bangladesh sees itself as a small state |

**Critical perspective**: Smallness is not an objective category — it is a relationship. A state is small in relation to others. Bangladesh is small compared to India and China but large compared to Bhutan and Maldives.

### 1.2 Rosenau's Framework for Small States

Rosenau predicted: For small, developing, closed states — **idiosyncratic variables matter most**.

| Variable | Small State Prediction |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Idiosyncratic** | High — leaders matter more in states with weak institutions |
| **Role** | Moderate — small states have fewer role options |
| **Governmental** | Low — weak bureaucracies, personalised decision-making |
| **Societal** | Low — less organised civil society, weaker media |
| **Systemic** | Very high — systemic constraints dominate; small states are system-takers, not makers |

**Key insight**: Systemic AND idiosyncratic variables are both very important for small states — creating a tension between structural constraint and leader agency.

## 2. Small State Vulnerabilities

| Vulnerability | Mechanism | Examples |
|---------------|-----------|----------|
| **Economic dependence** | One or two exports (commodities, RMG), one or two trading partners, tourism dependence | Bangladesh (RMG), Sri Lanka (tourism/remittances), Gulf states (oil) |
| **Security dependence** | Cannot defend territory independently — relies on alliances or great power guarantees | Baltic states (NATO), Singapore (US security), Gulf (US) |
| **Geographic vulnerability** | Small territory, limited strategic depth, border disputes | Singapore surrounded by Malaysia/Indonesia; Bangladesh surrounded by India |
| **Climate vulnerability** | Disproportionate impact of climate change | Maldives (sea-level rise), Bangladesh (cyclones, floods), Pacific islands |
| **Institutional weakness** | Small bureaucracies, limited diplomatic capacity | Post-conflict states, new states |
| **Policy space** | Limited room to manoeuvre — cannot defy great powers, international markets, or IFIs | During the 2022 debt crisis, Sri Lanka had no real alternatives |

## 3. Small State Foreign Policy Strategies

### 3.1 Bandwagoning

| Strategy | Aligning with the dominant power |
|----------|----------------------------------|

| When | Why | Example |
|------|-----|---------|
| The dominant power is nearby | Proximity = threat | Canada vs US |
| The dominant power is the only source of security | Dependency | Baltic states in NATO; South Korea with US |
| The dominant power offers economic benefits | Incentives | Poland in the EU; Mexico in NAFTA |

### 3.2 Hedging

| Strategy | Maintaining relations with multiple powers, avoiding alignment |
|----------|---------------------------------------------------------------|

| How | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Balancing security alignment with one power against economic relations with another | Bangladesh: security with India, economic with China |
| Joining multiple multilateral organisations | Singapore: ASEAN, UN, Commonwealth, APEC, WTO |
| Avoiding formal alliances | India: non-aligned/no formal alliance, but strategic partnerships |
| Maintaining ambiguity | Vietnam: balancing US, China, Russia |

### 3.3 Bypass Diplomacy

| Strategy | Going directly to global institutions or powerful states to bypass regional constraints |
|----------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Example | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Bangladesh takes Rohingya issue to UN, OIC, and international courts | Bypasses bilateral blockade with Myanmar |
| Caribbean states take climate change to UNFCCC and ICJ | Bypasses US resistance to climate action |
| Maldives raises sea-level rise at UNGA | Bypasses regional bodies that are slow to act |

### 3.4 Niche Diplomacy (also for small states)

| Strategy | Specialising in specific issues to gain influence disproportionate to material power |
|----------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

| Small State Niche | Example |
|-------------------|---------|
| Peacekeeping | Bangladesh is the 2nd largest contributor to UN peacekeeping |
| Climate diplomacy | Maldives, Bangladesh, Marshall Islands (High Ambition Coalition) |
| Mediation | Norway, Qatar |
| Development model | Bangladesh's poverty reduction story; Rwanda's post-conflict recovery |
| Diaspora diplomacy | Leveraging migrant communities for remittances, investment, lobbying |

### 3.5 Multilateral Activism

Small states working through international institutions:
- **One state, one vote** — the UNGA gives small states formal equality
- **Coalition-building** — AOSIS (Alliance of Small Island States), LDC group, G77
- **Norms entrepreneurs** — Small states can champion norms (landmines, climate, human rights)
- **Weakness**: Influence in formal voting rarely translates into outcomes — great powers dominate

## 4. Developing Country Foreign Policy

### 4.1 Distinctive Features

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Colonial legacy** | Borders, institutions, and identities shaped by colonialism — foreign policy is partly response to this history |
| **Weak capacity** | Limited diplomatic resources (fewer embassies, less expertise, smaller budgets) |
| **Post-colonial identity** | Non-alignment, anti-colonialism, South-South cooperation, Third World solidarity |
| **Foreign policy as domestic politics** | In post-colonial states, foreign policy is deeply tied to nation-building and regime legitimacy |
| **Aid dependence** | Foreign aid shapes policy space — donors have leverage |
| **Elite-driven** | Foreign policy is often decided by a small group — sometimes just the leader |
| **Personality-driven** | Weak institutions mean individual leaders have disproportionate influence |

### 4.2 CFP Frameworks and Developing Countries

**Critique of mainstream CFP**:
- Most CFP theories were developed to explain Western, industrialised states
- They assume bureaucratic rationality, institutional capacity, and clear foreign policy processes
- Developing countries do not fit these assumptions — their foreign policy is more personalised, less institutionalised, and shaped by colonial legacy and development constraints

**Adaptations needed**:
- More attention to **leader psychology** (idiosyncratic variable)
- More attention to **colonial history** and **post-colonial identity**
- More attention to **dependence** (aid, trade, security)
- More attention to **informal politics** (patronage, clientelism, family networks)

## 5. Bangladesh as a Small State

### 5.1 Bangladesh's Small State Characteristics

| Characteristic | Bangladesh |
|----------------|------------|
| **Population** | 175 million — not small in absolute terms, but "small" relative to India and China |
| **Economy** | $450bn GDP — growing but low per capita; RMG-dependent |
| **Military** | Professional but limited — cannot match India or China |
| **Diplomatic capacity** | Expanding but limited — foreign ministry is overstretched |
| **Vulnerability** | Climate change, India-China competition, Rohingya crisis, water sharing |

### 5.2 Bangladesh's Strategies

| Strategy | How Bangladesh Uses It |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Hedging** | Maintains good relations with India, China, and the US simultaneously |
| **Bypass diplomacy** | Uses UN, OIC, and international courts for Rohingya; raises climate issues multilaterally |
| **Niche diplomacy** | Peacekeeping, climate adaptation, development model, RMG governance |
| **Multilateral activism** | Active in SAARC, BIMSTEC, OIC, UN, CVF |
| **Economic diplomacy** | Pursues trade, investment, and remittances as primary foreign policy goals |
| **Diaspora diplomacy** | 15 million diaspora — remittances ($20bn+), political lobbying, skills transfer |

## 6. Comparative Small State Strategies

| State | Main Strategy | Rosenau Emphasis |
|-------|--------------|------------------|
| **Singapore** | Hedging + niche diplomacy (trade, finance, tech) | Systemic dominates; high bureaucratic capacity |
| **UAE** | Hedging + niche diplomacy (finance, aviation, mediation) | Systemic + idiosyncratic (leadership vision) |
| **Qatar** | Niche diplomacy (mediation, Al Jazeera, LNG) + hedging | Idiosyncratic (Emir's activist diplomacy) |
| **Sri Lanka** | Bandwagoning (with China) — then crisis | Systemic error: over-relied on China |
| **Bangladesh** | Hedging + niche diplomacy + bypass diplomacy | Systemic constraints + idiosyncratic (Hasina) |

## Summary

Small states are defined not just by size but by vulnerability, dependence, and limited policy space. Their foreign policy strategies — bandwagoning, hedging, bypass diplomacy, niche diplomacy, and multilateral activism — seek to maximise influence within severe constraints. Developing countries add further features: colonial legacy, weak capacity, personalisation, and the deep entanglement of foreign and domestic politics. Bangladesh is a prototypical small state in a difficult neighbourhood, pursuing a sophisticated hedging strategy through economic diplomacy, niche specialisation, and active multilateralism.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Small state** — A state vulnerable to external pressure due to limited capacity
- **Bandwagoning** — Aligning with the dominant power
- **Hedging** — Maintaining relations with multiple powers to avoid dependence
- **Bypass diplomacy** — Going to global institutions to bypass regional constraints
- **Niche diplomacy** — Specialising in specific issues for disproportionate influence
- **Policy space** — Room to manoeuvre — limited for small states
