# Module 1 — What is Comparative Foreign Policy?

## Course Relevance

- **Prerequisites:** IR2411 (Foreign Policy Analysis)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define CFP and distinguish it from general FPA
2. Explain Rosenau's pre-theory framework
3. Identify the five variable categories shaping foreign policy
4. Critically evaluate the universalism of Rosenau's framework
5. Apply the framework to a comparative case

## 1. What is Comparative Foreign Policy?

### 1.1 Definition

Comparative Foreign Policy (CFP) is a subfield of Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) focused on **systematic, cross-national comparison** of foreign policy behaviour. While FPA asks "how do states make foreign policy decisions?", CFP asks **"why do different states make different foreign policy decisions?"**

| FPA | CFP |
|-----|-----|
| Focuses on decision-making processes | Focuses on patterns of foreign policy behaviour |
| Often single-case or small-N | Seeks generalizable explanations across cases |
| Emphasises theory development | Emphasises theory testing and comparison |
| Can be idiographic (case-specific) | Aims for nomothetic (law-like) generalisations |

### 1.2 The Origins of CFP

CFP emerged in the late 1950s–1960s as part of the behavioural revolution in IR:
- Dissatisfaction with single-case, descriptive studies
- Desire for scientific, generalizable knowledge
- Development of quantitative data sets (e.g., CREON — Comparative Research on the Events of Nations)
- Cold War context — need to understand both superpower and non-superpower behaviour

## 2. Rosenau's "Pre-Theories" Framework (1966)

James N. Rosenau's "Pre-theories and Theories of Foreign Policy" (published in *Approaches to Comparative and International Politics*, ed. R. B. Farrell) is the foundational text of CFP.

### 2.1 The Problem Rosenau Identified

- Foreign policy analysis had no systematic theory — just single-case narratives
- Scholars used different variables for different cases — no basis for comparison
- What was needed was a **pre-theory**: a framework identifying which variables matter, and when

### 2.2 The Five Variable Categories

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|----------|------------|----------|
| **Idiosyncratic** | Individual leader characteristics | Personality, beliefs, operational code, risk tolerance, leadership style |
| **Role** | The decision-maker's official position | President vs. foreign minister vs. diplomat; constitutional powers |
| **Governmental (Bureaucratic)** | Domestic state structure and processes | Bureaucratic competition, agency rivalries, interdepartmental committees |
| **Societal** | Society-level factors | Public opinion, media, interest groups, national culture, class structure |
| **Systemic** | International system structure | Polarity (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar), alliances, international law, power distribution |

### 2.3 When Does Each Variable Matter?

Rosenau did not simply list variables — he specified the conditions under which each variable's importance changes:

| Condition | Categories | Prediction |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| **Large country vs small country** | Large: systemic and societal matter more; small: idiosyncratic and role matter more | Small states' foreign policy is more shaped by specific leaders |
| **Developed vs underdeveloped** | Developed: role and governmental matter more; underdeveloped: idiosyncratic matters more | Developing countries' FP is less institutionalised |
| **Open vs closed polity** | Open: societal matters more; closed: idiosyncratic matters more | Democracies respond to public opinion; autocracies reflect leader preferences |
| **Issue area** | Military/security: systemic matters more; economic: societal and governmental matter more | Different issues activate different variables |

### 2.4 Rosenau's Theoretical Contribution

| Insight | Significance |
|---------|--------------|
| Foreign policy is multi-causal | No single variable explains foreign policy — different variables interact |
| The relative importance of variables varies systematically | We can predict which variables will matter for which country on which issue |
| Comparison is possible | Using the same variable categories, we can compare US, India, Bhutan, and Bangladesh |

## 3. The CREON Project

The **Comparative Research on the Events of Nations (CREON)** project was the first major empirical test of CFP:
- Led by Rosenau and others in the 1970s
- Coded foreign policy events of 36 countries over a decade
- Tested hypotheses about the relative importance of different variable categories
- **Findings**: Mixed — some support for Rosenau's framework, but the systemic and idiosyncratic variables performed better than societal and governmental

**Critique**: CREON was ambitious but flawed — event data were unreliable, coding was inconsistent, and the framework was too abstract to produce clear hypotheses.

## 4. Critiques of Rosenau's Pre-Theories

### 4.1 Theoretical Critiques

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Western-centric** | Rosenau's framework assumes Western states are the norm; "developing" countries are defined by lack |
| **Overly structural** | Ignores history, colonialism, and the specific context of each country |
| **Static** | The framework is a snapshot — cannot explain foreign policy change over time |
| **Variable categories are too broad** | "Societal" covers everything from culture to class to media — not analytically useful |
| **No mechanism** | Lists variables but does not explain how they produce outcomes |
| **Assumes state as unitary actor** | Ignores the bureaucratic politics of all states |

### 4.2 Empirical Critiques

- The CREON project did not strongly confirm the framework
- Later scholars found that the framework's predictions did not hold in many cases
- Small developing states do not always have more idiosyncratic-driven policy — many have highly institutionalised foreign ministries (Bangladesh, India)
- Large democracies are not always more societal-driven — the US (supposedly open/large/developed) has a foreign policy that is surprisingly insulated from public opinion

### 4.3 Postcolonial Critique

- Rosenau's categorisation of "developed" vs "underdeveloped" is problematic — it imposes a Western development hierarchy on the world
- The framework assumes a universal political science — but states are products of specific colonial and postcolonial histories
- The "idiosyncratic" variable is applied differently to Western (individual leader) and non-Western (cultural/religious) leaders

---

## 5. The Evolution of CFP Since Rosenau

| Period | Development |
|--------|-------------|
| **1960s–70s** | Behavioural revolution — large-N quantitative studies, CREON project |
| **1980s** | Retreat from grand theory — turn to case studies and middle-range theories |
| **1990s–2000s** | Integration of FPA models (poliheuristic, two-level games, prospect theory) into comparative framework |
| **2010s–present** | Post-colonial CFP, non-Western FPA, decolonising the discipline |

---

## Summary

Rosenau's "pre-theories" framework (1966) founded CFP by providing a systematic set of variable categories (idiosyncratic, role, governmental, societal, systemic) and specifying when each matters. The framework was ambitious and influential, but it has been criticised for Western-centrism, static analysis, broad categories, and weak empirical confirmation. CFP has evolved away from grand theory toward middle-range theories, case-study comparisons, and postcolonial critiques. Rosenau's core insight — that foreign policy is multi-causal and the weight of causes varies systematically — remains foundational.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Comparative Foreign Policy** — Systematic, cross-national comparison of foreign policy behaviour
- **Pre-theory** — A framework identifying which variables matter and when, before a full theory is developed
- **Idiosyncratic variable** — Individual leader characteristics
- **Role variable** — Official position and duties
- **Governmental variable** — Domestic state structure and bureaucratic processes
- **Societal variable** — Public opinion, media, culture, interest groups
- **Systemic variable** — International system structure and power distribution
- **CREON** — Comparative Research on the Events of Nations — first large-scale CFP project

### Key Thinkers
- **James N. Rosenau** — Pre-theories (1966), turbulence theory
- **Richard C. Snyder** — Decision-making approach
- **Harold & Margaret Sprout** — Ecological framework (psycho-milieu vs operational milieu)

1. "What is Comparative Foreign Policy? How does it differ from Foreign Policy Analysis?"
2. "Outline and evaluate Rosenau's pre-theories framework."
3. "Does Rosenau's framework still help us understand foreign policy behaviour in the 21st century?"
4. "Critically evaluate the CREON project. Did it confirm or challenge Rosenau's framework?"
5. "Is CFP a Western-centric discipline? How might we decolonise it?"
