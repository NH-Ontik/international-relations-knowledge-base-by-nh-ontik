# Module 6 — Great Powers and the International System

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2416 — Comparative Foreign Policy
- **Module:** 6 — Great Powers: US, China, Russia
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–5

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define what makes a "great power"
2. Compare US, Chinese, and Russian foreign policy
3. Analyse the effect of polarity on great power behaviour
4. Explain the rise-of-China debate
5. Apply Rosenau's framework to great powers

## 1. Defining Great Powers

### 1.1 What Makes a Great Power?

| Dimension | Indicator | US | China | Russia |
|-----------|-----------|----|-------|--------|
| **Military** | Nuclear arsenal, conventional forces, global reach | Nuclear triad, 800+ bases, $850bn budget | Nuclear modernisation, regional force projection, $230bn | Nuclear superpower, Ukraine war, $110bn |
| **Economic** | GDP, trade, financial centrality | $27 trillion, reserve currency | $18 trillion, largest trading partner of 120+ countries | $2 trillion, energy exporter |
| **Political** | UNSC veto, alliances, influence | UNSC P5, NATO, QUAD, AUKUS | UNSC P5, SCO, BRI | UNSC P5, CSTO, energy leverage |
| **Soft power** | Culture, values, diplomacy | Hollywood, universities, alliances | Confucius Institutes, BRI, development model | Energy, media (RT), anti-Western appeal |

### 1.2 Rosenau's Framework Applied to Great Powers

Rosenau predicted: For large, developed, open states — **systemic and societal variables matter most**.

| Variable | US Prediction | China Prediction | Russia Prediction |
|----------|---------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Systemic** | Most important (superpower) | Increasingly important (rising power) | Important (declining power, threat perception) |
| **Societal** | Highly important (democracy, public opinion, media) | Less important (authoritarian, controlled society) | Moderately important (controlled media but nationalist public) |
| **Idiosyncratic** | Moderate (constitutional constraints but presidential power) | High (Xi's personal dominance) | Very high (Putin's personalised system) |
| **Governmental** | High (fragmented bureaucracy, legislative-executive competition) | Moderate (party-state, elite consensus) | Low (personalised, siloviki dominated) |

## 2. US Foreign Policy

### 2.1 The Unipolar Moment (1991–present)

The end of the Cold War left the US as the sole superpower:
| Characteristic | Implication |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Primacy** | Unmatched military, economic, and institutional power |
| **Liberal hegemonic strategy** | Expand NATO, promote democracy, free trade, international institutions |
| **Unilateralism after 9/11** | Afghanistan (2001), Iraq (2003) — wars of choice |
| **Retrenchment debate** | Obama's pivot to Asia, Trump's "America First", Biden's return to alliances |

### 2.2 Explaining US Foreign Policy

| Variable | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Systemic** | Unipolarity gave US freedom of action; rising China constrains it |
| **Societal** | Public opinion constrains (casualty aversion, war fatigue); elite divides on strategy |
| **Idiosyncratic** | Trump disrupted US foreign policy fundamentally — shows the leader matters |
| **Governmental** | Congress vs President is a constant tension (war powers, trade, treaties) |
| **Role** | US sees itself as "indispensable nation" — global leadership is self-imposed |

## 3. Chinese Foreign Policy

### 3.1 The Rise of China

| Period | Foreign Policy Doctrine | Strategy |
|--------|------------------------|----------|
| **1949–1978** | Revolutionary Maoist | Support for communist movements, self-reliance |
| **1978–1990s** | Deng Xiaoping's "hide and bide" | Low profile, focus on economic development |
| **2000–2012** | "Peaceful rise/development" | Economic integration, reassurance, non-confrontation |
| **2012–present** (Xi Jinping) | "Great rejuvenation," assertive | BRI, South China Sea, military modernisation, "wolf warrior" diplomacy |

### 3.2 Explaining Chinese Foreign Policy

| Variable | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Systemic** | Rising power in a US-dominated system — China seeks to revise but also to avoid confrontation |
| **Societal** | Nationalist public supports assertiveness; CCP legitimacy depends on national strength |
| **Idiosyncratic** | Xi Jinping has centralised foreign policy — his personal vision drives the shift from Dengist caution to assertiveness |
| **Governmental** | Party controls foreign policy; Ministry of Foreign Affairs implements; PLA is increasingly influential |
| **Role** | "Responsible great power" / "major country diplomacy with Chinese characteristics" |

### 3.3 Key Debates

| Debate | Positions |
|--------|-----------|
| **Revisionist or status quo?** | Mearsheimer: China will seek hegemony (offensive realism); Ikenberry: China can be integrated into liberal order |
| **Assertiveness — real or perception?** | Some scholars argue China's post-2009 assertiveness is real; others argue it is a response to US policies (pivot to Asia, TPP) |
| **Will China overtake the US?** | Economic and military trajectory suggests parity by 2035–2050; but China faces major internal challenges |

## 4. Russian Foreign Policy

### 4.1 From Superpower to Regional Power

| Period | Foreign Policy Doctrine | Strategy |
|--------|------------------------|----------|
| **1991–1999** (Yeltsin) | Pro-Western, then disillusionment | Hoped to join the West; NATO expansion created bitterness |
| **2000–2008** (Putin I) | Pragmatic, energy-driven | Economic growth from oil; cautious cooperation with West |
| **2008–2014** (Medvedev/Putin) | Assertive resistance | Georgia war (2008); Libya intervention rejected |
| **2014–2022** (Post-Crimea) | Confrontation | Crimea annexation (2014), Syria intervention (2015), hybrid warfare |
| **2022–present** (Full-scale war) | Total confrontation | Full-scale invasion of Ukraine; break with the West |

### 4.2 Explaining Russian Foreign Policy

| Variable | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Systemic** | NATO expansion, perceived Western encirclement, loss of superpower status — domain of loss |
| **Societal** | Nationalist public, state-controlled media, anti-Western consensus |
| **Idiosyncratic** | Putin's worldview — grievance, great power nostalgia, belief that the West is seeking to destroy Russia — is the single most important factor |
| **Governmental** | Siloviki (security services) dominate; no institutional constraints |
| **Role** | Russia sees itself as a sovereign great power — will not accept secondary status |

### 4.3 Comparative: US, China, Russia

| Dimension | US | China | Russia |
|-----------|----|-------|--------|
| **System position** | Status quo (declining relatively) | Rising (approaching parity) | Declining (relative to US and China) |
| **Primary instrument** | Alliances, institutions, military | Economic statecraft, infrastructure, military (regional) | Military, energy, anti-Western narrative |
| **Risk orientation** | Moderate (cautious after Iraq/Afghan) | Calculated (risk-acceptant in grey zone, risk-averse for direct confrontation) | Highly risk-acceptant (Ukraine) |
| **Domestic constraint** | High (Congress, public opinion) | Low (party control) | Very low (personalised) |
| **Ideology** | Liberal internationalism (eroding) | Socialism with Chinese characteristics, nationalism | Eurasianism, anti-Western, great power nostalgia |

## 5. Polarity and Great Power Behaviour

### 5.1 Historical Patterns

| Polarity | Period | Characteristics |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Bipolar** | 1947–1991 | US and Soviet competition; stability at centre; proxy wars at periphery |
| **Unipolar** | 1991–present (declining?) | US dominance; expansion of liberal order; US freedom of action |
| **Multipolar/emerging** | Present/emerging | US, China, Russia, India, EU — multiple poles; uncertainty |

### 5.2 The Power Transition Debate

Power transition theory (Organski 1958) argues that the most dangerous period is when a rising power approaches parity with the dominant power:

| Factor | Current US-China Dynamic |
|--------|-------------------------|
| **Satisfaction** | US (status quo) vs China (partially revisionist) |
| **Power parity** | China approaching US in GDP (PPP); military convergence slower |
| **Timing** | 2030s–2040s as the most dangerous period |
| **Comparison** | Peloponnesian War (Athens vs Sparta), WWI (Germany vs Britain), Cold War |

**Thucydides's Trap** (Allison 2017): Of 16 historical cases where a rising power challenged a dominant one, 12 ended in war.

## 6. Great Powers and Bangladesh

Bangladesh is a small state caught between US, China, and India:
- **US**: Development partner, RMG market, democracy promotion
- **China**: Infrastructure investor (BRI), military cooperation, largest trading partner
- **India**: Security partner, shared border, emotional-political connection
- **Balancing act**: Bangladesh maintains good relations with all three — this requires diplomatic skill and comes at the cost of exclusive alignment

---

## Summary

Great powers (US, China, Russia) dominate the international system and their foreign policies are the most consequential. Rosenau's framework applied to great powers shows that systemic variables are most important, but leader psychology (idiosyncratic variable) is increasingly significant in China (Xi) and Russia (Putin). The current power transition between the US and China is the defining geopolitical dynamic of the 21st century. For small states like Bangladesh, managing relations with great powers is the central foreign policy challenge.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Great power** — A state with military, economic, and political reach beyond its region
- **Unipolarity** — Single dominant power (US post-Cold War)
- **Power transition** — Rising power approaches dominant power — dangerous period
- **Thucydides's Trap** — Rising power challenging dominant power usually leads to war
- **Offensive realism** — Great powers seek hegemony because anarchy forces them to
- **Defensive realism** — Great powers seek security, not hegemony

### Key Thinkers
- **John Mearsheimer** — *Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001) — China will seek hegemony
- **Graham Allison** — *Destined for War* (2017) — Thucydides's Trap
- **A.F.K. Organski** — Power transition theory (1958)
- **Robert Gilpin** — Hegemonic war theory
- **Henry Kissinger** — Great power diplomacy
- **John Ikenberry** — Liberal international order
