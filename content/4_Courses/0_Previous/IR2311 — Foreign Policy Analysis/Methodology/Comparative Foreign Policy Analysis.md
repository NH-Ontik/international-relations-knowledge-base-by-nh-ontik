# Comparative Foreign Policy Analysis

> **Source:** Breuning, M. (2007). *Foreign Policy Analysis: A Comparative Introduction*.

## The Comparative Method in FPA

Foreign Policy Analysis studies foreign policy *comparatively* — it compares across countries, leaders, time periods, and issue areas to build general theories rather than single case histories.

## Core Concepts

**Foreign Policy** (Breuning): "The totality of a country's policies toward and interactions with the environment beyond its borders."

**Components of Foreign Policy**:
- **Options** — Range of possible choices
- **Decision** — The option chosen
- **Behaviour** — The acting out of the decision
- **Outcome** — The interactive result (depends on other actors' reactions)

## Key Methodological Questions
1. **What do we wish to explain?** Options, decisions, behaviours, or outcomes?
2. **At what level of analysis?** Individual, state, or system?
3. **Why compare?** To avoid the single-case trap, to build general theory, to test hypotheses

## The Comparative Advantage
- Avoids the "n=1" problem of single-country studies
- Tests generalizability of concepts developed in US foreign policy studies
- But: practical difficulties — record-keeping varies, classification policies differ, access is unequal across countries

## Key Methodological Distinction
"Judging foreign policy decisions by the process used is more helpful than assessing by the outcome" (Breuning). Good process does not guarantee good outcome, and vice versa. Process-oriented assessment helps us understand *why* leaders arrive at decisions.
