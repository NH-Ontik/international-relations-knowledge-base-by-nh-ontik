# IR2311 — Foreign Policy Analysis

**Semester:** 3
**Status:** ⬜ Seeded (needs lectures, tutorials, and exam prep)

---

## Course Overview

Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) is a subfield of International Relations that opens the "black box" of the state to examine **who makes foreign policy, under what constraints, and with what cognitive and psychological baggage**. Unlike traditional IR theories that treat the state as a unitary rational actor, FPA places the individual decision maker at the heart of the analysis and examines multi-level, multi-actor, and process-driven explanations of state behaviour.

---

## Learning Outcomes

1. Understand the major theoretical approaches to analysing foreign policy decision-making
2. Apply levels-of-analysis frameworks (individual, state, system) to real-world cases
3. Evaluate the role of cognitive biases, group dynamics, and domestic politics in shaping foreign policy
4. Compare decision-making models: rational actor, bounded rationality, poliheuristic, bureaucratic politics, groupthink
5. Critically assess case studies using FPA concepts

---

## Topics Covered

1. **Introduction to FPA** — Defining foreign policy; FPA as a critique of realism's black-box state; the nested game (Neack)
2. **Levels of Analysis** — Individual, state, and system; connecting levels to specific FPA explanations
3. **Rational Actor & Bounded Rationality** — Expected utility theory; Herbert Simon's cognitive misers; satisficing
4. **Poliheuristic Theory** — Two-stage decision process; noncompensatory rules; bridging rational and cognitive schools
5. **Bureaucratic Politics Model** — Allison's models of the Cuban Missile Crisis; Miles' Law; inter-agency bargaining
6. **Group Decision-Making** — Groupthink (Janis), polythink (Mintz), group polarization
7. **Prospect Theory & Risk** — Kahneman & Tversky; domain of loss; risk-acceptant vs risk-averse behaviour
8. **Cognitive Biases & Perceptions** — Cognitive consistency, evoked set, operational code, analogical reasoning, emotions
9. **Decision Units Framework** — Predominant leader vs single group vs multiple autonomous actors (Neack)
10. **The Two-Level Game** — Putnam's framework; domestic ratification constraints as international bargaining leverage
11. **Comparative Method in FPA** — Cross-national comparison; avoiding the single-case trap (see [[Comparative Foreign Policy Analysis]])
12. **FPA and Grand Strategy** — Connecting decision-making analysis to strategic outcomes

## Key Concepts

- **Levels of Analysis** — Individual, state, system (Singer; Breuning)
- **Decision-Making Models** — Rational actor, bounded rationality, poliheuristic, bureaucratic politics, group decision-making
- **Cognitive Biases** — Cognitive consistency, evoked set, operational code, analogies, images, emotions
- **Nested Game** — Leaders play domestic and international politics simultaneously (Neack)
- **Two-Level Game** — International negotiation + domestic ratification (Putnam)
- **Decision Units** — Predominant leader, single group, multiple autonomous actors (Neack)
- **Satisficing** — Choosing a "good enough" option rather than optimal (Simon)
- **Polythink** — Group fragmentation with multiple conflicting views and no consensus (Mintz)
- **Operational Code** — Philosophical and instrumental beliefs guiding leaders' decisions (George)
- **Strategic Culture** — National attitudes and beliefs about the use of force

## Assessments (Exam-Style Questions)

1. "Foreign policy decision-making is best understood as a rational process." Discuss.
2. Compare and contrast the rational actor model with the bureaucratic politics model. Which better explains the Cuban Missile Crisis?
3. Apply the levels-of-analysis framework to explain a foreign policy decision of your choice.
4. Evaluate the role of cognitive biases in foreign policy. To what extent do leaders' misperceptions cause conflict?
5. "Groupthink leads to disastrous foreign policy outcomes." Critically evaluate with reference to at least two cases.
6. Explain Putnam's two-level game framework. How do domestic constraints affect international negotiations?
7. Does poliheuristic theory successfully bridge the rational and cognitive approaches to FPA?
8. Are individuals or systemic factors more important in explaining foreign policy?
