# Deterrence

> **Source:** Baylis et al. (1975). *Contemporary Strategy: Theories and Policies*, Ch. 4 (Phil Williams); Baylis et al. (2019). *Strategy in the Contemporary World*, Ch. 12 (C. Dale Walton).

## Definition
Deterrence is the use of threats by one party to convince another party to refrain from taking some action. It is a psychological relationship: it seeks to prevent, not to compel. **Deterrence** (persuading an opponent not to act) is distinct from **compellence** (persuading an opponent to stop/undo and action) — Schelling.

## Core Logic
- **Actor A** threatens unacceptable punishment if **Actor B** takes action X
- **Actor B** believes the threat is credible → does not take action X
- Deterrence succeeds when it never has to be used

## Types of Deterrence

### By Mode
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Deterrence by Punishment** | Threaten unacceptable retaliation | Nuclear retaliation against cities |
| **Deterrence by Denial** | Make attack fail by defending | Missile defence, fortified borders |

### By Context
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **General Deterrence** | Long-term maintenance of military capability to prevent challenges |
| **Immediate Deterrence** | Short-term explicit threats in a specific crisis |
| **Extended Deterrence** | Deterring attack on an ally (NATO's Article 5; US "nuclear umbrella" over Japan, South Korea) |

## Key Requirements for Successful Deterrence
1. **Capability** — Must possess the military capacity to inflict unacceptable damage
2. **Credibility** — The threat must be believed by the opponent
3. **Communication** — The opponent must understand what action triggers what response
4. **Rational opponent** — The opponent must be able to process the threat rationally (the "rationality assumption")

## The Nuclear Deterrence Revolution

Brodie (1946): "Thus far the chief purpose of our military establishment has been to win wars. From now on its chief purpose must be to avert them. It can have almost no other useful purpose."

Key developments:
- **Massive Retaliation** (Eisenhower-Dulles) — Threaten nuclear response to any aggression
- **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)** — Both sides can survive a first strike and retaliate → neither side strikes
- **The stability-instability paradox** — Strategic nuclear stability (MAD) may paradoxically encourage lower-level conflict (proxy wars, insurgencies) because neither side wants escalate to nuclear war
- **Second-strike capability** — The ability to retaliate after absorbing a first strike (submarines, hardened silos) → makes MAD stable

## The Problem of Credibility: The Rationality of (Apparent) Irrationality

**The rationality problem**: if both sides know that a nuclear war would be catastrophic, the threat to retaliate may not be believed. Schelling's solution: make the threat credible through:
- **Commitment tactics** — "Burning bridges" to remove the option of backing down
- **Brinkmanship** — Deliberately creating a shared risk of war to pressure the other side
- **Graduated response** — Not all threats need to be nuclear; flexible response escalates step by step

## Critiques of Deterrence
- Requires **rational opponent** — what if the opponent has different values?
- **Offensive realism** (Mearsheimer): states will still compete for hegemony even under deterrence
- **Accidental war risk** — technological failures, miscommunication, false alarms
- **Counterforce targeting** — nuclear weapons aimed at enemy forces rather than cities may undermine stability (makes first strike more attractive)
- **Deterrence after the Cold War** — How does deterrence work against non-state actors or "rogue states"?
- Feminist critique ([[Feminism]]): Carol Cohn — the masculinist language of nuclear deterrence ("penetration", "missile gaps", "hardening")

## The First and Second Nuclear Ages (Walton, 2019)

- **First Nuclear Age** (1945–1991) — Bipolar, US-USSR, stable MAD, arms control regimes
- **Second Nuclear Age** (1991–present) — More nuclear powers (India, Pakistan, North Korea, Israel); less stable deterrence relationships; fewer arms control agreements
- **Third Nuclear Age?** — Possible emergence with new technologies (hypersonic weapons, autonomous systems, cyber threats to command and control)
