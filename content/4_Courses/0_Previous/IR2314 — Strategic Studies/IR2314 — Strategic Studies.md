# IR2314 — Strategic Studies

**Semester:** 3
**Status:** ⬜ Seeded (needs lectures, tutorials, and exam prep)

---

## Course Overview

Strategic Studies examines the role of military power in achieving political objectives. Rooted in the classical realist tradition, it asks how states use, threaten, and manage force in an anarchic international system. The field has evolved from a narrow focus on nuclear strategy during the Cold War to encompass limited war, irregular warfare, arms control, cyber strategy, humanitarian intervention, and non-Western strategic traditions. As Liddell Hart defined it, strategy is "the art of distributing and applying military means to fulfil the ends of policy."

---

## Learning Outcomes

1. Understand the core concepts, assumptions, and evolution of strategic studies as a field
2. Analyse deterrence theory, arms control, and the nuclear revolution in strategy
3. Evaluate contemporary strategic challenges: cyber war, irregular warfare, the second nuclear age
4. Compare Western and non-Western strategic traditions (Clausewitz, Sun Tzu, Kautilya)
5. Apply strategic thinking to historical and contemporary case studies

---

## Topics Covered

1. **What is Strategy?** — Definitions from Clausewitz, von Moltke, Liddell Hart, Osgood; the political-military relationship
2. **Classical Realist Foundations** — Pessimism about human nature, conflict as inescapable, power centrality, moral neutrality
3. **Nuclear Strategy & Deterrence** — Massive retaliation, MAD, second-strike capability, the stability-instability paradox
4. **The Nuclear Revolution** — Brodie's "absolute weapon"; Schelling on commitment, brinkmanship, and bargaining
5. **Arms Control & Disarmament** — From idealist disarmament to realist arms control; Cold War treaties; counterproliferation
6. **Limited War & Escalation** — Constraints on geography, scope, and weapons; graduated response; crisis management
7. **Irregular & Revolutionary Warfare** — Insurgency, counterinsurgency, terrorism, guerrilla warfare (Mao, Galula)
8. **Cyber Strategy & Emerging Technologies** — Cyber power, autonomous systems, hypersonic weapons, space warfare
9. **Non-Western Strategic Traditions** — Kautilya's *Arthashastra*, Sun Tzu, Islamic strategic thought, Mao's protracted war
10. **Grand Strategy & Geopolitics** — The intersection of geography, resources, and strategic planning
11. **Humanitarian Intervention & Peace Operations** — The use of force for humanitarian purposes; R2P
12. **The Future of Strategic Studies** — Broadening beyond nuclear bias; gender critique; strategic culture

## Key Concepts

- **Strategy** — The connection between military means and political ends
- **Deterrence** — Preventing action through threat; punishment vs denial; general vs immediate vs extended
- **Compellence** — Persuading an opponent to stop or undo an action (Schelling)
- **MAD (Mutually Assured Destruction)** — Both sides can survive and retaliate → neither strikes
- **Stability-Instability Paradox** — Strategic stability enables lower-level conflict
- **Second-Strike Capability** — The ability to retaliate after absorbing a first strike
- **Massive Retaliation** — Threaten nuclear response to any aggression (Eisenhower-Dulles)
- **Arms Control** — Regulation of weapons policy for stability (distinct from disarmament)
- **Counterproliferation** — Preventing acquisition by rogue states/non-state actors (post-9/11)
- **Escalation Ladder** — Herman Kahn's model of 44 rungs from crisis to spasm war
- **Strategic Culture** — National beliefs about the use of force (Lantis & Howlett)
- **Limited War** — Conflict constrained to avoid escalation to general war
- **The Saptanga Theory** — Kautilya's seven elements of state (sovereign, minister, territory, fortress, treasury, army, ally)
- **The Mandala Theory** — Kautilya's circle of states: neighbour = natural enemy, neighbour's neighbour = natural ally
- **The Shadgunya** — Kautilya's six-fold policy: peace, war, staying quiet, marching, seeking shelter, dual policy
- **Garrison State Hypothesis** — Growing influence of "specialists in violence" threatens democratic control (Lasswell)

## Assessments (Exam-Style Questions)

1. Define strategy from Clausewitz through Liddell Hart to Osgood. What does each definition emphasise?
2. "Deterrence is a psychological relationship." Critically examine the conditions for successful deterrence.
3. Does nuclear deterrence work? Evaluate with reference to the Cold War and the second nuclear age.
4. Why did disarmament fail and arms control succeed? What lessons does this hold for the 21st century?
5. Compare Kautilya's *Arthashastra* with Clausewitz's *On War*. Which offers a more complete theory of strategy?
6. Explain the stability-instability paradox. Does it apply to India-Pakistan relations?
7. Has arms control become obsolete in the 21st century? Consider cyber, hypersonic, and autonomous weapons.
8. Can strategic studies accommodate non-Western traditions, or is it inherently Eurocentric?
9. How has the nature of war changed from the nuclear age to the age of cyber and irregular warfare?
10. "The prime task of strategic doctrine is not to wage war but to avert it" (Brodie). Discuss.
