# Great Debates in IR

## Why "Great Debates"?

The "great debates" narrative is the dominant way IR scholars understand the discipline's intellectual history. Schmidt (1998) argues the narrative is partly mythologised — few actual debates occurred in the way textbooks describe — but the framework remains the primary pedagogical scaffold for teaching IR theory (Wæver, 1996: 151).

The key question: Are these "debates" real intellectual shifts, or pedagogical fictions imposed retroactively to create a coherent narrative of disciplinary progress?

## The First Great Debate: Realism vs Idealism (1930s–1940s)

### Context
- **Idealism** dominated interwar IR: Wilson's Fourteen Points (1918), League of Nations (1920), Kellogg-Briand Pact (1928)
- Faith in international law, collective security, and rational progress through institutions
- The failure of appeasement at Munich (1938) and the outbreak of WWII discredited idealism

### The Pivotal Text: Carr's *The Twenty Years' Crisis* (1939)
- E.H. Carr attacked "utopianism" — the belief that reason and morality could transcend power politics
- Argued idealists ignored the role of power, interest, and class in international relations
- But Carr was not a pure realist — he sought a synthesis between utopia and realism (Carr, 1939: 10)

### Realist Consolidation: Morgenthau's *Politics Among Nations* (1948)
- "Politics is governed by objective laws that have their roots in human nature"
- Six principles of realism: politics governed by objective laws; national interest defined as power; power is the central concept; universal moral principles do not apply to state action; no distinction between states' moral aspirations and actual behaviour; the political sphere is autonomous (Morgenthau, 1948: 4–15)
- Defined IR as the study of power politics in an anarchic system

### Framing
- "Lessons of Munich" (appeasement fails, strength deters) vs "Lessons of Versailles" (punitive peace breeds war, cooperation prevents conflict)
- The debate was real in the 1930s-40s, but simplified by later textbook writers (Schmidt, 1998: 8)

## The Second Great Debate: Traditionalism vs Scientism (1960s)

### Context
- Behavioural revolution in social science: economics, political science, and sociology embraced quantitative methods
- IR scholars debated whether the discipline should be "scientific" (positivist, empirical) or "philosophical" (normative, interpretive)

### Key Texts
- **Waltz, K. (1959). *Man, the State and War*** — Bridging text; categorised explanations of war into three "images" (individual, state, system)
- **Kaplan, A. (1964). *The Conduct of Inquiry*** — Philosophy of social science methodology
- **Bull, H. (1966). "International theory in quest of a method"** — Defended traditional/philosophical methods against positivism

### The Debate
- **Traditionalists** (Bull, Wight, Butterfield): IR is a practical discipline rooted in history, philosophy, and judgement; cannot be reduced to laws and models
- **Scientists** (Kaplan, Singer, Ransom): IR must develop testable hypotheses, quantitative models, and law-like generalisations
- **Singer (1969)** — "The Level-of-Analysis Problem in International Relations" — Exemplified the push for methodological rigour

### Outcome
- A stalemate: both sides acknowledged the value of the other's approach
- Laid the groundwork for the methodological pluralism of later decades (Wæver, 1996: 155)

## The Third Great Debate / Inter-Paradigm Debate (1980s)

### Context
- The "paradigm wars" of the 1970s-80s: Realism, Liberalism, and Marxism competed for dominance
- Waltz's *Theory of International Politics* (1979) galvanised neorealism; critics responded from multiple directions

### Key Texts
- **Keohane, R.O. (1986). *Neorealism and Its Critics*** — Defended realism while opening to institutional analysis
- **Keohane, R.O. (1984). *After Hegemony*** — Neoliberal institutionalism: cooperation is possible even under anarchy
- **Walt, S. (1987). *The Origins of Alliances*** — Balance of threat theory as a neorealist refinement
- **Wendt, A. (1987). "The agent-structure problem in international relations theory"** — Constructivism as an alternative to the paradigm wars

### The Debate
- Realism vs Liberalism: Can cooperation be sustained without hegemony? (Keohane's answer: yes)
- Realism vs Marxism: Is the international system about power or about capitalism? (Wallerstein's world-systems theory challenged both)
- Keohane (1986) framed the debate as "inter-paradigm" — each paradigm captures different dimensions of reality

### Constructivism's Emergence
- Wendt (1992) — "Anarchy is what states make of it" — Ideas, not material structure, constitute the international system
- Constructivism was not initially part of the "three paradigm" debate but emerged as a fourth way
- Finnemore & Sikkink (1998) — Norm life cycle; ideas have causal power in IR (Wendt, 1992: 396)

## The Post-Paradigm Period (1990s–present)

### Context
- End of the Cold War (1991) as a watershed: the realist paradigm had "failed" to predict the Soviet collapse
- Proliferation of new approaches: constructivism, critical theory, postcolonialism, feminism, green theory

### Constructivist Turn
- Wendt's *Social Theory of International Politics* (1999) — Constructivism became the dominant alternative to realism
- Onuf, N. (1989). *World of Our Making* — Rule-oriented constructivism
- Finnemore, M. (1996). *National Interests in International Society* — Norms and international organisations
- Constructivism "mainstreamed" ideas into IR without displacing materialist approaches

### Critical Theory, Postcolonialism, and Feminism
- **Critical Theory** (Cox, Linklater): Emancipation as the purpose of IR theory; critique of power structures
- **Postcolonialism** (Said, Spivak, Chakrabarty): Colonialism as the foundation of the modern international system; Eurocentrism in IR
- **Feminism** (Enloe, Tickner, Sylvester): Gender as a structuring force in international politics; IR as masculine
- These approaches challenged the paradigm framework itself, arguing it was too narrow and Eurocentric

### Is There a "Fourth Debate"?
- Some scholars (Hollis & Smith, 1990) argue for a fourth debate: rationalism vs reflectivism
- Others (Wæver, 1996) argue the "debate" era is over; IR has moved to a post-paradigm landscape
- The Global IR debate (Acharya, 2014) may constitute the next major intellectual shift

## The 2020s Frontiers

### The Global IR Debate
- Acharya, A. (2014). "Global International Relations and Regional Worlds" — IR must become genuinely global, not just Western IR applied globally
- Calls for including non-Western theories, histories, and experiences as equal partners
- Resistance from mainstream IR: Is "Global IR" a paradigm or a call for inclusion?

### Decolonising IR
- Curriculum reform, knowledge production, and methodological decolonisation
- [[Postcolonialism]] and [[African Political Philosophy in IR]] as foundations
- Challenge: Can IR be decolonised, or is it inherently Western?

### Quantum Approaches
- [[Quantum IR]] — Uncertainty, entanglement, and non-locality as metaphors for world politics
- Sterzl, J. (2021). "Quantum questions, quantum answers" — Quantum thinking as a methodological revolution

### AI and IR Theory
- Large language models, autonomous weapons, and AI governance as IR problems
- Whether AI is a "general purpose technology" reshaping the nature of power (Farrell & Newman, 2019: 120)

### Return of Geoeconomics
- US-China decoupling, chip wars, industrial policy revival
- Whether economics or security drives 21st-century IR — a question the "great debates" never fully resolved
