# IR Glossary

Alphabetical glossary of essential IR terminology. Each term receives a concise definition (2–4 lines) with school attribution and cross-references.

---

**Anarchy** — The absence of a central authority above sovereign states; the foundational structural condition of the international system. Core to [[Realism]] and [[Neorealism]]. (Waltz, 1979: 88)

**Appeasement** — Concession to aggressive demands to avoid conflict. Associated with Chamberlain's policy toward Nazi Germany; "lessons of Munich" shaped Cold War containment. (Morgenthau, 1948: 92)

**ASEAN Way** — Consensus-based, non-interference diplomacy among Southeast Asian states. Contrasts with Western multilateralism. (Acharya, 2001: 64)

**Bandwagoning** — Aligning with a stronger power rather than balancing against it. Rational when balancing is futile or the stronger power's goals are acceptable. (Walt, 1987: 29)

**Balance of Power** — Theory that states form coalitions to prevent any one state from achieving hegemony. Core to [[Realism]]. (Morgenthau, 1948: 161)

**Balancing** — Opposition to a rising or dominant power through alliances, armaments, or institutional counterweights. The "default" realist expectation. (Waltz, 1979: 117)

**Belief Systems** — Organised sets of ideas that guide foreign policy decision-making. Relevant to [[Foreign Policy Analysis]] and cognitive psychology approaches. (Jervis, 1976: 15)

**Bounding** — Defining the scope and limits of a research question or policy problem. Methodological term in [[Research Design for IR]].

**Buffer State** — A state situated between two rival powers, serving as a geographic and political buffer. Historical examples: Afghanistan, Poland, Thailand.

**Carrying Capacity** — The maximum population an environment can sustain. Relevant to [[Eco-Critical IR]] and environmental security studies.

**Collective Security** — An arrangement where an attack on one member is treated as an attack on all. Basis of the UN Charter (Art. 51) and NATO (Art. 5). (Bull, 1961: 79)

**Constructivism** — IR theory emphasising that social reality (norms, identities, ideas) is constructed, not given. (Wendt, 1992: 396). See [[Constructivism]].

**Containment** — US Cold War strategy of preventing Soviet expansion through alliances, deterrence, and proxy conflicts. Kennan's "Long Telegram" (1946). (Gaddis, 2005: 5)

**Conventional Deterrence** — Dissuading an adversary from attacking through the threat of conventional (non-nuclear) military retaliation. (Morgan, 2003: 11)

**Counterinsurgency (COIN)** — Military and political strategies to defeat insurgencies. Emphasises winning "hearts and minds." (Galula, 1964: 10)

**Critical Theory** — IR approach (Cox, Linklater) that questions power structures and seeks emancipation. Distinguished from "problem-solving" theory. (Cox, 1981: 128). See [[Critical Theory]].

**CSCE/Helsinki Process** — Conference on Security and Co-operation in Europe; produced the Helsinki Final Act (1975) linking human rights to security. (Dobbs, 2001: 14)

**Decolonisation** — The process of gaining independence from colonial rule. Mid-20th century transformation of the international system. (Springhall, 2001: 12). See [[Postcolonialism]].

**Defensive Realism** — Neorealist variant arguing states seek survival, not hegemony; balancing is rational, expansion is counterproductive. (Waltz, 1979: 117). See [[Neorealism]].

**Democratic Peace** — Liberal theory that democracies rarely, if ever, go to war with each other. (Doyle, 1983: 333; Russett, 1993: 42)

**Dependent Development** — Development that occurs within and is structured by the global capitalist system, reproducing dependency. (Cardoso & Faletto, 1979: 165). See [[Latin American Dependency Theory]].

**Deterrence** — Preventing an adversary from acting through the threat of unacceptable retaliation. Nuclear deterrence: massive retaliation (Kennedy era) vs mutual assured destruction (MAD). (Schelling, 1960: 11)

**Détente** — Relaxation of tensions between rival powers. US-Soviet détente in the 1970s; arms control agreements (SALT I, ABM Treaty). (Sivachev & Kolokolov, 1979: 5)

**Diplomacy** — The conduct of negotiations and communication between political entities. Classical: Berridge (2015). Public diplomacy: outreach to foreign publics.

**Dollar Hegemony** — The dominant role of the US dollar in the international monetary system. Enables US economic power through reserve currency status. (Eichengreen, 2011: 3)

**Escalation** — Increase in the intensity of conflict. Broad (scope) vs vertical (intensity) escalation. (Kahn, 1965: 27)

**Ethnic Cleansing** — Systematic forced removal of an ethnic group from a given territory. Applied to Bosnia (1990s), Myanmar (Rohingya). (Akmeşeh, 2007: 6)

**Eurocentrism** — The practice of centring European history, culture, and experience as universal. Critiqued by [[Postcolonialism]], [[African Political Philosophy in IR]], [[Chinese Strategic Thought]].

**Failed State** — A state unable to perform basic functions: monopoly on violence, providing public services, controlling territory. (Rotberg, 2003: 55)

**Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA)** — Sub-field examining how states make foreign policy decisions. Individual, group, bureaucratic, and organisational levels. ([[Foreign Policy Analysis]])

**Formal Alliance** — Treaty-based military commitment between states. NATO, SEATO, Warsaw Pact. (Walt, 1987: 17)

**Functionalist Spillover** — Neofunctionalist theory: integration in one sector creates pressure for integration in related sectors. (Haas, 1958: 113). See [[Liberalism]].

**Gatekeeper State** — A state whose primary function is controlling access to resources or territory rather than providing services. (Cooper, 2002: 24)

**Geneva Conventions** — Four treaties (1949) and protocols establishing international legal standards for humanitarian treatment in war. Core of International Humanitarian Law.

**Global Commons** — Areas beyond national jurisdiction: high seas, outer space, Antarctica, cyberspace. Governance challenges. (Buzan, 1998: 68)

**Global Governance** — The sum of organisations, policies, norms, and processes governing global affairs. Not world government. (Rosenau & Czempiel, 1992: 4)

**Global South** — Collective term for developing countries, primarily in Africa, Latin America, and Asia. Not merely geographic but political-economic. (Narayan, 2007: 15). See [[Postcolonialism]].

**Grotius** — Hugo Grotius (1583–1645): "Father of international law." *Mare Liberum* (1609), *De Jure Belli ac Pacis* (1625). Law of the sea, natural rights, just war.

**Hard Power** — Coercive power: military force and economic sanctions. Contrast with [[Soft Power]]. (Nye, 2004: 2)

**Hegemon** — A dominant state exercising systemic leadership through material and ideational means. (Organski, 1958: 364)

**Hegemonic Stability Theory** — The international system is more stable when a single hegemon provides public goods (security, open trade). (Gilpin, 1981: 18). See [[International Political Economy]].

**Hub-and-Spoke** — Alliance structure where a central power maintains bilateral alliances with multiple partners (no lateral connections). US alliance system in Asia. (Ikenberry, 2004: 12)

**Humanitarian Intervention** — Military intervention to prevent or stop mass atrocities. Contested legality: UN authorisation vs responsibility. (Bellamy, 2009: 3). See [[Responsibility to Protect]].

**Identity Politics** — Political mobilisation based on shared identity (ethnic, religious, gender, national). Constructivist and feminist IR. (Wendt, 1992: 398)

**Import Substitution** — Economic policy replacing imports with domestic manufactures. Central to [[Latin American Dependency Theory]]. (Prebisch, 1950: 12)

**Interdependence** — Mutual reliance between states; not necessarily symmetrical. Complex interdependence: Keohane & Nye (1977: 26). See [[Liberalism]].

**International Law** — Rules and norms governing relations between states and international actors. Sources: treaties, custom, general principles, judicial decisions. (Shaw, 2017: 4)

**International Regime** — Set of implicit or explicit principles, norms, rules, and decision-making procedures around which actor expectations converge in a given issue-area. (Krasner, 1982: 186)

**International Society** — States form a society bound by shared norms, rules, and institutions, not merely a system in contact. (Bull, 1977: 8). See [[English School]].

**Just War** — Criteria for morally permissible war: *jus ad bellum* (just cause, last resort, proportionality) and *jus in bello* (distinction, proportionality). (Walzer, 2006: 45)

**Liberal Internationalism** — Foreign policy doctrine combining democracy promotion, free trade, and international institutions. Wilsonian tradition. (Ikenberry, 2011: 5). See [[Liberalism]].

**Liberalism** — IR theory emphasising cooperation, institutions, democracy, and economic interdependence as paths to peace. (Keohane & Nye, 1977: 12). See [[Liberalism]].

**Lobby** — Organised group seeking to influence government policy. AIPAC (Israel), NRA (guns), fossil fuel industry. (Barnett & Finnemore, 2004: 22)

**Long Peace** — The absence of great power war since 1945. Attributed to nuclear deterrence, institutions, or economic interdependence. (Gaddis, 1986: 23)

**Middle Power** — States with significant but not decisive influence; often coalition-builders and norm entrepreneurs. Canada, Australia, South Korea, Turkey. (Cooper & Thakur, 2013: 3)

**Military-Industrial Complex** — The institutional relationship between the military, defence industry, and government. Eisenhower's warning (1961). (Perlo, 1963: 12)

**MIRV** — Multiple Independently-targetable Reentry Vehicle; warhead technology allowing a single missile to strike multiple targets. Transformed nuclear deterrence. (Sagan, 1994: 88)

**Multilateralism** — Coordination of policies among three or more states based on principles of diffuse reciprocity. (Ruggie, 1992: 571)

**Multipolarity** — A system with three or more great powers. Contrast with unipolarity (one) and bipolarity (two). (Waltz, 1979: 131)

**National Interest** — The goals and objectives a state pursues in international affairs. Debated: objective (survival) vs subjective (ideology, prestige). (Morgenthau, 1948: 5)

**Neocolonialism** — Formal independence without economic sovereignty; foreign economic control persists. (Nkrumah, 1965: 5). See [[African Political Philosophy in IR]].

**Neoconservatism** — US foreign policy doctrine advocating democracy promotion through military force. Iraq War (2003). (Halberstam, 2008: 25)

**Neoliberalism** — Economic policy emphasising free markets, privatisation, deregulation, and open trade. "Washington Consensus." (Harvey, 2005: 2)

**Neoliberalism (IR Theory)** — Neoliberal institutionalism: cooperation is possible under anarchy through institutions and regimes. (Keohane, 1984: 11). See [[Liberalism]].

**Neorealism** — Structural realism: international system structure, not human nature, drives state behaviour. (Waltz, 1979: 88). See [[Neorealism]].

**NIMBY** — "Not In My Back Yard"; opposition to locally undesirable facilities. Relevant to environmental politics and global commons governance.

**Norm Entrepreneur** — An actor (state, NGO, individual) that actively promotes new international norms. (Finnemore & Sikkink, 1998: 896)

**Nuclear Taboo** — The normative prohibition against the use of nuclear weapons since 1945. (Tannenwald, 2007: 8)

**Nuclear Triad** — Three-legged nuclear delivery system: land-based ICBMs, submarine-launched SLBMs, and strategic bombers. Ensures second-strike capability.

**Oligarchy** — Rule by a small, wealthy elite. Robert Michels' "iron law of oligarchy." Relevant to domestic politics in IR.

**Ontological Security** — Security of identity and narrative, not just physical safety. States seek stable self-identity. (Steele, 2008: 23). See [[Ontological Security Theory]].

**Offensive Realism** — States maximise relative power to achieve hegemony; the system rewards expansion. (Mearsheimer, 2001: 34). See [[Neorealism]].

**Open Door** — US policy (1899–1901) opposing colonial partition of China; advocating equal commercial access. (Ikenberry, 2011: 40)

**Operational Code** — A decision-maker's beliefs about the nature of politics and the best means of achieving goals. (George, 1969: 192)

**Pan-Africanism** — Movement for political, economic, and cultural unity of African peoples. (Nkrumah, 1963: 217). See [[African Political Philosophy in IR]].

**Pan-Arabism** — Arab nationalist ideology advocating political, economic, and cultural unity of Arab states. Nasser, Ba'ath Party. (Dawisha, 2003: 15)

**Peace Research** — Academic field studying conditions for peace; includes peace education, peace studies, and conflict resolution. (Galtung, 1996: 5)

**Peacekeeping** — Deployment of military or police personnel to maintain peace after a conflict. UN peacekeeping: Chapter VI (consent-based). (Bellamy & Williams, 2010: 3)

**Pivot** — US strategic "rebalance" to the Asia-Pacific under Obama (2011). Signalled the priority of Asia over the Middle East. (Campbell, 2016: 12)

**Polarisation** — Ideological or political division into opposing camps. Domestic polarisation affects foreign policy consensus. Relevant to [[Foreign Policy Analysis]].

**Polarity** — Distribution of power capabilities in the international system: unipolar, bipolar, multipolar. (Waltz, 1979: 131)

**Power Transition** — Theory that hegemonic war is most likely when a rising power approaches parity with the hegemon. (Organski, 1958: 364)

**Preemptive War** — Military action taken to prevent an imminent attack. Distinct from preventive war (against a future threat). Iraq 2003 as preventive, not preemptive. (Fisher, 2003: 6)

**Primacy** — Hegemonic dominance of a single state. US primacy in the unipolar moment (1990s–2000s). (Krauthammer, 1990: 23)

**Prisoner's Dilemma** — Game-theoretic model showing how rational actors may fail to cooperate even when mutual cooperation is optimal. Central to [[Rational Choice and Game Theory]]. (Axelrod, 1984: 10)

**Proxy War** — Conflict where external powers support opposing sides without direct military confrontation. Cold War: Korea, Vietnam, Angola.

**Public Diplomacy** — Government communication with foreign publics to influence opinion. Soft power tool. (Nye, 2004: 117)

**Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA)** — Set-theoretic method for cross-case comparison using Boolean algebra. (Ragin, 1987: 25). See [[Methodology in IR]].

**Quiet Diplomacy** — Discreet, behind-the-scenes negotiations to resolve disputes. Associated with South Africa's post-apartheid approach and AU mediation.

**Raison d'état** — The principle that the interests of the state justify actions that might otherwise be morally questionable. (Bull, 1977: 156)

**Regime Change** — The replacement of a government by external force. Contested legality: Kosovo 1999, Iraq 2003. (Chomsky, 2003: 15)

**Regime Type** — Classification of political systems: democracy, autocracy, hybrid. Polity IV, V-Dem as measurement frameworks.

**Relative Gains** — States concern themselves not just with absolute gains but with gains relative to others. Realist critique of neoliberalism. (Waltz, 1979: 105)

**Responsibility to Protect (R2P)** — Norm that sovereignty entails a responsibility to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity. (ICISS, 2001: 15). See [[International Law]].

**Rules-Based International Order** — Post-1945 order founded on international law, multilateral institutions, and shared norms. Contested: whose rules? (Ikenberry, 2011: 5)

**Sanctions** — Economic or political measures imposed to compel a change in behaviour. Smart sanctions target elites; broad sanctions affect populations. (Baldwin, 1985: 10)

**Satellites** — States formally independent but effectively under the control of a more powerful state. Soviet bloc: Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia.

**Security Dilemma** — Measures taken by one state to increase its security decrease the security of others, potentially triggering an arms race. (Jervis, 1976: 62). See [[Realism]].

**Securitisation** — The process of framing an issue as an existential threat to justify extraordinary measures. (Buzan, Wæver & de Wilde, 1998: 23). See [[English School]].

**Shaping** — Military and diplomatic actions to influence the strategic environment in one's favour before a crisis. US military doctrine.

**Soft Balancing** — Non-military strategies to constrain a hegemon: institutional alignment, diplomatic pressure, economic interdependence. (Lamborn, 2003: 3). See [[Realism]].

**Soft Power** — Attraction and persuasion rather than coercion: culture, political values, and foreign policy. (Nye, 2004: 5)

**Sphere of Influence** — A geographic area where a great power claims exclusive or dominant influence. Cold War: NATO vs Warsaw Pact. (Morgenthau, 1948: 165)

**State-Centric** — The assumption that states are the primary actors in international politics. Core to [[Realism]]; critiqued by [[Constructivism]], [[Postcolonialism]], [[Feminism]].

**Status Quo** — The existing distribution of power and norms. Status quo vs revisionist states. (Organski, 1958: 364)

**Structural Violence** — Indirect harm caused by social structures: poverty, inequality, discrimination. (Galtung, 1969: 171). See [[Peace Research]].

**Subsidiarity** — Decisions should be taken at the lowest competent level. EU principle; relevant to [[Global Governance]].

**Symbolic Politics** — The use of symbols, language, and performance in political communication. Relevant to constructivism and securitisation. (Wendt, 1999: 258)

**Tabula Rasa** — "Blank slate." Critiqued in colonial contexts: the idea that colonised peoples had no pre-existing culture or governance. (Locke, 1689; critiqued by Fanon, 1961)

**Tactical Nuclear Weapons** — Low-yield nuclear weapons designed for battlefield use. Controversial: blur the line between conventional and nuclear war. (Sagan, 1994: 88)

**Terra Nullius** — "Nobody's land." Legal fiction used to justify colonisation: land deemed unoccupied or uncultivated. (Banner, 2005: 12)

**Thalassocracy** — Naval or maritime power and dominance. Athenian, British, US naval hegemony. (Modelski & Thompson, 1988: 14)

**Theorising** — The process of constructing abstract explanations of international phenomena. Distinct from "theory" as a fixed body of knowledge. (Wight, 1991: 15)

**Tripwire** — A military force deployed in a forward position to signal commitment and deter aggression. NATO forces in the Baltic states.

**Unipolarity** — A system with a single dominant power. US unipolarity post-1991. (Waltz, 1979: 131; Krauthammer, 1990: 23)

**War of Attrition** — A conflict strategy aiming to exhaust the adversary's resources and will rather than achieve a decisive battlefield victory. WWI Western Front. (Hart, 1944: 18)

**War of Ideas** — Competition for ideological influence and narrative dominance. Cold War: communism vs liberal democracy. (Brandtner & Forsberg, 2007: 15)

**World-Economy** — A single division of labour extending across multiple political units. Wallerstein's world-systems theory. (Wallerstein, 1974: 15). See [[Marxism]].
