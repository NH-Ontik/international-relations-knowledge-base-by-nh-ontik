# State of the Field 2025–2026

## Overview

This note surveys the major debates, developments, and research frontiers in IR as of mid-2026. It is not exhaustive but captures the most significant intellectual and policy shifts affecting the discipline.

## The Return of Geoeconomics

### What Is Happening
- US-China technological decoupling: semiconductor export controls (CHIPS Act 2022, October 2023 controls), TikTok restrictions, entity list expansion
- Industrial policy revival in the US (IRA, CHIPS Act), EU (Green Deal Industrial Plan), and China (Made in China 2025)
- De-dollarisation efforts: BRICS payment systems, bilateral currency swap agreements, Saudi Arabia's consideration of yuan-denominated oil sales
- Trade weaponisation: sanctions as a primary tool of statecraft (Russia, Iran, North Korea)

### Key Questions
- Is the post-1945 liberal economic order collapsing, or merely evolving?
- Does economics or security drive IR in the 21st century?
- Can geoeconomic competition be managed without military conflict?
- Is "decoupling" feasible, or does interdependence make it impossible?

- Farrell, H. & Newman, A. (2019). "Weaponized interdependence." *International Security*, 44(1), 42–79.
- Farrell, H. & Newman, A. (2023). *Underground Empire: How America Weaponized the World Economy*. Henry Holt.
- Lighthizer, R. (2023). *No Trade Is Free*. Crown.
- Tooze, A. (2021). *Shutdown: How Covid Shook the World's Economy*. Viking.

## AI and International Order

### What Is Happening
- LLMs in diplomacy: AI-assisted negotiation, translation, and drafting; "AI diplomats" concept
- Autonomous weapons systems (AWS): UN discussions on lethal autonomous weapons; "meaningful human control" debate
- AI governance races: EU AI Act (2024), US executive order on AI safety (2023), China's AI governance regulations
- AI as a "general purpose technology" reshaping state power: compute as a strategic resource

### Key Questions
- Does AI constitute a revolution in military affairs (RMA)?
- Can AI governance be internationalised, or will it follow a bloc model?
- How does AI change the epistemology of IR research (computational IR)?
- Is AI a source of power, or a distribution of power?

### Key Sources
- Scharre, P. (2018). *Army of None: Autonomous Weapons and the Future of War*. W.W. Norton.
- Horowitz, M. (2018). "Artificial intelligence, international competition, and the balance of power." *Texas National Security Review*, 1(3), 36–57.

- Zwetsloot, R. & Dafoe, A. (2019). "Thinking about compute as a strategic national resource." *Georgetown Security Studies Review*, 7(2), 40–51.

## Climate and IR

### What Is Happening
- COP failures: COP28 (Dubai) produced the "UAE Consensus" but critics called it insufficient; COP29 (Baku) focused on climate finance with mixed results
- Loss and damage fund operationalised at COP28 (2023) after decades of negotiation
- ICJ advisory opinion on climate change (2024): states have legal obligations under international law to prevent climate harm
- Climate security entering mainstream IR: NATO's 2022 Strategic Concept includes climate as a "threat multiplier"

### Key Questions
- Is climate change the defining IR issue of the 21st century, or will geopolitics overshadow it?
- Can climate governance succeed without major emitters' full participation?
- How does climate change interact with existing IR theories (security dilemma, collective action, sovereignty)?
- What is the role of climate litigation (ICJ, domestic courts) in shaping state behaviour?

### Key Sources
- Burke, A. et al. (2024). "Climate change and international relations." *Annual Review of Political Science*, 27, 315–338.
- Horton, J. (2024). *Climate Change and the Limits of Law*. Oxford University Press.
- McDonald, M. (2021). "Climate change and global security." *Global Policy*, 12(1), 5–15.
- Stern, N. (2024). "The economics of climate change." *Annual Review of Economics*, 16, 1–26.

## Global South Agency

### What Is Happening
- BRICS expansion: Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, UAE, Saudi Arabia joined in 2024; Indonesia, Nigeria, others considered
- Middle power activism: Turkey, India, Brazil, South Africa asserting independent foreign policies
- De-dollarisation: trade in local currencies, BRICS payment alternatives
- "Neutrality" in the Ukraine war: most Global South states refused to condemn Russia, abstained at UN votes

### Key Questions
- Is "Global South" a useful analytical category, or does it erase diversity?
- Does BRICS represent a genuine challenge to Western-led institutions, or a forum for bilateral grievances?
- How do middle powers shape IR beyond the great power competition framework?
- Is the Ukraine war accelerating or slowing Global South agency?

### Key Sources
- Acharya, A. (2024). *The Global South and International Order*. Oxford University Press.
- Ikenberry, G. J. (2024). "The end of the liberal international order?" *International Affairs*, 100(1), 7–24.
- Mazzucato, M. & Kattel, R. (2023). "The rise of the developmental state." *Global Policy*, 14(1), 1–12.
- Nayak, M. & Ulrich, R. (2024). "The Global South and IR theory." *International Studies Review*, 26(1), 1–25.

## Decolonising IR

### What Is Happening
- Curriculum reform: UK and South African universities revising IR syllabi to include non-Western theories
- Knowledge production: emergence of journals dedicated to non-Western IR (e.g., *Chinese Journal of International Politics*, *Third World Quarterly*)
- Methodological decolonisation: participatory methods, indigenous knowledge systems, decolonial epistemologies
- Institutional efforts: ISA's Global South Caucus, APSA's diversity initiatives

### Key Questions
- Can IR be decolonised, or is the discipline inherently Western?
- Is the inclusion of non-Western theories sufficient, or does the foundational ontology of IR need to change?
- Does "decolonising" risk becoming performative without structural reform?
- How do [[African Political Philosophy in IR]], [[Chinese Strategic Thought]], [[Islamic Political Thought in IR]], and [[Latin American Dependency Theory]] relate to mainstream IR?

- Shilliam, R. (Ed.). (2010). *International Relations and Non-Western Thought*. Routledge.

## Russia-Ukraine War

### What Is Happening
- Frozen conflict dynamics: prolonged war of attrition; no clear military victory for either side
- Nuclear escalation risks: Russia's nuclear threats; tactical nuclear weapons doctrine
- European security architecture transformation: NATO expansion (Finland, Sweden), increased European defence spending
- Sanctions effectiveness debate: Russia's economy adapted; secondary sanctions debates

### Key Questions
- Has the war vindicated or undermined realist IR theory?
- How will the war reshape the European security order?
- What are the implications for nuclear deterrence theory and the non-proliferation regime?
- How does the Global South's response reshape the international order?

### Key Sources
- Gaddis, J. L. (2023). "Russia, Ukraine, and the lessons of history." *Foreign Affairs*, 102(4), 8–22.
- Kofman, M. & Lee, C. (2022). "The real Russian military: What we know." *War on the Rocks*, April 2022.
- Stent, A. (2024). *Putin's Russia and the End of the Cold War*. Yale University Press.
- Syse, H. (2024). "Nuclear deterrence in the age of hybrid warfare." *Strategic Studies Quarterly*, 18(1), 45–68.

## Israel-Palestine

### What Is Happening
- ICJ genocide case: South Africa v. Israel (2024); ICJ provisional measures ordered
- ICC arrest warrants: warrants issued for Netanyahu, Hamas leaders (2024)
- Normalisation vs occupation: Abraham Accords (2020) challenged by the war
- Settler colonialism framework: growing academic and political use of the term

### Key Questions
- Does the settler colonialism framework illuminate or obscure the conflict?
- How does the ICJ case reshape international humanitarian law?
- What is the future of the two-state solution?
- How does the conflict affect the broader Middle East regional order?

### Key Sources
- Khalidi, R. (2020). *The Hundred Years' War on Palestine*. Metropolitan Books.

## US Domestic Politics and IR

### What Is Happening
- Trump 2.0 foreign policy: "America First 2.0" — tariffs, alliance renegotiation, withdrawal from multilateral agreements
- Alliance reliability concerns: NATO, US-Japan, US-South Korea
- WTO crisis: Appellate Body still non-functional; US blocking appointments
- Congressional polarization on foreign policy: bipartisan hawkishness on China, divided on Ukraine/Israel

### Key Questions
- Does domestic polarization undermine US foreign policy consistency?
- Can alliances survive "America First"?
- Is the WTO reformable, or is the multilateral trading system fundamentally shifting?
- How do democratic backsliding and populist nationalism affect international cooperation?

## Research Frontiers

### Computational IR
- Large language models for corpus analysis, text classification, and automated coding of political events
- NLP-based event datasets: GDELT, ICEWS, TABARI
- Ethical concerns: algorithmic bias, data privacy, reproducibility
- "Can LLMs do IR theory?" — Experimental use of AI for hypothesis generation

### Experimental IR
- Survey experiments on public opinion, threat perception, and foreign policy preferences
- Lab experiments on negotiation, cooperation, and deterrence
- Critique: external validity of experimental findings in IR (Druckman, 2015)

### Neuro-IR
- Neuroscience approaches to decision-making in IR: brain imaging, psychophysiology
- Applications: threat perception, leadership cognition, group decision-making
- Emerging but contested: Is the brain relevant to IR, or is this methodological overreach?

### Post-Human IR
- Challenges the human-centred ontology of IR
- Non-human actors: animals, ecosystems, algorithms, infrastructure
- Connected to [[Eco-Critical IR]], [[Assemblage Theory and ANT]], [[Quantum IR]]

### Queer IR
- Challenges heteronormative assumptions in IR theory
- Applies queer theory to security, identity, sovereignty, and diplomacy
- Emerging field with growing institutional support (ISA Queer Theory Caucus)
