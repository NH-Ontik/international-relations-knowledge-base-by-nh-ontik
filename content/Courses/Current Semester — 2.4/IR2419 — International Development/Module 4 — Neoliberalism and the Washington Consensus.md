# Module 4 — Neoliberalism and the Washington Consensus

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2419 — International Development
- **Module:** 4 — Neoliberalism and the Washington Consensus
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–3

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define neoliberalism and the Washington Consensus
2. Explain the policy components of structural adjustment
3. Critically evaluate the impact of SAPs on developing countries
4. Analyse the shift from Washington to Post-Washington Consensus
5. Assess Bangladesh's experience with neoliberalism

---

## 1. The Rise of Neoliberalism

### 1.1 Intellectual Origins

Neoliberalism draws on:
| Thinker | Idea |
|---------|------|
| **Friedrich Hayek** | Free markets, critique of central planning; *The Road to Serfdom* (1944) |
| **Milton Friedman** | Monetary policy, free trade, small government; *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962) |
| **Chicago School** | Economic rationalism applied to all social domains |
| **Public Choice Theory** | Government failure is worse than market failure |
| **New Institutional Economics** | Institutions matter — but the right institutions are market-supporting |

### 1.2 The Crisis of the 1970s

The shift to neoliberalism was driven by crisis:
- **Oil shocks (1973, 1979)**: Developing countries borrowed heavily from Western banks recycling petrodollars
- **Stagflation**: Rising unemployment + inflation — Keynesian demand management appeared ineffective
- **Debt crisis (1982)**: Mexico defaulted — the "lost decade" for Latin America and Africa
- **Rise of Reagan/Thatcher**: Political champions of deregulation, privatisation, and fiscal conservatism

---

## 2. The Washington Consensus

John Williamson (1989) coined the term "Washington Consensus" for a set of policy reforms he believed were broadly agreed among "the Washington-based institutions" — the US Treasury, IMF, and World Bank.

### 2.1 The Ten Original Reforms

| # | Reform | Rationale |
|---|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Fiscal discipline** | High deficits cause inflation and balance-of-payments crises |
| 2 | **Reorient public expenditure** | Shift from subsidies to health, education, and infrastructure |
| 3 | **Tax reform** | Broaden base, lower marginal rates |
| 4 | **Financial liberalisation** | Market-determined interest rates, free entry for banks |
| 5 | **Competitive exchange rates** | Realistic rate to boost exports |
| 6 | **Trade liberalisation** | Replace quotas with tariffs, then reduce tariffs |
| 7 | **Foreign direct investment** | Remove barriers to entry |
| 8 | **Privatisation** | State enterprises are inefficient |
| 9 | **Deregulation** | Remove barriers to competition (except for safety/environment) |
| 10 | **Secure property rights** | Essential for investment and formal economy |

### 2.2 The Consensus as Orthodoxy

The Washington Consensus became the dominant development framework of the 1980s–1990s:
- **Structural Adjustment Programmes (SAPs)** : IMF/World Bank required these reforms as condition for loans
- **Policy leverage**: Debt crisis gave creditors enormous leverage over developing countries
- **"There is no alternative"** : Thatcher's TINA — the consensus was presented as the only viable economic model
- **Spreading beyond Washington**: The WTO (1995) locked in trade liberalisation; capital account liberalisation spread globally

## 3. Structural Adjustment in Practice

### 3.1 The SAP Package

When a country approached the IMF/World Bank for debt relief or new loans, the standard package included:

| Policy | Condition | Intended Effect | Actual Effect |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Currency devaluation** | Lower exchange rate | Boost exports | Inflation, higher import costs |
| **Removal of subsidies** | Cut food, fuel, fertiliser subsidies | Reduce fiscal deficit | Austerity protests, social suffering |
| **User fees for services** | Charge for health, education | Improve efficiency, raise revenue | Reduced access for poor |
| **Privatisation** | Sell state enterprises | Improve efficiency, raise revenue | Job losses, crony capitalism |
| **Trade liberalisation** | Reduce tariffs, remove quotas | Competition, consumer benefits | Deindustrialisation |
| **Financial liberalisation** | Deregulate interest rates | Efficient capital allocation | Instability, crises |

### 3.2 Case Studies

**Ghana**: Reduced tariffs 80% → manufacturing collapsed, cocoa smuggling fell but producer prices were controlled, growth remained low. Overland and export routes shifted unpredictably — Ghana's reform programme was one of the most closely watched in Africa, and the results remained contested.

**Zambia**: Privatised copper mines, liberalised finance, removed subsidies. Results were catastrophic — GDP fell 40% in the 1980s, poverty rose from 40% to 70%. Copper prices collapsed, and even World Bank staff later criticised the programme's design.

**Bangladesh**: Relatively gradual SAP — maintained some food subsidies (food-for-work, VGD), did not fully liberalise until the 1990s. The RMG boom was partly enabled by trade liberalisation (duty-free access to EU under GSP, bonded warehouse facilities).

**Latin America (Argentina, Mexico)**: Privatisation, trade liberalisation, and financial deregulation produced growth in the 1990s — followed by crises (Tequila crisis 1994, Argentine default 2001).

## 4. Critiques of Structural Adjustment

### 4.1 Economic Critiques

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **One-size-fits-all** | The same policy package applied to all countries — ignored specific conditions |
| **Shock therapy** | Rapid liberalisation (Russia, 1990s) destroyed industrial capacity; gradual reform (China, Vietnam) succeeded |
| **No sequencing** | Trade liberalisation before safety nets, financial liberalisation before regulation — caused crises |
| **Recessionary bias** | Fiscal discipline + high interest rates = recession regardless of specific conditions |
| **Aid dependency** | Loans required to service existing loans — created cycle of dependence |

### 4.2 Social Critiques

| Critique | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| **Poverty increased** | Sub-Saharan Africa poverty rate rose from ~40% (1980) to ~50% (2000) |
| **Inequality rose** | Gini coefficients rose in most adjusting countries |
| **Health and education suffered** | User fees reduced access; government spending cuts devastated public services |
| **Austerity protests** | "IMF riots" across Africa, Latin America, Asia (e.g., Bolivia 1985, Jordan 1989, Egypt 1977, Zambia 1986) |
| **Labour exploitation** | Labour market deregulation reduced wages, job security, and union power |

### 4.3 Political Critiques

- **Erosion of sovereignty**: IMF/World Bank controlled national economic policy — "policy space" was dramatically reduced
- **Democracy deficit**: SAPs were imposed without democratic debate; technocrats implemented them
- **Strengthened authoritarianism**: The economic crisis combined with adjustment created political instability and authoritarian responses

## 5. From Washington Consensus to Post-Washington Consensus

### 5.1 Why the Shift?

By the late 1990s, even the IFIs recognised the Washington Consensus had failed:
- **Slow growth**: Many adjusting countries grew more slowly than before
- **East Asian crisis (1997–98)**: The financial liberalisation promoted by the Consensus was a major cause
- **Rising criticism**: From civil society (Seattle WTO protests 1999), from academic economists (Stiglitz, Rodrik), from developing country governments
- **Self-criticism**: World Bank's *World Development Report 1997* and later reports acknowledged the role of states, institutions, and social policy

### 5.2 The Augmented Washington Consensus

The "second generation" reforms added:

| Addition | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Governance** | Anti-corruption, rule of law, civil service reform |
| **Institutional reform** | Effective states, strong property rights |
| **Social safety nets** | Targeted poverty programmes (PRSPs, CCTs) |
| **Poverty focus** | MDGs (2000) and later SDGs |
| **Selective state intervention** | Industrial policy, education, health |

### 5.3 Stiglitz's Critique

Joseph Stiglitz (Nobel laureate, former World Bank chief economist) argued:
- The problem was not the goal of market-friendly reforms but their **ideological implementation**
- **Privatisation without competition** creates private monopolies, not efficiency
- **Capital account liberalisation** caused crises (East Asia 1997, Argentina 2001)
- **Sequencing matters** — safety nets before liberalisation; regulation before financial opening
- The IMF's **austerity** deepened recessions — a counter-Keynesian error

### 5.4 Rodrik's Trilemma

Dani Rodrik (Harvard) argued that the global economy faces a "trilemma" — it is impossible to simultaneously maintain:
1. **Deep economic integration** (globalisation)
2. **National sovereignty** (independent policy-making)
3. **Democratic politics** (popular accountability)

You can have at most two. The Washington Consensus chose (1) + (2) = globalisation and technocratic sovereignty, sacrificing democratic accountability.

## 6. Neoliberalism and Bangladesh

### 6.1 Bangladesh's Hybrid Approach

Bangladesh never fully adopted the Washington Consensus:
| Policy | Bangladesh's Approach | Comment |
|--------|----------------------|---------|
| **Fiscal discipline** | Maintained moderate deficits ~5% of GDP | Pragmatic |
| **Trade liberalisation** | Gradual, selective — protected some industries | Partial liberalisation |
| **Privatisation** | Limited, slow — many SOEs survive | Political constraints |
| **Subsidies** | Maintained food, fertiliser, energy subsidies | Politically essential |
| **Social safety nets** | Expanded (VGD, old-age allowance, cash transfers) | Continued despite adjustment |
| **Financial liberalisation** | Gradual — state-owned banks still dominate | Partial |
| **Exchange rate** | Managed float, generally competitive | Pragmatic |

### 6.2 The RMG Success — Neoliberal or Not?

The RMG success story had neoliberal elements:
- Trade liberalisation gave duty-free access to EU
- Labour market flexibility (no minimum wage until 2006, weak unions in EPZs)
- Private sector-driven (not state-led)
- Export-oriented (the neoliberal prescription)

And non-neoliberal elements:
- The state played a critical role in infrastructure, EPZs, bonded warehouse facilities, and trade diplomacy
- The state maintained social stability through subsidies and safety nets
- Gradual, not shock — Bangladesh did not follow the full SAP

## 7. Neoliberalism Today

### 7.1 Continued Dominance

Despite its critics, neoliberalism remains the dominant framework:
- **WTO rules** lock in trade liberalisation
- **Bilateral trade agreements** often include deeper liberalisation than WTO
- **IMF programmes** still contain familiar elements (fiscal consolidation, structural reforms)
- **Washington Consensus assumptions** underpin many development policies (growth first, private sector as driver)

### 7.2 The Neoliberal-Populist Backlash

The 2010s–20s saw a backlash against neoliberalism:
- **Brexit** (2016) — rejection of EU/international integration
- **Trump** (2016) — trade wars, "America First"
- **Rise of populism** in both North and South — rejection of globalisation, elites, "Washington Consensus"
- **China's model** as alternative — state capitalism, authoritarian development
- **Post-pandemic turn**: Growing support for industrial policy, state intervention, and social protection

---

## Summary

Neoliberalism and the Washington Consensus dominated development from the 1980s through the 1990s, promoting fiscal discipline, liberalisation, privatisation, and free trade — imposed through structural adjustment. The results were controversial: some countries benefited (East Asia), but many (Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America) experienced rising poverty, inequality, and deindustrialisation. Critiques from Stiglitz, Rodrik, and others produced a "Post-Washington Consensus" that added governance, institutions, and social policy — but fundamental neoliberal assumptions persist. Bangladesh's hybrid approach — gradual liberalisation, maintained safety nets, and selective state intervention — offers an alternative development model.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Neoliberalism** — Ideology: markets are the best mechanism for allocating resources; state should be minimal
- **Washington Consensus** — Specific policy package: fiscal discipline, liberalisation, privatisation (Williamson 1989)
- **Structural Adjustment Programme (SAP)** — IMF/World Bank loan conditional on policy reforms
- **Post-Washington Consensus** — Augmented package adding governance, institutions, safety nets
- **TINA** — "There Is No Alternative" — Thatcher's slogan for neoliberalism
- **Trilemma** — Rodrik: impossible to have deep globalisation, sovereignty, and democracy simultaneously
- **Policy Space** — A government's ability to pursue policies different from neoliberal orthodoxy

### Key Thinkers
- **John Williamson** — Coined "Washington Consensus"
- **Joseph Stiglitz** — *Globalization and Its Discontents* (2002) — critical of IMF
- **Dani Rodrik** — Trilemma, critique of hyper-globalisation
- **Milton Friedman** — Intellectual godfather of neoliberalism
- **Friedrich Hayek** — *The Road to Serfdom* (1944)
- **Naomi Klein** — *The Shock Doctrine* (2007) — crisis capitalism
