## 1. Foreign Aid

### 1.1 What is Aid?

Official Development Assistance (ODA) — defined by the OECD DAC:
- **Official** — from government agencies
- **Concessional** — grant element ≥25%
- **Development purpose** — economic development and welfare

| Aid Type | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Bilateral** | Country to country (e.g., DFID, USAID, JICA) |
| **Multilateral** | Through international organisations (World Bank, UNDP, ADB) |
| **Tied aid** | Requires recipient to buy goods/services from the donor |
| **Untied aid** | No such requirement — more effective |
| **Programme aid** | Budget support, sector-wide programmes |
| **Project aid** | Specific projects (schools, roads, clinics) |
| **Technical assistance** | Expertise, training, consultants |
| **Humanitarian aid** | Emergency relief (natural disasters, conflict) |

### 1.2 The Evolution of Aid

| Era | Dominant Rationale | Critique |
|-----|-------------------|----------|
| **1950s–60s** | "Big Push" — fill the savings gap, finance investment (Rostow, Harrod-Domar) | Ignored absorptive capacity, governance, and structural barriers |
| **1970s** | Basic needs — direct poverty reduction (McNamara, ILO) | Top-down, donor-driven, ignored local priorities |
| **1980s** | Structural adjustment — policy reform (Washington Consensus) | Imposed neoliberal policies, ignored social costs |
| **1990s** | Good governance — institution building | Political conditionality — sovereignty violation |
| **2000s** | MDGs, results-based aid — measurable targets | Focused on quantifiable goals, ignored structural transformation |
| **2010s–2020s** | SDGs, climate finance, blended finance | Proliferation of actors, fragmentation, accountability gaps |

### 1.3 The Aid Effectiveness Debate

| Argument | Pro-aid | Anti-aid |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **Does aid work?** | Yes — health (vaccination, HIV), education (enrolment), disasters (relief) | No — 50+ years of aid has not produced development in Sub-Saharan Africa |
| **What explains success?** | Good policies, strong institutions, country ownership (Burnside & Dollar 2000) | Even with good policies, aid does not reliably produce growth (Rajan & Subramanian 2008) |
| **Does aid cause dependency?** | Conditionality and selectivity can prevent it | Yes — "aid trap" (Moyo 2009), Dutch disease, fungibility |
| **Does aid undermine democracy?** | Aid supports civil society and elections | Aid strengthens autocrats, removes accountability (Bueno de Mesquita & Smith) |
| **Micro vs macro** | Aid works at micro level (specific projects) — the problem is how we measure | Micro successes do not add up to macro transformation |

### 1.4 Key Critics

| Thinker | Critique | Solution |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **William Easterly** | *The White Man's Burden* (2006) — bureaucrats cannot plan development; "planners" vs "searchers" | Bottom-up, feedback-based, market-driven |
| **Dambisa Moyo** | *Dead Aid* (2009) — aid creates dependency and corruption | Cut aid, encourage trade and investment |
| **Jeffrey Sachs** | *The End of Poverty* (2005) — aid works if we provide enough, correctly | Big Push for health, agriculture, infrastructure |
| **Paul Collier** | *The Bottom Billion* (2007) — aid works in the right conditions (traps differ) | Differentiated policies: aid + trade + security + governance |

## 2. Debt

### 2.1 Origins of the Debt Crisis

| Factor | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Oil shocks (1973, 1979)** | Developing countries borrowed to finance oil imports; Western banks recycled petrodollars |
| **Low interest rates (1970s)** | Banks were eager to lend — "no country ever went bankrupt" |
| **Volcker shock (1979)** | US interest rates rose to 20% — variable-rate debt payments quadrupled |
| **Commodity price collapse (1980s)** | Terms of trade worsened — export revenues fell |
| **Mexico default (1982)** | Triggered the Third World debt crisis |

### 2.2 Debt and Development

| Problem | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Debt service** | Paying interest diverts resources from health, education, infrastructure |
| **Conditionality** | Debt relief requires structural adjustment — painful reforms |
| **Sovereignty** | Creditors control policy |
| **Haircuts** | Rescheduling delays the inevitable — debt accumulates |
| **Vulture funds** | Private creditors buy distressed debt at discount, sue for full value |

### 2.3 Debt Relief Initiatives

| Initiative | Year | What It Did |
|------------|------|-------------|
| **Naples Terms** | 1995 | Paris Club reduced bilateral debt by 67% |
| **HIPC Initiative** | 1996 | Comprehensive debt relief for Highly Indebted Poor Countries |
| **Enhanced HIPC** | 1999 | Faster, deeper, more countries |
| **MDRI** | 2005 | Multilateral Debt Relief Initiative (IMF, World Bank, ADF cancelled remaining debt) |
| **DSSI** | 2020 | Debt Service Suspension Initiative (COVID-19 relief) |
| **Common Framework** | 2020 | G20 framework for sovereign debt restructuring |

### 2.4 Bangladesh's Debt

| Debt Indicator | Bangladesh | Comparator (IDA countries) |
|----------------|------------|---------------------------|
| External debt (% of GNI) | ~18% | ~36% |
| Public debt (% of GDP) | ~38% | ~60% |
| Debt service (% of exports) | ~5% | ~12% |
| Credit rating | BB− (stable) | — |

Bangladesh has avoided a debt crisis due to:
- Conservative borrowing policy
- Low external debt (mostly domestic)
- Strong export growth (RMG)
- Large remittance inflows ($20bn+ per year)
- Gradual, not shock, liberalisation

## 3. The Global Financial Architecture

### 3.1 The Bretton Woods System

The Bretton Woods institutions (1944) are the centre of global development finance:

| Institution | Purpose | Governance |
|-------------|---------|------------|
| **World Bank (IBRD + IDA)** | Development lending, grants, technical assistance | Weighted voting — US 16%, Japan 7%, China 6% |
| **IMF** | Balance of payments support, macroeconomic stability | Weighted voting — US 16.5% (effective veto on major decisions) |
| **WTO** | Trade rules, dispute settlement | One country, one vote — but negotiations are unequal |

**Critique of governance**:
- Developing countries have inadequate voice and representation
- Weighted voting reflects 1944, not today
- US dominance (16% veto power on major IMF decisions)
- Emerging economies (China, India, Brazil) are under-represented

### 3.2 Alternative Institutions

| Institution | Year | Members | Focus | Key Feature |
|-------------|------|---------|-------|-------------|
| **New Development Bank (NDB)** | 2014 | BRICS | Infrastructure | Equal voting power |
| **Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB)** | 2015 | 100+ | Infrastructure | China-led, non-concessional |
| **Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA)** | 2014 | BRICS | Currency stabilisation | Alternative to IMF |
| **Chiang Mai Initiative** | 2000 | ASEAN+3 | Currency swap | Stabilisation |
| **Arab Monetary Fund** | 1976 | 22 Arab states | Balance of payments | Regional |

### 3.3 Proposals for Reform

| Reform | Rationale | Progress |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **IMF quota reform** | Reflect current economic weights | Minimal — 2010 reform delayed |
| **World Bank voting reform** | Increase developing country voice | Modest increases |
| **UN Tax Convention** | Global tax cooperation to prevent tax avoidance | Under negotiation |
| **Sovereign debt restructuring mechanism** | Orderly, fair, timely debt resolution | Blocked by creditors |
| **Climate finance targets** | $100bn/year rich-to-poor for climate action | Unmet |
| **SDG financing gap** | $2.5–4 trillion/year needed | Private capital not flowing |

## 4. New Actors in Development Finance

### 4.1 China

China has become a major development financier:

| Instrument | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)** | Infrastructure lending — ports, railways, highways, energy |
| **Export-Import Bank of China** | Concessional and commercial loans tied to Chinese contractors |
| **China Development Bank** | Large infrastructure loans |
| **AIIB** | Multilateral infrastructure bank |

**Critique**:
- Debt trap diplomacy — infrastructure projects create unsustainable debt (Sri Lanka's Hambantota port)
- Lack of transparency — loans are opaque, environmental and social standards are weak
- Chinese labour and materials required — limited local benefit
- Western response: building alternative infrastructure initiatives (G7's Build Back Better World)

### 4.2 Private Capital

| Source | Size | Challenge |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Foreign direct investment (FDI)** | $1.5 trillion/year globally | Concentrated in few countries, extractive sectors |
| **Remittances** | $800 billion/year — exceeds ODA | Transaction costs, not development-focused |
| **Private equity/venture capital** | Growing but small | Focus on tech, excludes poorest |
| **Blended finance** | Public capital de-risks private investment | Limited scale, complex |
| **Impact investing** | Intention to generate social impact + return | Measurement challenges, greenwashing |

## 5. Bangladesh and Aid

### 5.1 Aid in Bangladesh's History

| Period | Dominant Donors | Emphasis | Assessment |
|--------|-----------------|----------|------------|
| **1970s** | UN, US, Japan, World Bank | Reconstruction, food aid | Critical for survival |
| **1980s** | World Bank, ADF, bilateral donors | Structural adjustment, population control | Mixed — SAPs imposed, but family planning succeeded |
| **1990s** | World Bank, DFID, ADB, Japan | Poverty reduction, education, health | Effective in education and health |
| **2000s–2020s** | World Bank, ADB, Japan, China | Infrastructure, climate, social protection | Declining importance — aid is <2% of GNI |

### 5.2 Aid Dependency

| Indicator | Peak (1980s–90s) | Current |
|-----------|------------------|---------|
| **Aid as % of GNI** | 6–8% | ~1.6% |
| **Aid as % of government budget** | 40–50% | ~15–20% |
| **Aid per capita** | ~$10 | ~$30 |

Bangladesh has successfully **graduated** from high aid dependency through:
- Growth in exports, remittances, and domestic revenue
- LDC graduation (approved 2021, effective 2026) — will lose concessional finance
- Active aid management — government prioritised projects, refused unwanted conditions

## Summary

Foreign aid has a contested track record — successes in health and education but persistent questions about dependency, effectiveness, and conditionality. Debt crises have recurred cyclically (1980s, 1990s, COVID-19), with partial relief (HIPC, MDRI) but no permanent solution. The Bretton Woods institutions remain dominant but face challenges from new actors (China, AIIB, NDB) and demands for reform. Bangladesh is a example of successful "aid graduation" — reducing dependency through growth while maintaining active aid management. Understanding the global financial architecture is essential for analysing any developing country's policy space.

- **ODA** — Official Development Assistance (concessional, development-oriented)
- **SAP** — Structural Adjustment Programme
- **HIPC** — Heavily Indebted Poor Countries Initiative
