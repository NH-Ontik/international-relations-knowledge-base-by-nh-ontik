## 1. Defining Peace and Conflict Studies

### 1.1 What Is Peace and Conflict Studies?

Peace and conflict studies is an interdisciplinary field that examines the causes of violence and war, the conditions for peace, and the processes by which conflicts can be transformed from destructive to constructive. It emerged after World War II as a distinct academic field — a response to the failure of existing disciplines (history, political science, international relations) to prevent the two world wars.

**Key characteristics of the field**:
- **Interdisciplinarity**: Draws on IR, political science, sociology, psychology, anthropology, law, economics, philosophy
- **Normative commitment**: Unlike "value-free" social science, peace research explicitly aims to reduce violence and promote justice
- **Both analytical and practical**: Seeks to understand conflict AND to intervene constructively
- **Multi-level**: Analyses conflict at interpersonal, communal, national, regional, and international levels
- **Multi-method**: Uses quantitative, qualitative, ethnographic, participatory, and action research methods

### 1.2 Peace Studies vs Conflict Studies vs Strategic Studies

The field encompasses distinct but overlapping traditions:

| Tradition | Focus | Key Question | Key Thinkers |
|-----------|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Peace Research** | Causes of war, conditions for peace | What prevents war? What creates peace? | Galtung, Boulding, Singer |
| **Conflict Resolution** | Resolving specific conflicts through intervention | How can parties reach agreement? | Burton, Azar, Mitchell |
| **Conflict Transformation** | Transforming relationships and structures | How can conflict become constructive? | Lederach, Curle |
| **Strategic Studies** | Military power, deterrence, war-fighting | How do states use force effectively? | Clausewitz, Schelling, Waltz |
| **Security Studies** | Threats to states and people | What makes people secure? | Buzan, Booth, UNDP |

**Tensions**: Peace research has historically defined itself in opposition to strategic studies — peace researchers see strategic studies as part of the problem (focus on military power perpetuates the war system), while strategic studies scholars see peace research as naive about the realities of power.

### 1.3 The Field's Relationship to IR

Peace and conflict studies draws heavily on IR theory but is not reducible to it:

- **Realism**: Provides important insights about power, anarchy, and security dilemmas — but peace researchers argue realism is a theory of war, not peace
- **Liberalism**: Democratic peace, commercial peace, institutional peace — liberalism is the IR tradition most compatible with peace research
- **Constructivism**: Norms of peace, identity transformation, culture of peace — constructivism provides the theoretical tools for understanding how peaceful relations can be built
- **Critical theory**: Structural violence, emancipation, peace as justice — Frankfurt School and neo-Gramscian approaches
- **Feminism**: Gendered violence, militarised masculinity, women's peace activism — central to contemporary peace research
- **Postcolonialism**: Peace as Western imposition, colonial violence, alternative peace traditions — essential for decolonising peace studies

## 2. Historical Development of the Field

### 2.1 Pre-Academic Roots

Peace thinking has ancient roots across civilisations:

- **Kautilya's *Arthashastra***: Realist tradition — peace as a strategic calculation (no moral content)
- **Ashoka's edicts** (3rd c. BCE): Buddhist-inspired peace — *dhamma* as non-violence and tolerance
- **Islamic peace traditions**: *Sulh* (reconciliation), *Salaam* (peace as submission to divine will), just war rules
- **Gandhi's Satyagraha** (early 20th c.): Non-violent resistance as political strategy
- **Kant's *Perpetual Peace*** (1795): Republican constitutions, federation of states, cosmopolitan right — foundational for liberal peace theory

### 2.2 The Emergence of Peace Research

Peace research as an academic field emerged after 1945:

- **1945–1960s**: Cold War context — peace research focused on preventing nuclear war. Early centres: University of Oslo (1950, Galtung), University of Michigan (1955, Boulding), PRIO (1959).
- **1964**: *Journal of Peace Research* founded by Johan Galtung. First editorial: "Violence, Peace, and Peace Research" — the foundational text distinguishing negative and positive peace.
- **1970s–1980s**: Expansion beyond nuclear war to include structural violence, development, and North-South relations. Feminist peace research emerges. Galtung's "violence triangle" (direct, structural, cultural).
- **1990s**: Post-Cold War optimism — "liberal peace" consensus, UN Agenda for Peace (Boutros-Ghali 1992), multiplication of peace operations. Conflict resolution professionalised.
- **2000s**: Liberal peace critique (Roland Paris, Oliver Richmond). Local turn. Hybrid peace debate.
- **2010s–present**: Authoritarian peace, shrinking civic space, climate conflict, technology and peace, post-liberal peace.

### 2.3 Institutionalisation of the Field

The field is now globally institutionalised:
- **Key journals**: *Journal of Peace Research* (1964, PRIO/Sage), *Journal of Conflict Resolution* (1957, Michigan/Sage), *Peace & Change* (1973, COPRED), *International Peacekeeping* (1994), *Cooperation and Conflict* (1965, Nordic)
- **Professional associations**: International Peace Research Association (IPRA, 1964), Peace Science Society (PSS, 1963), Consortium on Peace Research, Education and Development (COPRED), ISA section on Peace Studies
- **Major research institutes**: PRIO (Oslo, 1959), SIPRI (Stockholm, 1966), IISS (London, 1958), USIP (Washington, 1984), IEP (Sydney, 2007), GRIP (Brussels, 1979)
- **Global South institutes**: CODESRIA (Dakar, 1973), APISA (Singapore, 2003), ACCORD (South Africa, 1992), BIISS (Dhaka, 1978)
- **Training centres**: UNITAR, UN Institute for Training and Research; UN University for Peace (Costa Rica); Kofi Annan International Peacekeeping Training Centre (Ghana); Bangladesh Institute of Peace Support Operations (BIPSOT)

### 2.4 Key Foundational Works

| Work | Author (Year) | Contribution |
|------|------|------|------|
| "Violence, Peace, and Peace Research" | Galtung (1964) | Negative vs positive peace; launched peace research |

## 3. Negative Peace vs Positive Peace

### 3.1 The Foundational Distinction

Galtung's 1964 distinction between negative and positive peace is the field's foundational conceptual contribution:

| Dimension | Negative Peace | Positive Peace |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Definition** | Absence of direct, organised violence (war) | Absence of structural violence + presence of social justice |
| **Focus** | Stopping violence, conflict cessation | Building just structures, addressing root causes |
| **Approach** | Reactive, curative | Proactive, preventive |
| **Timeframe** | Immediate/short-term | Long-term |
| **Goal** | Ceasefire, peace agreement, de-escalation | Integrated society, fair institutions, cooperation |
| **Violence addressed** | Direct (physical, visible) | Structural + cultural (systemic, invisible) |
| **Measurement** | Battle deaths, war incidence, absence of armed conflict | Life expectancy, inequality, human rights, access to justice |
| **Critique** | May perpetuate unjust status quo | May be overly ambitious, difficult to operationalise |

**Key implications**:
1. **Negative peace is not enough**: Ending war without addressing structural violence merely creates a "cold peace" — a ceasefire that perpetuates injustice
2. **Positive peace is the ultimate goal**: Peace research should aim for the absence of all forms of violence, not just war
3. **The distinction is political**: Defining peace as "absence of war" serves powerful interests (it justifies inaction on inequality) — defining peace as "justice" challenges the status quo

### 3.2 Positive Peace Index (Operationalising the Concept)

The Institute for Economics and Peace (IEP) has operationalised positive peace through the **Positive Peace Index (PPI)**, measuring eight pillars across 163 countries (IEP 2024):

1. **Well-functioning government** — Effective public services, rule of law
2. **Sound business environment** — Economic conditions for private sector
3. **Equitable distribution of resources** — Inequality, access to services
4. **Acceptance of the rights of others** — Human rights, gender equality, tolerance
5. **Good relations with neighbours** — Regional stability, low external conflict
6. **Free flow of information** — Media freedom, internet access
7. **High levels of human capital** — Education, health outcomes
8. **Low levels of corruption** — Institutional trust, transparency

**Bangladesh on the PPI**: Bangladesh has improved on the PPI over the past decade, driven by economic growth (pillar 2), human capital (pillar 7), and women's empowerment (pillar 4). However, it scores lower on pillar 1 (governance quality), pillar 5 (neighbour relations — Rohingya crisis), and pillar 6 (media freedom). This illustrates that positive peace gains can be uneven and fragile.

### 3.3 Empirical Evidence on Peace

Peace research has produced significant empirical findings about the causes of war and conditions for peace:

- **The decline of war thesis**: Major armed conflict has declined dramatically since 1945 — "The Long Peace" (Gaddis 1987), "The New Peace" (Goldstein 2011), "The Better Angels of Our Nature" (Pinker 2011). This is one of the most important-but-underappreciated facts about international relations.
  - Battle deaths per 100,000 population: ~300 in WWII, ~30 in Korea/Vietnam, ~0.5 in 2010s.
  - Interstate war has nearly disappeared — the deadliest contemporary conflicts are civil wars.
  - **Critiques**: The decline may be overstated (UCDP/PRIO data includes low thresholds; civilian deaths from sanctions, drone strikes, and structural violence are undercounted); the decline may be reversed (Ukraine, Gaza, Sudan).

- **The democratic peace**: Democracies rarely if ever fight each other (the closest thing to an empirical law in IR). Heavily debated: is it democracy per se, or capitalism, or shared interests, or institutional constraints?

- **The territorial peace**: Settled borders reduce war. Interstate war is overwhelmingly about territory — when borders are settled, war becomes much less likely. This explains why the post-1945 territorial settlement (despite its injustices) contributed to peace in Europe.

- **The commercial peace**: Economic interdependence reduces conflict. Countries that trade more fight less. But interdependence can also create vulnerabilities and frictions.

- **The nuclear peace**: Nuclear weapons may have prevented WWIII (the "nuclear revolution" — Waltz). But nuclear deterrence created proxy wars, arms races, and existential risk.

### 3.4 Critiques of the Distinction

- **Too broad?**: If peace means the absence of all structural violence (poverty, inequality, discrimination), does it become indistinguishable from "development" or "justice"? Galtung responds that peace research must retain its focus on *violence* but acknowledge that violence takes many forms.
- **Politically paralysing?**: The demand for positive peace as a precondition can create a standard so high that nothing can be achieved in practice. Better to achieve negative peace first and build toward positive peace.
- **Cultural imposition**: The concept of "positive peace" reflects Western values. Other traditions define peace differently (as harmony, as submission to divine will, as balance) — see Module 8.

## 4. The Violence Triangle (Galtung)

Galtung's most influential analytical framework identifies three forms of violence:

```
            Direct Violence
                 /\
                /  \
               /    \
              /______\
             /        \
            / Cultural \
           /  Violence  \
          /______________\
         /   Structural  \
        /    Violence     \
       /__________________\
```

- **Direct Violence**: Physical, visible — war, assault, terror. Has a clear actor, victim, and event. "Killing someone with a gun."
- **Structural Violence**: Systemic harm embedded in social, political, and economic structures. Unequal power → unequal life chances. No single perpetrator. "People dying because they are poor."
- **Cultural Violence**: Aspects of culture (religion, ideology, language, art, science) used to legitimise direct and structural violence. Changes the "moral colour" of violence from wrong to right. "Making violence acceptable."

**How they interact**: Structural violence creates conditions for direct violence (poverty → grievance → rebellion). Cultural violence legitimises both (nationalism → willingness to kill). Direct violence reinforces structural and cultural violence (war → centralised power → nationalist ideology).

**This framework is the basis for Modules 2–4 of this course.**

## 5. Key Debates in the Field

### 5.1 Is Peace Research Science?

Early peace researchers (Galtung, Singer, Boulding) argued peace research should be scientific — empirical, quantitative, hypothesis-testing. This reflected the behavioural revolution in social science. Critics argued peace research cannot be value-free because it is normatively committed to peace. Galtung's response: peace research should be **value-committed but methodologically rigorous** — explicit about its values while maintaining scientific standards of evidence.

### 5.2 Conflict: Problem or Opportunity?

- **Traditional view**: Conflict is pathological — it is dysfunction, breakdown, failure. The goal is to eliminate or suppress conflict.
- **Peace research view**: Conflict is normal and inevitable. It is not inherently destructive — it is a motor for social change. The goal is not to eliminate conflict but to channel it constructively (Coser 1956; Lederach 1997).

### 5.3 Should Peace Researchers Engage with Power?

- **Engagement**: Peace research should influence policy — engage with governments, international organisations, and militaries to make them more peaceful
- **Critical distance**: Engagement co-opts peace research — it becomes technocratic, loses its critical edge, and serves the very power structures that perpetuate violence

### 5.4 Universalism vs Context-Specificity

- **Universal peace**: Peace concepts (negative/positive, structural violence) apply everywhere — peace research identifies general patterns
- **Context-specific peace**: Peace is culturally specific — what "peace" means in Bangladesh differs from what it means in Norway. Peace research must be grounded in local contexts

### 5.5 Does Peace Research Matter?

After 60+ years of peace research, has the world become more peaceful?

**The case for impact**:
- The decline in war since 1945 is partly attributable to peace research: the democratic peace thesis influenced US and EU foreign policy; the territorial peace argument has informed border settlement and recognition policies; peacekeeping research has improved mission design; the R2P norm (Module 9 of IR2417) emerged from the peace research community
- Peace education, conflict resolution training, and mediation have become professionalised and widely practised
- The UN's emphasis on conflict prevention, peacebuilding, and the "root causes of conflict" reflects peace research findings

**The case against**:
- Wars continue — Ukraine, Gaza, Sudan, Myanmar, Yemen, Ethiopia. The decline of interstate war is mainly attributable to nuclear deterrence, US hegemony, and economic globalisation, not peace research
- Peace research insights are ignored when they conflict with powerful state interests (the Iraq War, the arming of Ukraine, Israel's offensive in Gaza)
- The field is marginalised within academic IR — IR theory (realism, liberalism) dominates the discipline, and peace research is often seen as too normative, too applied, and not "theoretical" enough

**Evaluation**: Peace research has influenced policy at the margins but has not transformed the international system. Its greatest impact has been in reframing the problem — away from "how do states manage war" toward "how do societies create peace" — and in providing analytical tools (negative/positive peace, structural violence, conflict transformation) that practitioners use.

## 6. Bangladesh and Peace Studies

### 6.1 Why Bangladesh Matters for This Field

Bangladesh is a case of unusual importance for peace and conflict studies:

- **Liberation War 1971**: One of the 20th century's most significant cases of self-determination, genocide (estimates: 300,000–3,000,000 killed), mass displacement (10 million refugees to India), and post-conflict reconstruction
- **Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT) conflict**: South Asia's longest-running internal armed conflict (1977–1997), resolved through a peace accord (1997) that remains imperfectly implemented — a key case for conflict resolution and peacebuilding
- **Peacekeeping**: One of the world's largest contributors to UN peacekeeping — over 160,000 Bangladeshi troops deployed since 1988 — a case study in the Global South's role in international peace
- **Political violence**: Periodic electoral violence, political polarisation, extrajudicial killings — the tension between formal democratic institutions and substantive peace
- **Rohingya crisis**: Hosting over 1 million Rohingya refugees since 2017 — a case of both humanitarian response and the limits of peaceful coexistence
- **Climate vulnerability**: Bangladesh is the most climate-vulnerable large country — a case where structural violence (poverty, geography) intersects with direct violence (climate-induced conflict risks)

### 6.2 CHT Peace Accord: Lessons for Conflict Resolution

The Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT) conflict pitted the Bangladeshi state against the Parbattya Chattagram Jana Sanghati Samiti (PCJSS), led by Manabendra Narayan Larma, over issues of autonomy, land rights, and cultural recognition. The conflict lasted from 1977 to 1997 — one of South Asia's longest-running internal armed conflicts.

**Key features that explain the peace process:**
- **Mutual exhaustion**: By the mid-1990s, neither side could achieve military victory — a classic "mutually hurting stalemate" (Zartman's ripe moment)
- **Track I + Track II**: Official negotiations (the National Committee on CHT, 1991) combined with informal Track II dialogue through civil society, including the Bangladesh Adivasi Forum and international mediators
- **Power-sharing compromise**: The 1997 CHT Peace Accord provided for the CHT Regional Council, district councils, local governance, and land commission — a recognition of autonomy within the unitary state
- **Flawed implementation**: 25+ years later, implementation remains incomplete — land commission progress has been slow, disarmament of PCJSS is partial, and Bengali settlement in CHT continues. The accord created a framework for peace but not yet full positive peace

**Comparative relevance**: The CHT accord is often compared to other South Asian peace processes (India's Bodoland, Nepal's Madhesi, Sri Lanka's post-war devolution) and offers lessons for balancing autonomy with territorial integrity.

### 6.3 Bangladesh's Broader Peace Contributions

- **Peacekeeping excellence**: Bangladesh has contributed over 160,000 troops since 1988, currently ranking among the top three contributor countries. BIPSOT (Bangladesh Institute of Peace Support Operations Training, 1999) trains peacekeepers for Bangladesh and other countries. Bangladesh's peacekeepers served in UNMIN (Nepal), supporting Nepal's post-civil war peace process.
- **Civil society peacebuilding**: BRAC (world's largest NGO), Ain o Salish Kendra (ASK, legal aid and human rights), Centre for Peace and Justice (BRAC University, research and practice), and the Liberation War Museum all contribute to peace research and practice from a Global South perspective.
- **Non-alignment and peaceful coexistence**: Bangladesh's foreign policy tradition draws on Panchsheel (five principles of peaceful coexistence), non-alignment, and the "friendship to all, malice to none" principle of its founder. These principles challenge the realist dominance in peace and security studies.
- **OIC engagement**: Bangladesh has been active in OIC peace mediation, including hosting Rohingya advocacy at OIC platforms and supporting OIC humanitarian initiatives.
- **Regional peace diplomacy**: As the chair of the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA, 2021–2023) and the CVF/V20, Bangladesh has used multilateral platforms for peace advocacy — connecting climate vulnerability to peace and security.

## Summary

Peace and conflict studies is an interdisciplinary field that emerged after WWII to understand and reduce violence. Galtung's foundational distinction between negative peace (absence of war) and positive peace (absence of structural violence + justice) structures the entire field. The violence triangle (direct, structural, cultural) provides an analytical framework that the subsequent modules will develop. The field is marked by productive tensions — science vs normative commitment, engagement vs critical distance, universalism vs context. Bangladesh offers particularly rich case material across all of these dimensions.

### Key Definitions
- **Negative Peace** — Absence of direct, organised violence (war)
- **Positive Peace** — Absence of structural violence + presence of social justice
- **Direct Violence** — Physical, visible harm with identifiable actor and victim
- **Structural Violence** — Systemic harm embedded in institutions
- **Cultural Violence** — Cultural legitimation of violence
- **Peace Research** — Scientific study of the causes of war and conditions for peace
- **Conflict Transformation** — Changing relationships and structures to make conflict constructive

### Key Thinkers
- **Johan Galtung** — Founder of peace research; negative/positive peace; violence triangle; transcend method
- **Kenneth Boulding** — Stable peace, peace economics
- **John Paul Lederach** — Conflict transformation, peacebuilding pyramid, middle-range actors
- **John Burton** — Basic human needs theory of conflict
- **Lewis Coser** — The Functions of Social Conflict (conflict as normal and potentially constructive)

### Potential Exam Questions
1. "Explain Galtung's distinction between negative and positive peace. Is positive peace a realistic goal for international relations?"
2. "Critically evaluate the argument that peace research should be scientific and value-free."
3. "How useful is Galtung's violence triangle for understanding contemporary conflicts? Use examples."
4. "Compare the peace research tradition with the strategic studies tradition. Which better explains the persistence of war?"
5. "Analyse Bangladesh's Liberation War (1971) through the lens of Galtung's concepts of violence and peace."

### Essay Framework — "Is positive peace achievable?"
- **Thesis**: Positive peace is a regulative ideal (guiding aspiration) rather than a fully achievable end-state, but the field's value lies in orienting policy toward it.
- **Evidence**: Nordic welfare states approximate positive peace internally but participate in structural violence globally (arms exports, resource extraction). The EU has achieved positive peace among members but struggles with external violence (migration, border regimes). Bangladesh has built significant development progress (positive peace) but remains within structures of global inequality.
- **Counter-arguments**: Positive peace is achievable at certain levels (community, region) and in certain dimensions (gender equality, education). The standard should not be perfection but continuous improvement.
- **Evaluation**: The distinction matters because different definitions lead to different policy approaches — if peace means only the absence of war, the status quo is acceptable; if peace means justice, the status quo demands transformation.
