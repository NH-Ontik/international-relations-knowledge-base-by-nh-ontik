# Module 9 — Reconciliation, Transitional Justice, and Post-Conflict Peace

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 9 — Reconciliation, Transitional Justice, and Post-Conflict Peace
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–8

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define transitional justice and its four pillars
2. Explain the tension between peace and justice in post-conflict settings
3. Compare major transitional justice mechanisms (prosecutions, truth commissions, reparations, reform)
4. Analyse the role of reconciliation in peacebuilding
5. Apply transitional justice concepts to Bangladesh's post-1971 and post-CHT experiences
6. Critically evaluate the relationship between retributive and restorative justice

## 1. Transitional Justice

### 1.1 Definition

Transitional justice refers to the range of approaches that societies use to address legacies of large-scale past abuses in order to build a more just and peaceful future (UN Secretary-General 2004).

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Transition** | The period of political change from conflict/violence to peace/democracy |
| **Justice** | Accountability for past abuses — not just punishment but truth, reparation, reform |
| **Transitional** | Temporary measures that would not be acceptable in a stable democracy (prosecuting government officials, amnesties, vetting) |

### 1.2 The Four Pillars of Transitional Justice

| Pillar | Definition | Mechanisms | Goal |
|--------|-----------|------------|------|
| **Prosecutions** | Criminal accountability for perpetrators | National courts, international courts (ICC), hybrid courts (Special Court for Sierra Leone) | Punish, deter, end impunity |
| **Truth** | Acknowledging and documenting past abuses | Truth commissions, inquiries, memorialisation | Establish facts, recognise victims, create historical record |
| **Reparations** | Making amends to victims | Material (compensation, services), symbolic (apologies, memorials) | Restore dignity, address harm |
| **Guarantees of non-recurrence** | Structural reform to prevent recurrence | Security sector reform, constitutional reform, vetting of officials, human rights education | Prevent future violence |

## 2. The Peace vs Justice Debate

### 2.1 The Central Tension

| Position | Argument | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **Justice imperative** | Perpetrators must be held accountable — no peace without justice. Amnesty entrenches impunity and makes peace unsustainable | ICC prosecution of war crimes; no amnesty for genocide |
| **Peace imperative** | Justice can threaten peace — if perpetrators fear prosecution, they will not negotiate. Peace first, justice later | South Africa TRC (amnesty in exchange for truth); Mozambique (no prosecutions for Renamo) |
| **Complementarity** | Different forms of justice at different stages — the ICC complements, does not replace, national processes | Kenya (ICC cases = complementarity failure; post-election TJ processes) |

### 2.2 Amnesties

The debate about amnesties illustrates the tension:

| Type of Amnesty | Example | Assessment |
|-----------------|---------|------------|
| **Blanket amnesty** (all crimes) | Mozambique (1992), Chile (1978) | Achieved peace but entrenched impunity |
| **Conditional amnesty** (truth in exchange) | South Africa TRC (1995) | Truth-justice trade-off — controversial but enabled transition |
| **No amnesty for international crimes** | Bosnia (ICTY), Rwanda (ICTR) | Consistent with international law — but may deter peace |

**International law shift**: Since the 1990s, international law has increasingly rejected amnesties for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes. The ICC prohibits amnesty for its core crimes. But in practice, amnesties remain common in peace agreements.

## 3. Major Transitional Justice Mechanisms

### 3.1 Prosecutions

**International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY, 1993–2017)**:
- Established by UNSC to prosecute war crimes in the Balkans
- 161 indicted, 90 convicted, 18 acquitted — mixed record
- Established important jurisprudence (genocide at Srebrenica, sexual violence as war crime)
- Criticised for selectivity (focus on Serbs), slow pace, distance from affected populations

**International Criminal Court (ICC, 2002–present)** :
- Permanent court for genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, aggression
- 124 member states; 30+ cases (DRC, Uganda, CAR, Mali, Sudan, Libya, Kenya, Côte d'Ivoire)
- Criticised for Africa focus (10 of 11 investigations by 2016 were in Africa), slow pace, acquittal rate
- The complementarity principle: ICC acts when states are "unwilling or unable" to prosecute

**Gacaca Courts (Rwanda, 2002–2012)** :
- Community-based courts for post-genocide justice
- 12,000 community courts tried ~2 million cases
- Aims: truth-telling, speed, community healing, reducing prison overcrowding
- Criticised: due process concerns, intimidation, re-traumatisation

### 3.2 Truth Commissions

| Commission | Mandate | Strengths | Limitations |
|------------|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **South Africa TRC** (1995–2002) | Apartheid-era abuses (1960–1994) | Amnesty for truth; restorative justice (Ubuntu); victim-centred; public hearings | No prosecutions; limited reparations; economic apartheid unaddressed |
| **Chile CNVR** (1990–1991) | Pinochet abuses (1973–1990) | Established facts; recognised victims | Limited mandate (only deaths); no names of perpetrators |
| **Peru CVR** (2001–2003) | Internal conflict (1980–2000) | Comprehensive; rooted in local context; gender focus | Recommendations implemented unevenly |
| **Kenya TJRC** (2009–2013) | Post-election violence (2007–2008) | Four pillars approach | Politicised; commissioners resigned; recommendations unimplemented |
| **Bangladesh-1971** | Liberation War genocide | (ICT-BD established 2009) | Truth commission not well developed |

### 3.3 Reparations

| Type | Example | Challenge |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Individual compensation** | Chile, Argentina, South Africa | Cost, identification of victims |
| **Collective reparations** | Peru (community development) | Who benefits? May not reach individuals |
| **Symbolic reparations** | Memorials, apologies, naming of streets | May substitute for material justice |
| **Service-based** | Healthcare, education, housing | Capacity, sustainability |

### 3.4 Institutional Reform

- **Security Sector Reform (SSR)**: Reforming military, police, intelligence to respect human rights and civilian control
- **Vetting/Lustration**: Removing human rights abusers from public office (Bosnia, Iraq, post-communist Europe)
- **Constitutional reform**: Addressing structural causes of conflict (power-sharing, federalism, minority rights)
- **Memorialisation**: Museums, monuments, commemorations (Rwanda Genocide Memorial, Bangladesh Liberation War Museum)

## 4. Reconciliation

### 4.1 Defining Reconciliation

Reconciliation is the most contested concept in transitional justice. Key definitions:

| Scholar | Definition | Key Element |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Lederach** | Reconciliation is "a meeting place where truth, mercy, justice, and peace meet" | Relationship restoration |
| **Bloomfield** | "A process through which a society moves from a divided past to a shared future" | Shared future |
| **De Gruchy** | "Restoring broken relationships" | Healing |

**The four components of reconciliation** (Lederach):
- **Truth**: Acknowledging what happened — no reconciliation without truth
- **Mercy**: Compassion, forgiveness — not retribution
- **Justice**: Accountability, fairness — must be genuine, not impunity
- **Peace**: Security, coexistence, non-violence

### 4.2 Reconciliation vs Forgiveness

| Concept | Individual/Collective | Required? |
|---------|----------------------|-----------|
| **Forgiveness** | Personal choice — releasing anger toward the perpetrator (individual) | Not required — cannot demand forgiveness |
| **Reconciliation** | Relational — rebuilding trust and coexistence (collective) | Long-term goal — may not be achievable |
| **Coexistence** | Minimal peace — living together without violence | Pragmatic minimum — often the realistic goal |

### 4.3 Reconciliation in Practice

**South Africa**: The TRC process aimed at "truth and reconciliation" — but 30 years later, South Africa remains deeply divided (economic inequality, racial tension). Reconciliation at the elite level (Mandela, Tutu, de Klerk) was not matched by grassroots reconciliation.

**Rwanda**: Post-genocide reconciliation has been officially promoted through *gacaca* courts, Ingando (re-education camps), and a unified national identity. But critics argue reconciliation has been imposed by the RPF government and masks ongoing ethnic tensions and authoritarian control.

**Bangladesh 1971**: Reconciliation after the Liberation War was partial — the 1972 Indemnity Ordinance protected collaborators; the 1975 political breakdown reversed the reconciliation process; the 2009 war crimes tribunal (ICT-BD) re-opened prosecutions 38 years later. The unresolved tension between justice and reconciliation continues.

## 5. Bangladesh Case Studies

### 5.1 Post-1971 Transitional Justice

| Mechanism | Description | Assessment |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Collaborators Act (1972)** | Intended to prosecute collaborators of Pakistan army | Repealed after 1973 (political reconciliation) |
| **Indemnity Ordinance (1972)** | Protected military and political actors from prosecution for wartime actions | Criticised as impunity |
| **Constitutional recognition** | Constitution acknowledged genocide | Symbolic but did not lead to systematic TJ |
| **1992 Gono Adalat (People's Court)** | Civil society trial of Golam Azam in absentia | Surybolic — no legal force |
| **ICT-BD (2010–present)** | International Crimes Tribunal for prosecution of 1971 collaborators | 6 executions (including Jamaat leaders); criticised for due process violations |
| **Liberation War Museum** | Memorialisation | Only comprehensive memorial — no official genocide museum |

**Assessment**: Bangladesh's transitional justice record is mixed — the ICT-BD achieved some accountability but has been criticised for due process, politicisation, and selective prosecution (mainly Jamaat-e-Islami leaders). The deeper reconciliation agenda — integrating the 1971 experience into national identity, addressing the suffering of both Bengali and non-Bengali communities — remains incomplete.

### 5.2 Post-CHT Reconciliation

The 1997 CHT Peace Accord included:
- Land Commission to resolve land disputes (functioning but slow)
- Regional Council and District Councils (autonomy)
- Cultural recognition (Hill Day, indigenous language rights)
- Rehabilitation programmes for former combatants

**Reconciliation progress**: The accord ended armed conflict but reconciliation remains partial — inter-community trust is limited, land disputes continue, Bengali settlement in CHT has not stopped, and indigenous communities feel marginalised.

### 5.3 Rohingya — The Unresolved TJ Challenge

The Rohingya crisis (2017–present) presents the most acute transitional justice challenge:
- **Prosecutions**: ICJ (*The Gambia v. Myanmar*), ICC investigation (jurisdiction challenge), International Court of Justice provisional measures (2020)
- **Truth**: UN Fact-Finding Mission (2018) — recommended prosecution for genocide; Myanmar refuses to cooperate
- **Reparations**: None — Rohingya remain in refugee camps in Bangladesh
- **Non-recurrence**: Requires Myanmar constitutional reform, citizenship law change, military reform — all politically impossible under current conditions

## 6. Critiques of Transitional Justice

| Critique | Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Western imposition** | TJ reflects Western legal norms (individual accountability, human rights) — not all societies want or need this | International law creates binding obligations; but TJ must be locally owned |
| **Peace vs justice unresolved** | TJ prioritises justice over peace — in some contexts, peace is more urgent | The ICC complementarity principle allows context-specific approaches |
| **Too legalistic** | TJ is dominated by lawyers — it focuses on criminal prosecutions at the expense of social justice, economic inequality, and structural reform | The four pillars framework is broader, but implementation remains legalistic |
| **Reconciliation overused** | The language of reconciliation is used to justify inadequate justice (South Africa: truth without redistribution) | Reconciliation without justice is empty; justice without reconciliation may deepen division |
| **Bangladesh: politicised TJ** | The ICT-BD has been used to target political opponents (Jamaat), not to achieve comprehensive justice | A legitimate concern — TJ mechanisms must be independent to be credible |

---

## Summary

Transitional justice addresses past abuses through four pillars: prosecutions, truth, reparations, and guarantees of non-recurrence. The central tension is between peace and justice — pursuing accountability can threaten peace, but impunity makes peace unsustainable. Reconciliation — rebuilding relationships after violence — is the most ambitious and contested goal. Bangladesh's TJ journey is instructive: the 1971 experience shows the long arc from impunity (1972) through partial accountability (ICT-BD 2010–) to ongoing reconciliation gaps. The CHT accord illustrates the limits of institutional reform without deeper reconciliation. The Rohingya crisis presents a TJ challenge that the international system has not resolved.
