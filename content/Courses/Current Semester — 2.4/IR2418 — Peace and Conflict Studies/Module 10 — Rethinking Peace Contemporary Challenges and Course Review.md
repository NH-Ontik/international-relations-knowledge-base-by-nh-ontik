## 1. Climate Change and Peace

### 1.1 Climate as a Threat Multiplier

Climate change is described as a "threat multiplier" — it does not directly cause conflict but exacerbates the conditions that lead to conflict:

| Climate Impact | Conflict Mechanism | Example |
|---------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Resource scarcity** | Competition for water, land, food → inter-community violence | Darfur (climate-water-pastoralism conflict); Sahel farmer-herder conflict |
| **Displacement** | Climate-induced migration → tensions in receiving areas | Bangladesh: 20M+ climate migrants by 2050; land stress in host areas |
| **Livelihood collapse** | Destroyed agriculture/fisheries → grievance, recruitment into armed groups | Lake Chad region (Boko Haram recruitment); coastal Bangladesh |
| **State capacity strain** | Climate disasters overwhelm state capacity → governance gaps | Cyclone Amphan (2020) — Bangladesh response was effective, but future capacity uncertain |
| **Geopolitical tension** | Arctic melting, resource competition → interstate tension | Arctic, South China Sea |

### 1.2 Climate Peacebuilding

Climate change is not only a threat — it can also be a peacebuilding opportunity:
- **Environmental cooperation** as a peacebuilding tool: Transboundary water management (Indus Waters Treaty survived India-Pakistan conflicts)
- **Climate diplomacy** creating new coalitions: CVF/V20 (Bangladesh as chair) — climate vulnerability as a basis for cooperation
- **Green peacebuilding**: Renewable energy cooperation, climate adaptation as peacebuilding
- **Bangladesh**: The Bangladesh Delta Plan 2100 integrates climate adaptation with long-term planning but faces capacity and resource gaps

### 1.3 Bangladesh and Climate Conflict

Bangladesh is the world's most climate-vulnerable large country. Key risks:
- **Sea-level rise**: 15–30M climate refugees by 2050; displacement from coastal areas
- **Cyclones and floods**: Increasing frequency and intensity — Cyclone Sidr (2007), Aila (2009), Amphan (2020)
- **Salinity intrusion**: Agricultural collapse in coastal areas — internal migration to Dhaka
- **Rohingya camps**: Cyclone and flood risk in Cox's Bazar — 1M refugees in climate-vulnerable location

## 2. Technology and Peace

### 2.1 Drones and Autonomous Weapons

- **Drone warfare**: Remote killing reduces political costs of war — may increase conflict (US drone strikes in Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia)
- **LAWS (Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems)**: "Killer robots" — weapons that select and engage targets without human control. Campaign to Stop Killer Robots (NGO coalition) pushing for a ban treaty (like landmines, cluster munitions)
- **Peacekeeping technology**: Drones for surveillance, monitoring, force protection — but also risk of mission creep

### 2.2 Cyber Conflict

- **Cyber warfare**: Stuxnet (Iran 2010), Russian cyber attacks on Ukraine, election interference
- **Cyber peace**: The debate about whether international law applies, whether a "digital Geneva Convention" is needed, and whether cyber can be governed like other domains
- **Bangladesh**: Growing cyber threat landscape — Bangladesh Bank heist (2016, $101M stolen by North Korean hackers), election security, digital surveillance

### 2.3 AI and Peace

| Application | Opportunity | Risk |
|-------------|-------------|------|
| **Conflict early warning** | AI can analyse vast datasets to predict conflict | Algorithmic bias; false positives |
| **Peace mediation** | AI-assisted negotiation analysis, language processing | Dehumanisation of peace process |
| **Disinformation** | Detection of hate speech, fake news | Inaccurate detection; censorship risk |
| **Autonomous weapons** | (None) | Loss of human control, escalation risk |

### 2.4 Social Media and Conflict

- **Hate speech amplification**: Myanmar (Facebook facilitated anti-Rohingya hate speech), India (WhatsApp lynchings), Ethiopia (social media mobilisation in Tigray war)
- **Mobilisation for peace**: #CandleLight (Bangladesh 2025 protests), Arab Spring, #EndSARS (Nigeria)
- **Bangladesh**: Social media regulation debates — balancing prevention of hate speech with freedom of expression; section of the Digital Security Act (2018, replaced 2023) used against critics

## 3. Gender, Peace and Security

### 3.1 The WPS Agenda — Twenty-Five Years On

UNSCR 1325 (2000) was a landmark — the first resolution linking women to peace and security. The WPS agenda now includes 10 resolutions:

| Category | Resolutions | Key Provisions |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Participation** | 1325 (2000), 1889 (2009), 2122 (2013), 2242 (2015) | Women's participation in peace processes, decision-making |
| **Protection** | 1820 (2008), 1888 (2009), 1960 (2010), 2467 (2019) | Protection from sexual violence in conflict |
| **Prevention** | 1325, 2242 | Gender equality prevents conflict |
| **Relief & recovery** | 1325, 1889 | Gender-sensitive post-conflict reconstruction |

### 3.2 Implementation Gaps

- **Participation**: Women are still <20% of peace negotiators, <5% of mediators. No major peace agreement has had a woman lead negotiator on the government side.
- **Funding**: <1% of peacebuilding funding goes to women's organisations
- **Protection**: Sexual violence in conflict remains widespread and under-prosecuted
- **National Action Plans**: 100+ countries have NAPs on WPS (Bangladesh: NAP 2019–2022, new NAP under development) — but implementation is weak and underfunded

### 3.3 Bangladesh and WPS

| Area | Progress | Gap |
|------|----------|-----|
| **Peacekeeping** | Top contributor of female peacekeepers (~600); female engagement teams | <10% of overall contribution |
| **National Action Plan** | NAP (2019–2022) — first WPS NAP in South Asia | Weak implementation, limited civil society involvement |
| **Women in peace processes** | CHT process did include women's voices | Limited; formal peace structures (Regional Council) have few women |
| **Sexual violence** | Domestic law criminalises sexual violence; Rohingya sexual violence cases documented | Implementation; Rohingya survivors face justice gaps |

## 4. Authoritarian Peace

### 4.1 The Rise of Authoritarian Peacebuilding

The liberal peace model (democracy + markets + rule of law) is being challenged by an alternative: the **authoritarian peace**:

| Dimension | Liberal Peace | Authoritarian Peace |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Political** | Democracy, elections, civil liberties | Strong state, limited political competition, social control |
| **Economic** | Markets, liberalisation, private sector | State-led development, economic growth as legitimation |
| **Security** | Human security, civilian protection | Regime security, sovereignty, stability |
| **International** | Multilateralism, intervention, norms | Sovereignty, non-interference, bilateralism |
| **Key proponents** | Western states, UN | China, Russia, some Global South states |

### 4.2 Is Authoritarian Peace Sustainable?

- **Argument for**: China has maintained internal stability despite massive economic inequality through state capacity and social control. The "Chinese model" suggests strong state capacity can maintain order without liberal democracy.
- **Counter-argument**: Authoritarian peace suppresses conflict rather than resolving it. Without democratic institutions, grievances accumulate — when the state weakens, conflict erupts violently (Arab Spring, Myanmar 2021, Bangladesh 2024/2025 protests).
- **Bangladesh relevance**: Bangladesh's political system has authoritarian elements (dominant-party tendencies, limited media freedom, judicial pressure). The state's capacity has maintained order (no civil war) but political violence and repression remain concerns.

### 4.3 Peace and Democracy — An Uncomfortable Relationship

The liberal peace assumes that democracy promotes peace. But:
- **Democratisation can increase conflict**: New democracies are vulnerable to ethnic conflict, civil war (Mansfield & Snyder 2005)
- **Authoritarian regimes can maintain peace**: But at the cost of human rights, freedom, and long-term stability
- **Bangladesh**: Has maintained democratic forms (regular elections, parliament) with authoritarian practices (crackdowns, limited opposition space) — a "hybrid regime" that has experienced political violence but not civil war

## 6. Exam Strategy

### 6.1 IR2418 Exam Question Types

1. **Conceptual definition**: "Define negative peace / structural violence / conflict transformation / etc."
2. **Theoretical comparison**: "Compare [theory A] and [theory B] on [question]."
3. **Case analysis**: "Apply [framework] to analyse [case]."
4. **Critical evaluation**: "Critically evaluate the claim that [argument]."
5. **Synthesis**: "How has peacebuilding evolved? What challenges does it face today?"
6. **Bangladesh focus**: "Analyse [case] in Bangladesh through [framework]."

### 6.2 Analytical Framework — The Peace Studies Matrix

For any exam question, consider:

| Dimension | Question to Ask | Frameworks |
|-----------|----------------|------------|
| **Violence** | What forms of violence are present? | Violence triangle (Galtung) |
| **Conflict** | What are the causes and dynamics? | ABC triangle, root causes, escalation |
| **Peace** | What kind of peace is being pursued? | Negative vs positive (Galtung) |
| **Level** | At what level is intervention needed? | Pyramid (Lederach) |
| **Approach** | What approach to peacebuilding? | Liberal, local, hybrid, authoritarian |
| **Culture** | Whose peace concepts are being used? | Non-Western/critical (Module 8) |
| **Justice** | How are past abuses being addressed? | TJ pillars (Module 9) |
| **Bangladesh** | How does the Bangladesh experience inform this? | Cases across all modules |

### 6.3 Common Traps

| Trap | Why It's Wrong | Better Approach |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| "Peace = absence of war" | Only negative peace — ignores structural violence | Define both negative and positive peace; show relationship |
| "Galtung's framework solves everything" | Violence triangle is conceptual, not empirical | Acknowledge critiques; use the framework critically |
| "Liberal peace is always wrong" | Liberal peace has had some successes | Evaluate with evidence — use specific cases (Liberia success, Mali failure) |
| "All non-Western peace is good" | Romanticises local traditions | Treat non-Western approaches critically (Buddhist nationalism in Myanmar) |
| "Bangladesh is unique" | Bangladesh is not incomparable | Use Bangladesh to illustrate broader patterns; compare with other cases |

### 6.4 Sample Essay Plan

**Question**: "Critically evaluate Galtung's distinction between negative and positive peace. Is positive peace a realistic goal?"

| PEEEL Paragraph | Content |
|-----------------|---------|
| **Point 1**: Distinction is foundational | Galtung (1964, 1969) — negative peace = no war; positive peace = no structural violence + justice |
| **Evidence**: CHT case | Negative peace achieved (ceasefire 1997); positive peace remains incomplete (land, autonomy, cultural recognition) |
| **Evidence**: South Africa | Negative peace (end of apartheid 1994); positive peace limited (economic inequality, racial division) |
| **Point 2**: Critique — too broad | If peace = justice, is it distinguishable from development? |
| **Point 3**: Defence | Galtung's response: peace research must focus on violence broadly — different forms require different responses |
| **Point 4**: Positive peace as regulative ideal | Not fully achievable but orients policy toward structural change |
| **Link**: To course themes | Power, universality, and the limits of liberal peace |

## Summary

Contemporary peace and conflict studies faces new challenges — climate change, technology, authoritarian peace, and the unfinished WPS agenda — that require rethinking established frameworks. The liberal peace orthodoxy of the 1990s is fragmenting, giving way to a more contested, plural field. Non-Western and critical approaches have enriched the field but face their own limitations. Bangladesh continues to be a rich source of cases — from climate vulnerability through peacekeeping to transitional justice. The enduring challenge for peace studies is to remain analytically rigorous, normatively committed, and contextually sensitive — balancing universal principles with local realities, and the goal of peace with the demands of justice.
