# Module 8 — Non-Western and Critical Approaches to Peace

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 8 — Non-Western and Critical Approaches to Peace
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–4

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Identify at least four non-Western peace traditions
2. Explain how feminist approaches transform peace and security studies
3. Analyse postcolonial and decolonial critiques of liberal peace
4. Apply non-Western peace concepts to Bangladesh
5. Critically evaluate the relationship between culture, power, and peace

---

## 1. Why Non-Western Approaches Matter

### 1.1 The Problem of Eurocentrism

Peace and conflict studies has been dominated by Western concepts, thinkers, and experiences:
- The foundational thinkers are European (Galtung, Boulding) or North American (Lederach, Burton, Zartman)
- The key cases are European or North American (World Wars, Cold War)
- The institutions are Western-led (UN, EU, NATO)
- The theoretical frameworks are rooted in Western philosophy (Kant, liberal theory)

**The consequence**: Peace studies has been shaped by the experiences and perspectives of the West — but most contemporary conflicts are in the Global South, and most peacebuilding is done in non-Western contexts. This creates a fundamental mismatch between the concepts and the reality they seek to explain.

### 1.2 What Are Non-Western Approaches?

Non-Western approaches to peace draw on:
- **Cultural traditions**: Indigenous, religious, and philosophical traditions that predate and exist outside Western modernity
- **Anti-colonial struggles**: Freedom movements that developed alternative theories of peace, justice, and liberation
- **Postcolonial critique**: Recovering histories, concepts, and practices that Western peace studies has marginalised
- **Local knowledge**: Everyday peace practices that are embedded in specific communities

## 2. Major Non-Western Peace Traditions

### 2.1 Satyagraha (Gandhi)

Mahatma Gandhi's *satyagraha* ("truth-force") is the most influential non-Western peace philosophy:

| Principle | Meaning | Implication |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| *Ahimsa* | Non-violence — not passivity but active resistance | Violence is self-defeating — it destroys both the perpetrator and the victim |
| *Satya* | Truth — the moral universe is structured by truth | Non-violence works because it aligns with the nature of reality |
| *Tapasya* | Self-suffering — willingness to suffer without retaliation | The suffering of the non-violent activist transforms the oppressor's conscience |
| *Sarvodaya* | Welfare of all | Justice cannot be partial — it must serve everyone, including the oppressor |

**Key features of satyagraha**:
- **Active, not passive**: Satyagraha is not submission — it is the most active form of resistance, demanding more courage than violence
- **Means and ends are inseparable**: Violent means cannot produce peaceful ends — the method must be consistent with the goal
- **Transforms relationships**: The goal is not to defeat the opponent but to transform the relationship — to convert, not coerce
- **Builds community**: Satyagraha requires collective discipline, organisation, and solidarity

**Gandhi's limitations**:
- **Context-specific**: Gandhi's strategy was designed for British colonialism — it is not universally applicable (did not work against Nazi Germany, does not work against ISIS)
- **Elite bias**: Gandhi was a charismatic, upper-caste leader — satyagraha was not always accessible to the most marginalised
- **Unrealistic expectations**: Satyagraha requires extraordinary moral discipline that cannot be expected of ordinary people under threat

### 2.2 Ubuntu (African)

*Ubuntu* — from the Zulu/Xhosa proverb "umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" — "a person is a person through other persons":

| Principle | Meaning | Peacebuilding Implication |
|-----------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Interconnectedness** | Human beings are constituted through relationships | Peace is not individual security but relational well-being |
| **Reciprocity** | Mutual obligation and care | Justice must restore relationships, not just punish |
| **Dialogue** | Conflict resolution through communal discussion | The community, not just the parties, is involved |
| **Restorative justice** | Healing, not retribution | South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) drew on Ubuntu |

**Ubuntu in peacebuilding**:
- South Africa's TRC was explicitly based on Ubuntu — balancing truth, accountability, and reconciliation
- Gacaca courts in Rwanda were rooted in indigenous restorative justice (adapted for the post-genocide context)
- Ubuntu challenges the Western emphasis on individual rights and retributive justice

### 2.3 Sulh (Islamic Peace Traditions)

| Concept | Meaning | Practice |
|---------|---------|----------|
| *Sulh* | Reconciliation, settlement | Mediation by respected community figures; restoration of relationships |
| *Salaam* | Peace (from submission to divine will) | Not just absence of war but wholeness, well-being, harmony |
| *Musalaha* | Reconciliation, conflict resolution | Tribal reconciliation processes in Palestine, Yemen, Afghanistan |
| *Qisas* | Retributive justice | "Eye for an eye" — but can be waived through *diya* (compensation) |
| *Diya* | Blood money/compensation | Alternative to retributive justice — used in tribal reconciliation |

**Application**: Sulh practices are widely used in Muslim societies for community-level conflict resolution — including in Bangladesh, where *shalish* (village mediation) draws on both Islamic and Bengali traditions.

### 2.4 Buddhist Peace Traditions

| Principle | Meaning | Political implication |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| *Ahimsa* | Non-harm | Non-violence as a fundamental ethical commitment |
| *Karma* | Actions have consequences | Peace is cultivated, not imposed — it requires ethical action |
| *Interdependence* | All things are connected | Conflict is not just between parties but reflects systemic imbalance |
| *Compassion* | Empathy with all beings | Peacebuilding must be grounded in compassion, not just technique |
| *Mindfulness* | Present-moment awareness | Peace requires awareness of one's own emotions, biases, and reactions |

**Ashoka's edicts (3rd century BCE)**: The Indian emperor Ashoka, after the Kalinga war, converted to Buddhism and established the first known state policy of non-violence, religious tolerance, and welfare — sometimes called the first "peacebuilding state."

**Bangladesh connection**: The Buddhist *sangha* (monastic community) in the Chittagong Hill Tracts provides a model of community-based peacebuilding that predates Western peacebuilding concepts.

## 3. Critical Approaches

### 3.1 Feminist Peace

| Scholar | Contribution | Key Idea |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Elise Boulding** | *Cultures of Peace* (2000) | Women have been peacebuilders throughout history but their work is invisible |
| **Cynthia Enloe** | *Bananas, Beaches and Bases* (1990) | International politics is gendered — peace research must ask "where are the women?" |
| **Carol Cohn** | "Sex and Death in the Rational World of Defense Intellectuals" (1987) | The language of security is masculinised — phallic imagery, rationalised violence |
| **Cynthia Cockburn** | *From Where We Stand: War, Women's Activism and Feminist Analysis* (2007) | Women's peace activism is a form of knowledge production — not just practice |

**Key feminist contributions to peace studies**:

1. **Militarised masculinity**: The idea that war is linked to masculine identity. Boys are socialised to be aggressive, competitive, and violent. Military service is a "test of manhood." Peace is feminised (weak, passive). Changing this requires changing gender socialisation.

2. **Women, Peace and Security (WPS) Agenda**: UNSCR 1325 (2000) and subsequent resolutions (1820, 1888, 1889, 1960, 2106, 2122, 2242, 2467, 2493):
   - **Participation**: Women must be included in peace processes (currently <20% of negotiators are women)
   - **Protection**: Women and girls are disproportionately affected by conflict (sexual violence, displacement)
   - **Prevention**: Gender equality prevents conflict — peace is more sustainable when women are empowered
   - **Relief and recovery**: Post-conflict reconstruction must address gender-specific needs

3. **Gendering structural violence**: Feminist analysis shows that structural violence is gendered — economic inequality, political exclusion, cultural subordination affect women differently and more intensely

### 3.2 Postcolonial and Decolonial Approaches

| Thinker | Key Work | Contribution |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Frantz Fanon** | *The Wretched of the Earth* (1961) | Colonial violence and the necessity of anti-colonial violence; challenged Gandhian non-violence |
| **Edward Said** | *Orientalism* (1978) | Western representations of the "Orient" as backward, violent — justifying intervention |
| **Arturo Escobar** | *Encountering Development* (1995) | Development as a discourse of Western dominance — peacebuilding as a form of development |
| **Caroline Thomas** | *Global Governance, Development and Human Security* (2000) | Security is not just military — it is freedom from want |
| **Siba Grovogui** | *Sovereigns, Quasi Sovereigns, and Africans* (1996) | The international system is structured by colonial hierarchy |

**Key critiques**:

- **Liberal peace as neocolonialism**: The liberal peace agenda (democracy, markets, rule of law) is a continuation of the "civilising mission" — imposing Western models on non-Western societies under the banner of peace
- **Epistemic violence**: Western peace studies marginalises non-Western knowledge systems — it is a form of intellectual colonialism that assumes only the West has valuable knowledge about peace
- **The "local" as resistance**: Postcolonial approaches reframe the "local turn" — local actors are not passive recipients of peacebuilding but agents who resist, adapt, and subvert international peacebuilding
- **Structural violence is global**: The violence of colonialism, slavery, and ongoing global inequality is not past — it is the structural context in which peacebuilding operates

### 3.3 Liberation Theology

- **Rooted in Latin American Christianity** (Gustavo Gutiérrez, Leonardo Boff): Peace requires liberation from structural sin — poverty, oppression, injustice
- **Influenced peacebuilding**: Insisted on the link between peace and justice — no peace without structural transformation
- **Bangladesh relevance**: Liberation theology resonates in Bangladesh's Muslim-majority context through Islamic social justice traditions and Christian minority peacebuilding

## 4. Non-Western and Critical Approaches Compared

| Approach | Source | View of Peace | Strategy | Critique |
|----------|--------|---------------|----------|----------|
| **Satyagraha** | Hindu/Jain, Gandhi | Truth, non-violence, welfare of all | Active non-violent resistance | Context-specific, elite, demanding |
| **Ubuntu** | African philosophy | Relational well-being | Restorative justice, dialogue | May not scale to national level |
| **Sulh/Islamic** | Islamic law and tradition | Reconciliation, submission to divine | Community mediation, compensation | Can be patriarchal, conservative |
| **Buddhist** | Buddhist philosophy | Interdependence, compassion | Mindfulness, non-harm | Apolitical, individualistic |
| **Feminist** | Feminist theory and activism | Gender justice, non-hierarchical | Participation, WPS agenda | Liberal feminism co-opted |
| **Postcolonial** | Anti-colonial struggle, critical theory | Liberation from colonial structures | Resistance, decolonisation | Risks paralysis — all peace is complicity |
| **Liberation theology** | Latin American Christianity | Justice, liberation from structural sin | Preferential option for the poor | Religiously specific |

## 5. Bangladesh Applications

| Tradition | Bangladesh Manifestation | Peacebuilding Relevance |
|-----------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **Satyagraha** | Language Movement 1952 (non-violent protest for Bengali language); 1990 mass uprising | Non-violent resistance as a political tradition |
| **Sulh/Shalish** | Village-level mediation (*shalish*) by local elders | Community-level, low-cost, culturally legitimate — but can be patriarchal |
| **Buddhist peace** | Buddhist *sangha* in CHT | Inter-community peacebuilding (indigenous communities, Bengali-Buddhist relations) |
| **Feminist** | Women peacekeepers, civil society women's organisations | WPS implementation, gender-sensitive peacebuilding |
| **Postcolonial** | 1971 liberation as anti-colonial struggle | National identity shaped by anti-colonial resistance, sovereignty consciousness |

## 6. Critiques and Synthesis

### 6.1 The Romanticisation Problem

Non-Western approaches can be romanticised — presented as inherently peaceful, authentic, and superior to Western approaches. In reality:
- Gandhi was not anti-violence — he supported the British in WWI and the Indian army in Kashmir
- Ubuntu was used by the post-apartheid elite to deflect from economic justice (reconciliation without redistribution)
- Islamic peace traditions coexist with Islamic extremism
- Buddhist nationalism has been implicated in violence against Rohingya in Myanmar

### 6.2 The "Picking and Choosing" Problem

Peacebuilders often "pick and choose" elements of non-Western traditions that fit Western preferences — restorative justice (Ubuntu) is celebrated, but the political traditions of the same societies are ignored. This is a form of cultural appropriation.

### 6.3 Towards Synthesis

The most productive approach is not "Western OR non-Western" but **critical synthesis** — using the best analytical tools from all traditions while being aware of their limitations and contexts. Peacebuilding in Bangladesh, for example, draws on Islamic mediation traditions *and* Western conflict resolution frameworks *and* feminist peacebuilding *and* postcolonial critique. The interaction between these traditions, not any single one, produces contextually appropriate peacebuilding.

---

## Summary

Non-Western and critical approaches challenge the Eurocentrism of mainstream peace studies. Gandhi's satyagraha, Ubuntu, Islamic *sulh*, and Buddhist peace traditions offer alternative frameworks rooted in different cultural and philosophical traditions. Feminist approaches expose the gendered nature of violence and peacebuilding. Postcolonial critiques reveal the ways peacebuilding can perpetuate colonial power relations. The challenge is not to replace Western approaches with non-Western ones but to create a more plural, critical peace studies that draws on the full range of human experience.
