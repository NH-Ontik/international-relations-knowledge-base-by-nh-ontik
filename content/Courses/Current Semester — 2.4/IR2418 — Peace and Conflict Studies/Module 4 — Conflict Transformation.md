# Module 4 — Conflict Transformation

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 4 — Conflict Transformation
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 1–3 (foundations, violence, conflict analysis)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Define conflict transformation and distinguish it from resolution and management
2. Explain Lederach's four dimensions of transformation
3. Analyse the role of middle-range actors in peacebuilding
4. Apply conflict transformation to the Bangladesh context
5. Critically evaluate the transformation approach

---

## 1. From Resolution to Transformation

### 1.1 Why Transformation?

Conflict transformation emerged as a critique of two earlier approaches:

| Approach | Goal | Key Assumption | Limitation |
|----------|------|---------------|------------|
| **Conflict resolution** | End conflict, solve the problem | Conflict is a problem to be solved | May impose solutions that don't address root causes |
| **Conflict management** | Contain/limit conflict | Conflict is inevitable but can be managed | Leaves underlying issues unaddressed |
| **Conflict transformation** | Change relationships and structures | Conflict is a motor for positive change | Resource-intensive, long-term, difficult to evaluate |

**The shift**: In the 1990s, peace scholars and practitioners (Lederach 1997, Galtung 1996, Miall 2004) argued that "resolution" and "management" were inadequate. Conflict is not a problem to be eliminated but a natural social process that can be constructive or destructive. The goal is not to resolve conflict but to **transform it** — to channel its energy toward positive social change.

### 1.2 Lederach's Key Contribution

John Paul Lederach's *Building Peace: Sustainable Reconciliation in Divided Societies* (1997) is the foundational text of conflict transformation. Lederach draws on his experience as a mediator in Central America, Africa, and Asia to develop a framework that is:
- **Relational**: Focuses on the quality of relationships, not just the content of the dispute
- **Process-oriented**: Peace is not an end-state but an ongoing process of transformation
- **Contextual**: Strategies must be rooted in the specific cultural and historical context
- **Multi-level**: Transformation must happen at all levels of society

## 2. Lederach's Four Dimensions of Transformation

Conflict transformation operates across four interconnected dimensions:

| Dimension | Focus | Questions |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **Personal** | Individual attitudes, emotions, perspectives | How has conflict affected me personally? What have I lost? How have I changed? |
| **Relational** | Relationships between parties | How do we communicate? How do we perceive each other? What is our history? |
| **Structural** | Social, political, economic institutions | What structures perpetuate conflict? What changes are needed? |
| **Cultural** | Cultural patterns that shape conflict | What cultural resources can support peace? What cultural patterns legitimise violence? |

### 2.1 Personal Transformation

Conflict changes individuals. It can create trauma, anger, hatred — but it can also create insight, growth, and commitment to change. Personal transformation involves:
- **Acknowledging grief and loss** — Recognising what has been destroyed
- **Overcoming victimhood** — Moving from "we are victims" to "we are agents"
- **Transforming enemy images** — Seeing the humanity of the "other"
- **Forgiveness and healing** — At individual level (not necessarily reconciliation)

### 2.2 Relational Transformation

Conflict damages relationships. Transformation involves rebuilding the quality of interaction between parties:
- **Communication**: From adversarial to cooperative communication
- **Trust-building**: From suspicion to confidence
- **Empathy**: From demonisation to understanding (not agreement)
- **Interdependence**: From zero-sum to positive-sum thinking

**Lederach's key insight**: Relational transformation is not about "being friends" — it is about developing the capacity to handle conflict constructively.

### 2.3 Structural Transformation

Conflict reflects and is shaped by social, political, and economic structures. Structural transformation involves changing the institutions and systems that generate or perpetuate conflict:
- **Political**: Power-sharing, decentralisation, inclusive governance
- **Economic**: Redistribution, land reform, livelihood support
- **Social**: Anti-discrimination laws, education reform, media reform
- **Legal**: Constitutional reform, transitional justice mechanisms

**Example — CHT**: The 1997 CHT Peace Accord aimed at structural transformation: Regional Council, District Councils, Land Commission, devolution of authority. But incomplete implementation means structural transformation remains partial.

### 2.4 Cultural Transformation

Culture is both a source of conflict (cultural violence) and a resource for peace (cultural peace). Cultural transformation involves:
- **Identifying cultural resources for peace**: Religious traditions of reconciliation, indigenous conflict resolution practices, art and music for peace
- **Challenging cultural violence**: Deconstructing nationalist ideologies, racist discourses, militaristic values
- **Creating a culture of peace**: Peace education, media for peace, inter-cultural dialogue

## 3. The Peacebuilding Pyramid

Lederach's most influential framework is the peacebuilding pyramid, which identifies three levels of actors and appropriate strategies for each:

```
        Top Level (Level 1)
       Military, political,
       religious leaders
          (Elite level)
         /              \
        /                \
       /    Middle Range  \
      /    (Level 2)       \
     /  NGOs, academics,     \
    /  religious leaders,     \
   /   ethnic leaders          \
  /______________________________\
  Grassroots (Level 3)
  Community leaders, local
  peace committees, women's
  groups, youth groups
```

### 3.1 Top Level (Elite)

- **Who**: Political leaders, military commanders, senior religious figures
- **Strengths**: Authority to negotiate, sign agreements, command compliance
- **Weaknesses**: Captured by political interests, less flexible, public positions hard to change
- **Strategies**: Track I diplomacy, high-level mediation, ceasefire negotiations
- **Example**: The 1997 CHT accord negotiations between the Bangladesh government and PCJSS

### 3.2 Middle Range (Level 2 — Lederach's Priority)

- **Who**: NGO leaders, academics, religious leaders, ethnic leaders, business leaders
- **Strengths**: Connected to both elite and grassroots; less constrained by political positions; can generate innovative solutions
- **Weaknesses**: May lack authority to enforce agreements; can be co-opted
- **Strategies**: Problem-solving workshops, Track II dialogue, conflict resolution training, peace commissions
- **Why Lederach prioritises this level**: Middle-range actors are best positioned to bridge the gap between top-level negotiations and grassroots reality. They have access to both levels but are not captured by either.

### 3.3 Grassroots (Level 3)

- **Who**: Community leaders, local peace committees, women's groups, youth groups, indigenous leaders
- **Strengths**: Deep local knowledge, trust, sustainability
- **Weaknesses**: Limited influence on national-level decisions; resource constraints
- **Strategies**: Local peace committees, community dialogue, trauma healing, inter-community exchange

### 3.4 Bangladesh in the Peacebuilding Pyramid

| Level | Actors | Activity |
|-------|--------|----------|
| **Top** | Prime Minister, opposition leaders, military, CHT task force | CHT accord negotiations (1997), ongoing political dialogue |
| **Middle** | BRAC, Centre for Peace and Justice (BRACU), Ain o Salish Kendra, SUF, academic researchers | Peace research, advocacy, training, mediation support |
| **Grassroots** | CHT village peace committees, local women's organisations, madrasa teachers, NGO field workers | Cross-community dialogue, conflict resolution, trauma healing |

**Critical point**: Bangladesh has strong middle-range and grassroots peacebuilding capacity (especially through BRAC and NGOs) but weak institutional mechanisms linking these levels to elite decision-making.

## 4. Conflict Transformation in Practice

### 4.1 The Transformative Mediation Model

Lederach's mediation approach differs from conventional models:

| Conventional Mediation | Transformative Mediation |
|-----------------------|-------------------------|
| Focus on agreement | Focus on relationship |
| Problem-solving orientation | Empowerment and recognition |
| Neutral third party | Active facilitator |
| Parties as rational actors | Parties as emotional beings |
| Solution imposed/designed by mediator | Solution generated by parties |
| Short-term resolution | Long-term capacity-building |

### 4.2 Applied — CHT Peace Process

The CHT peace process illustrates the strengths and limits of conflict transformation:

- **Personal transformation**: Individual PCJSS leaders and government negotiators developed mutual understanding through years of negotiation — but this did not always translate to their constituencies
- **Relational transformation**: The accord created new channels of communication — but trust remains limited between Bengali and indigenous communities
- **Structural transformation**: New institutions (Regional Council, District Councils, Land Commission) created — but implementation has been slow and partial
- **Cultural transformation**: Some progress in recognising indigenous cultures (Hill Day, indigenous language rights) — but Bengali settlement and assimilation pressure continues

**Lesson**: The CHT accord succeeded in achieving negative peace (end of armed conflict) but has not yet achieved positive peace (structural justice, cultural recognition). This is a typical pattern in peace processes — negative peace comes first, positive peace is a longer struggle.

### 4.3 Applied — Bangladesh Political Violence

Bangladesh's cycle of political violence (hartals, election violence, crackdowns) is resistant to transformation because:
- **Personal**: Deep personal animosity between party leaders
- **Relational**: Zero-sum political competition; communication channels broken
- **Structural**: Winner-take-all political system; weak institutions (EC, judiciary); no power-sharing norms
- **Cultural**: Political identity as total identity; "us vs them" framing; violence as legitimate political tool

**A conflict transformation approach** would prioritise middle-range actors (civil society, business, religious leaders) to create Track II processes that could prepare the ground for structural reform (electoral reform, institutional strengthening, power-sharing mechanisms).

## 5. Critiques of Conflict Transformation

### 5.1 Conceptual Critiques

- **Too ambitious**: Transformation demands change at personal, relational, structural, and cultural levels simultaneously — an almost impossible standard. Most peacebuilding achieves change at one or two levels at most.
- **Under-theorises power**: Lederach's framework assumes that "transformation" is a neutral good — but some parties benefit from the existing conflict system (warlords, arms dealers, political elites who gain from polarisation). Transformation threatens their interests, and they will resist.
- **Western/Christian bias**: Lederach draws heavily on his Mennonite Christian background (reconciliation, forgiveness, relationship). Critics argue the framework may not be universally applicable.

### 5.2 Practical Critiques

- **Difficult to evaluate**: How do you measure "transformed relationships"? The field's emphasis on qualitative, process-oriented evaluation makes it hard to demonstrate impact.
- **Requires long time horizons**: Genuine transformation can take decades. Donors and policymakers want quick results. This creates a tension between transformation ideals and the practical demands of funding cycles.
- **Co-optation risk**: Middle-range actors can be co-opted by elite interests or grassroots demands. The "bridge" becomes a "buffer" that absorbs pressure rather than transmitting it.

### 5.3 Global South Critique

- **Resource-intensive**: Transformation requires sustained investment in human relationships, capacity-building, and long-term presence. Most conflict-affected countries lack these resources.
- **May ignore structural violence**: The emphasis on "relationship" can divert attention from material injustice. Reconciliation without redistribution can be hollow.
- **Bangladesh relevance**: The CHT experience suggests that without structural change (land rights, devolution, economic justice), relational transformation is fragile. This supports the critique that transformation must prioritise the structural dimension.

---

## Summary

Conflict transformation represents a shift from seeing conflict as a problem to be solved to seeing conflict as a motor for positive change. Lederach's framework identifies four dimensions of transformation (personal, relational, structural, cultural) and three levels of actors (top, middle, grassroots), with the middle range as the strategic priority. The approach has been influential in peacebuilding practice, especially in contexts where standard resolution and management approaches have failed. Critiques highlight its ambitious scope, under-theorisation of power, and resource demands. The CHT peace process in Bangladesh illustrates both the potential and the limits of conflict transformation.
