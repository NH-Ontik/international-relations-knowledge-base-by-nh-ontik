# Module 6 — Track Diplomacy and Mediation

## Course Relevance
- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies
- **Module:** 6 — Track Diplomacy and Mediation
- **Prerequisites:** Modules 3–4 (conflict analysis, transformation)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this module, students will be able to:
1. Distinguish between Track I, Track II, and Multi-Track diplomacy
2. Explain the key conditions for effective mediation (ripeness, leverage, timing)
3. Analyse the strengths and limitations of different mediation strategies
4. Evaluate Track II diplomacy's role in conflict resolution
5. Apply mediation and Track II concepts to Bangladesh and South Asian conflicts

## 1. Track I Diplomacy

### 1.1 Definition and Characteristics

Track I diplomacy is official, government-to-government diplomacy conducted by state representatives:

| Feature | Characteristic |
|---------|---------------|
| **Actors** | State officials — diplomats, ministers, heads of state |
| **Venue** | Formal — embassies, summits, international organisations |
| **Communication** | Official diplomatic channels |
| **Authority** | Binding — representatives can make commitments on behalf of states |
| **Audience** | Governments and international public |
| **Tools** | Negotiation, mediation, arbitration, adjudication, sanctions |

### 1.2 Mediation as Track I

Mediation is "a process of conflict management where disputants seek the assistance of, or accept an offer of help from, an individual, group, state, or organisation to resolve their conflict without resorting to physical force or invoking the authority of the law" (Bercovitch 1997).

**Types of mediators**:
| Mediator Type | Examples | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---------------|----------|-----------|------------|
| **States** | US, Norway, Turkey | Resources, leverage, political will | Self-interest, bias |
| **International organisations** | UN, AU, EU | Legitimacy, multilateral, norms | Bureaucratic, slow |
| **Regional organisations** | ASEAN, SAARC, OIC | Regional knowledge, proximity | Limited capacity, internal divisions |
| **Individuals** | Carter, Mitchell, Annan | Flexibility, creativity, persistence | No institutional backing |

### 1.3 Zartman's Ripeness Theory

I. William Zartman's ripeness theory (1989, 2000) is the most influential framework for understanding when mediation can succeed.

**The core claim**: Parties will only negotiate seriously when they perceive a **Mutually Hurting Stalemate (MHS)** — a situation where:
1. Both parties are locked in a conflict that they cannot win militarily
2. Continued conflict is costly and painful for both
3. Both parties perceive that the pain will continue or worsen
4. There is a **Way Out (WO)** — a credible, attractive alternative to continued conflict

**Ripeness is perceptual** — it is not an objective condition but a subjective assessment by the parties. Mediators can help create ripeness by:
- **Increasing pain**: Sanctions, threat of intervention, arming the opponent
- **Offering a way out**: Guarantees, incentives, face-saving mechanisms
- **Highlighting the costs**: Information about the human and economic costs of continued conflict

**Critiques of ripeness**:
- **Static**: Ripeness is not a single moment but a dynamic process that can be created, lost, and re-created
- **Elite-focused**: Parties may be ripe at the leadership level but not at the constituency level — and vice versa
- **Self-fulfilling**: If a mediator believes a conflict is "not ripe," they will not engage — and the conflict continues

### 1.4 Mediation Strategies

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Facilitation** | Low intervention — convene meetings, manage communication, clarify issues | Parties ready to negotiate, low mistrust |
| **Formulation** | Moderate intervention — propose options, suggest language, frame issues | Parties stuck but open to suggestions |
| **Manipulation** | High intervention — use leverage, pressure, incentives, threats | Parties unwilling or unable to reach agreement alone |

## 2. Track II Diplomacy

### 2.1 Definition

Track II diplomacy is "unofficial, non-structured interaction between members of adversary groups or nations with the goal of developing strategies, influencing public opinion, and organising human and material resources in ways that might help resolve the conflict" (Montville 1991).

| Feature | Track I | Track II |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Actors** | Official state representatives | Unofficial — academics, NGOs, religious leaders |
| **Venue** | Formal, official | Informal, unofficial |
| **Communication** | Public/formal | Private/confidential |
| **Authority** | Can make binding commitments | Cannot make commitments |
| **Flexibility** | Limited by official positions | High — can explore ideas without commitment |
| **Accountability** | To governments, electorates | To participants and their communities |

### 2.2 Track II Functions

| Function | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Problem-solving workshops** | Facilitated dialogue between influential individuals from conflicting parties | Burton's workshops (Malaysian/Indonesian, Israeli/Palestinian) |
| **Conflict resolution training** | Building negotiation, mediation, and dialogue skills | Local peacebuilding training |
| **Policy dialogue** | Informal policy discussions | US-China security dialogues |
| **People-to-people** | Grassroots exchange programmes | Israeli-Palestinian youth encounters |
| **Media and public diplomacy** | Changing public discourse | Peace journalism, public opinion research |

### 2.3 Track II in Practice — The Norway Channel (Oslo Accords)

The Oslo Accords (1993) are the most famous Track II success:

- **Process**: Norwegian academics (Terje Rød-Larsen, Mona Juul) facilitated secret talks between Israeli academics (Yair Hirschfeld, Ron Pundak) and PLO officials (Ahmed Qurei) in Norway (1992–1993)
- **From Track II to Track I**: Informal talks became official when they produced the Oslo Declaration of Principles — signed at the White House (September 1993)
- **Lessons**: Track II can lay the groundwork for Track I by creating relationships, testing ideas, and building trust
- **Limitations**: The Oslo process ultimately failed — Track II created the framework, but implementation required political will that was not sustained

### 2.4 Track II in South Asia

- **India-Pakistan**: Track II dialogues have been extensive — Neemrana Dialogues (1991–present), Chaophraya Dialogues (Thailand), Pakistan-India Track II dialogues. They have kept communication open during diplomatic breakdowns but have not produced breakthroughs.
- **Bangladesh-India**: Track II mechanisms exist through academic exchanges, the Bangladesh-India Dialogue (Centre for Policy Dialogue), and water-sharing discussions — but have limited impact on core issues (Teesta, border management).
- **Sri Lanka**: Norwegian Track II (which became Track I) facilitated the 2002 Ceasefire Agreement — but the process collapsed in 2009.

## 3. Multi-Track Diplomacy

Multi-Track diplomacy (Diamond & McDonald 1996) recognises that peace requires activity across multiple tracks simultaneously:

| Track | Focus | Actors |
|-------|-------|--------|
| **Track 1** | Government/official | Diplomats, leaders |
| **Track 2** | Unofficial/nongovernmental | NGOs, academics |
| **Track 3** | Business/commercial | Private sector |
| **Track 4** | Personal/individual | Citizens, activists |
| **Track 5** | Research/training | Peace researchers, trainers |
| **Track 6** | Activism/advocacy | Social movements |
| **Track 7** | Religion/faith | Religious leaders |
| **Track 8** | Funding/donors | Foundations, donors |
| **Track 9** | Media/public opinion | Journalists, media |

## 4. Conditions for Effective Mediation

### 4.1 Leverage

Leverage is the mediator's ability to influence the parties. Sources of leverage:
- **Carrots**: Incentives — aid, investment, political support, security guarantees
- **Sticks**: Costs — sanctions, aid cuts, legal action, military intervention
- **Persuasion**: Information, expertise, moral authority

**Bangladesh relevance**: When the World Bank withdrew from the Padma Bridge project (2012), Bangladesh chose self-financing — demonstrating that leverage is not one-directional. The borrower's "exit option" limits creditor leverage (see IR2417 Module 6).

### 4.2 Timing

- **Early mediation**: Can prevent escalation — but parties may not be ready to make concessions
- **Ripe moment mediation**: Parties are exhausted and ready — but making concessions requires political cover
- **Late mediation**: After maximum damage — parties may be willing but reconstruction costs are enormous

### 4.3 Impartiality vs Bias

| Approach | Argument | Evidence |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **Impartiality** | Mediators must be neutral to be trusted by both sides | UN mediation, good offices |
| **Bias as asset** | A biased mediator has leverage over "their" side | US mediation in Israel-Palestine; Norway's relationship with Israel and PLO |

### 4.4 Mediator Authority

| Type | Example | Advantage |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Mandated** | UN, AU (UN Charter Ch. VIII) | Legitimacy, resources |
| **Invited** | Norway in Sri Lanka, Carter in North Korea | Trust, flexibility |
| **Self-appointed** | Track II, private mediation | Independence, creativity |

## 5. Bangladesh and Track Diplomacy

| Context | Track | Activity | Assessment |
|---------|-------|----------|------------|
| **CHT peace process** | Mixed — official Track I (government-PCJSS) + Track II (civil society dialogue) | The 1997 accord was primarily Track I, but Track II created space for pre-negotiation and confidence-building | Effective — one of South Asia's few successful peace accords |
| **Bangladesh-India water sharing** | Track I (Joint Rivers Commission) + limited Track II (academic dialogues) | Ongoing negotiations on Teesta, Feni, border rivers | Limited progress — Track I stuck; Track II underutilised |
| **Bangladesh-Myanmar (Rohingya)** | UN Track I (UNSC, UNGA) + OIC + humanitarian Track II | ICJ case, UNF/RP, bilateral agreements | Track I has UN legitimacy but no enforcement; no sustainable Track II |
| **Domestic political violence** | Weak Track I (government-opposition dialogue); underdeveloped Track II | Civil society dialogue initiatives, election observation | Cyclical — no institutionalised dialogue mechanism |
| **BCIM (Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar corridor)** | Track I with limited Track II | Economic corridor dialogue | Stalled — no breakthrough |

**Bangladesh's capacity for Track II**: Bangladesh has a strong civil society (NGOs, think tanks, universities) with capacity for Track II work. The Centre for Policy Dialogue (CPD), Bangladesh Institute of Peace and Security Studies (BIPSS), and the Centre for Peace and Justice (BRACU) are potential Track II actors. However, Track II is underutilised — the state tends to centralise foreign policy and limit civil society's role in Track II.

## 6. Critiques of Track Diplomacy

| Critique | Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Elite capture** | Both Track I and Track II are dominated by elites — grassroots voices excluded | Multi-track framework attempts to address this, but in practice Track II is also elite |
| **No accountability** | Track II participants are not accountable to the populations they claim to represent | Track II is not representative — it is supplementary, not substitutional |
| **Co-optation** | Track II can be co-opted by government or donor agendas | Independence is essential but difficult to maintain |
| **Impact difficult to measure** | How do we know Track II works? Many Track II processes produce no tangible outcomes | Track II success is often invisible — crisis prevention, relationship-building, trust |
| **Liberal bias** | Track II assumes that communication and relationship-building will resolve conflict — this may not hold in power-based or identity-based conflicts | Track II is most effective when combined with structural change and political pressure |

---

## Summary

Track diplomacy spans a spectrum from official government negotiations (Track I) through unofficial civil society dialogue (Track II) to multi-track approaches that integrate business, religious, media, and other actors. Mediation is most likely to succeed when there is a "ripe moment" — a mutually hurting stalemate and a credible way out. Track II creates space for relationship-building, creative problem-solving, and pre-negotiation that Track I cannot provide. Bangladesh has had mixed success: the CHT peace process is a Track I success with Track II elements, but political violence and Rohingya crisis remain intractable.
