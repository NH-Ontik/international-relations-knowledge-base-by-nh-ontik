# Module 5 — Peacebuilding: Approaches and Practice

- **Course:** IR2418 — Peace and Conflict Studies

1. Define peacebuilding and distinguish it from related concepts
2. Trace the evolution of peacebuilding from the UN Agenda for Peace (1992) to the present

4. Analyse the "local turn" in peacebuilding
5. Compare top-down and bottom-up peacebuilding approaches
6. Apply peacebuilding concepts to Bangladesh

## 1. Defining Peacebuilding

### 1.1 Origin and Evolution

Peacebuilding entered UN discourse with Boutros-Ghali's *An Agenda for Peace* (1992), which defined it as "action to identify and support structures which will tend to strengthen and solidify peace in order to avoid a relapse into conflict."

| Era | Dominant Approach | Key Documents |
|-----|-------------------|---------------|
| **1992–2000** | Post-conflict reconstruction, liberal institution-building | *An Agenda for Peace* (1992), *Supplement* (1995), *Brahimi Report* (2000) |
| **2000–2010** | Expansion, Peacebuilding Commission (2005), integrated missions | *In Larger Freedom* (2005), *World Summit Outcome* (2005), *Capstone Doctrine* (2008) |
| **2010–2020** | Liberal peace critique, "local turn," hybrid peace | World Bank *World Development Report 2011*, *Sustaining Peace* resolutions (2016) |
| **2020–present** | Authoritarian peace, climate peacebuilding, technology | *Our Common Agenda* (2021), *New Agenda for Peace* (2023) |

### 1.2 The Peacebuilding Spectrum

Peacebuilding is one element of a broader peace continuum:

```
Conflict Prevention → Conflict Resolution → Peacemaking → Peacekeeping → Peacebuilding → Reconciliation
         |                                        |                |                |
    Early warning,                          Ceasefire,     Troop deployment,  Post-conflict
    structural prevention                   negotiation     monitoring        reconstruction
```

**Distinctions**:
- **Conflict prevention**: Actions taken *before* violent conflict erupts
- **Peacemaking**: Ceasefire and peace agreement negotiations (usually Track I)
- **Peacekeeping**: Third-party military/civilian deployment to monitor/implement agreements
- **Peacebuilding**: Long-term measures to address root causes and prevent recurrence
- **Reconciliation**: Healing relationships between former enemies

### 1.3 Four Pillars of Peacebuilding

The UN Peacebuilding Architecture (PBC, PBSO, PB Fund) identifies four pillars:
1. **Security**: DDR, SSR, mine action, small arms control
2. **Political**: Governance, rule of law, elections, constitutional reform, power-sharing
3. **Economic**: Livelihoods, employment, infrastructure, natural resource management
4. **Social**: Reconciliation, transitional justice, education, health, culture

## 2. The Liberal Peace

### 2.1 What Is the Liberal Peace?

The "liberal peace" is a set of assumptions that have dominated peacebuilding since the 1990s:

| Assumption | Mechanism | Critique |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| Democracy promotes peace | Elections, democratic institutions | Elections can fuel conflict (Bosnia, Angola) |
| Free markets promote peace | Economic liberalisation, private sector | Market reform creates inequality, winners/losers |
| Rule of law promotes peace | Legal reform, human rights | Law is culturally specific; Western legal models may not fit |
| Civil society promotes peace | NGO capacity-building, civil society strengthening | Civil society may not be representative or locally legitimate |

### 2.2 Roland Paris's Critique

Roland Paris's *At War's End* (2004) is the most influential critique of liberal peacebuilding. Paris argues that peacebuilders have been applying a "one-size-fits-all" model of liberalisation without adequate attention to local conditions. His key arguments:

- **The "Iraq syndrome"** : Post-conflict liberalisation can actually increase conflict — democratisation opens space for ethnic nationalism; market reform creates inequality and resentment
- **"Institutionalisation before liberalisation"** : Paris recommends building strong institutions *before* introducing elections and market reform. State-building before democratisation.
- **Case evidence**: Bosnia (elections strengthened ethnic parties), Cambodia (UNTAC created democracy without rule of law), Rwanda (post-genocide elections under RPF dominance)

### 2.3 The Liberal Peace Debate

| Position | Argument | Key Scholars |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Defenders** | Liberal peace works — the record is better than critics claim. Failures are due to inadequate resources, not flawed design | Doyle, Paris (qualified), UN |
| **Reformists** | Liberal peace can work but needs to be adapted to local conditions, made less technocratic | Paris 2004, Barnett 2006 |
| **Critical** | Liberal peace is a form of Western hegemony — it imposes Western models and perpetuates the inequalities that caused conflict | Duffield, Richmond, Pugh, Chandler |
| **Post-liberal** | Peacebuilding should be based on local agency, hybrid institutions, and everyday peace | Richmond, Mac Ginty, Björkdahl |

## 3. The Local Turn

### 3.1 Critique of Top-Down Peacebuilding

By the 2010s, it was widely recognised that liberal peacebuilding was failing:
- Peacebuilding missions in Bosnia, Cambodia, Sierra Leone, Liberia, and Timor-Leste had achieved negative peace but not sustainable positive peace
- Local ownership was more rhetoric than reality — peacebuilding was driven by international actors, Western models, and donor funding
- The gap between international peacebuilders and local populations was enormous — international staff lived in compounds, drove white SUVs, and had minimal contact with the populations they were supposed to help

### 3.2 What Is the "Local"?

The "local" is a contested concept:

| Conception | Definition | Critique |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| **Geographic local** | People living in the conflict-affected area | Not homogeneous — "local" includes beneficiaries and spoilers |
| **Cultural local** | Indigenous/traditional peace practices | Can be romanticised, patriarchal, or exclusionary |
| **Agency local** | Those who have the most at stake and the least power | Can be co-opted; not all locals want peace |
| **Hybrid local** | The interface between international and local | May perpetuate inequality if power imbalance not addressed |

### 3.3 Hybrid Peace

The hybrid peace approach (Mac Ginty 2011, Richmond 2011, Björkdahl 2012) argues:
- Peacebuilding outcomes are neither purely "international" nor purely "local" — they are **hybrid** — the product of negotiation, contestation, and compromise between international norms and local realities
- The goal should not be to impose liberal peace or defer entirely to local tradition — but to facilitate constructive interaction between the two
- Hybridity is not necessarily positive — some hybrid arrangements entrench inequality or violence

**Example — Bangladesh CHT**: The CHT accord is a hybrid arrangement: it incorporates international norms (indigenous rights, autonomy) but is implemented through Bangladesh's constitutional framework (unitary state, Bengali nationalism). The result is an imperfect but functioning hybrid.

## 4. Peacebuilding Approaches Compared

### 4.1 Top-Down vs Bottom-Up

| Dimension | Top-Down (Liberal Peace) | Bottom-Up (Local Peace) |
|-----------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| **Driven by** | International organisations, donors, states | Local communities, civil society |
| **Focus** | Institutions, elections, rule of law | Relationships, culture, everyday needs |
| **Modality** | Programs, funding, technical assistance | Dialogue, mediation, community organising |
| **Temporality** | Short-medium term (project cycles) | Long-term (decades) |
| **Accountability** | Upward (to donors) | Downward (to communities) |
| **Strengths** | Resources, expertise, legitimacy (international) | Local knowledge, trust, sustainability |
| **Weaknesses** | Culturally insensitive, extractive, fragile | Under-resourced, limited political influence |

### 4.2 The Sustaining Peace Agenda

The 2016 "Sustaining Peace" resolutions (GA Res. 70/262, SC Res. 2282) shifted UN peacebuilding from a reactive (post-conflict) to a preventive (continuous) approach:
- Peacebuilding is not a post-conflict phase — it is a continuous process of preventing conflict at all stages
- Focus on national ownership, inclusive politics, and conflict-sensitive development
- Integrated across the UN system — not just the PBC but all UN agencies

### 4.3 Gender and Peacebuilding

- **UNSCR 1325 (2000)**: Women, Peace and Security Agenda — requires women's participation in peacebuilding
- **Critique**: Women are still largely excluded from peace negotiations (participation as mediators or negotiators is < 20% of peace processes include women meaningfully)
- **Women's peacebuilding**: Women's organisations often do the most effective grassroots peacebuilding — but are the least funded and least recognised
- **Bangladesh**: Women peacekeepers are a Bangladesh success story (top contributor of female peacekeepers); civil society women's organisations (e.g., Nari Pokkho, BLAST, ASK) are active in peacebuilding

## 5. Bangladesh Peacebuilding Cases

| Case | Approach | Successes | Limitations |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Post-1971 reconstruction** | Top-down, state-led | Nation-building, constitutional consensus, economic recovery | Reconciliation with collaborators incomplete; 1975 political breakdown |
| **CHT peace process** | Mixed (top-down accord + local implementation) | Ceasefire, institutional framework (Regional Council), cultural recognition | Partial implementation, land commission slow |
| **Rohingya humanitarian response** | Primarily humanitarian, not peacebuilding | Massive shelter and relief (1M refugees), international partnerships | No peacebuilding dimension — no resolution of structural causes in Myanmar |
| **Political violence** | Weak peacebuilding — primarily security-focused | Avoidance of civil war, constitutional order maintained | Cycle of election violence, extrajudicial killings, polarisation |

## 6. Critiques of Peacebuilding

### 6.1 The Neoliberal Critique

Peacebuilding has been criticised as a neoliberal project (Duffield 2001, Pugh 2005):
- Peacebuilding operations are coupled with neoliberal economic reforms (liberalisation, privatisation, deregulation)
- This creates a contradiction: peacebuilding aims to stabilise conflict-affected societies, but neoliberal reforms increase inequality and insecurity
- "Liberal peace is not peace — it is the continuation of war by other means" (Pugh)

### 6.2 The Authoritarian Peace Critique

Rising powers (China, Russia) and authoritarian states promote an alternative model:
- **Authoritarian peace**: Peace through state capacity, sovereignty, non-interference, economic development — not democracy or human rights
- **Key features**: Strong state, social control, economic growth as legitimacy, no political liberalisation
- **Bangladesh relevance**: Some scholars argue Bangladesh's internal peace (no civil war since 1971) has been maintained through state capacity and development — not democracy — which raises questions about the liberal peace thesis

### 6.3 The Evaluation Critique

- **Does peacebuilding work?**: The evidence is mixed. Some peacebuilding operations succeed (Mozambique, Sierra Leone, Liberia). Others fail (South Sudan, Mali, Afghanistan).
- **Counterfactual problem**: We don't know what would have happened without peacebuilding — countries that receive peacebuilding are the hardest cases
- **Measurement**: Different criteria yield different assessments. By the criteria of negative peace (no relapse into war), peacebuilding has a reasonable success rate (~60%). By the criteria of positive peace (structural transformation, reconciliation), the record is weaker.

## Summary

Peacebuilding has evolved from the UN's post-Cold War optimism (*Agenda for Peace* 1992) through the liberal peace orthodoxy, the critical and local turn, to the contemporary fragmentation of peacebuilding models (liberal, hybrid, authoritarian). The liberal peace — democracy, markets, rule of law — has been the dominant framework but has been challenged by its failures and by alternative approaches. The local turn, hybrid peace, and sustaining peace agenda represent attempts to make peacebuilding more context-sensitive, locally owned, and sustainable. Bangladesh illustrates the full spectrum: post-conflict reconstruction (1971), peace accord implementation (CHT), humanitarian crisis (Rohingya), and the tension between development-led stability and democratic governance.
