## 1. Theoretical Frameworks for Analysing IOs

### 1.1 IR Theories Applied to IOs — Comparative Table

| Theory | What IOs Are | Who IOs Serve | IO Autonomy | Explains | Weakness |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Realism** | Instruments of state power | Powerful states; reflect power distribution | Minimal | Why powerful states dominate IOs (IMF weighted voting, UNSC P5) | Cannot explain why IOs survive when power shifts; underestimates IO bureaucratic autonomy |
| **Neorealism / Neoliberal Institutionalism** | Facilitate cooperation, reduce transaction costs | All states (by solving collective action problems) | Moderate | Why states create IOs (Keohane 1984), institutional resilience | Underestimates power; struggles with institutional failure |
| **Liberalism** | Actors in their own right | Multiple actors (states, NGOs, firms, citizens) | Significant | Democratic peace, IO creation by liberal states, NGO influence | Overestimates IO influence, underestimates state resistance |
| **Constructivism** | Norm-setters, identity-shapers | International community; diffuse norms, shape state identities | High | Why IOs have independent influence (Barnett & Finnemore), norm diffusion (R2P, human rights) | Overestimates IO autonomy; difficult to measure normative influence |
| **English School** | Key component of international society | The society of states; create and maintain order | Moderate | How IOs reflect different depths of international society (regional vs global) | Less applicable to issue-specific or technical IOs |
| **Principal-Agent (PA)** | Agents of member-state principals | Principals (states) — but agents develop autonomy via agency slack | Variable: depends on delegation design | Why IO secretariats have more autonomy than states intended (European Commission, UN Secretariat) | Assumes rational design; underestimates socialisation effects |
| **Feminist IR** | Gendered institutions that reflect and reproduce gender hierarchies | Men's interests, masculine norms | Varies | Gender-blind IO policies, gendered effects of IFI conditionality, WPS gaps | Sometimes overemphasises gender relative to other hierarchies |
| **Critical/Marxist/Neo-Gramscian** | Legitimise capitalist global order | Global elite, transnational capitalist class | Ideological (legitimating function) | Structural adjustment, debt discipline, IOs as mechanisms of neoliberal hegemony | Overemphasises structural determinism; limited agency |
| **Postcolonial/TWAIL** | Continue colonial hierarchies in new forms | Global North; reproduce North-South inequality | Low (captured by Northern interests) | Development discourse (Escobar), TRIPS and access to medicines, weighted voting | Risks treating all IOs as uniformly neocolonial |

### 1.2 Key Theoretical Debates in the Course

1. **Do IOs matter independently of state power?**
   - Realists: No — IOs are epiphenomenal
   - Liberal institutionalists: Yes — IOs reduce transaction costs and enable cooperation
   - Constructivists: Yes — IOs shape state identities and diffuse norms
   - Course answer: IOs matter, but their autonomy varies dramatically (European Commission >> SAARC Secretariat)

2. **Why do states create and join IOs?**
   - Functional: solve collective action problems (Keohane)
   - Instrumental: assert power, constrain others (realism)
   - Normative: signal liberal identity, diffuse values (constructivism)
   - Course answer: All three, in varying combinations

3. **Why do some IOs succeed while others fail?**
   - Institutional design: clear mandate, autonomous secretariat, binding rules, effective enforcement
   - Political context: hegemonic support, shared interests, democratic institutions
   - Constructivist: shared identity and normative consensus
   - Course answer: Institutional design and political context interact; SAARC fails because India-Pakistan rivalry overrides any institutional design; WTO's Doha Round fails because consensus decision-making and single undertaking create a "lowest common denominator" trap

### 3.2 Bangladesh's Institutional Strategy: Themes

- **Strategic diversification**: No single institutional dependency — engage across all available forums
- **Pragmatic multilateralism**: Strong support for UN and multilateral rules; critical when norms conflict with national interests (Padma Bridge, sovereignty on Rohingya)
- **Minilateral entrepreneurship**: CVF/V20 leadership shows how a vulnerable middle power can shape global agendas outside traditional IOs
- **Graduation anxiety**: LDC graduation (2026) creates systemic pressure — Bangladesh must transition from preference-dependent export model to competitiveness-based model
- **Sovereignty consciousness**: Bangladesh defends sovereignty in regional IOs (SAARC non-interference) but supports R2P in some contexts (Rohingya)

## 4. Exam Strategy

### 4.1 Types of Questions Likely to Appear

1. **Theory application**: "Compare realist and constructivist perspectives on [IO]. Which better explains [case]?"
2. **Comparative analysis**: "Compare [IO] and [IO] in terms of [feature]. What explains the difference?"
3. **Case analysis**: "Analyse the [crisis/issue] and the role of IOs in responding to it."
4. **Bangladesh-focused**: "How has Bangladesh's engagement with [IO] evolved? What explains the strategy?"
5. **Contemporary challenge**: "Assess the challenges facing [IO]. Is it fit for purpose in 2026?"
6. **Reform debate**: "Should the [IO] be reformed? What reforms would you recommend and why?"

### 4.2 Analytical Frameworks for Answer Construction

**PEEEL structure**: Point → Evidence → Explanation (theoretical) → Example → Link (to question)

**The "IR theory triad"**: For any IO question, consider:
1. **Realist dimension**: Power distribution, state interests, great-power influence
2. **Liberal dimension**: Institutional design, cooperation, rules, transaction costs
3. **Constructivist dimension**: Norms, identity, legitimacy, socialisation
4. **Critical dimension**: North-South, gender, inequality, who benefits?

**The Bangladesh dimension**: For any IO question, consider the Bangladesh angle:
- How does this IO affect Bangladesh?
- How does Bangladesh engage with it?
- What does Bangladesh's experience tell us about the IO's limitations or strengths?

### 4.3 Common Exam Traps to Avoid

| Trap | Why It's Wrong | Better Approach |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| "The UN is irrelevant" | Overstates — UN does peacekeeping, humanitarian, normative work | The UN is constrained by P5 power but valuable in specific functions |
| "IOs are tools of the powerful" (pure realism) | Understates IO autonomy and normative influence | IO autonomy varies by institution; some IOs (European Commission, CJEU) exercise significant independent power |
| "The EU is a model for other regions" | Eurocentric; EU is the outlier, not the template | Comparative regionalism: each region's IOs reflect its specific historical and political context |
| "Reform will solve everything" | Ignores entrenched power and political resistance | Reform is political, not technical; power constrains institutional change |
| Bangladesh exceptionalism | Bangladesh is not unique in its challenges | Bangladesh illustrates patterns common in the Global South but with specific features (RMG, climate, peacekeeping) |

### 3.3 Bangladesh's Institutional Strategy Through IR Lenses

| IR Theory | Predicts about Bangladesh's IO strategy | Evidence |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|----------|
| **Liberal institutionalism** | Bangladesh maximises institutional access to solve collective action problems (climate, trade preferences, peacekeeping) | Joining all available IOs (AIIB, NDB, OIC, CHOGM, BIMSTEC, IORA), leveraging institutional platforms for development |
| **Realism** | Bangladesh hedges between great powers, avoids dependence on any single institution, uses IOs instrumentally | Padma Bridge self-finance (WB rejection → Chinese/own funding), strategic diversification across AIIB/NDB/WB, Rohingya ICJ case as instrumental use of legal institutions |
| **Constructivism** | Bangladesh uses IOs to shape its identity as a responsible developing country, a climate leader, and a humanitarian contributor | CVF/V20 chair as norm entrepreneurship, peacekeeping identity (top contributor), Rohingya advocacy as framing identity |
| **Critical/postcolonial** | Bangladesh is constrained by the unequal structure of IOs; its agency is limited by power asymmetries | Weighted voting in IFIs, LDC graduation as structural vulnerability, limited influence in SAARC due to India asymmetry |
| **Feminist IR** | Bangladesh's IO engagement is gendered — women peacekeepers, RMG labour rights, women in leadership | Top contributor of female peacekeepers, Rana Plaza → ILO conventions, Amb. Rabab Fatima and women in diplomacy |

### 3.4 Cross-Module Case Study: Rohingya

The Rohingya crisis is the course's most cross-cutting case study, spanning Modules 2, 3, 4, 9, 10, and 11:

- **Module 2 (UN System)**: UNSC paralysis — China/Russia vetoes on Myanmar resolutions (2017, 2019, 2021, 2024). UN fact-finding mission (2018). ICJ *Order on Provisional Measures* (2020). ICC investigation. HRC/UPR Myanmar reports.
- **Module 3 (UN Reform)**: Rohingya as evidence for SC reform (G4/L69 argument for constraints on the veto in mass atrocity cases). ACT Group's Code of Conduct.
- **Module 4 (Regional IOs)**: ASEAN's Five-Point Consensus failure (2021 — Myanmar ignored all five points). ASEAN non-interference principle prevents meaningful regional action. SAARC's irrelevance. BIMSTEC's silence. ASEAN's exclusion of Myanmar leaders from summits (2021–present).
- **Module 9 (R2P)**: The Rohingya crisis is the most significant R2P failure since Libya: clear evidence of ethnic cleansing (UN FFM, US Holocaust Memorial Museum determination, ICJ *prima facie* case), but no R2P response — no SC resolution, no humanitarian intervention, no sanctions regime. R2P's "third pillar" collapsed against the combination of P5 geopolitics and ASEAN non-interference.
- **Module 10 (Global Challenges)**: The humanitarian crisis — 1M+ Rohingya in Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar camps, the world's largest refugee camp. Bangladesh's burden-sharing appeal at UNGA. WFP, UNHCR, IOM operations. Regional security implications.
- **Module 11 (New Institutions)**: Bangladesh's Rohingya advocacy across CVF and other minilateral platforms. OIC engagement.

**Lesson**: No single IO was able to respond effectively to the Rohingya crisis. The UNSC was blocked by great-power politics; ASEAN was paralysed by non-interference; regional IOs (SAARC, BIMSTEC) were irrelevant; and the R2P framework collapsed. The response came primarily from Bangladesh (unilateral hosting), the ICJ (provisional measures), the ICC (investigation), and humanitarian agencies (UNHCR, WFP, IOM). This is an institutional failure across all levels of governance.

### 4.5 Practice Questions with Marking Guidance

**Sample Q1**: "Compare the European Union and the Association of Southeast Asian Nations as models of regional integration. What explains the differences in their institutional design and effectiveness?"

| Mark Band | Criteria | Indicative Content |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **70%+ (First)** | Theoretically informed comparison, specific evidence, critical evaluation | Institutional anatomy (supranational vs intergovernmental), historical context (post-WWII vs post-colonial), theoretical framing (neofunctionalism vs liberal intergovernmentalism vs ASEAN Ways), specific failures (Brexit, Myanmar) |
| **60–69% (Upper Second)** | Solid comparison, good evidence, some theory | Must cover EU (Commission, CJEU, Parliament, qualified majority) and ASEAN (consensus, non-interference, ASEAN Way); must explain one major success and one failure for each |
| **50–59% (Lower Second)** | Descriptive comparison, limited theory | Describes institutional structures but doesn't fully explain differences; limited use of specific examples |
| **<50%** | Factual errors, no comparison, no evidence, no theory | Describes only one IO; no theoretical framework; irrelevant material |

**Sample Q2**: "Critically evaluate the claim that the IMF and World Bank serve the interests of their most powerful member states, not the developing countries they claim to help."

| Mark Band | Criteria | Indicative Content |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **70%+ (First)** | Multiple theoretical perspectives, specific evidence including Bangladesh, critical evaluation | Realist (weighted voting, US veto), critical (structural adjustment, Escobar, SAP failures), counter-arguments (IDA replenishment, Bangladesh $47.1B portfolio, RST climate lending), own evaluation |
| **60–69% (Upper Second)** | Clear argument, good evidence, some critical perspective | Should explain weighted voting, one major criticism (SAPs, Padma Bridge cancellation, Greek austerity), one defence (poverty reduction, IDA, COVID response), reach a balanced conclusion |
| **50–59% (Lower Second)** | Descriptive, one-sided, limited evidence | Describes IMF/World Bank structure and one critique but doesn't consider counter-arguments; limited Bangladesh evidence |
| **<50%** | Mostly or wholly inaccurate | Factual errors about governance; no understanding of weighted voting; no specific examples |

**Sample Q3**: "Analyse the role of international institutions in responding to the Ukraine War (2022–present). What does the Ukraine case tell us about the strengths and weaknesses of global governance?"

| Mark Band | Criteria | Indicative Content |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| **70%+ (First)** | Multi-institutional analysis, theoretical sophistication, evaluation | UNSC paralysis (Russia veto), UNGA resolutions (141 for), NATO revival (Finland/Sweden), EU sanctions (11 packages), IMF lending ($15.6B programme), WTO trade measures; theoretical: realists point to UNSC failure, liberal institutionalists to EU/NATO/IMF resilience, constructivists to norm contestation; own judgement on institutional effectiveness |

### 4.6 Exam Technique Tips

1. **Structure under time pressure**: 5 minutes planning → 30 minutes writing → 5 minutes checking. For a 40-minute essay question, spend 5 minutes on an outline (3 main points, evidence for each, theoretical framing), 30 minutes writing (PEEEL structure per paragraph), 5 minutes checking (have I answered the question? evidence correct? missing anything?).

2. **Number of cases**: For a comparative question, 2 cases minimum (the two IOs/comparators). For an analytical question, 3 cases is optimal (enough for depth, manageable under time). For a Bangladesh-specific question, 2–3 Bangladesh case studies with one comparative example from another country.

3. **Theory-to-evidence ratio**: Aim for roughly 30% theoretical framing, 60% empirical evidence, 10% critical evaluation. A paragraph that has no theory or no evidence is incomplete.

4. **The IR triad check**: Before writing, ask yourself: (a) have I considered the realist dimension (power, interests)? (b) the liberal dimension (institutions, rules, cooperation)? (c) the constructivist dimension (norms, identity, legitimacy)? (d) a critical dimension (who benefits, who is excluded)?

5. **The Bangladesh connection**: Even when the question does not mention Bangladesh, you can strengthen your answer by showing how Bangladesh's experience illustrates the broader point. A 2–3 sentence Bangladesh example in the right paragraph signals higher-level thinking.

6. **Common question opening phrases and what they mean**:
   - "**Critically evaluate**" = present arguments for AND against, then reach a judgement
   - "**Compare**" = identify similarities AND differences, do not just describe each separately
   - "**Analyse**" = break down into components, explain causes and effects
   - "**Discuss**" = present multiple perspectives, evaluate evidence
   - "**To what extent**" = argue for a position but acknowledge the limits of your argument

7. **Don't try to cover everything**: Depth over breadth. 3 well-developed arguments with specific evidence and theoretical framing will score higher than 6 superficial points.

This review module synthesises IR2417 across three dimensions: (1) **theoretical frameworks** for analysing IOs (realism, liberal institutionalism, constructivism, critical, feminist, postcolonial, PA, English School), (2) **substantive modules** covering the UN system, Bretton Woods institutions, regional IOs, security institutions, and global governance challenges, and (3) **cross-cutting themes**: Bangladesh's institutional strategy (strategic diversification, pragmatic multilateralism), the fragmentation of global governance (institutional proliferation, competitive complementarity), and the enduring tension between power and rules in international organisation.
