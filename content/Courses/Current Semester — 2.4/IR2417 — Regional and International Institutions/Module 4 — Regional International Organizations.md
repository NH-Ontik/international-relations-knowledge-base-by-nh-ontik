5. Assess the causes of SAARC's limited effectiveness and Bangladesh's regional IO strategy
6. Apply postcolonial and comparative regionalism perspectives to the study of regional IOs

## 1. Theories of Regional Integration

Regional integration theory was developed primarily to explain European integration, but has been applied and adapted to other regions.

### 1.1 Neofunctionalism

Neofunctionalism (Haas 1958, *The Uniting of Europe*; Lindberg 1963; Schmitter 1969) was the first comprehensive theory of regional integration. Its core claims:

- **Spillover**: Integration in one sector creates pressures for integration in related sectors (functional spillover). Economic integration creates political pressures for supranational governance (political spillover). Supranational actors (the European Commission, the European Court of Justice) act as policy entrepreneurs to engineer further integration.
- **Elite socialisation**: Interest groups and national elites shift their expectations, political activities, and loyalties toward a new centre (Haas's definition of "political integration").
- **The state disaggregated**: Neofunctionalism does not treat states as unitary actors; it sees sub-state actors (bureaucrats, interest groups, firms) as driving integration through their cross-border interactions.

Neofunctionalism successfully predicted the early trajectory of European integration (the Coal and Steel Community → EEC → Single Market → Maastricht Treaty). However, it struggled to explain periods of stagnation ("Eurosclerosis"), the 1965 Empty Chair Crisis (when de Gaulle halted EEC decision-making), and the 2016 Brexit referendum (where public opinion reversed elite-led integration).

### 1.2 Liberal Intergovernmentalism

Liberal intergovernmentalism (Moravcsik 1993, 1998; Moravcsik & Schimmelfennig 2009) emerged as neofunctionalism's main rival. It explains integration in three stages:

1. **National preference formation**: State preferences are shaped by powerful domestic economic groups (not supranational actors). The state aggregates these interests nationally.
2. **Intergovernmental bargaining**: States negotiate agreements; outcomes reflect relative bargaining power. Asymmetric interdependence — states least in need of a deal get the best terms.
3. **Institutional choice**: States create international institutions to commit credibly to agreements and reduce transaction costs. They delegate only enough authority to ensure compliance — the "lowest common denominator" outcome.

LI explains the EU's major bargains (Single European Act, Maastricht, the Euro) as outcomes of intergovernmental negotiation, not supranational entrepreneurship. It has been applied to the Eurozone crisis (Schimmelfennig 2015) and the migration crisis (Biermann et al. 2019), showing how creditor states (Germany) dominated bailout terms.

**Critique**: LI underestimates the role of supranational actors (the Commission, ECJ) in day-to-day EU governance, and it struggles to explain post-Maastricht developments where member states pooled sovereignty in ways that do not fit the "lowest common denominator" logic.

### 1.3 Postfunctionalism

Postfunctionalism (Hooghe & Marks 2009) argues that European integration has entered a new phase where **mass politics** and **identity** constrain integration. The earlier "permissive consensus" (elite-led integration accepted by indifferent publics) has been replaced by a "constraining dissensus" — Eurosceptic parties, referendums, and identity-based opposition now limit what governments can agree to at the European level.

Postfunctionalism explains:
- The difficulty of further integration in politically salient areas (migration, social policy)
- The rise of Eurosceptic parties across the EU
- The UK's Brexit vote (2016) — a case where identity politics overrode economic interests
- The EU's inability to agree on mandatory refugee quotas

### 1.4 Constructivism and Regional Integration

Constructivist approaches (Ruggie, Checkel, Christiansen, Jørgensen & Wiener) focus on how **norms, ideas, and identities** shape regional integration:

- **European identity**: Does European integration produce a shared European identity? Evidence suggests a thin "European" identity coexists with (rather than replaces) national identities.
- **Norm diffusion**: The EU externalises its norms through enlargement (the Copenhagen criteria, the acquis communautaire), shaping the domestic institutions of candidate countries.
- **Regional identity beyond Europe**: The "ASEAN Way" (consultation, consensus, non-interference) is a regional norm system that shapes Southeast Asian cooperation. Similarly, the AU's normative framework (the Constitutive Act's Art. 4(h) right to intervene, the African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance) constitutes a distinct African regional normative order.

### 1.5 Postcolonial Approaches to Regionalism

Postcolonial scholars critique mainstream integration theories for their Eurocentrism. Regionalism in the Global South is not simply a "less developed" version of European integration — it reflects different historical conditions:
- Postcolonial states prioritise **sovereignty protection** over supranational delegation, having recently won independence
- Regionalism in Africa and Asia is shaped by the legacy of colonial borders, extractive economies, and the Cold War
- The "Westphalian" sovereignty model that European integration transcends was never fully established in postcolonial states
- South–South regionalism (e.g., SAARC) often functions as a diplomatic forum rather than a governance institution because member states resist delegating authority

Acharya (2012, *The Making of Southeast Asian Regionalism*) argues for **comparative regionalism**: regional integration should be studied as a global phenomenon with regional variations, not as a European template applied elsewhere.

### 1.6 Feminist Approaches to Regional Integration

Feminist IR scholars argue that regional integration theory and practice are gendered. Mainstream theories (neofunctionalism, LI) ignore how regional institutions affect men and women differently, and how gender hierarchies are embedded in regional governance (Tickner 1992; True 2012; Rai 2008).

- **Gender-blind institutions**: Regional IOs rarely address gender inequality as a structural issue. The EU has the most developed gender equality framework (Article 8 TFEU — gender mainstreaming, the Gender Equality Strategy, binding directives on equal pay and parental leave), but even the EU treats gender as a "social" issue rather than a dimension of economic governance.
- **WPS and regional IOs**: Regional organisations have adopted Women, Peace and Security (WPS) agendas at different rates. The AU has the most comprehensive framework (the AU Gender Strategy, the Women, Gender and Youth Directorate, gender parity in the AUC — achieved 50% female Commissioners since 2017). ASEAN has a less developed WPS agenda (ASEAN WPS Regional Plan of Action adopted 2022). SAARC has no meaningful WPS framework — reflecting the deeper patriarchal structures in South Asian societies.
- **Trade and gender**: Free trade agreements have gendered effects, yet regional trade institutions rarely incorporate gender impact assessments. The AfCFTA's Protocol on Women and Youth in Trade (2024) is a rare attempt.
- **Constructivist feminist approaches**: Regional norms around gender are not "natural" but constructed — the EU's gender equality norms emerged from decades of feminist mobilisation. The absence of similar norms in ASEAN and SAARC reflects weaker civil society pressure and stronger traditional gender ideologies.

Feminist analysis contributes a critical dimension: it shows that "integration" is not a neutral technical process but one that reproduces or disrupts gender hierarchies, depending on institutional design and political mobilisation.

### 1.7 The English School and Regional International Society

English School scholars (Bull, Buzan, Hurrell) offer a distinctive perspective on regionalism through the concept of **regional international society**:

- **Bull on the "new medievalism"** (1977, *The Anarchical Society*): Bull speculated that the state might be giving way to a "new medieval" system of overlapping authority — regional and global institutions competing with sovereign states. This anticipates the contemporary language of "global governance" and multi-level governance.
- **Buzan & Waever's Regional Security Complex Theory** (2003, *Regions and Powers*): Security dynamics are primarily regional, not global. The global level provides overlay (e.g., superpower interventions in regions), but the main arena of security interaction is the regional security complex — a set of states whose security concerns are so interlinked that they cannot be analysed separately. This explains why regional IOs in different parts of the world develop different security architectures (NATO, APSA, ASEAN's ADMM+).
- **Buzan's "regional international society"** (2012, *Global International Society*): Regional IOs are the institutional expression of regional international society — the shared norms, rules, and institutions that characterise a group of states. European regional international society is "thick" (sovereignty pooling, shared law, shared identity). South Asian regional international society is "thin" (minimal shared norms beyond sovereignty and non-interference). This variation, Buzan argues, is as important as the global international society.
- **English School methodology**: The ES focuses on the **depth** of shared norms (pluralism vs solidarism) rather than the formal institutional framework, offering a richer account of why the EU, AU, ASEAN, and SAARC differ.

### 1.8 Principal-Agent (PA) Theory

PA theory (Pollack 1997, 2003; Tallberg 2002; Hawkins et al. 2006, *Delegation and Agency in International Organizations*) applies rational choice institutionalism to the relationship between member states (principals) and IO secretariats (agents):

- **Delegation**: States delegate authority to IO secretariats to reduce transaction costs, solve coordination problems, and enhance credibility. But delegation creates "agency slack" — the agent pursues its own preferences (bureaucratic expansion, issue linkage, policy entrepreneurship) at the expense of principals.
- **Supplying the demand**: PA theory asks: under what conditions do IO secretariats exercise autonomous influence? The European Commission has the highest autonomy of any regional IO secretariat because it has strong formal powers (legislative initiative, competition enforcement, budget management), multiple principals with divergent preferences (making it harder to control the agent), and long tenure. The ASEAN Secretariat has minimal autonomy because it has weak formal powers, a small budget, and is closely controlled by member states.
- **Empirical application to regional IOs**:
  - *EU*: The Commission uses its agenda-setting power, the ECJ expands its jurisdiction through judicial interpretation, and the Parliament increases its powers through treaty revisions — textbook agency slack within a delegated framework.
  - *AU*: The AUC Chairperson (currently Moussa Faki) has exercised significant diplomatic autonomy (engaging with Sudan, Ethiopia's Tigray crisis, Sahel coups), sometimes exceeding member-state preferences. The PSC similarly acts more autonomously than its formal mandate suggests.
  - *ASEAN*: The Secretariat remains the weakest — member states deliberately limit its budget (roughly 300 staff for 10 member states) and keep decision-making intergovernmental.
  - *SAARC*: The Secretariat is purely administrative, with no delegated authority whatsoever.

### 1.9 Neo-Gramscian and Critical IPE Approaches

Critical International Political Economy scholars (Cox 1981, 1987; van Apeldoorn 2002; Gill 1998; Bieler & Morton 2001) analyse regional integration as a **class project** embedded in global capitalist restructuring:

- **Cox's "forces of production, social classes, forms of state, world orders" framework**: Regional IOs are not neutral technical institutions but reflect the social forces and class coalitions that dominate the states creating them. European integration, for Cox, was a project of European capital to transcend nationally bounded accumulation and create a continental market under the ideological hegemony of "embedded neoliberalism" (later transformed into the neoliberal "competition state").
- **The Amsterdam School** (van Apeldoorn 2002, *Transnational Capitalism and the Struggle over European Integration*): European integration is driven by a transnational capitalist class fraction that operates through the Commission and the European Roundtable of Industrialists. The single market and the Euro serve the interests of transnational capital, not European workers or citizens.
- **New constitutionalism** (Gill 1998): Regional trade agreements and economic integration regimes "lock in" neoliberal policies by constitutionalising them — making them legally binding and difficult for future governments to reverse. The EU's Stability and Growth Pact, the WTO's TRIPS agreement, and the AfCFTA's investment rules are all instances of new constitutionalism.
- **Resistance and counter-hegemony**: Critical approaches also study resistance to regional integration — from labour movements, peasant organisations, and social movements. The EU's 2005 Constitutional Treaty rejection in France and the Netherlands, the "No" votes in the Eurozone referendums, and civil society opposition to free trade agreements (EU-Mercosur, AfCFTA) all illustrate the contested nature of regional integration.

### 1.10 China and the New Regionalisms

No account of contemporary regionalism is complete without analysing China's role as an external driver and shaper of regional institutional dynamics across all four regions:

- **China and ASEAN**: China is ASEAN's largest trading partner and a key actor in the South China Sea disputes. China leverages economic interdependence (RCEP, BRI, infrastructure investment) to influence ASEAN's internal unity. ASEAN states are divided between those who align with China (Cambodia, Laos) and those who contest its claims (Vietnam, Philippines). The RCEP (2022), the world's largest FTA, was a Chinese-backed initiative that excluded the United States, reinforcing China's economic centrality in the region.
- **China and Africa**: Through FOCAC (Forum on China-Africa Cooperation) and BRI infrastructure investment, China has created parallel institutional channels that compete with the AU's agenda. Chinese financing for African infrastructure does not go through the AU or the AfDB, creating a dual-track regionalism. China's non-interference principle (which aligns with African sovereignty concerns) is attractive to African elites but undermines the AU's normative framework (democracy, anti-coup standards, human rights).
- **China and South Asia**: The China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) is a flagship BRI project that deepens China-Pakistan bilateral relations at the expense of SAARC. China's observer status in SAARC (since 2005) gives it a platform in South Asian regionalism. China also promotes alternative frameworks: the BCIM Economic Corridor (Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar, stalled since 2014 due to Indian opposition), the SCO (which includes India, Pakistan, and Central Asian states), and the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB).
- **China and Europe**: The EU-China relationship is one of strategic competition dressed as partnership (the EU's 2019 "Systemic Rival" framing). China has sought to divide the EU through bilateral "16+1" (now 14+1) cooperation with Central and Eastern European states, undermining a common EU-China policy.
- **Theoretical implication**: China's regional engagement challenges Eurocentric integration theory. China operates not through supranational institutions but through bilateral deals, infrastructure investment, and minilateral platforms — a model of "transactional regionalism" that mainstream integration theory struggles to capture. Postcolonial perspectives are essential: China's non-interference principle resonates in postcolonial states where EU-style conditionality is resented.

## 2. The European Union

The EU is the most developed regional IO — the only case of **supranational governance** in which member states have pooled sovereignty in a binding legal order with direct effect on citizens.

### 2.1 Institutional Structure

- **European Commission**: The executive and policy initiator. Proposes legislation, enforces EU law, manages the budget. 27 Commissioners (one per member state) appointed by the European Council with EP approval. Supranational — Commissioners swear allegiance to the EU, not their home state.
- **Council of the European Union** (Council of Ministers): The primary legislator alongside the EP. Composed of national ministers from each member state depending on the policy area. Represents member-state interests. QMV (Qualified Majority Voting) for most decisions; unanimity for sensitive areas (tax, foreign policy, social security).
- **European Council**: Heads of state/government; defines the EU's political direction and priorities. Chair: President of the European Council (currently António Costa, term 2024–2028). Intergovernmental — operates by consensus.
- **European Parliament**: The only directly elected supranational assembly in the world. 720 MEPs (2024–2029 term). Co-legislator with the Council in most policy areas. Approves the Commission and the EU budget.
- **Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)**: Ensures uniform interpretation of EU law. Landmark doctrines: **direct effect** (individuals can invoke EU law in national courts) and **supremacy** (EU law prevails over conflicting national law). The CJEU is the most powerful international court in the world.
- **European Central Bank (ECB)**: Manages the Euro and Eurozone monetary policy; independent of political authorities.

### 2.2 The Integration Journey

| Treaty | Year | Significance |
|--------|------|--------------|
| Treaty of Paris (ECSC) | 1951 | Coal and steel under supranational authority (High Authority) |
| Treaties of Rome (EEC, Euratom) | 1957 | Common market, customs union |
| Single European Act | 1986 | Qualified majority voting for single market legislation |
| Maastricht Treaty (TEU) | 1992 | EU created; EMU, EU citizenship, CFSP, Justice and Home Affairs |
| Amsterdam Treaty | 1997 | Social policy, Schengen integrated, flexibility |
| Nice Treaty | 2001 | Institutional reform for enlargement |
| Lisbon Treaty | 2007 (in force 2009) | QMV expansion, EP co-legislator, HR/VP, exit clause (Art. 50) |

### 2.3 Crises and Resilience

The EU has faced multiple existential crises in the past two decades:
- **Eurozone crisis (2010–2015)**: Sovereign debt crisis in Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Cyprus. Produced the European Stability Mechanism (ESM), Banking Union, and ECB's "whatever it takes" commitment (Draghi 2012). Liberal intergovernmentalist interpretation: creditor states (Germany) dominated bailout terms.
- **Migration crisis (2015–2016)**: Over 1 million asylum seekers entered the EU. The Common European Asylum System (CEAS) was unable to distribute responsibility. The EU-Turkey Statement (2016) externalised border control. Postfunctionalist interpretation: identity-based opposition prevented burden-sharing.
- **Brexit (2016–2020)**: The UK's withdrawal was the first and only exit of a member state. The EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement (2020) governs the post-Brexit relationship. Brexit confirmed the EU's resilience — the remaining 27 members maintained unity throughout negotiations.
- **Rule of law crisis (2015–present)**: Poland and Hungary have been accused of undermining judicial independence, media freedom, and democratic standards. The EU responded with Art. 7 procedures and the conditionality mechanism linking EU funds to rule of law compliance. The crisis tests whether the EU can defend its foundational values.

### 2.4 The EU and the Ukraine War (2022–Present)

The full-scale Russian invasion of Ukraine (February 2022) triggered the most consequential EU foreign policy response in its history:

- **Sanctions**: The EU adopted 13+ sanctions packages against Russia (as of mid-2025), including asset freezes, financial restrictions, energy import bans, export controls, and visa restrictions. For the first time, the EU sanctioned Russian oil and gas — the very sectors that had previously divided member states. Maintaining unanimity across 27 member states for successive sanction packages is a significant diplomatic achievement.
- **Military assistance**: The European Peace Facility (EPF), originally a non-lethal instrument, was repurposed to provide lethal military aid to Ukraine — the first time the EU directly financed arms supplies for a third country. Total EU military assistance to Ukraine exceeded €40 billion (including bilateral contributions).
- **Enlargement revived**: The Ukraine war revived EU enlargement — a policy that had been dormant for a decade. Ukraine and Moldova were granted EU candidate status in June 2022 (a symbolic and political breakthrough). Georgia was given the "European perspective" (candidate status in December 2023). The Western Balkan accession process was accelerated. This "geopolitical enlargement" represents a fundamental shift from the previous enlargement fatigue.
- **Energy decoupling**: The EU reduced its dependence on Russian gas from ~40% of imports (2021) to below 10% (2024) — a transformation of European energy security that would have been considered impossible before the war.
- **Institutional implications**: The Ukraine response demonstrates that existential security threats can generate extraordinary collective action, even in an IO as divided as the EU. It also reveals the limits of intergovernmentalism: decisions on sanctions (unanimity) require constant diplomatic management, creating a new pattern of "minilateralism within multilateralism" (the G7, the Ramstein format, the Ukraine Contact Group).

### 2.5 The EU as a Normative Power

Manners (2002, "Normative Power Europe") argued that the EU's most distinctive international role is not military or economic but **normative** — the power to shape what is "normal" in international politics by projecting its values:

- **The EU principle**: The EU's normative power rests on five "core norms": peace, liberty, democracy, rule of law, respect for human rights. These are embedded in the EU Treaty (Art. 2 TEU) and externalised through conditionality (enlargement, trade agreements, development assistance).
- **The EU conditionality model**: The EU's most powerful external instrument is enlargement conditionality — candidate countries must adopt the acquis communautaire (35 chapters of EU law) to join. This has been the most successful democracy-promotion instrument in modern international relations (transforming Central and Eastern Europe, the Western Balkans).
- **Critique of normative power**: Critics argue that "normative power Europe" is a form of liberal imperialism (Diez 2005) — the EU projects its norms without acknowledging alternative normative orders. The EU's double standards (e.g., selectively applying conditionality, failing to address its own rule-of-law crisis in Hungary and Poland, the externalisation of migration control to Libya and Turkey) undermine its normative claims.
- **EU normative power applied to regions**: The EU's European Neighbourhood Policy (ENP) and the Eastern Partnership sought to export EU norms to its near abroad, with limited success in the South Caucasus and North Africa. The limits of normative power are evident: where EU norms conflict with member state interests (migration control, energy security), norms are compromised.

## 3. The African Union

The AU (est. 2002, replacing the OAU) represents Africa's most ambitious attempt at continental governance. Unlike the OAU (which was based on strict non-interference), the AU's Constitutive Act includes a right to intervene in member states under grave circumstances.

### 3.1 Institutional Structure

- **Assembly of Heads of State and Government**: The AU's supreme organ; meets biannually. Decides by consensus or two-thirds majority.
- **Executive Council**: Foreign ministers responsible for coordinating AU policy.
- **African Union Commission (AUC)**: The secretariat led by the Chairperson (currently Moussa Faki, Chad). Based in Addis Ababa.
- **Peace and Security Council (PSC)**: 15-member organ responsible for conflict prevention, management, and resolution. Modelled on the UN Security Council but without veto power. Decisions are binding on member states.
- **Pan-African Parliament**: Advisory body with limited legislative powers; located in Midrand, South Africa.
- **African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights**: Based in Arusha, Tanzania; adjudicates human rights violations. Complementarity with the African Commission on Human and Peoples' Rights.
- **Regional Economic Communities (RECs)**: Eight recognised RECs (ECOWAS, SADC, EAC, COMESA, IGAD, CEN-SAD, ECCAS, AMU) constitute the "building blocks" of the AU.

### 3.2 The African Peace and Security Architecture (APSA)

APSA is the AU's framework for conflict prevention, management, and resolution. Its five pillars:

| Pillar | Function | Status |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Peace and Security Council** | Decision-making organ for peace and security | Operational since 2004 |
| **African Standby Force (ASF)** | Rapid deployment capability from five regional standby forces | Initial operational capability declared 2016; full operationalisation ongoing |
| **Continental Early Warning System (CEWS)** | Data collection and analysis for conflict prevention | Partial operationalisation |
| **Panel of the Wise** | Preventive diplomacy and mediation | Active, composed of five eminent Africans |
| **Peace Fund** | Financing for AU peace and security activities | Crisis Reserve Facility operational; Peace Fund capitalisation target $400M |

**Art. 4(h) of the Constitutive Act** gives the AU the right to intervene in a member state in respect of grave circumstances: war crimes, genocide, and crimes against humanity. This is the most expansive supranational intervention authority of any regional IO — more far-reaching than any equivalent in ASEAN or the OAS.

The ASF consists of five regional standby forces (ECOWAS, SADC, EASF, ECCAS, NARC). In practice, the AU has relied on **regional peace support operations** (e.g., ECOWAS in The Gambia 2017, SADC/SAMIDRC in DRC 2023) rather than centrally deployed ASF forces. The gap between APSA's institutional design and operational reality reflects the tension between continental ambition and regional autonomy.

### 3.3 AU Norms and Governance

The AU has developed a distinctive normative framework:
- **African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance** (2007): Binding instrument against unconstitutional changes of government. The AU has suspended member states following coups (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Gabon, Sudan) — a stronger response than any other regional IO.
- **Agenda 2063**: The AU's 50-year development blueprint (2013); includes the flagship goal of "Silencing the Guns by 2030."
- **African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA)**: Launched 2021; aims to create a single continental market for goods and services. Implementation faces challenges: limited infrastructure, non-tariff barriers, and the economic disruption of COVID-19.

### 3.5 The AU's Financing Crisis

The AU's greatest structural weakness is **financing**:

- **Member-state arrears**: AU member states have historically failed to pay their assessed contributions. In 2016, the AU adopted a **0.2% levy on eligible imports** (Kigali Decision) to fund at least 100% of the operational budget, 75% of programme budget, and 25% of peace and security budget. Implementation has been slow — only a minority of states collect the levy effectively.
- **The Peace Fund capitalisation**: The AU Peace Fund targets $400 million (with $325 million from the AU and $75 million from partners). By 2024, the Crisis Reserve Facility ($85 million tranche) was operational, but the full fund remains under-resourced. This means the AU relies on external donors (EU, US, UN) to fund peace operations — compromising African ownership.
- **Donor dependency**: Approximately 60–70% of AU peace and security activities are funded by external partners (the EU is the largest donor). The AU's budgetary dependence on former colonial powers contradicts its stated goal of "African solutions to African problems."
- **Strategic implication**: The financing gap means the AU's institutional ambition exceeds its fiscal capacity. Agenda 2063's flagship projects (the ASF, the AfCFTA, the African Passport, Silencing the Guns) will remain aspirational unless the levy is implemented universally.

### 3.6 The Coup Epidemic and the AU's Inconsistent Response

Since 2020, West and Central Africa have experienced a "coup belt" — military takeovers in Mali (2020, 2021), Burkina Faso (2022), Niger (2023), Guinea (2021), Sudan (2021), Chad (2021, transitional), and Gabon (2023). The AU's response reveals its normative strengths and operational weaknesses:

- **Consistency of suspension**: The AU has suspended every coup-affected member state — consistent application of the African Charter on Democracy, Elections and Governance. This distinguishes the AU from ASEAN (which did not suspend Myanmar after the 2021 coup) and the OAS (which has been inconsistent).
- **Inconsistency of enforcement**: Despite suspension, the AU has been unable to reverse any coup. The juntas in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger have consolidated power, expelled French forces, and sought alternative security partnerships (Russia's Wagner Group/Africa Corps). The three Sahel states formed the **Alliance of Sahel States (AES)** in 2023 — a new minilateral regional organisation that directly challenges the AU's authority.
- **The Chad exception**: When Mahamat Déby assumed power after his father's death (2021) in what was effectively a dynastic succession, the AU did not suspend Chad — a decision widely criticised as inconsistent with its anti-coup norms.
- **Au sortir (exit) transitions**: The AU has struggled to design credible transitions back to civilian rule. Transition timelines are often extended (Mali's initial 18-month transition stretched to 5 years); elections are held under conditions that favour incumbent juntas.
- **The AES challenge**: The withdrawal of Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger from ECOWAS (January 2024) and their formation of the AES represents the most serious fragmentation of African regionalism since the OAU era. It tests whether the AU can hold its normative framework together when member states reject it.

## 4. ASEAN

The Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN, est. 1967) represents a distinctive model of regionalism: **intergovernmental, consensus-based, and non-interventionist**.

### 4.1 The ASEAN Way

The "ASEAN Way" is a set of informal norms: consultation (musyawarah), consensus (mufakat), non-interference in internal affairs, and respect for sovereignty. These norms emerged from the specific historical context of postcolonial Southeast Asia, where newly independent states prioritised regime security and territorial integrity over supranational integration.

The ASEAN Way has been both a strength and a weakness:
- **Strength**: It allowed ASEAN to survive the Cold War, manage intra-regional tensions (Indonesia-Malaysia, Cambodia-Vietnam), and eventually expand to all 10 Southeast Asian states. It created a "zone of peace" in a conflict-prone region.
- **Weakness**: Consensus decision-making produces lowest-common-denominator outcomes. The non-interference norm prevents ASEAN from addressing domestic crises in member states (Myanmar's post-2021 coup, human rights abuses in Cambodia). ASEAN's response to the Myanmar crisis (the Five-Point Consensus of 2021) has been widely criticised as ineffective.

### 4.2 Institutional Structure

ASEAN's institutionalisation has deepened significantly since the **ASEAN Charter** (2007, in force 2008), which gave ASEAN legal personality:
- **ASEAN Summit**: The highest decision-making body (heads of state/government).
- **ASEAN Coordinating Council**: Foreign ministers; coordinates summit implementation.
- **ASEAN Community Councils**: Three pillars: Political-Security, Economic, Socio-Cultural.
- **ASEAN Secretariat**: Based in Jakarta; headed by the Secretary-General. Small by IO standards (roughly 300 staff).
- **Sectoral ministerial bodies**: Covering trade (ASEAN Economic Community), defence (ADMM+), health, education, environment.

The ASEAN Intergovernmental Commission on Human Rights (AICHR, est. 2009) represents a cautious step toward human rights governance, but it operates under the non-interference principle and has no enforcement powers.

### 4.3 ASEAN + Mechanisms

ASEAN's diplomatic weight comes partly from its "hub" function in wider Asian architecture:
- **ASEAN+3**: ASEAN plus China, Japan, South Korea (finance, trade)
- **East Asia Summit (EAS)**: ASEAN+3 plus India, Australia, New Zealand, US, Russia
- **ASEAN Regional Forum (ARF)**: Security dialogue (27 participants)
- **ASEAN Defence Ministers' Meeting Plus (ADMM+)**: Defence cooperation

### 4.4 ASEAN and Great Powers

ASEAN's structural challenge is the **great-power competition** in its region — particularly the US-China rivalry. ASEAN has pursued a strategy of **hedging**: maintaining relations with both powers without aligning with either. The South China Sea disputes (involving China, Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, Taiwan) test ASEAN's unity: ASEAN cannot reach a common position on the legally binding Code of Conduct (COC) being negotiated with China, and China has exploited differences among member states.

### 4.5 Myanmar and the Collapse of the ASEAN Way

Myanmar's post-2021 military coup is the most serious crisis ASEAN has faced and reveals the limits of the ASEAN Way:

- **The coup and ASEAN's response**: The February 2021 coup by Myanmar's military (Tatmadaw) was immediately condemned by ASEAN. In April 2021, ASEAN leaders reached a **Five-Point Consensus** (5PC) with Myanmar's junta chief Min Aung Hlaing: an immediate cessation of violence, dialogue among all parties, a humanitarian envoy, a special ASEAN envoy, and an ASEAN Chair visit to Myanmar.
- **The 5PC's failure**: The junta systematically violated every point of the Consensus. Violence escalated into a full-scale civil war (pro-democracy resistance, ethnic armed organisations, and the People's Defence Forces versus the Tatmadaw). The junta refused to engage with the ASEAN envoy, blocked humanitarian access, and executed political prisoners.
- **ASEAN's institutional paralysis**: ASEAN was unable to enforce the 5PC because the non-interference norm prohibits ASEAN from compelling any member state. The "ASEAN Way" — designed to preserve regime security — prevented ASEAN from acting when a member state's own regime was the source of the crisis.
- **The admission of failure**: By late 2022, ASEAN modified its approach — excluding Myanmar's junta-appointed representatives from ASEAN meetings (an unprecedented step). But this "partial exclusion" strategy has neither changed junta behaviour nor ended the conflict. The 2024 ASEAN Chair (Laos) continued engagement with the junta, effectively returning to the non-interference baseline.
- **Myanmar's shadow**: The crisis has three structural effects on ASEAN: (1) it erodes ASEAN's international credibility, (2) it exposes the ASEAN Way as incompatible with liberal norms of human rights and democratic governance, and (3) it deepens the divide between "old ASEAN" members (Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia, Myanmar — harder authoritarian positions) and "new" members (Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Thailand — favouring stronger engagement).
- **Scholarly assessment**: The Myanmar crisis is widely regarded as ASEAN's worst failure since its founding (Beeson 2022; Haacke 2022). It confirms the postfunctionalist prediction that identity-based opposition (in this case, the regime's survival identity) can block regional cooperation — even when all other members agree on the desired outcome.

### 4.6 RCEP and ASEAN's Economic Architecture

The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP, signed 2020, entered into force 2022) is the world's largest free trade agreement by GDP share (including ASEAN+5: China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand):

- **ASEAN centrality**: RCEP was designed and driven by ASEAN — the first time ASEAN negotiated a trade agreement of global scale. It operationalises the concept of "ASEAN centrality" in the wider Asian economic architecture.
- **RCEP vs CPTPP**: RCEP is a Chinese-backed framework (it excludes the US, has weaker labour and environmental standards) in contrast to the US-backed Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP). The two agreements represent competing regional economic architectures in the Indo-Pacific.
- **Trade and development implications**: RCEP consolidates ASEAN's position as a hub of Asian supply chains. For Bangladesh and other South Asian states, RCEP creates a trade diversion risk — ASEAN+5 trade preferences make Bangladesh's exports relatively less competitive if it remains outside the agreement.

## 5. SAARC and South Asian Regionalism

The South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC, est. 1985) is the least effective major regional IO — a case study in the obstacles to regional integration in the Global South.

### 5.1 Institutional Structure

- **SAARC Charter**: Established 1985 at the Dhaka Summit. Founders: Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, Sri Lanka (Afghanistan joined 2007). Secretariat in Kathmandu.
- **Principles**: Sovereign equality, territorial integrity, political independence, non-interference, unanimity, bilateral disputes excluded from SAARC agenda.
- **Organs**: Summit (annual; heads of state), Council of Ministers, Standing Committee (foreign secretaries), Technical Committees, SAARC Secretariat (headed by Secretary-General; rotating by country alphabetically).
- **SAARC Agreements**: SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area, 2004, entered into force 2006), SAARC Food Bank (2007), SAARC Development Fund, SAARC Convention on Terrorism.

### 5.2 Why SAARC Has Failed to Deliver

The gap between SAARC's institutional structure and its performance is the central puzzle of South Asian regionalism:

- **India-Pakistan rivalry**: The defining obstacle. Bilateral tensions between India and Pakistan (Kashmir, terrorism, water sharing) have paralysed SAARC since its founding. The SAARC Charter's exclusion of bilateral disputes from the agenda was designed to prevent this, but in practice, every SAARC summit becomes a forum for Indo-Pakistani tensions.
- **Unanimity rule**: All SAARC decisions require consensus. A single member (usually Pakistan) can block any initiative. This produces lowest-common-denominator outcomes.
- **India's size asymmetry**: India accounts for roughly 80% of SAARC's GDP, 70% of its population, and the majority of its land area. Smaller neighbours fear Indian domination. This asymmetry creates a trust deficit that SAARC's governance structure cannot overcome. Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka pursue "balancing" strategies — engaging India bilaterally while also looking to China and other external partners.
- **Limited economic interdependence**: Intra-SAARC trade is less than 5% of member states' total trade — the lowest of any region. Poor connectivity (physical infrastructure, border restrictions, visa barriers) prevents economic integration. SAFTA has failed to increase intra-regional trade significantly.
- **Non-interference**: As elsewhere in the Global South, SAARC's commitment to non-interference means it cannot address transnational challenges (terrorism, climate displacement, water sharing, forced marriage migration) that require coordinated action.

### 5.3 Alternative Institutional Frameworks

The failures of SAARC have produced institutional alternatives:

- **BIMSTEC** (Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi-Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation): Founded 1997; includes Bangladesh, India, Myanmar, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Nepal, Bhutan. Bypasses Pakistan, allowing cooperation on connectivity, energy, and counter-terrorism. BIMSTEC has gained momentum since the 2014 SAARC summit cancellation (when Pakistan blocked India's connectivity initiatives). A Permanent Secretariat was established in Dhaka (2014). A BIMSTEC Free Trade Area is under negotiation.
- **BBIN** (Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Nepal): Sub-regional initiative launched in 2015 focusing on connectivity (the BBIN Motor Vehicles Agreement facilitates cross-border movement of goods and people). Excludes Pakistan, allowing India and its eastern neighbours to advance integration unimpeded.
- **South Asia Subregional Economic Cooperation (SASEC)**: ADB-supported programme covering Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Myanmar, Nepal, Sri Lanka.

### 5.4 COVID-19 and SAARC

The COVID-19 pandemic briefly revived SAARC when India's Prime Minister Modi proposed a **SAARC COVID-19 Emergency Fund** (March 2020) and convened a video conference of SAARC leaders — the first SAARC-level interaction in years. However:

- The Emergency Fund raised ~$18 million (India contributed $10 million; Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, Maldives, Afghanistan contributed smaller amounts; Pakistan delayed, ultimately contributing $3 million).
- No joint SAARC public health protocol was developed. The pandemic was managed through bilateral and multilateral channels (WHO COVAX, bilateral vaccine diplomacy — notably India's "Vaccine Maitri" programme, later disrupted by India's own domestic crisis).
- The pandemic illustrated both SAARC's potential (the fact that leaders did meet) and its limitations (no sustained institutional response, no joint procurement, no shared health infrastructure).

The COVID-19 episode confirms the pattern: crisis can temporarily reactivate SAARC at the rhetorical level, but the underlying structural obstacles (India-Pakistan rivalry, unanimity, low trust) prevent sustained cooperation.

### 5.5 Bangladesh and South Asian Regionalism

Bangladesh's regional IO strategy reflects the constraints of South Asian geopolitics:
- As SAARC's founding host (Dhaka 1985), Bangladesh has invested diplomatic capital in SAARC but has become increasingly frustrated with its paralysis.
- Bangladesh has been one of the leading advocates for **SAARC reform**, proposing graduated integration (allowing willing members to move faster), but India supports while Pakistan blocks.
- Bangladesh has diversified its regional engagement: it is an active member of **BIMSTEC** (the Secretariat is in Dhaka), a participant in **BBIN**, a founding member of the **Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA)**, and has sought closer ties with ASEAN (Bangladesh is a sectoral dialogue partner).
- Bangladesh chairs the **Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF)** and has used minilateral platforms (CVF, V20) to advance its climate advocacy when regional forums are blocked.
- The **Rohingya crisis** has tested South Asian regionalism: SAARC has been silent; BIMSTEC has limited capacity. Bangladesh has relied on bilateral engagement (with China, Japan, and ASEAN) and the UN system rather than regional IOs.
- Beyond South Asia, Bangladesh has pursued wider Indian Ocean regionalism: it is a founding member of the **Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA)** and has used its chairmanship of the **Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF)** and **V20** to advance climate advocacy through minilateral channels when regional IOs are unresponsive.
- Bangladesh has engaged with ADB-mediated connectivity frameworks — specifically the **South Asia Subregional Economic Cooperation (SASEC)** programme, which funds infrastructure projects (energy, transport, trade facilitation) in Bangladesh, Bhutan, Nepal, India, Myanmar, the Maldives, and Sri Lanka. These functional cooperation channels produce more tangible results than SAARC's political negotiations.
- The **BCIM Economic Corridor** (Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar) — a BRI-proposed subregional initiative — has stalled since 2014 due to Indian concerns about Chinese strategic penetration. The initiative illustrates how great-power competition (India-China rivalry) constrains South Asian regional connectivity even outside SAARC.
- Bangladesh's ASEAN engagement is deepening: Bangladesh became a **Sectoral Dialogue Partner** of ASEAN in 2016 and is seeking full membership. The possibility of Bangladesh's future ASEAN membership is a significant development in Southeast Asian regionalism, though it faces competition from Timor-Leste (admitted as an observer in 2022, with a roadmap to full membership by 2025–2026).
- **Pattern summary**: Bangladesh's regional IO strategy can be characterised as **strategic diversification** — when one regional institution is blocked (SAARC), Bangladesh creates or joins alternative channels (BIMSTEC, BBIN, SASEC, IORA, CVF/V20, ASEAN dialogue). This pragmatic, multi-layered approach reflects a broader South Asian pattern: states use regional IOs as instruments of strategic flexibility rather than as deep integration commitments.

## 6. Comparative Regionalism

### 6.1 Sources of Variation

| Dimension | EU | AU | ASEAN | SAARC |
|-----------|----|----|--------|-------|
| **Depth of integration** | Supranational | Intergovernmental + limited supranational authority (Art. 4(h)) | Intergovernmental, consensus-based | Intergovernmental, unanimity |
| **Supranational authority** | High (Commission, CJEU, ECB) | Medium (PSC binding decisions, AUC) | Low (Secretariat weak, no enforcement) | Minimal (Secretariat administrative only) |
| **Sovereignty pooling** | Extensive (QMV, direct effect, supremacy) | Partial (PSC decisions binding; AfCFTA) | Minimal (consensus, non-interference) | None (unanimity, exclusion of bilateral disputes) |
| **Conflict management** | Not a primary function | Core function (PSC, ASF, APSA) | Limited (non-interference prevents action) | Non-existent (bilateral disputes excluded) |
| **Economic integration** | Single market, single currency | AfCFTA in early implementation | AEC (low tariffs, significant non-tariff barriers) | SAFTA (minimal impact) |
| **Normative framework** | Democracy, human rights (enforced via conditionality) | Anti-coup norms, democracy charter, Art. 4(h) | Non-interference, ASEAN Way | Non-interference, unanimity |

### 6.2 Explaining Variation

The differences can be explained through:
- **Historical context**: The EU emerged after WWII in a context where European states sought to "tame" nationalism through integration. The AU emerged from decolonisation and the failure of the OAU's non-interference model. ASEAN and SAARC emerged during the Cold War with regime security as the priority.
- **Hegemonic presence**: The EU has no single dominant state (Germany is economically dominant but constrained by institutions). SAARC is dominated by India; ASEAN contains a mix of middle powers; the AU has Nigeria, South Africa, and Kenya as regional powers without one hegemon.
- **Economic interdependence**: European trade and investment linkages created demand for integration. South Asia's low intra-regional trade undermines the economic logic of SAARC.
- **Domestic politics**: European states had stable democratic institutions that enabled delegation to supranational bodies. Many postcolonial states prioritise regime survival, making sovereignty pooling politically costly.

### 6.3 Applying Theories to the Comparison

The four theories of regional integration generate distinct explanations for the variation observed across the four IOs:

| Theory | EU (deepest integration) → Why? | AU (partial supranationalism) → Why? | ASEAN (consensus-based) → Why? | SAARC (failed) → Why? |
|--------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------|--------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Neofunctionalism** | Spillover from ECSC → EEC → Single Market → EMU; Commission/ECJ as entrepreneurial agents | Functional spillover from OAU failure to APSA; peacekeeping creates demand for political integration? Limited — spillover blocked by sovereignty protection | Spillover from trade to services (AEC), but blocked by non-interference norm; little elite socialisation | No initial integration to trigger spillover; India-Pakistan rivalry blocks any sectoral transfer |
| **Liberal Intergovernmentalism** | Major bargains reflect state preferences (Germany/France); lowest-common-denominator in sensitive areas | PSC reflects state preferences for non-intervention with limited delegation; Art. 4(h) a compromise between Rwanda's interventionism and defenders of sovereignty | ASEAN serves state interests (regime security, economic growth); minimal delegation keeps control in capitals | States (India, Pakistan) prefer bilateral dominance to multilateral constraints; no gains from delegation |
| **Postfunctionalism** | Identity-based Euroscepticism constrains integration after Maastricht; Brexit, migration crisis | Elite-driven integration faces weak mass opposition because publics expect little from continental institutions | The "ASEAN Way" is an identity-based constraint — non-interference is an identity commitment, not just an institutional rule | Nationalist identities (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi) override regional identity; no "South Asian" demos |
| **Constructivism** | European identity (thin but real), shared norms of democracy and rule of law enable deep integration | Pan-African identity (solidarity, anti-colonialism) enables normative framework (anti-coup, Art. 4(h)) beyond material interests | ASEAN Way is a constructed norm system; the weakness is that norms are "cheap talk" when interests diverge | Absence of shared identity; India's "civilisational" identity is not shared by neighbours; Pakistan's identity defined in opposition to India |

### 6.4 Methodological Caveats

Any comparative analysis of regional IOs must acknowledge its methodological limitations:

- **Small-N problem**: This module compares four cases with many variables and few observations. Causal claims (e.g., "economic interdependence causes deep integration") cannot be rigorously tested on four cases. Alternative explanations cannot be ruled out (e.g., the role of World War II, the European Coal and Steel legacy, US security guarantees in Europe).
- **Selection bias**: The four cases were chosen because they represent "most" and "least" integration. This overstates the contrast — there are intermediate cases (Mercosur, ECOWAS, the EAC) that would complicate the EU→SAARC gradient. A wider case selection would reveal more variation.
- **The EU as implicit standard**: Despite postcolonial critiques, the comparison table uses the EU's dimensions and categories (supranationality, sovereignty pooling, normative framework) as the implicit standard. An alternative comparison could use different dimensions (conflict management, sovereignty protection, regime security) that would rank the AU or ASEAN higher.
- **Measurement problems**: "Integration depth" is not an objective metric — it depends on what dimensions are measured (legal, economic, political, security, social) and what indicators are used (treaty commitments, trade flows, institutional capacity, compliance rates). Different studies using different metrics produce different rankings.
- **Endogeneity**: Regional IOs and the conditions that create them are endogenous — states create regional IOs because they already have shared interests. The "causes" of variation (historical context, interdependence) are themselves products of deeper processes (colonialism, geography, industrialisation).
- **Static comparison**: The comparison is a snapshot of current configurations. Regional IOs evolve over time — the EU was not supranational in 1958; the AU's right to intervene was not in the 1963 OAU Charter. A dynamic or historical comparison would reveal different patterns.

## 7. Other Regional IOs (Overview)

The course's four-case focus (EU, AU, ASEAN, SAARC) covers the main theoretical cases, but other regional IOs are important for comparative reference:

### 7.1 Mercosur and Latin American Regionalism
- **Mercosur** (Southern Common Market, 1991): Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay (+ Venezuela suspended, Bolivia acceding). Customs union with imperfect implementation. Mercosur has fluctuated between more and less integration depending on domestic political shifts (the left-turn of the 2000s, the right-turn of the 2010s). The EU-Mercosur Association Agreement (concluded in principle 2019, not yet ratified) would be one of the world's largest trade agreements.
- **Pacific Alliance** (2011): Chile, Colombia, Mexico, Peru. A more flexible, outward-oriented alternative to Mercosur, focused on free trade with Asia-Pacific. Illustrates "open regionalism" — integration aimed at global competitiveness, not inward-looking protection.
- **Organization of American States (OAS)**: The Western Hemisphere's political IO. Has been divided between US influence and Latin American resistance. The OAS suspended Cuba (1962–2009) and invoked the Democratic Charter against Venezuela, Honduras, and Nicaragua, but its effectiveness is limited by US-Latin American tensions.

### 7.2 The Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO)
- **SCO** (2001): China, Russia, India, Pakistan, Iran, and Central Asian states (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, Belarus as full members). A security-focused IO addressing terrorism, extremism, and separatism (the "three evils"). The SCO represents Chinese-Russian cooperative hegemony in Eurasia. It has expanded rapidly (India-Pakistan 2017, Iran 2023, Belarus 2024) but remains a "talk shop" with limited operational capacity. The SCO is the primary institutional vehicle for China's vision of "Central Asian" regionalism — alternative to Western-dominated IOs.

### 7.3 The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC)
- **GCC** (1981): Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain. The most successful regional IO in the Middle East, with a customs union, monetary council (pre-currency union), and joint military force (Peninsula Shield). The GCC's strength derives from shared monarchical regimes, oil-based economies, and external security threats (Iran). The Qatar blockade (2017–2021) tested GCC unity but did not destroy it. The Abraham Accords and normalisation with Israel have created new regional dynamics.

### 7.4 The Pacific Islands Forum (PIF)
- **PIF** (1971, formerly SPF): 18 members (Pacific Island states + Australia, New Zealand). Focused on climate change (the Boe Declaration 2018, the Pacific Resilience Facility), ocean governance, and sustainable development. The PIF splits between large developed members (Australia, New Zealand) and small island developing states. Geopolitical competition between China and the West has intensified in the Pacific, producing the 2022 PIF Joint Statement on regional security and the 2024 Pacific Policing Initiative.

### 7.5 The East African Community (EAC)
- **EAC** (2001, revived): Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, South Sudan, DR Congo, Somalia. The most successful REC in Africa after ECOWAS, with a customs union, common market, and monetary union plans. The EAC's expansion (DRC 2022, Somalia 2023) has created integration strain — the DRC is a vast, conflict-affected country that challenges the EAC's institutional capacity. The EAC demonstrates that African regionalism can succeed when there is genuine political will and economic complementarity.

### Summary of Other IOs for Comparative Reference

| Dimension | Mercosur | SCO | GCC | PIF | EAC |
|-----------|----------|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| Integration depth | Intergovernmental customs union | Loose security coordination | Customs union + joint defence | Dialogue forum with small secretariat | Customs union + common market |
| Supranational authority | Weak (Mercosur Secretariat limited) | Minimal (SCO Secretariat, no enforcement) | Moderate (GCC Secretariat, joint institutions) | Minimal (PIF Secretariat) | Moderate (EAC Secretariat, East African Court of Justice) |
| Key challenge | Domestic political volatility | Clash between Chinese and Russian agendas | Internal rivalries (Qatar blockade) | Great-power competition (China-US) | Expansion strain (DRC, South Sudan, Somalia) |

---

Regional integration varies dramatically across the world. The EU represents supranational governance at its most developed, but it is the exception, not the template. The AU has built the most ambitious peace and security architecture of any regional IO, with a right to intervene unmatched elsewhere. ASEAN's consensus-based model has created a "zone of peace" but cannot manage internal crises. SAARC's paralysis reflects the deeper geopolitical and structural obstacles to South Asian regionalism. For Bangladesh, the failure of SAARC has driven the search for alternatives (BIMSTEC, BBIN, minilateral platforms), illustrating how states adapt their regional strategies to institutional constraints.

### Key Definitions
- **Neofunctionalism** — Theory of regional integration emphasising spillover and supranational entrepreneurship (Haas 1958)
- **Liberal Intergovernmentalism** — Theory emphasising national preference formation, intergovernmental bargaining, and institutional choice (Moravcsik 1998)
- **Postfunctionalism** — Theory emphasising identity politics and mass opposition as constraints on integration (Hooghe & Marks 2009)
- **Spillover** — Neofunctionalist concept: integration in one sector creates pressure for integration in related sectors
- **Qualified Majority Voting (QMV)** — EU voting system requiring a double majority of member states and population; supranational decision-making
- **Direct Effect** — CJEU doctrine: EU law can be invoked by individuals in national courts
- **Supremacy** — CJEU doctrine: EU law prevails over conflicting national law
- **Normative Power Europe** — The concept that the EU's international influence is exercised through shaping what is considered "normal" in international politics (Manners 2002)
- **Regional International Society** — English School concept: the degree of shared norms, rules, and institutions that characterise a group of states in a region (Buzan 2012)
- **Agency Slack** — PA theory concept: the autonomous pursuit of secretariat preferences beyond what member states (principals) intended
- **Comparative Regionalism** — Approach that studies regional integration as a global phenomenon with regional variations, rejecting a European template (Acharya 2012)
- **Art. 4(h) of the AU Constitutive Act** — The AU's right to intervene in a member state for war crimes, genocide, or crimes against humanity
- **APSA** — African Peace and Security Architecture: PSC, ASF, CEWS, Panel of the Wise, Peace Fund
- **African Standby Force (ASF)** — Rapid deployment capability from five regional standby forces
- **ASEAN Way** — Norms of consultation, consensus, non-interference, and respect for sovereignty
- **ASEAN Charter (2007)** — Legal personality for ASEAN; deepened institutionalisation
- **Five-Point Consensus (5PC)** — ASEAN's failed 2021 agreement with Myanmar's junta; symbol of ASEAN's institutional paralysis
- **RCEP** — Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (2022); world's largest FTA, ASEAN-centred economic architecture
- **SAARC** — South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (1985); the weakest major regional IO
- **SAFTA** — South Asian Free Trade Area (2004); minimal impact on intra-regional trade
- **BIMSTEC** — Bay of Bengal Initiative (1997); alternative to SAARC bypassing Pakistan
- **BBIN** — Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Nepal sub-regional connectivity initiative (2015)
- **SASEC** — South Asia Subregional Economic Cooperation (ADB-supported connectivity programme)
- **Alliance of Sahel States (AES)** — Breakaway Sahel regional organisation (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, 2023) challenging AU/ECOWAS authority
- **GCC** — Gulf Cooperation Council (1981); the most successful Middle Eastern regional IO, based on monarchical solidarity and oil wealth
