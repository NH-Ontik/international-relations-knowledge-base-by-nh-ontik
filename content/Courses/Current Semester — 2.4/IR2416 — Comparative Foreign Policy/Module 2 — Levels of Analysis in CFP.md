# Module 2 — Levels of Analysis in Comparative Foreign Policy

## Course Relevance

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

2. Distinguish between individual, state, and system levels
3. Analyse how different levels explain different outcomes
4. Critically assess the relationship between levels
5. Apply levels of analysis to comparative cases

---

## 1. The Level-of-Analysis Problem

### 1.1 Origins

J. David Singer's 1961 article "The Level-of-Analysis Problem in International Relations" identified a fundamental issue: the same event can be explained at different levels, and the level chosen determines the kind of explanation produced.

| Level | Focus | Explains... |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Individual** | Leaders, perceptions, psychology | Why specific decisions were made |
| **State/Societal** | Domestic politics, institutions, society | Why states have different foreign policy orientations |
| **International System** | Power distribution, polarity, anarchy | Why states behave similarly given structural constraints |

### 1.2 The Problem

- **No level is complete**: Each level captures part of the picture and misses other parts
- **Levels are not additive**: Combining levels is theoretically difficult — they rest on different assumptions
- **Explaining the same case differently**: A decision to go to war can be explained by leader psychology (individual), regime type (state), or power transition (system) — and all three may be valid

## 2. The Individual Level

### 2.1 Core Claim

Foreign policy is made by human beings — not by states. Individual leaders matter because:
- They interpret information
- They make decisions under uncertainty
- Their personalities, beliefs, and experiences shape their choices

### 2.2 Key Approaches

| Approach | Focus | Example |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Operational code** | Leaders' fundamental beliefs about politics | George F. Kennan's analysis of Soviet leaders |
| **Personality traits** | Traits like need for power, distrust, narcissism | Hitler, Stalin, Trump — high need for power |
| **Cognitive biases** | Systematic errors in information processing | A leader overconfidently predicting victory |
| **Risk propensity** | Willingness to take risks | JFK during Cuban missile crisis vs. Putin 2022 |
| **Health and age** | Physical and mental capacity | Ageing leaders and declining decision quality |

### 2.3 Applied Comparatively

From a CFP perspective, the individual level is most important when:
- The state is small, developing, or has weak institutions (Rosenau's prediction)
- The leader has concentrated power (autocracies, personalist regimes)
- The state is in crisis — time pressure reduces institutional input
- The issue is high-stakes (war, alliance, crisis)

### 2.4 Critique

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Reductionism** | Explaining international outcomes through individual leaders ignores structure |
| **Leaders are constrained** | Democrats face legislative and electoral constraints; autocrats face regime survival constraints |
| **Post-hoc attribution** | We attribute outcomes to leaders because it makes a good story — not because it is scientifically valid |
| **Not falsifiable** | Any outcome can be attributed to a leader's psychology after the fact |

## 3. The State/Societal Level

### 3.1 Core Claim

States have different foreign policies because they have different **domestic structures, institutions, and societies**:

| Variable | Mechanism | Comparative Prediction |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Regime type** | Democracies make foreign policy differently from autocracies | Democratic peace — democracies rarely fight each other |
| **Political institutions** | Separation of powers, parliamentary vs. presidential, electoral systems | Presidential systems produce less predictable FP |
| **Bureaucracy** | Ministry structures, interagency processes | Fragmented bureaucracies produce inconsistent FP |
| **Public opinion** | Voting, protests, approval ratings | Public opinion constrains foreign policy |
| **Interest groups** | Businesses, diaspora, ethnic lobbies | Israel lobby in US; Indian diaspora in UK |
| **Media** | Free vs. controlled media; agenda-setting | Media shapes what the public and leaders pay attention to |
| **National identity** | Collective self-understanding | Neutrality of Sweden; "civilian power" of Germany; "exceptionalism" of US |

### 3.2 Applied Comparatively

The state/societal level is most important when:
- The state is large, developed, and open (Rosenau's prediction)
- The issue is economic or diplomatic — not existential security
- The political system creates multiple veto points
- Civil society is strong and organised

### 3.3 Critique

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Anthropomorphising the state** | The state is not a person — we cannot attribute motives or preferences to it |
| **Domestic politics is endogenous** | What looks like a domestic cause may itself be shaped by international forces |
| **National identity is constructed** | Identity is not fixed — it is manipulated by elites for political purposes |
| **Level of aggregation** | Which domestic factors matter? How do we weigh them? |

## 4. The International System Level

### 4.1 Core Claim

The international system's structure constrains all states — regardless of their domestic politics:

| Systemic Variable | Effect on Foreign Policy |
|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **Anarchy** (absence of world government) | States must prioritise security — self-help system |
| **Polarity** (distribution of power) | Bipolarity = stability (Cold War); unipolarity = US hegemony; multipolarity = uncertainty |
| **Alliances** | NATO, QUAD, SCO — alliance commitments shape FP |
| **International institutions** | UN, WTO, IMF — constrain and enable FP choices |
| **International law** | Treaties, norms, conventions — create obligations |
| **Global economy** | Interdependence, supply chains, capital flows |
| **Power transitions** | Rising powers challenge dominant powers — risk of war |

### 4.2 Applied Comparatively

The systemic level is most important when:
- The state is large or a great power (systemic pressures are most intense)
- The issue is military/security
- The system is in transition (power shifts, new threats)
- The state is small and vulnerable — systemic constraints dominate

### 4.3 Critique

| Critique | Argument |
|----------|----------|
| **Structural determinism** | Leaves no room for agency — all states in the same position should behave the same (they do not) |
| **Cannot explain variation** | Why do states with similar structural positions have different policies? (Saudi Arabia vs UAE) |
| **Ignores domestic politics** | Treats states as unitary actors — misses internal contestation |
| **Status quo bias** | The system tends to reproduce itself — difficult to explain change |

## 5. Integrating Levels

### 5.1 The Need for Integration

Most scholars agree that foreign policy is produced by factors at all three levels simultaneously. The question is how to integrate them.

### 5.2 Putnam's Two-Level Games

Robert Putnam (1988) provided the most influential framework:
- **Level I**: International negotiation — the state bargains with other states
- **Level II**: Domestic ratification — the agreement must be approved domestically
- **The "win-set"**: The set of international agreements that can be ratified at home — the smaller the win-set, the more domestic constraints matter

| Country Type | Win-Set | International Bargaining Power |
|--------------|---------|-------------------------------|
| Strong democracy, divided government | Small | Strong (can claim "I cannot sell this at home") |
| Autocracy, no constraints | Large | Weak (can be pushed to agree to anything) |
| Weak state, dependent on agreement | Must ratify | Weak (must accept what is offered) |

### 5.3 Neoclassical Realism

Neoclassical realism attempts to integrate system and state levels:
- **Systemic variables** set the parameters (relative power, threat)
- **Domestic variables** filter and mediate — leaders perceive and respond to systemic pressures differently
- **Leader perceptions** are the transmission belt between system and policy

| Variable | Role | Example |
|----------|------|---------|
| Relative power | Independent variable | US primacy creates permissive environment |
| Leader perception | Intervening variable | Trump perceives allies as free-riders |
| Domestic politics | Intervening variable | Congressional constraints, interest groups |

## 6. Levels of Analysis and Bangladesh

| Level | Bangladesh Application |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Individual** | Sheikh Hasina's leadership style (2009–present) — personal dominance shapes FP significantly |
| **State/Societal** | Strong foreign ministry, but PM dominates; public opinion matters for India policy but not for most issues |
| **Systemic** | Small state, geopolitical location (India-China competition), climate vulnerability — systemic constraints dominate |

**Key insight**: Bangladesh's foreign policy cannot be explained at one level alone. The systemic level sets the constraints, the PM's preferences determine the response, and domestic politics (India card, China card, diaspora) influence the choices.

---

## Summary

The level-of-analysis problem is central to CFP. The individual level captures leader psychology and agency; the state/societal level captures domestic structure and politics; the systemic level captures international structure and constraints. Each level is incomplete alone. Integration frameworks (Putnam's two-level games, neoclassical realism) attempt to bridge levels, but the theoretical challenges remain. A complete CFP analysis must consider all levels and their interactions.

## Exam-Ready

### Key Definitions
- **Level of analysis** — The unit or level at which we locate the causes of foreign policy
- **Individual level** — Leaders, perceptions, psychology
- **State level** — Domestic institutions, regime type, society
- **System level** — Anarchy, polarity, power distribution
- **Two-level games** — Putnam: international negotiation + domestic ratification
- **Win-set** — The range of international agreements that can be ratified domestically
- **Neoclassical realism** — System + domestic politics + leader perception

### Key Thinkers
- **J. David Singer** — Level-of-analysis problem (1961)
- **Robert Putnam** — Two-level games (1988)
- **Kenneth Waltz** — *Man, the State, and War* (1959) — three images
- **Gideon Rose** — Neoclassical realism (1998)
