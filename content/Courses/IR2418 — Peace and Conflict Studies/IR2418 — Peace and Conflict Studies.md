## Core Concepts

### 1. Defining Peace: Negative vs. Positive Peace
Johan Galtung (1964), in the founding editorial of the *Journal of Peace Research*:

| Aspect | Negative Peace | Positive Peace |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Definition | Absence of direct violence/war | Absence of [[Structural Violence|structural violence]] + presence of justice |
| Focus | Stopping conflict, curative | Building justice, preventive |
| Approach | Reactive | Proactive |
| Violence type addressed | Direct (visible) | Structural + cultural (invisible) |
| Outcome | Ceasefire, truce | Integrated society, cooperation |

### 2. Galtung's Violence Triangle

**Direct Violence** — Physical, visible harm (war, assault, terror)
- Clear actor → clear victim → clear event
- See: [[Ukraine War]], [[Rwanda Genocide]], [[WWI]]

**Structural Violence** — Systemic harm built into institutions
- Unequal power → unequal life chances
- No single identifiable perpetrator
- See: [[Apartheid]], [[Scramble for Africa]], [[Bretton Woods Institutions|IMF structural adjustment]]
- Related: [[Marxism]], [[Postcolonialism]], [[International Development]]

**Cultural Violence** — Aspects of culture used to legitimize violence
- Religion, ideology, art, language, science
- Changes "moral colour" of violence from wrong to right/acceptable
- Related: [[Constructivism]], [[Postcolonialism]]

### 3. Conflict Analysis Frameworks

#### Galtung's Conflict Triangle (ABC Model)
- **A**ttitudes — Perceptions, emotions, misperceptions
- **B**ehaviour — Cooperation or coercion
- **C**ontradiction — Incompatible goals/values

#### [[Conflict Transformation]] (John Paul Lederach)
- Beyond "resolution" or "management"
- Conflict as a motor for positive change
- Focus on relationships, not just issues
- Four dimensions: Personal, Relational, Structural, Cultural

| Approach | Goal | Timeframe | Depth |
|----------|------|-----------|-------|
| Conflict Resolution | End conflict, solve problem | Medium-term | Moderate |
| Conflict Management | Contain/limit conflict | Short-term | Surface |
| Conflict Transformation | Change relationships + structures | Long-term | Deep |

### 4. Levels of Peacebuilding (Lederach)
1. **Top Level** — Elite leaders, high-level negotiations
2. **Middle Range** — NGOs, academics, religious leaders, ethnic leaders
3. **Grassroots** — Community leaders, local peace committees

Lederach argues the middle range is most effective for sustainable peace.

### 5. Track Diplomacy
- **Track I** — Official, government-to-government
- **Track II** — Unofficial, civil society, academics, NGOs
- **Multi-Track** — Combined approach at all levels

### 6. Peacebuilding Approaches
- **Conflict Prevention** — Early warning, structural prevention
- **Peacekeeping** — [[United Nations|UN missions]], military/observer deployments
- **Peacemaking** — Mediation, negotiation, diplomacy
- **Peacebuilding** — Post-conflict reconstruction, reconciliation
- **Peace Enforcement** — Coercive action (Chapter VII UN)

### 7. Non-Western Approaches to Peace
- [[Gandhi Satyagraha|Satyagraha]] — Gandhi's non-violent resistance ([[India Foreign Policy|Indian tradition]])
- **Ubuntu** (African) — "I am because we are"; [[Justice|restorative justice]]
- **Sulh** (Islamic) — Reconciliation, customary dispute resolution
- **Buddhist peace** — Interconnectedness, compassion-based

## Key Terms Glossary
- **Negative Peace**: Absence of direct violence
- **Positive Peace**: Absence of structural violence + social justice
- **Structural Violence**: Systemic harm through institutions
- **Cultural Violence**: Legitimization of violence through culture
- **Conflict Transformation**: Changing relationships behind conflict
- **Track Diplomacy**: Official vs. unofficial peace processes
- **Peacebuilding**: Post-conflict reconstruction
- **Satyagraha**: Gandhi's non-violent resistance philosophy
