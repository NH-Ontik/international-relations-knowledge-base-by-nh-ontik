# Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA)

> **Sources:** Breuning, M. (2007). *Foreign Policy Analysis: A Comparative Introduction*; Neack, L. (2008). *The New Foreign Policy: Power Seeking in a Globalized Era*, 2nd ed.; Mintz, A. & DeRouen, K. (2010). *Understanding Foreign Policy Decision Making*.

## Definition

Foreign Policy Analysis is a subfield of International Relations that places the **individual decision maker at the heart of the foreign policy process** (Breuning). It asks: who makes foreign policy, under what constraints, and with what cognitive and psychological baggage?

Neack (2008: 5) defines foreign policy as **"a nested game"** in which national leaders play **two games simultaneously** — domestic politics and international politics — and attempts to win both. The core proposition: foreign policy is best understood as multi-level, multi-actor, and process-driven.

## Levels of Analysis

Breuning's framework (three levels, connected to specific FPA focus):

| Level | Focus | Key Question |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Individual** | Options/Decisions | Do leaders shape foreign policy? |
| **State** | Behaviours | How do domestic factors shape foreign policy? |
| **System** | Outcomes | How does the international system constrain states? |

Neack (2008) describes this as a **nested game** — leaders must satisfy domestic constituencies AND international imperatives simultaneously, often pulling in different directions.

## Decision-Making Models

### 1. Rational Actor Model
- Decision makers identify goals, assess costs/benefits of options, choose the option that maximises utility
- Requires: clearly defined goals, complete information, ability to rank preferences
- **Critique**: Assumes perfect information and cognitive processing that real leaders never have

### 2. Bounded Rationality (Cybernetic Model)
- Herbert Simon: decision makers are "cognitive misers" — they satisfice rather than maximise
- Use heuristics, standard operating procedures, shortcuts
- Decision makers operate within "bounded rationality" — constrained by incomplete information, limited time, and cognitive capacity

### 3. Poliheuristic Theory (Mintz)
- A two-stage decision process:
  1. **Noncompensatory stage**: Eliminate options that are unacceptable on a key dimension (usually domestic politics)
  2. **Compensatory stage**: Evaluate remaining options more carefully
- Aims to bridge rational and cognitive schools
- Case study: US decision NOT to invade Iraq in 1991 (Mintz & DeRouen). Bush Sr. eliminated the invasion option because it failed on the domestic politics dimension

### 4. Bureaucratic Politics Model
- Foreign policy outcomes result from bargaining and compromise among government agencies
- "Where you stand depends on where you sit" (Miles' Law)
- Allison's model of the Cuban Missile Crisis
- **Critique**: Overemphasises bureaucratic conflict, underemphasises presidential authority

### 5. Group Decision-Making Models
- **Groupthink** (Janis): Pressure for consensus overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives
  - Symptoms: invulnerability illusion, stereotyping outgroups, pressure on dissenters
  - Case: Bay of Pigs (Kennedy administration); US decision to invade Iraq 2003 (Mintz & DeRouen)
- **Polythink** (Mintz): The opposite problem — group fragmentation, multiple conflicting views, no consensus
  - Case: Camp David 2000
- **Group Polarization**: Groups often make more extreme decisions than individuals — risky shift vs cautious shift

### 6. Prospect Theory
- From behavioural economics (Kahneman & Tversky)
- Decision makers are **risk-averse for gains** (prefer certain gains) but **risk-acceptant for losses** (will take risks to avoid/recoup losses)
- Key insight: states in the domain of loss (territory lost, status declined) will take greater risks
- Case: Argentina's decision to invade the Falklands (1982) — the junta was losing domestic support and saw the invasion as a way to recoup losses

## Cognitive Biases in Foreign Policy

Mintz & DeRouen (2010) identify:
- **Cognitive consistency** — Tendency to perceive information consistent with existing beliefs; ignore/interpret away contradictory info
- **Evoked set** — The information a decision maker actually draws upon (often narrow, recency-biased)
- **Emotions** — Anger and fear affect risk perception and decision-making
- **Images** — Simplified pictures of other states (enemy image → hostile actions; ally image → trust)
- **Operational code analysis** — Philosophical beliefs (nature of politics, predictability) + instrumental beliefs (best strategies for achieving goals)
- **Analogies and learning** — Decision makers reason by analogy (Munich 1938 → avoid appeasement → Vietnam; 9/11 → Pearl Harbor → forceful response)

## Decision Units Framework (Neack)

Who actually makes foreign policy decisions? The answer depends on the **decision unit**:

| Unit | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Predominant Leader** | Single individual has authority | Putin (Russia), Pinochet (Chile) |
| **Single Group** | Small group makes decisions collectively | Cabinets, national security councils |
| **Multiple Autonomous Actors** | Bargaining among independent institutions | US: Congress vs President, France: Cohabitation |

## Levels of Decision Complexity (Breuning)

### Types of Decisions
- **Options** — The range of possible choices available
- **Decision** — The option actually chosen
- **Behaviour** — The acting out of the decision (implementation has its own problems — orders misunderstood, ignored, subverted)
- **Outcome** — The interactive result (depends on other actors' reactions, not just the decision)

### Factors at Each Level

**Individual-level factors:**
- Personality, leadership traits, operational code
- Cognitive biases, perceptions, misperceptions
- Belief systems, schemas, analogies

**State-level factors:**
- Political institutions (presidential vs parliamentary; democracy vs autocracy)
- Government bureaucracy (how is it organised?)
- Domestic politics and public opinion
- National culture and national self-image
- Interest groups, ethnic groups, media

**System-level factors:**
- Relative power of states
- International institutions and regimes
- Globalisation and economic interdependence

## The Two-Level Game (Putnam, 1988)

A framework bridging domestic and international levels:
- **Level I**: International negotiations between states
- **Level II**: Domestic ratification of international agreements
- Leaders negotiate at both levels simultaneously
- Domestic constraints can be used as bargaining leverage ("I'd like to accept your offer, but my parliament won't ratify it")

## States by Power Category (Neack)

| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Great Powers** | Define global order, project force worldwide | US, China, Russia |
| **Rising Great Powers** | Transitioning toward great power status | India, China's ongoing rise |
| **Secondary Powers** | Significant regional influence but not global | Germany, Japan, Brazil |
| **Middle Powers** | Seek influence through niches (peacekeeping, environment, trade) | Canada, Australia, Norway, Sweden |
| **Small Powers** | Vulnerable, constrained, seek allies | Many Global South states |
| **Client States** | Depend heavily on a patron for economic/military survival | |

- **Alex Mintz** — Poliheuristic theory, decision-making models, cognitive biases

## Case Studies Used Across the Texts
- **Cuban Missile Crisis** (1962) — Rational actor, bureaucratic politics, groupthink
- **Iraq 1991** — Why Bush Sr. did NOT invade (poliheuristic explanation)
- **Iraq 2003** — Groupthink in the Bush administration; cognitive biases
- **Camp David 2000** — Polythink breakdown
- **Chamberlain at Munich** (1938) — Puzzling decisions, lessons from history
- **Saddam's invasion of Kuwait** (1990) — Misperception, opportunity, miscalculation
- **Falklands War** (1982) — Prospect theory, loss domain
- **New Zealand and ANZUS** — Rational choice under system constraints
