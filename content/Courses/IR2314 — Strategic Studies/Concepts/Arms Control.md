# Arms Control and Disarmament

> **Source:** Baylis et al. (1975). *Contemporary Strategy*, Ch. 5 (John Garnett); Baylis et al. (2019). *Strategy in the Contemporary World*, Ch. 13 (John Baylis).

## Definition
Arms control refers to any arrangement that regulates some aspect of a state's weapons policy. It's the "management of the military potential of a nation in the context of its foreign policy" — not simply reduction or abolition, but **cooperative management of arms**.

Distinction:
- **Disarmament** — The reduction or abolition of weapons; idealist, maximalist
- **Arms Control** — The regulation and management of weapons; realist, limited, pragmatic

## Why Arms Control (Not Disarmament)?

The shift from idealist disarmament (1920s-30s, Philip Noel-Baker) to arms control (post-1945) reflected the rise of **realism**. Realists argued:
- Abolishing weapons is impractical if political conflicts remain
- Weapons are symptoms, not causes, of international tension
- Arms control aims for *stability*, not abolition
- As John Garnett notes: "Today we are all arms controllers" — the goal of general and comprehensive disarmament is "almost dead"

## Key Cold War Arms Control Agreements
| Agreement | Year | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Partial Test Ban Treaty (PTBT) | 1963 | Limited testing to underground |
| Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) | 1968 | Prevent spread of nuclear weapons |
| SALT I | 1972 | Limit strategic launchers numbers |
| SALT II | 1979 | Limit strategic launchers (never ratified but observed) |
| START I | 1991 | Reduce and limit strategic offensive arms — verification |
| INF Treaty | 1987 | Eliminate intermediate-range nuclear forces |

## Arms Control in the Post-Cold War Era (Baylis, 2019)

Three phases:
1. **Cooperative phase** (early 1990s) — START II, Chemical Weapons Convention (1993)
2. **Erosion of the architecture** (2000s-2010s) — US withdrawal from ABM Treaty (2002), Russian suspension of INF (2019)
3. **From arms control to counterproliferation** — Focus shifted from bargaining to preventing acquisition by "rogue states" and non-state actors, including:
   - Pre-emptive military action
   - Export controls, sanctions
   - Missile defence systems
   - Intelligence operations

## The Counterproliferation Challenge
"Counterproliferation" (post-9/11) differs from classic arms control:
- **Targets**: "Rogue states" and non-state actors, not great powers
- **Methods**: Military force, interdiction, pre-emption, not bilateral negotiation
- **Problem**: No dialogue partner — how do you negotiate with a non-state actor? How do you inspect a closed regime?

## Does Arms Control Have a Future? (Baylis, 2019)
- New technologies (cyber, hypersonics, AI, space weapons) are not covered by existing treaties
- China is outside most bilateral US-Russia agreements
- But arms control remains essential for: crisis stability, preventing arms races, verification and confidence-building, non-proliferation
