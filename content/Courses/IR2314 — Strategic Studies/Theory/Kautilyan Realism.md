# Kautilyan Realism

> **Source:** Shamasastry, R. (trans.) (1915). *Kautilya's Arthashastra*.

## Overview

The *Arthashastra* ("Science of Political Economy" or "Science of Statecraft"), attributed to **Kautilya** (also known as Chanakya or Vishnugupta), was written around the 4th century BCE in India. It is one of the earliest and most comprehensive treatises on statecraft, strategy, and governance in world history — predating Machiavelli by nearly 1,800 years and Clausewitz by over 2,000.

As Amitav Acharya & Jiajie He (2019) argue: Kautilyan thought represents a major **non-Western contribution to strategic thinking** that mainstream IR and strategic studies have largely ignored.

## The Saptanga Theory (Seven Elements of State)

Kautilya identifies seven constituent elements (*prakritis*) of the state that determine its strength and stability (Book VI, "The Source of Sovereign States"):

1. **Svami** (The Sovereign/Ruler) — The king's qualities, energy, leadership
2. **Amatya** (The Minister) — The quality of advisers and bureaucracy
3. **Janapada** (The Territory and Population) — The land and people
4. **Durga** (The Fortress/Capital) — Fortifications, strategic defence positions
5. **Kosa** (The Treasury) — Wealth and financial resources
6. **Danda** (The Army/Force) — Military power
7. **Mitra** (The Ally) — Friends and allies in the international system

A state is strong if all seven are strong. Weakness in any element invites aggression from neighbours.

## The Mandala Theory (Circle of States)

Kautilya's theory of international relations posits that the king's immediate neighbour is the **natural enemy** (*ari*), while the neighbour's neighbour is the **natural ally** (*mitra*). This creates a pattern of concentric circles:

- **Vijigishu** — The would-be conqueror (the central state)
- **Ari** — The enemy (adjacent state)
- **Mitra** — The ally (state beyond the enemy)
- **Ari-mitra** — The enemy's ally (state beyond the ally)
- **Mitra-mitra** — The ally's ally
- And so on in expanding circles

This is Kautilya's **structural theory of IR**: enmity and alliance are determined by geography, not by ideology, regime type, or culture.

## The Shadgunya (Six-Fold Policy)

Book VII, "The End of the Six-Fold Policy": A king has six foreign policy options based on his relative strength:

| Policy | Sanskrit | When to Use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Peace** | *Sandhi* | When weaker than the enemy — make a treaty |
| **War** | *Vigraha* | When stronger than the enemy — wage war |
| **Staying Quiet** | *Asana* | When equal — remain neutral, wait for advantage |
| **Marching / Preparing** | *Yana* | When stronger — mobilise and march against the enemy |
| **Seeking Shelter** | *Samsraya* | When very weak — take refuge with a powerful ally |
| **Dual Policy** | *Dvaidhibhava* | When unique advantage exists — make peace with one, war with another |

These correspond to modern concepts: balancing, bandwagoning, neutrality, alliance-seeking, entrapment, and strategic hedging — all in one 4th-century BCE framework.

## The Four Upayas (Strategic Means)

Kautilya's methods for achieving policy objectives:

1. **Sama** (Conciliation) — Diplomatic negotiation, persuasion
2. **Dana** (Gift/Inducement) — Bribery, reward, buying off opponents
3. **Bheda** (Dissension/Divide) — Creating division among enemies
4. **Danda** (Force/Punishment) — The last resort: military force

## Intelligence and Espionage

Kautilya devotes extensive attention to espionage (Book I). Spies are categorised by type:
- **Student spies** — Posing as students
- **Householder spies** — Posing as ascetics
- **Merchant spies** — Disguised as merchants
- **Ascetic spies** — Disguised as wandering holy men

Spying is considered essential governance — not a moral breach but a routine tool of state.

## Relation to Western IR Theory

| Kautilyan Concept | Western Parallel |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Immediate neighbour = natural enemy | Offensive realism: proximity breeds conflict |
| Mandala (circles of states) | Neorealism: structural logic of the system |
| Sandhi (peace when weak) | Bandwagoning / balancing |
| Vigraha (war when strong) | Offensive realism's logic |
| Kosa (treasury) | State power: economic foundations |
| Danda (force) | Military power |
| Spies and deception | Intelligence studies |

## Key Parallel with Machiavelli
Kautilya predates Machiavelli by ~1,800 years but shares the same **reason of state** logic — the ruler must do whatever is necessary (including deception, violence, breaking treaties) to preserve the state. Both separate governance from Christian morality.

## Key Parallel with Thucydides
The Melian Dialogue's "the strong do what they can, the weak suffer what they must" is mirrored throughout the Arthashastra, but Kautilya provides a more systematic framework for *when* a weak state can avoid suffering.

## Critiques

- Some scholars question whether Kautilya reflects actual Mauryan policy or a normative ideal
- The text may describe an idealized, absolutist monarchy that never fully existed
- Overemphasis on deception and force may not fit all states or eras
- The text is written for a monarch, not a democracy — relevance to modern statecraft is debated

## Contemporary Relevance
- Provides a non-Western foundation for realist theory
- Systematic approach to statecraft parallels modern strategic planning
- The six-fold policy is a sophisticated early theory of alignment choices
- Increasingly cited in IR literature since the rise of India as a major power

## Exam Preparation
- **Compare**: Kautilya vs Machiavelli vs Thucydides — who real-politics more systematically?
- **Apply**: Modern analogues for the six-fold policy (Indian non-alignment as *Asana*? Pakistan seeking shelter with China? Small states balancing/bandwagoning)
- **Evaluate**: "Kautilya is the Indian Machiavelli" — is this comparison fair?
