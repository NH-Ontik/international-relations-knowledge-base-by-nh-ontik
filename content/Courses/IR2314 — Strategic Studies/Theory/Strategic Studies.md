# Strategic Studies

> **Source:** Baylis, Booth, Garnett & Williams (1975). *Contemporary Strategy: Theories and Policies*; Baylis, Gray & Wirtz (2019). *Strategy in the Contemporary World*, 6th ed.

## Definition

Strategy is fundamentally about the ways in which military power may be used to achieve political objectives — and waging war is only one of those ways. As Liddell Hart put it, strategy is "the art of distributing and applying military means to fulfil the ends of policy." In the nuclear age, the prime task of strategic doctrine is not to wage war but to create alternatives less catastrophic than thermonuclear holocaust.

**Key thinkers defining strategy:**
- **Clausewitz:** "The employment of battle as the means towards the attainment of the object of war"
- **von Moltke:** "The practical adaptation of the means placed at a general's disposal to the attainment of the object in view"
- **Liddell Hart:** "The art of distributing and applying military means to fulfil the ends of policy"
- **Robert Osgood:** "Nothing less than the overall plan for utilizing the capacity for armed coercion — in conjunction with the economic, diplomatic, and psychological instruments of power — to support foreign policy most effectively"

Strategic studies is not a discipline in its own right. It has "a sharp focus — the role of military power — but no clear perimeter, and it is parasitic upon arts, science and social science subjects for the ideas and concepts which its practitioners have developed" (Garnett). Herman Kahn was a physicist, Thomas Schelling an economist, Albert Wohlstetter a mathematician, Henry Kissinger a historian.

## Core Assumptions (from the Classical Realist Tradition)

Most contemporary strategists share a common set of assumptions drawn from **realism**:

1. **The state system is normal** — The world of sovereign states is accepted as the permanent condition of international society
2. **Pessimism about human nature** — Humans are seen as "inherently destructive, selfish, competitive and aggressive" (Raymond Aron, Niebuhr)
3. **Conflict is inescapable** — The starting point of strategic analysis
4. **Power is central** — National power and interest dominate the literature
5. **Scepticism about idealist solutions** — World government, general disarmament, collective security are viewed as impractical
6. **Moral neutrality** — Strategic analysis examines the world "as it is" not "as it ought to be"

## Relation to IR

Strategy and foreign policy are inseparable. As Raymond Aron said: "Strategic thought draws its inspiration each century, or rather at each moment in history, from the problems which events themselves pose." Henry Kissinger argued that "a separation of strategy and policy can only be achieved to the detriment of both. Since the difficult problems of national policy are in the area where political, economic, psychological and military factors overlap we should give up the fiction that there is such a thing as 'purely' military advice."

## The Strategic Studies—Security Studies Relationship

Strategic studies has moved from a narrow military focus to encompass:
- Non-violent responses to conflict
- The political, economic, and psychological instruments of power
- The peacetime applications of military power to achieve political objectives
- Broader security concerns (human security, environmental security, health security)

## Key Assumptions Underpinning Contemporary Strategic Thought

Most strategists belong to the same intellectual tradition — they share a common set of assumptions:
- They accept a world of sovereign states as the normal condition
- They are cautious in what can be achieved in international relations
- They are sceptical of comprehensive solutions (world government, general disarmament)
- They see conflict as an inescapable condition of international life
- They operate with "realist" assumptions about power, national interest, and self-help

## Major Areas
- **Deterrence** — Preventing action through threat of retaliation
- **Arms Control** — Managing weapons policy rather than abolishing weapons
- **Limited War** — Conflict constrained in scope, geography, or weapons used
- **Revolutionary Warfare** — Insurgency, counterinsurgency, guerrilla warfare
- **Crisis Management** — Controlling escalation during high-stakes confrontation
- **Alliances** — Cooperative security arrangements between states
- **Irregular Warfare** — Terrorism and insurgency
- **Cyber Strategy** — The rise of cyberpower and cyber warfare
- **Geopolitics and Grand Strategy** — The intersection of geography and strategic planning
- **Humanitarian Intervention and Peace Operations** — The use of force for humanitarian purposes

## Key Thinkers in Strategic Studies
- **Carl von Clausewitz** — *On War* (1832) — the trinitarian theory of war, friction, fog of war
- **Sun Tzu** — *The Art of War* — strategy without battle, deception, knowledge of enemy
- **Bernard Brodie** — "The absolute weapon" — nuclear revolution in strategy
- **Thomas Schelling** — *The Strategy of Conflict* — commitment, brinkmanship, bargaining
- **Herman Kahn** — Escalation ladder, thinking about the unthinkable
- **Albert Wohlstetter** — Nuclear strategy, vulnerable vs invulnerable forces
- **Lawrence Freedman** — Evolution of nuclear strategy, future of strategic studies
- **Colin S. Gray** — Strategic culture, geography and strategy, defence planning
- **Beatrice Heuser** — History of the practice of strategy from antiquity to Napoleon
- **Amitav Acharya & Jiajie He** — Non-western contributions to strategic thinking

## Strategic Culture
Different states have different strategic cultures — "the set of attitudes and beliefs held within a military establishment concerning the political objective of war and the most effective way of achieving it" (Lantis and Howlett). The concept bridges [[Constructivism]] and strategic studies: culture affects how states perceive threats, choose strategies, and employ military force.

## Critiques of Strategic Studies
1. **Moral neutrality** — The clinical, anodyne language of strategy ("take out" cities, "collateral damage", "counterforce strikes") desensitises observers to human suffering
2. **Ethnocentrism** — Dominated by Western, especially American, strategic thinking. "Strategic Studies: The West and the Rest" (Acharya & He) critiques this
3. **Nuclear bias** — Overwhelming focus on nuclear issues during the Cold War
4. **Gender blindness** — Strategic studies neglects gender dimensions of war, militarism, and conflict ([[Feminism]])
5. **State-centrism** — Strategy neglects non-state actors, insurgents, transnational forces
6. **Garrison state hypothesis** (Harold Lasswell) — The growing influence of "specialists in violence" threatens democratic control

## Non-Western Strategic Traditions
Key contribution from Acharya & He (Ch. 19, *Strategy in the Contemporary World*):
- **Kautilya** (India, 4th cent. BCE) — *Arthashastra* — Realpolitik, statecraft, the seven elements of state, six-fold policy (*Sandhi*, *Vigraha*, etc.)
- **Sun Tzu** — China — strategic deception, winning without fighting
- **Mao Zedong** — Revolutionary war, protracted people's war
- **Islamic strategic traditions** — Concepts of jihad, dar al-Islam/dar al-harb

## Exam Preparation
- Define strategy: from Clausewitz to Osgood to Beaufre (political-military coordination)
- Nuclear strategy: deterrence, compellence, arms control
- Apply strategic thinking to a contemporary case: Ukraine, US-China rivalry, cyber conflict
- Evaluate: does strategic studies have a future? (Lawrence Freedman: yes, but it must broaden)
