# Migration and Refugee Studies

## Definition

The study of cross-border population movements as an international political phenomenon, encompassing forced displacement, labour migration, and the governance regimes that regulate them. Migration has become one of the most salient issues in contemporary IR, sitting at the intersection of [[Sovereignty]], [[Security Dilemma]], [[Human Rights]], and [[Global Governance]].

## Key Debates

- **Migration as security (securitization thesis)** — States frame migration as an existential threat to justify extraordinary measures (Buzan, Wæver & de Wilde 1998). The Copenhagen School's [[Securitization]] framework applies directly: migration is "securitized" when political actors successfully present it as a threat requiring emergency response.
- **Migration as development** — The "migration-development nexus" argues that remittances, diaspora engagement, and circular migration benefit sending states (OECD 2014). This challenges the zero-sum realist framing.
- **Migration as human rights** — International refugee law provides normative protections; the 1951 Refugee Convention and its 1967 Protocol codify non-refoulement (Hathaway 2005).
- **Border externalization** — States increasingly outsource border control to third countries, extending sovereignty beyond territorial boundaries (Gammeltoft-Hansen & Tan 2017).

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
Migration is a **security threat**. States prioritise territorial integrity and demographic control. Population movements destabilise host states and create security externalities. The EU-Turkey deal exemplifies realist logic: paying a third state to contain refugees.

### Liberalism
Migration as **economic opportunity** requiring institutional management. Free movement of labour as economic efficiency (Bhagwati 2003). Institutional frameworks (UNHCR, IOM) enable cooperative management. [[Complex Interdependence]] predicts that economic interdependence reduces the political salience of migration.

### Constructivism
Migration is **socially constructed** through identity, belonging, and othering. Who counts as a "refugee" versus "migrant" is a political and normative category (Zetter 2007). Norm diffusion shapes asylum policies. The [[Norm Dynamics]] of refugee protection demonstrate how norms of hospitality compete with norms of securitisation.

### Critical Theory
The neoliberal **migration regime** serves global capital. Migration management is racialised: wealthy Global North states control movement while extracting labour from the Global South (Mezzadra & Neilson 2013). [[Postcolonialism]] reveals how colonial borders created the conditions for contemporary displacement.

### Feminism
Gendered dimensions of forced migration: sexual violence as weapon of war, women's disproportionate burden in displacement, gender-specific protection gaps (Enloe 2014). Women and children constitute over 70% of refugees globally.

## Key Institutions

- **UNHCR** — United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (1950). Mandate to protect refugees and seek durable solutions.
- **IOM** — International Organization for Migration (1951). Intergovernmental body managing migration governance.
- **1951 Refugee Convention** + **1967 Protocol** — Core legal framework defining refugee rights and state obligations.
- **Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (2018)** — First intergovernmental framework on migration (non-binding).
- **Global Compact on Refugees (2018)** — Complementary framework emphasising burden-sharing.

## Key Cases

- **EU-Turkey deal (2016)** — €6 billion payment for Turkey to host Syrian refugees; reduced crossings dramatically but raised human rights concerns (Gamal & Léonard 2019).
- **Rohingya crisis (2017-present)** — Myanmar's ethnic cleansing forced 1 million+ to Bangladesh; statelessness as a governance failure.
- **Central American caravans (2018-present)** — Mixed migration flows challenging US border governance; intersection of poverty, violence, and climate.
- **Mediterranean crossings (2015-present)** — Over 25,000 deaths since 2014; EU's "Fortress Europe" debate.
- **Australia's offshore processing** — Nauru and Manus Island; extraterritorial asylum management as deterrence model.
- **Syrian refugee crisis (2011-present)** — Largest displacement crisis since WWII; 6.8 million refugees, 6.9 million internally displaced.

## Key Concepts

- **Securitization of migration** — Framing migration as existential threat (Buzan et al. 1998)
- **Irregular migration** — Movement outside state-regulated channels
- **Internally displaced persons (IDPs)** — Displaced within own state; no international legal protection equivalent to refugees
- **Asylum seekers** — Persons seeking international protection but not yet recognised as refugees
- **Returns and pushbacks** — Contested state practices of forced return or denial of entry
- **Brain drain/gain** — Skilled migration from developing to developed states (Docquier & Rapoport 2012)

## Contemporary Issues

- **Climate migration** — Projected 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 (World Bank 2021). No legal framework for "climate refugees."
- **COVID-19 and border closures** — Pandemic accelerated securitisation of borders; health screening as migration control.
- **Weaponisation of migration** — Belarus-Poland (2021): Lukashenko deliberately pushed migrants to EU border as political leverage.
