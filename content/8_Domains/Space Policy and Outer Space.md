# Space Policy and Outer Space

## Definition

The geopolitics of outer space including militarisation, commercialisation, and governance. Space has evolved from a Cold War prestige competition to a domain of critical strategic and economic importance, linking [[Deterrence]], [[Sovereignty]], [[International Law]], and [[Global Governance]].

## Key Treaties

- **Outer Space Treaty (1967)** — Foundational: prohibits weapons of mass destruction in orbit, national appropriation of celestial bodies, and establishes space as the "province of all mankind." Limitations: does not ban conventional weapons or military activities in space.
- **Moon Agreement (1979)** — Extends "common heritage of mankind" to the Moon and celestial bodies. Not ratified by any major space power; effectively dead letter.
- **Artemis Accords (2020)** — US-led framework for lunar exploration; emphasises "safety zones" and resource extraction rights. Critiqued as bilateral rather than multilateral; Russia and China refuse to participate.

## Key Issues

- **Anti-satellite weapons (ASAT)** — Kinetic, co-orbital, and directed-energy systems capable of destroying satellites. Tests generate debris fields threatening all space actors.
- **Space debris** — Over 36,000 tracked objects; Kessler Syndrome risk of cascading collisions rendering orbits unusable (Kessler & Cour-Palais 1978).
- **Space resource extraction** — Legal grey area: OST prohibits "national appropriation" but not resource extraction. US Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act (2015) and Artemis Accords assert private property rights.
- **GPS/geolocation as strategic asset** — Military and civilian dependence on satellite positioning creates critical vulnerability.
- **Satellite communications in warfare** — Starlink's role in Ukraine demonstrated military utility of commercial constellations.
- **Space Force militarisation** — US Space Force (2019), China's Strategic Support Force, Russia's Aerospace Forces; space increasingly treated as warfighting domain.

## Key Cases

- **US-Soviet space race** — Cold War competition as [[Deterrence]] logic in space; Sputnik (1957), Apollo (1969), mutual restraint through shared vulnerability.
- **China ASAT test (2007)** — Destroyed own defunct weather satellite; created 3,500+ debris pieces; demonstrated anti-satellite capability.
- **India ASAT test (2019)** — Mission Shakti; destroyed own satellite at low orbit; criticised for debris generation.
- **Starlink in Ukraine (2022-present)** — Commercial satellite constellation providing internet to Ukrainian military; blurred civilian-military boundary.
- **Lunar south pole competition** — China-Russia ILRS vs US Artemis; strategic competition over lunar resources and footholds.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
Space as the **new high ground** for power projection. States pursue space-based capabilities for surveillance, communication, and weapons. ASAT development reflects security dilemma logic: each state's defensive capability is perceived as offensive by others. [[Deterrence]] theory applies to anti-satellite and anti-ballistic missile systems.

### Liberalism
Space **governance regimes** enable cooperation. The ISS as liberal institutional success; multilateral frameworks for debris mitigation, spectrum allocation, and search-and-rescue. The Artemis Accords reflect liberal institutionalist logic of rule-based order, though contested.

### Common Heritage of Mankind
A legal and normative principle: space resources belong to all humanity. Enshrined in OST (1967) and Moon Agreement (1979). Contested by states favouring property rights regimes. This principle links to [[Global Governance]] and [[International Law]] debates about global commons.

## Commercial Dimension

- **SpaceX** — Reusable launch vehicles (Falcon 9, Starship); Starlink satellite internet constellation (6,000+ satellites)
- **Blue Origin** — New Shepard (suborbital), New Glenn (orbital); lunar lander contract
- **Satellite constellations** — Mega-constellations for global broadband; light pollution and debris concerns
- **Space economy** — Estimated $546 billion+ (2023); growing rapidly with commercial launch and satellite services
- **Resource extraction legal frameworks** — OST ambiguity; US and Luxembourg assert private property rights; Russia and China argue for multilateral governance
