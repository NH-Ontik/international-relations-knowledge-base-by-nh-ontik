# Terrorism and Counter-Terrorism

## Definition

The study of politically motivated violence by non-state actors and the state responses to it. Terrorism occupies a contested conceptual space in IR: simultaneously a [[Security Dilemma]], a question of [[Sovereignty]], and a challenge to [[Humanitarian Intervention]] norms.

## Waves of Terrorism (Rapoport)

David Rapoport's four-wave model (2004) identifies distinct generational waves:

| Wave | Period | Character | Examples |
|------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **First** | Late C19 | Anarchist/international | Assassination campaigns, propaganda of the deed |
| **Second** | Mid-C20 | Anti-colonial/national liberation | FLN (Algeria), Viet Cong, ANC |
| **Third** | 1960s-70s | New Left/ideological | Red Brigades, Baader-Meinhof, Weather Underground |
| **Fourth** | 1980s-2000s | Religious fundamentalism | Al-Qaeda, Hezbollah, Aum Shinrikyo |
| **Fifth** | 2010s-present | Lone wolf/incel/accelerationist | Christchurch, El Paso, January 6 |

**Critique**: Rapoport's waves overlap; religious terrorism predates the fourth wave; the model underestimates state terrorism (Ganor 2002).

## Key Concepts

### Radicalisation Models
- **Moghaddam's staircase** (1960s) — Ground floor (perceived injustice) → higher floors (displacement of aggression, moral engagement, categorical thinking, legitimisation of violence) → top floor (terrorism)
- **McCauley & Moskalenko's two pyramids** — Radicalisation of opinion vs. radicalisation of action; not all who hold extreme views engage in violence

### Lone Wolf Terrorism
- Individual actors with no direct organisational ties; inspired by but not commanded by groups
- Christchurch mosque shooting (2019), Christchurch attack; 51 killed
- Difficult to detect; challenges traditional intelligence approaches

### State Sponsorship of Terrorism
- States providing funding, training, safe haven to non-state armed groups
- Iran-Hezbollah relationship; Pakistan-Taliban nexus (contested)
- Complicates [[Sovereignty]] and [[International Law]]

### Blowback
- Unintended consequences of covert operations; states radicalised by their own foreign policy
- CIA funding of Afghan mujahideen → al-Qaeda (Johnson 2004)

### Securitisation of Terrorism
- 9/11 as paradigm shift: "Global War on Terror" securitised an entire category of political violence
- [[Securitization]] framework: terrorism as existential threat justifying emergency measures (Buzan et al. 1998)

### Counter-Terrorism Strategies
- **Kinetic** — Military force, drone strikes, special operations
- **CVE/PVE** — Countering/Preventing Violent Extremism; soft power approaches
- **Hearts and minds** — Winning population support against insurgents
- **Financial** — Disrupting terror financing networks (FATF, banking regulations)

## Key Cases

- **9/11 (2001)** — 2,977 killed; catalysed "War on Terror," invasions of Afghanistan and Iraq; restructured global security architecture.
- **7/7 London (2005)** — 52 killed; homegrown radicalisation; British Muslim community as site of both vulnerability and resilience.
- **ISIS caliphate (2014-2019)** — Territorial control in Syria/Iraq; state-building by non-state actor; attracted foreign fighters; unprecedented social media strategy.
- **Boko Haram (2009-present)** — Chibok girls kidnapping (2014); regional instability in Lake Chad Basin; 350,000+ killed.
- **Christchurch mosque shooting (2019)** — White supremacist terrorism; 51 killed; livestreamed attack; accelerated right-wing extremism discourse.
- **January 6 Capitol insurrection (2021)** — Domestic violent extremism; attempted overthrow of democratic process; debated classification as terrorism.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
Terrorism as **asymmetric warfare**. Non-state actors exploit the gap between state military superiority and vulnerability to low-cost attacks. State responses reflect security dilemma logic: aggressive measures may generate more terrorism (blowback).

### Liberalism
Root causes analysis: **governance failures, poverty, and lack of political participation** create conditions for terrorism. Institution-building, development, and democracy promotion as long-term solutions (Cronin 2009).

### Constructivism
Terrorism is **socially constructed** through identity, narrative, and securitisation. Who counts as "terrorist" vs. "freedom fighter" is a political and normative distinction. The "War on Terror" as a constructed narrative that shaped state responses.

### Critical Theory
**Western foreign policy as cause** of terrorism. Colonialism, intervention, and support for authoritarian regimes generate grievances. Terrorism is a symptom of deeper structural violence (Galtung 1996).

## Policy Frameworks

- **UN Counter-Terrorism Committee** — Established post-9/11 (Resolution 1373); monitors state compliance
- **FATF** — Financial Action Task Force; combats terror financing through banking regulations
- **Global Coalition to Defeat ISIS** — 80+ member coalition; military and de-radicalisation efforts
- **No-fly lists and surveillance programmes** — Civil liberties vs. security trade-offs
