# Norm Dynamics

## Definition

How international norms emerge, spread, become internalised, and sometimes decay. Norm dynamics is a core research programme within [[Constructivism]] and has significant implications for [[International Law]], [[Global Governance]], and [[Norm Dynamics]] themselves.

## Key Frameworks

### Finnemore & Sikkink Norm Life Cycle (1998)

The foundational model of norm evolution:

1. **Norm emergence** — Norm entrepreneurs (usually NGOs, states, or IOs) frame ideas to attract attention. Mechanism: "framing" and strategic persuasion.
2. **Norm cascade** — Once a critical mass of states adopt the norm, rapid diffusion occurs. Mechanism: socialisation, demonstration effects, shaming.
3. **Norm internalisation** — Norm becomes so taken-for-granted it no longer requires deliberate enforcement. Mechanism: institutionalisation, habit formation.

**Key mechanism**: Norm entrepreneurs — actors who invest resources to promote new norms against prevailing standards (Finnemore & Sikkink 1998: 895).

### Risse & Kappen Spiral Model (1994)

A model of human rights norm diffusion through domestic and international pressure:

1. **Domestic repression** — States violate human rights norms
2. **Transnational advocacy** — NGOs, international organisations raise awareness
3. **Norm diffusion** — International pressure and domestic civil society engagement
4. **Domestic compliance** — States modify behaviour (at least outwardly)

**Key mechanism**: Transnational advocacy networks (Keck & Sikkink 1998); "boomerang effect" — domestic actors bypass the state to seek international support.

### Checkel Norm "Lifecycle" Critique

Norms don't die — they get **contested**. The life cycle model implies teleological progress, but norms can be weakened, reinterpreted, or challenged. Norm decay is possible but contested (Wiener 2014).

### Wiener Norm Contestation Theory

Norms are **always contested**; contestation is constitutive, not pathological. Norms have:
- **Internal validity** (compliance within a community)
- **External validity** (recognition by outsiders)

Contestation occurs when actors disagree about interpretation, scope, or applicability. The process of contestation is how norms evolve (Wiener 2014: 221).

## Key Concepts

- **Norm entrepreneurs** — Actors who invest resources to promote new norms; often NGOs (ICAN for landmines) or states (Canada for landmines ban)
- **Norm localization** (Acharya 2004) — Process by which imported norms are adapted to local cultural and political contexts; resistance to "Western" norms through selective adoption
- **Norm subsidiarity** (Acharya 2011) — Local actors use international norms as raw material for constructing local norms; bottom-up norm creation
- **Appropriateness vs. consequences** — Do actors follow norms because they believe it's "right" (logic of appropriateness) or because of expected outcomes (logic of consequences)?
- **Tipping point** — The moment when a critical mass of states adopt a norm, triggering rapid cascade
- **Norm resistance** — Active rejection of norms by states or actors; backlash against human rights norms, for example

## Key Cases

- **Anti-personnel mine ban (Ottawa Treaty, 1997)** — Paradigmatic norm entrepreneur case: NGOs (ICAN), middle powers (Canada, Norway), and ICRC framed landmines as humanitarian issue; 164 states parties. Non-signatories include US, Russia, China.
- **R2P (Responsibility to Protect)** — Norm emergence: International Commission on Intervention and State Sovereignty (2001). Norm contestation: Libya 2011 as "responsibility while protecting"; Syrian civil war as norm failure.
- **Torture prohibition** — Deep norm; near-universal internalisation but contested in practice (enhanced interrogation, Abu Ghraib). Demonstrates gap between norm internalisation and compliance.
- **Human rights norm diffusion** — Universal Declaration (1948) → regional systems → domestic adoption; uneven implementation; backlash in "illiberal" democracies (Hungary, Poland).
- **Responsibility to protect** — Norm contestation after Libya; "selective application" critique; Syrian crisis as norm failure.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Constructivism
Norms are **constitutive**: they define what actors are and what counts as legitimate behaviour. Norms are not just constraints on behaviour but constitutive of identity and interest. [[Constructivism]] provides the theoretical foundation for norm dynamics research.

### Liberalism
Norms enable **cooperation** by reducing transaction costs, providing focal points, and creating shared expectations. Institutional frameworks (UN, ICJ, ICC) support norm enforcement and monitoring.

### Realism
Norms are **tools of power**: powerful states promote norms that serve their interests. "Norm entrepreneurs" are often Western states and NGOs promoting Western liberal values. Norm compliance reflects power distribution, not genuine belief.

### Critical Theory
**Whose norms?** Norm diffusion reproduces Eurocentrism. International human rights norms emerged from Western liberal tradition; their "universal" status is contested by Global South actors. Norm localization (Acharya) as resistance to cultural imperialism.
