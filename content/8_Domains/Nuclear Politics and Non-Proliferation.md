# Nuclear Politics and Non-Proliferation

## Definition

The international politics of nuclear weapons including [[Deterrence]], proliferation, [[Arms Control]], and disarmament. The nuclear age (1945-present) has structured international relations more profoundly than perhaps any other technological development, creating the conditions for both existential risk and strategic stability.

## Key Treaties

| Treaty | Year | Key Provisions | Status |
|--------|------|---------------|--------|
| **NPT** (Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty) | 1968 | Non-proliferation; disarmament obligation (Art. VI); peaceful nuclear cooperation | 191 states; India, Pakistan, Israel, South Sudan non-parties |
| **CTBT** (Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty) | 1996 | Bans all nuclear explosions; 187 signatories | Not entered into force (US, China, etc. ratification pending) |
| **INF Treaty** | 1987 | Eliminated intermediate-range nuclear missiles (US-Soviet) | Collapsed 2019 (US withdrawal) |
| **New START** | 2010 | Limit deployed strategic warheads to 1,550 each (US-Russia) | Extended 2021; expired 2026 amid Ukraine war tensions |
| **RCPNW** (Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons) | 2017 | Comprehensive ban on nuclear weapons; entered into force 2021 | Not signed by any nuclear-armed state |
| **JCPOA** (Iran Nuclear Deal) | 2015 | Limits Iran's nuclear programme in exchange for sanctions relief | Collapsed after US withdrawal 2018; partially restored |

## Key Concepts

- **Nuclear deterrence** — The threat of nuclear retaliation to prevent nuclear or conventional attack. Core logic: mutual assured destruction (MAD) makes nuclear war irrational (Schelling 1960).
- **Extended deterrence** — A nuclear power's commitment to use nuclear weapons to protect non-nuclear allies (US nuclear umbrella over NATO, Japan, South Korea).
- **Nuclear taboo** — The norm against nuclear use that has persisted since 1945; not explicable by deterrence logic alone (Tannenwald 2007). Links to [[Norm Dynamics]] and [[Constructivism]].
- **Nuclear learning** — How states learn from nuclear history to calibrate behaviour; changes in nuclear doctrine over time (Haas 2015).
- **Hedging strategies** — States maintaining latent nuclear capability without weaponisation (Japan, Iran pre-2015).
- **Latency** — The technical capacity to rapidly produce nuclear weapons; Japan's plutonium stockpile as paradigmatic case.
- **Rollback** — Successful cases of nuclear disarmament: South Africa, Libya, post-Soviet Ukraine, Kazakhstan, Belarus.

## Key Cases

- **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** — Paradigmatic deterrence success: US-Soviet brinkmanship averted nuclear war. Demonstrated both the stability-instability paradox and the fragility of deterrence (Allison & Zelikow 1999).
- **India-Pakistan nuclear rivalry** — 1998 tests; crisis confrontations (Kargil 1999, 2001-02 standoff, Balakot 2019); tactical nuclear weapons risk.
- **North Korean nuclear programme** — Six-party talks failed; Pyongyang tested nuclear weapons (2006-2017); deterrence on the Korean Peninsula uncertain.
- **Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA, 2015-2018)** — Multilateral diplomacy; US withdrawal collapsed framework; Iran increased enrichment.
- **AUKUS (2021)** — US-UK providing Australia with nuclear-powered submarines; proliferation concerns (nuclear-powered ≠ nuclear-armed); IAEA verification challenges.
- **Israeli nuclear ambiguity** — Neither confirms nor denies nuclear arsenal; estimated 80-400 warheads; unique deterrence posture.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
**Nuclear stability theory** (Waltz 1981): nuclear weapons promote peace through deterrence. Offensive realism predicts proliferation; defensive realism predicts restraint. Mearsheimer argues nuclear weapons made great power war obsolete but did not eliminate security competition.

### Liberalism
**Institutional non-proliferation**: the NPT regime, IAEA safeguards, and export controls as institutional frameworks preventing proliferation. [[International Organizations]] as norm enforcers. Arms control as cooperative security through verification and transparency.

### Constructivism
**Nuclear taboo** (Tannenwald 2007): normative prohibition against nuclear use is not reducible to deterrence calculation. Identity politics of nuclear haves and have-nots: the NPT's Article V bargain (disarmament for non-proliferation) is deeply contested. The "nuclear club" as identity structure.

### Critical Theory
**Nuclear colonialism**: the NPT as discriminatory regime — nuclear-armed states retain weapons while prohibiting others. Underground testing in indigenous lands (Marshall Islands, Nevada, French Polynesia) as environmental racism. Disarmament as [[Human Rights]] issue.

## Current Issues

- **AUKUS and proliferation concerns** — South Pacific Nuclear Free Zone Treaty (Rarotonga) may be undermined; precedent for nuclear-powered submarine transfers.
- **Nuclear modernisation** — US ($1.7 trillion programme), Russia, China expanding arsenals; modernisation undermines disarmament norms.
- **Tactical nuclear weapons** — Russia's doctrine and Ukraine war rhetoric; lower threshold for nuclear use; destabilising.
- **Nuclear terrorism risk** — Loose materials, non-state actor interest; low probability but catastrophic consequences.
- **Disarmament movement** — Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (2017/2021); ICJ advisory opinion; humanitarian initiative.
