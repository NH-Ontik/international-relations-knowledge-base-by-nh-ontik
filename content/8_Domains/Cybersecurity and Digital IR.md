# Cybersecurity and Digital IR

## Definition

The intersection of information technology, state power, and international order. Cyberspace has emerged as a fifth domain of warfare (alongside land, sea, air, and space), fundamentally altering the logic of [[Deterrence]], [[Sovereignty]], and [[Global Governance]].

## Key Concepts

- **Cyber deterrence** — Applying classical deterrence logic to cyberspace; complicated by attribution problems, the low cost of attack, and uncertain escalation dynamics (Nye 2017).
- **Attribution problem** — The difficulty of definitively identifying the source of a cyber operation; enables plausible deniability.
- **Offense-defense balance** — Cyberspace generally favours the attacker; defensive postures are inherently reactive (Schmitt 2017).
- **Digital sovereignty** — States asserting control over data flows, internet governance, and digital infrastructure within their borders (Brousell 2020).
- **Info-ops** — Information operations including propaganda, disinformation, and social media manipulation as instruments of state power.
- **Hack-and-leak** — Exfiltrating and publishing stolen data for political effect (Healey 2017).
- **Critical infrastructure protection** — Defending power grids, financial systems, and telecommunications from cyber attack.

## Key Thinkers and Works

- **Joseph Nye** — *Cyber Power* (2011); cyber power as co-optive and relational; the "deterrence-plus" framework
- **Brandon Valeriano & Ryan Maness** — *Cyber War versus Cyber Peace* (2015); most cyber operations are low-level espionage, not war
- **Michael Schmitt** — *Tallinn Manual 2.0* (2017); legal analysis of cyber operations under international law
- **Thomas Rid** — *Active Measures: The Secret History of Disinformation and Political Warfare* (2020); Russia's long history of information warfare
- **Rid & Healey** — "Fifth Generation Espionage" (2019); espionage evolving from human to cyber to influence operations

## Key Cases

- **Stuxnet (US-Israel, ~2007-2010)** — First confirmed state-sponsored cyber weapon; destroyed Iranian nuclear centrifuges. Demonstrated cyber operations as substitute for kinetic strikes (Zetter 2014).
- **Russian interference in 2016 US election** — Social media manipulation, hack-and-leak (DNC), disinformation campaigns (Rid 2020).
- **SolarWinds hack (2020)** — Russian SVR compromise of US government networks through supply chain attack; unprecedented scale of espionage.
- **Colonial Pipeline ransomware (2021)** — Criminal (DarkSide) attack on US critical infrastructure; raised questions about private sector resilience.
- **Ukraine grid attacks (2015, 2016)** — BlackEnergy/Industroyer malware; first confirmed cyber attacks on power grids (Lee et al. 2016).
- **Microsoft Exchange hack (Hafnium, 2021)** — Chinese state-linked group exploiting zero-day vulnerabilities.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
Cyberspace as a **new domain of power competition**. States pursue cyber capabilities for strategic advantage; deterrence through punishment and denial. The offense-defense balance matters for [[Arms Control]] analogue in cyberspace.

### Liberalism
Governance **norms and institutions** are needed to manage cyber conflict. The Budapest Convention, UN processes (UN GGE), and multi-stakeholder internet governance reflect liberal institutionalist logic.

### Constructivism
**Norms of responsible state behaviour** in cyberspace are still emerging. The Tallinn Manual and UN frameworks attempt to construct shared understandings of what constitutes acceptable state activity online.

### Critical Theory
**Surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff 2019) and **digital colonialism** — Big Tech companies and powerful states extract data as a form of value; digital sovereignty discourse masks new forms of control. [[Assemblage Theory and ANT]] illuminates the human-technology assemblages constituting cyber power.

## Policy Frameworks

- **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001)** — First international treaty on cybercrime; limited adoption outside Council of Europe.
- **Tallinn Manual 2.0 (2017)** — Non-binding academic study applying international law to cyber operations.
- **UN GGE on ICT (2015, 2017, 2021, 2025)** — Group of Governmental Experts developing norms for responsible state behaviour.
- **Paris Call for Trust and Security in Cyberspace (2018)** — Multi-stakeholder initiative for internet stability.
