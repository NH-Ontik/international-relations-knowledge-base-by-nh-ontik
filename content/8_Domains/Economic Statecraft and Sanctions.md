# Economic Statecraft and Sanctions

## Definition

The use of economic tools — sanctions, trade policy, investment controls, and financial statecraft — as instruments of foreign policy. Economic statecraft sits at the intersection of [[International Political Economy]], [[Sovereignty]], [[Hegemony]], and [[Complex Interdependence]].

## Key Concepts

- **Sanctions types** — *Comprehensive* (all trade cut off, e.g., Iraq 1990s), *targeted/smart* (individuals, sectors, e.g., Magnitsky sanctions), *sectoral* (specific industries like energy or finance).
- **Sanctions effectiveness debate** — Hufbauer et al. (1985, revised 2007) find sanctions succeed ~34% of the time; critics argue selection bias and measurement problems (Peksen & Son 2015).
- **Sanctions resistance** — Target states develop evasion strategies: front companies, alternative payment systems, barter trade, cryptocurrency.
- **Secondary sanctions** — Sanctions imposed on third-party states/companies for doing business with a targeted state; extraterritorial application of national law (e.g., US sanctions on entities dealing with Iran).
- **Weaponised interdependence** (Farrell & Newman 2019) — States exploit asymmetric positions in global networks (financial, technological, data) as coercive leverage. The dollar's centrality enables financial coercion.
- **Decoupling vs. de-risking** — Decoupling: severing economic ties (US-China tech war). De-risking: reducing critical dependencies while maintaining broader economic engagement (EU approach).
- **Dollar weaponisation** — Using USD dominance and SWIFT access as coercive tools; accelerated de-dollarisation efforts.

## Key Thinkers

- **Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott & Oegg** — *Economic Sanctions Reconsidered* (1985, 3rd ed. 2007); foundational empirical study of sanctions effectiveness.
- **Baldwin, D.** — *Economic Statecraft* (1985); conceptual framework for economic instruments of power.
- **Farrell, H. & Newman, A.** — "Weaponized Interdependence" (2019, *International Security*); hub-and-spoke network structures as sources of coercive power.
- **Drezner, D.** — *The Sanctions Paradox: Statecraft and Strategy* (1999); economic coercion as rational state behaviour despite mixed results.

## Key Cases

- **US sanctions on Iran** — Comprehensive sanctions (2010-2015) forced JCPOA negotiations; maximum pressure campaign (2018-present) after US withdrawal; oil exports fell from 2.5M to <500K bbl/day.
- **Russia sanctions (2014, 2022)** — Post-Crimea: sectoral sanctions on finance, energy, defence. Post-invasion (2022): SWIFT disconnection, central bank asset freezes, oil price cap; unprecedented scale but limited regime change effect.
- **North Korea sanctions regime** — UN Security Council sanctions (2006-2017); near-total trade embargo; limited effect on nuclear programme; humanitarian concerns.
- **South Africa sanctions (anti-apartheid)** — Divestment, arms embargo, sports boycotts; contributed to apartheid's end; paradigmatic successful sanctions case.
- **Cuba embargo (1960-present)** — Longest-running sanctions regime; largely ineffective at regime change; demonstrated sanctions durability as political tool.
- **Tech export controls** — CHIPS Act, Huawei restrictions, semiconductor export controls; "small yard, high fence" approach to technology competition with China.

## Theoretical Lenses

### Realism
Economic tools as **instruments of power**. Sanctions project power without military force; secondary sanctions extend sovereignty extraterritorially. Realism predicts sanctions work when the target lacks alternatives (e.g., Iran's isolation vs. Russia's diversified partnerships).

### Liberalism
**Economic interdependence promotes peace** (Keohane & Nye 1977); sanctions violate liberal logic but reflect institutional capacity for collective action. International organisations (UNSC) can legitimise sanctions as rule enforcement.

### International Political Economy
**Structural power** (Strange 1988): control over credit, security, knowledge, and production shapes state leverage. Dollar hegemony and SWIFT centrality reflect structural US power in the global financial system. [[Hegemony]] depends on economic as well as military dominance.

### Critical Theory
Sanctions **harm civilians** disproportionately; humanitarian costs fall on the most vulnerable. Sanctions reproduce **neo-colonial** power relations: wealthy states coerce poorer ones. The "sanctions as warfare" critique: economic warfare kills (Gordon 2010).

## Contemporary Issues

- **De-dollarisation (BRICS)** — Russia, China, and others seeking alternatives to USD; digital currencies, bilateral trade agreements; limited but growing.
- **SWIFT as weapon** — Russia's SWIFT disconnection (2022) demonstrated both power and limits; alternative systems (CIPS, SPFS) emerging.
- **Sanctions evasion networks** — Turkey, UAE, Kazakhstan as sanctions circumvention hubs; cryptocurrency and hawala networks.
- **Secondary sanctions disputes** — EU "blocking statute" against US Iran sanctions; transatlantic tensions over extraterritorial application.
- **Sanctions fatigue** — Cost to sanctioning states (energy prices, trade disruption); political pressure to ease restrictions.
