# Health Governance

## Definition

The international governance of health issues, from infectious disease to health systems strengthening. Health has been progressively securitised since the late 1990s, linking [[Global Governance]], [[International Organizations]], [[Securitization]], and [[Norm Dynamics]].

## Key Institutions

- **WHO** — World Health Organization (1948); UN specialised agency for global health governance. Constitution defines health as "a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being."
- **World Health Assembly** — WHO's supreme decision-making body; sets policies and approves budgets.
- **Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria** (2002) — Partnership model for disease funding; liberal institutionalist success story.
- **Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance** (2000) — Public-private partnership for vaccine access in developing countries.
- **CEPI** — Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations (2017); vaccine development for emerging infectious diseases.
- **Pandemic treaty negotiations** (2022-present) — WHO member states negotiating a new instrument on pandemic prevention, preparedness, and response.

## Key Concepts

- **Health security (securitization of disease)** — Framing infectious disease as a national security threat. Enabled resource mobilisation but militarised responses and deprioritised health systems strengthening (Elbe 2006).
- **Pandemic preparedness** — State and international capacity to detect, assess, and respond to outbreaks. Critiqued as biased toward wealthy states.
- **Vaccine nationalism** — States securing vaccine supplies ahead of others; rich countries pre-purchased 70%+ of early COVID-19 doses.
- **COVAX** — Global facility for vaccine equity; co-led by Gavi, CEPI, WHO. Achieved 1.9 billion doses but fell short of targets.
- **Intellectual property (TRIPS waiver)** — Debate over temporarily suspending patent protections for COVID-19 vaccines to enable manufacturing in developing countries.
- **One Health approach** — Integrated human-animal-environment health framework; recognises zoonotic spillover risk (60%+ of emerging infections are zoonotic).

## Historical Arc

| Period | Event | Significance |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| 1967 | Smallpox eradication campaign begins | Triumph of international cooperation; eradicated 1980 |
| 1980s-present | HIV/AIDS crisis | Exposed structural inequalities; activist pressure reshaped global health architecture |
| 2003 | SARS outbreak | First 21st-century pandemic scare; led to revised IHR (2005) |
| 2009 | H1N1 influenza | Relatively mild; criticised for WHO overreaction |
| 2014-16 | Ebola outbreak (West Africa) | Exposed WHO weaknesses; 11,000+ deaths; delayed international response |
| 2020-23 | COVID-19 pandemic | 7+ million confirmed deaths; greatest global health crisis since 1918 |
| 2022-present | mpox (monkeypox) | Global outbreak; showed uneven response capacity |

## Theoretical Lenses

### Liberalism
Institutional **cooperation** as the mechanism for global health. The Global Fund, Gavi, and WHO as liberal institutions enabling collective action. COVID-19 COVAX initiative as both liberal success (cooperation attempted) and failure (vaccine nationalism undermined equity).

### Realism
**Vaccine nationalism** and **bioweapons** reflect realist state-centrism. States prioritise domestic populations; health cooperation collapses when it conflicts with national interest. Biological weapons programs (Soviet Biopreparat, alleged programmes) demonstrate health as security domain.

### Critical Theory
Structural **inequalities in global health** reproduce colonial power relations. Pharmaceutical capitalism extracts value from Global South populations while restricting access. The TRIPS waiver debate reveals how intellectual property regimes serve corporate and state interests over human need (Bose 2021).

### Securitization Theory
Health has been progressively **securitised**: the UN Security Council passed Resolution 1308 on HIV/AIDS (2000); WHO declared COVID-19 a Public Health Emergency of International Concern. Securitisation mobilises resources but distorts priorities toward threat response rather than systems strengthening (McInnes & Lee 2012).

## Contemporary Issues

- **WHO reform** — Structural and financial reforms post-COVID; balance between sovereignty and collective action
- **Pandemic treaty negotiations** — Binding or non-binding? Pathogen sharing and benefit sharing; equity provisions
- **mRNA technology transfer** — South Africa mRNA hub; debates over technology sharing and pharmaceutical monopolies
- **Antimicrobial resistance** — "Silent pandemic"; projected 10 million annual deaths by 2050 (O'Neill 2016)
- **Mental health as global governance challenge** — COVID-19 amplified mental health crisis; yet still marginalised in global health governance
