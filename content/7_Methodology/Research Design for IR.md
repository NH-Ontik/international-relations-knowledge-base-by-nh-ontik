# Research Design for IR

## Overview

Research design is the **logic of inference** — the strategy for moving from a research question to an evidence-based answer. A good research design specifies the causal claims to be evaluated, the evidence to be collected, and the procedures for drawing inferences from that evidence (Gerring, 2012).

In IR, research design matters because the discipline faces distinctive challenges: limited access to cases (most states rarely go to war), difficulty in measurement (concepts like "power" and "threat" are contested), and the complexity of international politics (many variables interact in non-linear ways). A poor design will produce misleading conclusions, no matter how sophisticated the methods.

---

## From Question to Design

The standard process of research design in IR follows a logical sequence:

```
Topic → Puzzle → Question → Theory → Hypothesis → Method → Evidence → Argument
```

### Step 1: From Topic to Puzzle

A topic is a broad area of interest ("civil war," "international institutions," "nuclear proliferation"). A **puzzle** is a specific anomaly or tension within that topic that requires explanation: why do civil wars recur in some countries but not others? Why did institutions form after World War II but not after World War I?

### Step 2: From Puzzle to Question

A research question narrows the puzzle to a tractable inquiry. Good research questions in IR are:

- **Specific** — Addressed to a defined set of cases and a defined time period
- **Answerable** — Amenable to evidence, not purely normative
- **Significant** — Address an important theoretical or empirical issue

### Step 3: From Question to Theory

A theory provides a causal explanation for the phenomenon under investigation. It specifies the **mechanism** — the process through which the independent variable produces the dependent variable.

### Step 4: From Theory to Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a testable prediction derived from theory. It specifies a relationship between variables:

- **Independent variable** (cause): the factor hypothesised to produce the effect
- **Dependent variable** (effect): the phenomenon to be explained
- **Control variables** (alternative explanations): factors that might also explain the outcome

### Step 5: From Hypothesis to Method

The choice of method depends on the type of question, the available cases, and the epistemological commitments of the researcher (see [[Epistemology in IR]]).

### Step 6: From Method to Evidence

Evidence is the empirical data that will be used to evaluate the hypothesis. The quality of evidence depends on:

- **Validity** — Does the measure capture what it claims to measure?
- **Reliability** — Would different observers arrive at the same measurement?
- **Relevance** — Is the evidence actually informative about the hypothesis?

### Step 7: From Evidence to Argument

An argument synthesises the evidence into a coherent causal claim, addressing alternative explanations and specifying the scope of the findings.

## King, Keohane & Verba (1994) — "Designing Social Inquiry"

### The Gold Standard

King, Keohane, and Verba's (KKV) *Designing Social Inquiry* (1994) is the most influential (and controversial) methodological text in political science. KKV argued that all scientific inference — whether qualitative or quantitative — should follow a common logic:

1. **Inference** — The goal of social science is to make valid causal inferences about the world.
2. **Bias** — Research designs should minimise bias (systematic error).
3. **Variance** — Research designs should maximise the amount of information about the causal relationship.

### KKV's Recommendations

- Use **systematic comparison** (not idiosyncratic case studies)
- Control for **confounding variables**
- Select cases that **maximise variance** on the independent variable
- Report **uncertainty** (standard errors, confidence intervals)

### The Brady & Collier Critique

Brady and Collier (2010) argued that KKV's "quantitative-centric" framework distorts qualitative research by imposing inappropriate standards:

- Qualitative researchers should seek **causal mechanisms**, not just covariation
- **Process tracing** (within-case evidence) is as valid as cross-case comparison
- The assumption that qualitative and quantitative research follow the "same logic" is misleading

## Gerring (2012) — Social Science Methodology

### Case Study Selection and Scope Conditions

John Gerring's *Social Science Methodology* (2012) provides a comprehensive framework for research design, including:

### The "When" and "How" Decision Tree

| Question Type | Method | Example |
|---------------|--------|---------|
| **How much?** (variation across cases) | Quantitative / cross-case | How much does trade reduce conflict? |
| **How many?** (frequency) | Quantitative | How many democracies go to war? |
| **What kind?** (classification) | Typological / comparative | What types of alliances are most durable? |
| **How?** (causal mechanism) | Qualitative / process tracing | How does securitisation work? |
| **Why?** (causal explanation) | Qualitative / comparative | Why did the Iraq War happen? |

### Scope Conditions

Gerring emphasises the importance of specifying **scope conditions** — the boundaries within which a theory applies. A theory that explains European great-power politics may not apply to Sub-Saharan African intrastate conflict. Scope conditions define:

- **Temporal boundaries** — During which time period does the theory apply?
- **Spatial boundaries** — In which regions or types of states does the theory apply?
- **Substantive boundaries** — For which types of issues does the theory apply?

## Van Evera (1997) — Guide to Methods for Students of Politics

### Hypothesis Testing and Boolean Logic

Stephen Van Evera's guide provides practical guidance for hypothesis testing:

### Types of Hypotheses

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Barn Door** | Hypothesis is so obviously true it needs no testing | "Great powers are militarily stronger than small states" |
| **Non-risky** | Hypothesis is plausible but uninformative | "Alliances affect war" |
| **Risky** | Hypothesis makes a strong, counterintuitive prediction | "Democratic states rarely fight each other" |

### Boolean Logic in Comparative Research

Van Evera applies Boolean logic to research design:

- **Necessary conditions** — Conditions that must be present for the outcome to occur (X is necessary for Y).
- **Sufficient conditions** — Conditions that guarantee the outcome (X is sufficient for Y).
- **INUS conditions** — **I**nsufficient but **N**ecessary parts of **U**nnecessary but **S**ufficient conditions — a condition that is part of one sufficient path but is not sufficient by itself.

## Research Questions in IR

### How and Why Questions

Yin (2018) argues that **"how" and "why" questions** are best suited to case study research because they require an explanatory, process-oriented approach:

- **How** does nuclear deterrence work?
- **Why** did the United States invade Iraq in 2003?
- **How** do international norms diffuse across states?

**"What" questions** are better suited to quantitative analysis:

- **What** is the correlation between trade and peace?
- **How many** states have acquired nuclear weapons?

### Question Design Principles

1. **Avoid normative framing** — "Should the US invade Iraq?" is a policy question, not a research question. "Why did the US invade Iraq?" is a research question.
2. **Specify the puzzle** — Why is the phenomenon surprising or requiring explanation?
3. **Be answerable** — Can the question be addressed with available evidence?

## Causal Inference in IR

### Correlation ≠ Causation

The most fundamental principle of research design is that **correlation does not imply causation**. A statistical association between two variables may be caused by:

- **Reverse causation** — Y causes X, not X causes Y
- **Omitted variable bias** — Z causes both X and Y
- **Selection bias** — The sample is not representative
- **Spurious correlation** — The association is coincidental

### Mechanisms vs Effects

Causal claims in IR should specify **mechanisms** — the processes through which X produces Y. A mechanism is a sequence of events or states that links cause to effect:

- **Intervening variable** — X → M → Y (M is the mechanism)
- **Concurrent cause** — X and Z together produce Y
- **Feedback loop** — X → Y → X (endogeneity)

### Counterfactuals

Counterfactual reasoning asks: what would have happened in the absence of the treatment? "Would the Cold War have occurred if Hitler had not invaded the Soviet Union?" Counterfactuals are:

- **Useful** for clarifying causal claims
- **Problematic** because they are inherently untestable
- **Necessary** for evaluating natural experiments and historical cases

Key references: Tetlock & Belkin (1996), *Counterfactual Thought Experiments in World Politics*.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Selection on the dependent variable** | Selecting only cases where Y occurred, preventing variation | Studying only democratic peace cases to test the democratic peace thesis |
| **Post hoc ergo propter hoc** | Assuming temporal precedence implies causation | "After A came B, therefore A caused B" |
| **Omitted variable bias** | Failing to control for alternative explanations | Studying trade and peace without controlling for regime type |
| **Small N problems** | Too few cases for reliable inference | Drawing general conclusions from one case study |
| **Overdetermination** | Multiple causes produce the same outcome | All major powers were democracies in 1990 — democracy or power? |
| **Ecological fallacy** | Inferring individual-level causation from aggregate data | Inferring that trade reduces war from state-level correlations |
