# Qualitative Methods in IR

## Overview

Qualitative methods constitute a broad family of research approaches united by their commitment to **in-depth analysis of a limited number of cases**. Rather than seeking statistical generalisation across many observations, qualitative researchers aim for **analytical generalisation** — generating and testing propositions through close engagement with cases, contexts, and causal mechanisms (Yin, 2018; George & Bennett, 2005).

In IR, qualitative methods have a long tradition. The discipline's foundational texts — Thucydides on the Peloponnesian War, Morgenthau's analysis of power politics, Waltz's structural theory — are built on careful case analysis, historical reasoning, and theoretical interpretation. While quantitative methods gained prominence from the 1960s onward, the "qualitative resurgence" of the 1990s and 2000s reaffirmed the importance of case-based, interpretive, and mechanism-oriented research (George & Bennett, 2005; Beach & Pedersen, 2019).

## Process Tracing

### Within-Case Causal Inference

Process tracing is a method for identifying the **causal mechanisms** that link an independent variable to an outcome within a single case or small number of cases. Unlike cross-case statistical methods, which establish correlations, process tracing examines the **intervening steps** — the causal chain — that connect cause to effect (George & Bennett, 2005; Beach & Pedersen, 2019).

### Four Test Types (Van Evera, 1997)

Stephen Van Evera (1997) distinguished four types of evidential tests:

| Test Type | Definition | Probative Value |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Hoop test** | Evidence that, if absent, disconfirms the hypothesis | Moderate |
| **Smoking gun** | Evidence that, if present, strongly confirms the hypothesis | High |
| **Doubly decisive** | Evidence that simultaneously confirms one hypothesis and disconfirms alternatives | Very high |
| **Straw-in-the-wind** | Evidence that supports the hypothesis but does not exclude alternatives | Low-moderate |

### Applications in IR

Process tracing is widely used in security studies, foreign policy analysis, and international political economy. Examples include:

- **Tracing NATO expansion → Russian threat perception → Ukraine intervention** (McFaul, 2018): The causal chain from Western alliance enlargement to Russian security anxiety to the 2014 and 2022 interventions can be traced through decision-making records, diplomatic cables, and elite statements.
- **Tracing norm diffusion** (Finnemore & Sikkink, 1998): How did the norm against chemical weapons spread? Process tracing the roles of international organisations, state advocates, and domestic norm entrepreneurs.
- **Tracing the causes of war** (Fearon, 1995; Reiter & Stam, 2002): Identifying the precise mechanisms through which miscalculation, commitment problems, or indivisibility produce conflict.

### Beach & Pedersen's (2019) Framework

Beach and Pedersen distinguish three variants:

1. **Theory-testing process tracing** — Testing a hypothesised causal mechanism against evidence.
2. **Theory-building process tracing** — Inductively constructing a causal mechanism from empirical evidence.
3. **Explaining outcome process tracing** — Providing a comprehensive causal account of a specific outcome in a specific case.

## Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA)

### Set-Theoretic Method

QCA, developed by **Charles Ragin** (1987), is a systematic cross-case comparison method that uses **Boolean algebra** and **set theory** to identify the combinations of conditions that produce an outcome. It occupies a middle ground between qualitative case-based analysis and quantitative variable-based analysis.

### Crisp-Set vs Fuzzy-Set QCA

| Variant | Conditions | Outcome | When to Use |
|---------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Crisp-set QCA** (csQCA) | Binary (present/absent) | Binary (present/absent) | Clear dichotomies (democracy/autocracy, war/peace) |
| **Fuzzy-set QCA** (fsQCA) | Degrees of membership (0-1) | Degrees of membership | Continuous or graded concepts (level of democracy, degree of integration) |

### Application in IR

QCA is well-suited for **medium-N studies** (10-50 cases), where the number of cases is too large for in-depth single-case analysis but too small for reliable statistical inference. Examples include:

- Conditions for democratic peace (which combinations of institutional, cultural, and economic factors produce peace?)
- Paths to state failure (what combinations of economic, political, and social factors lead to collapse?)
- Varieties of multilateral intervention (what combinations of mandate, capability, and local context produce success or failure?)

### Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:**
- Systematic comparison without losing case complexity
- Identifies **equifinality** — multiple paths to the same outcome
- Identifies **conjunctural causation** — conditions that matter only in combination

**Limitations:**
- Requires sufficient theoretical knowledge to select conditions
- Sensitive to the researcher's decisions about calibration (fsQCA) and logical minimisation
- Cannot establish temporal sequencing (which is where process tracing supplements QCA)

## Comparative Case Studies

### Most-Similar vs Most-Different Systems Designs

**Arend Lijphart** (1971, 1975) formalised two classic comparative designs:

| Design | Logic | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| **Most-similar systems** | Cases are similar in many respects but differ on the outcome of interest; the one key difference explains the different outcomes | India vs Pakistan (similar colonial origins, different democratic trajectories) |
| **Most-different systems** | Cases are very different but share the outcome of interest; the one common factor explains the shared outcome | Post-WWII Western Europe and post-WWII Japan (very different societies but shared democratic consolidation) |

### Application in IR

Comparative case studies are the backbone of IR research. Examples include:

- **Bretton Woods vs post-Cold War institutions** (Ikenberry, 2001): How did institutional design differ under hegemonic vs multipolar conditions?
- **EU integration vs ASEAN non-integration** (Acharya, 2014): Why did Southeast Asian states resist deeper integration while European states pursued it?
- **Nuclear proliferation cases** (Sagan, 1996): Why did some states pursue nuclear weapons and others not?

## Interviews

### Elite Interviews in IR

Interviews with decision-makers, diplomats, military officers, and policy elites are a cornerstone of IR research. They provide access to **motivations, reasoning, and information** that archival sources may not reveal.

### Structured vs Unstructured

| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Structured** | Pre-determined questions, fixed order | Comparative studies, hypothesis testing |
| **Unstructured** | Open-ended, conversational | Exploratory research, accessing tacit knowledge |
| **Semi-structured** | Guided interview protocol with flexibility | Most common in IR — balances comparability with depth |

### Challenges

- **Gatekeeping**: Access to elites is often restricted; interviewers must navigate political sensitivities.
- **Power dynamics**: The interviewer-interviewee relationship is shaped by institutional power asymmetries.
- **Retrospective rationality**: Elites reconstruct past decisions in light of known outcomes, potentially distorting the actual decision process (Kramer, 2000).
- **Security studies**: Interviews on nuclear policy, intelligence, and military operations face unique confidentiality constraints.

## Ethnography / Fieldwork

### Participant Observation in International Politics

Ethnography — extended immersion in a social setting through participant observation — has gained traction in IR as scholars recognise that **embodied, situated knowledge** cannot be obtained through interviews or archival research alone (Drezin & Abizadeh, 2005; Adler & Pouliot, 2011).

### Applications in IR

- **Conflict zones**: Living in conflict-affected communities to understand how war is experienced and reproduced (Kalyvas, 2006).
- **Embassy cultures**: Understanding diplomatic practice through observation of daily routines, social rituals, and informal interactions.
- **UN hallways**: The "social life" of international negotiations — how norms are debated, compromised, and institutionalised in practice.
- **Military organisations**: Understanding organisational culture, decision-making rituals, and professional identity through embedded observation (Snider, 1995).

## Discourse Analysis

### Language as Constitutive of Political Reality

Discourse analysis examines how **language constitutes political reality** rather than merely reflecting it. In IR, this approach has been most influential in:

### Securitisation Theory (Copenhagen School)

**Barry Buzan, Ole Wæver, and Jaap de Wilde** (1998) argued that security is not an objective condition but a **speech act**. When a securitising actor successfully frames an issue as an existential threat, it moves beyond normal politics into the realm of emergency measures. Securitisation theory is thus applied discourse analysis: it analyses how security is linguistically constructed.

### Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA)

**Norman Fairclough's** (1995) CDA framework examines the relationship between language, power, and ideology. Applied to IR, CDA can analyse:

- How political leaders construct threats through discourse (e.g., the "war on terror" as a discursive construction)
- How media framing shapes foreign policy preferences
- How international organisations produce knowledge through technical language

### Laclau and Mouffe's Discourse Theory

**Ernesto Laclau and Chantal Mouffe** (1985) argued that all social reality is discursively constituted. There is no "outside" to discourse — all political struggle is a struggle over the meaning of concepts like democracy, sovereignty, and security. Their framework has been applied to studies of populism, radical democracy, and post-structuralist IR theory.

## Strengths and Limitations of Qualitative Methods

### Strengths

- **Depth and nuance** — Can capture complexity, context, and meaning that quantitative methods miss
- **Causal mechanisms** — Can identify *how* and *why* something happens, not just *that* it correlates with something else
- **Flexibility** — Can adapt research design as new evidence emerges
- **Ecological validity** — Research is grounded in real-world contexts, not decontextualised variables

### Limitations

- **Generalizability** — Findings from a single case or small number of cases may not apply elsewhere
- **Researcher bias** — Interpretive analysis depends on the researcher's theoretical commitments and positionality
- **Replicability** — Qualitative research is difficult to replicate exactly
- **Selection bias** — Cases may be selected on the dependent variable, biasing causal inference
