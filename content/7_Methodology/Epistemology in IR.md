# Epistemology in IR

## Definition

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, and limits of knowledge. In the context of International Relations, epistemology addresses a foundational question: **how do we know what we know about international politics?** Every research design presupposes an epistemological stance — whether articulated or not. The choice between statistical analysis of alliance patterns and interpretive reading of diplomatic archives is not merely technical; it reflects deep commitments about what counts as knowledge, what kinds of claims are legitimate, and how evidence relates to theory (Gavrilița, 2021; Jackson, 2011).

The epistemological landscape of IR has been shaped by the discipline's peculiar position between the natural sciences and the humanities. As a social science that studies power, war, cooperation, and identity, IR occupies contested terrain where the methods of physics sit uneasily alongside the hermeneutics of meaning-making. The "Third Debate" in IR theory — between positivists, post-positivists, and critical theorists — is fundamentally an epistemological debate about the grounds of valid knowledge (Lapid, 1989).

## Positivism

### The Logical Positivist Tradition

Logical positivism holds that genuine knowledge is derived from **empirical observation** and **logical analysis**, and that the natural sciences provide the model for all legitimate inquiry. The Vienna Circle (Carnap, Schlick, Neurath) argued that only statements verifiable through observation or logic are meaningful — metaphysical and normative claims are cognitively empty (Ayer, 1936).

**Auguste Comte** established the positivist programme by arguing that human thought passes through theological, metaphysical, and finally positive (scientific) stages. Social phenomena, like natural phenomena, are governed by discoverable laws. **Karl Popper** refined this tradition by replacing verification with **falsification** as the criterion of scientific knowledge: a theory is scientific only if it specifies conditions under which it could be refuted (Popper, 1959).

### Positivism in IR

In International Relations, positivism has been the dominant epistemological orientation, particularly in American IR. Key applications include:

- **Kenneth Waltz's** *Theory of International Politics* (1979) — a systematic, law-like theory of how international system structure constrains state behaviour. Waltz explicitly modeled his approach on the natural sciences, seeking parsimonious, generalisable causal laws.
- **Quantitative security studies** — Large-N statistical analysis of conflict, alliance formation, and militarised disputes. The Correlates of War project, UCDP/PRIO datasets, and MID data all operate within a positivist framework.
- **Rational choice theory** — Game-theoretic models of deterrence, bargaining, and war assume observable, measurable utility functions and strategic interaction.

### Strengths

- **Rigour and replicability** — Hypotheses can be tested against evidence using standardised procedures.
- **Cumulative knowledge** — Science advances by building on and revising previous findings.
- **Value-free social science** — Separating facts from values protects against political bias.

### Critiques

- Positivism **cannot capture meanings, norms, and identities** that do not reduce to observable behaviour.
- Many of the most important phenomena in IR — anarchy, sovereignty, national interest — are **constituted by ideas**, not independently existing material facts (Wendt, 1999).
- The assumption of a **value-free science** is itself a value judgment (Cox, 1981).
- The natural science model may be inappropriate for social reality, which is **reflexive and self-interpreting** (Onuf, 1989).

## Post-Positivism / Interpretivism

### Understanding (Verstehen) over Explanation

Post-positivism rejects the idea that the natural sciences provide the model for social inquiry. Instead of seeking causal laws and prediction, interpretivists argue that social science should aim at **understanding** (*Verstehen*) — grasping the meanings that actors attach to their actions (Weber, 1922).

**Clifford Geertz** (1973) distinguished between "thin description" (reporting observable facts) and **thick description** (interpreting the layers of meaning in social action). A wink is not the same as a twitch — even though both are observable muscle contractions — because meaning constitutes the difference.

**Phenomenology** (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty) insists that we study the "lifeworld" (*Lebenswelt*) — the pre-theoretical, taken-for-granted background of meaning within which actors operate. Applied to IR, this means asking how diplomats, soldiers, and citizens *experience* and *make sense of* international politics, not merely what they do.

### Interpretivism in IR

- **Alexander Wendt's** constructivism — "Anarchy is what states make of it" (1992) is an interpretivist claim: the meaning of the international system is socially constructed, not given by material facts.
- **The English School** (Bull, 1977; Wight, 1977) — International relations constitute an "international society" with shared norms, rules, and institutions. Understanding this society requires interpretive analysis, not just statistical correlation.
- **Robert Cox's** critical theory — "Theory is always for someone and for some purpose" (1981). Theory cannot be separated from the interests it serves; critical theory aims at emancipation, not just prediction.

### Strengths

- **Captures meaning-making** — Can analyse how states construct identities, interpret threats, and justify actions.
- **Sensitivity to context** — Recognises that the same behaviour may have different meanings in different settings.
- **Reflexivity** — Acknowledges that the researcher's own position shapes knowledge production.

### Critiques

- **Relativism** — If all knowledge is perspective-dependent, how do we adjudicate between competing interpretations?
- **Limited generalizability** — Thick description of one case does not easily yield claims about broader patterns.
- **Hard to replicate** — Interpretive research depends on the researcher's judgment, making it difficult to standardise.

## Critical Realism

### Bhaskar's Stratified Ontology

Critical realism, developed by **Roy Bhaskar** (1975, 1979), occupies a middle ground between positivism and interpretivism. Bhaskar argued that:

1. **Reality is stratified** — There are three domains: the *real* (structures and mechanisms, whether or not we observe them), the *actual* (events that occur), and the *empirical* (events we directly observe).
2. **Causal mechanisms exist but are often unobservable** — We can study the mechanisms that generate observable events, even if we cannot directly see them.
3. **Knowledge is fallible** — Our theories are always approximations of reality, never perfect descriptions.

### Critical Realism in IR

Critical realism has been applied by structural Marxists (who analyse the underlying capitalist structures of the world system) and by some constructivists (who argue that norms and identities are real structures that causally shape behaviour). It bridges [[Epistemology in IR#Positivism|positivism]] and [[Epistemology in IR#Post-Positivism / Interpretivism|interpretivism]] by accepting:

- **An independent reality** (against strong social constructionism)
- **Fallibilism and theory-ladenness of observation** (against naive positivism)
- **The importance of mechanisms** — not just observable correlations, but the processes that produce them

## Constructivism

### Social Reality as Intersubjective Meaning

Constructivism is both an **ontological** and an **epistemological** position. Ontologically, it holds that much of social reality — states, anarchy, sovereignty, national interest — is **constructed through intersubjective meaning**. Epistemologically, it argues that we can study these constructions **systematically**, using methods appropriate to social meaning (Adler, 1997).

Wendt's (1999) dictum that "anarchy is what states make of it" is the paradigmatic constructivist claim: the self-help structure of the international system is not a brute material fact but a social structure constituted by shared ideas about self and other. Three states in anarchy can have radically different relationships (Hobbesian enemies, Lockean rivals, Kantian friends) depending on their shared understandings.

Constructivism thus occupies a distinctive epistemological position: it rejects the positivist claim that only observable, material facts are real, but it also rejects the strong post-positivist claim that we cannot systematically study social reality. It insists that **intersubjective meanings are real** and can be investigated through rigorous research (Adler, 1997; Hopf, 2018).

## Postcolonial/Decolonial Epistemology

### Challenging Eurocentric Knowledge Production

Postcolonial and decolonial epistemologies challenge the assumption that Western categories, theories, and methods provide universal frameworks for understanding international politics. **Walter Mignolo** (2007, 2011) calls for **"epistemic delinking"** — decolonising knowledge by recognising that Western epistemology is itself a local, historically specific tradition, not a universal standard.

**Sandra Harding's** (1993) standpoint epistemology argues that marginalised groups have **epistemic privilege** — their social position gives them distinctive insights into how power operates. Applied to IR, this means that the experiences of the Global South, indigenous communities, and colonised peoples are not merely "cases" to be studied by Western scholars but constitute **alternative knowledge systems** that can correct the blind spots of mainstream IR theory (Acharya, 2014; Shilliam, 2010).

### Why Western Theories Fail in the Global South

Mainstream IR theories — [[Realism]], [[Liberalism]], [[Constructivism]] — were developed primarily by American and European scholars studying the Western state system. Their concepts (sovereignty, anarchy, balance of power) may not map neatly onto non-Western experiences:

- The **Westphalian state** is a European historical product; many post-colonial states were constructed by colonial borders, not organic political communities.
- **Anarchy** may be an inadequate description of international relations in regions with different political cultures and traditions (Acharya, 2011).
- The distinction between "domestic" and "international" may be far more blurred in regions where colonial and post-colonial structures cross-cut state boundaries (Chakrabarty, 2000).

## Pragmatism

### Knowledge for Action

Pragmatism, developed by **John Dewey**, **William James**, and later **Richard Rorty**, rejects the correspondence theory of truth (that knowledge "mirrors" reality) and instead defines truth as **what works** — what enables us to navigate the world effectively. Knowledge is not a picture of the world but a tool for action (Dewey, 1938; Rorty, 1979).

In IR, pragmatism has been influential in **policy-relevant research** — the tradition of scholars who seek to bridge theory and practice. The "rational policy analysis" tradition, security studies as a problem-solving enterprise, and the movement toward evidence-based policy all reflect pragmatic epistemology.

Pragmatism also offers a way to **bridge the epistemological divide** in IR: rather than arguing about whether positivism or interpretivism is "correct," pragmatists ask which approach best addresses the question at hand.
