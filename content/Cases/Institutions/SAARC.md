# South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation

## Overview

The South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC) was founded on 8 December 1985 in Dhaka by Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, the Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka. Afghanistan joined as the eighth member in 2007. SAARC encompasses 1.9 billion people—nearly a quarter of the world's population—making it the most populous regional organisation in the world. Yet by nearly every measure of institutional effectiveness, it is the weakest of the major regional bodies: a textbook case of how geopolitics can paralyse regionalism.

## Key Themes

- **SAARC's Mandate**: The SAARC Charter commits members to "promote the welfare of the peoples of South Asia," accelerate economic growth, and strengthen collective self-reliance. It operates on unanimity (all decisions require consensus), excludes bilateral and contentious issues from discussion, and strictly adheres to non-interference—a clause inserted at Pakistan's insistence to prevent India from dominating the agenda.
- **Contrast with ASEAN and the EU**: SAARC is often compared unfavourably with ASEAN (similar founding date, also consensus-based) and the EU. Unlike ASEAN, SAARC lacks a meaningful norm of consultation and has no equivalent of the ASEAN Way's high-level socialisation. Unlike the EU, it has no supranational institutions, no pooled sovereignty, and no binding dispute-resolution mechanism. It remains a purely intergovernmental forum.
- **SAARC Summits as Barometers**: Summits are a proxy for South Asian diplomatic health. The 18th SAARC Summit (Kathmandu 2014) was the last successfully held; subsequent summits were cancelled after India boycotted the 2016 Islamabad summit following the Uri attack, which India blamed on Pakistan-based militants. The organisation has been effectively dormant since.

## Challenges

The central challenge is the **India-Pakistan rivalry**. Every SAARC initiative—SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area), SAARC Satellite, regional energy grid, motor vehicles agreement—has been slowed or blocked by bilateral mistrust. Pakistan blocks transit connectivity to Afghanistan through Indian support; India blocks SAARC-level counter-terrorism cooperation that might legitimise Pakistan-backed non-state actors. **India's growing preference for minilateralism** (BBIN—Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Nepal; BIMSTEC—Bay of Bengal grouping) reflects a strategic calculation that SAARC is institutionally captured by Pakistan's veto. Smaller SAARC states (Nepal, Bangladesh, Maldives, Sri Lanka) are caught in a structural dilemma: they need regional connectivity but cannot afford to alienate either India or China and increasingly look to **Chinese Belt and Road projects** as an alternative to SAARC-led infrastructure. The rise of China as a South Asian creditor and investor has further marginalised SAARC, as bilateral deals bypass the organisation entirely.

## Key Questions

- Is SAARC a failed institution, or merely dormant?
- Can South Asian regionalism exist without India-Pakistan rapprochement?
- Will BIMSTEC and BBIN replace SAARC as the region's functional organisations?
- Does China's presence in South Asia (through BRI) render SAARC irrelevant?
