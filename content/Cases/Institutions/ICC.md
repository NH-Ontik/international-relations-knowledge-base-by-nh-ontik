# International Criminal Court

## Overview

The International Criminal Court (ICC), established by the Rome Statute in 1998 and operational from 1 July 2002, is the first permanent international tribunal with jurisdiction over the world's most serious crimes: genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and (since 2017) the crime of aggression. It sits in The Hague and currently has 123 member states, representing a landmark achievement in the post-Cold War project of international justice. The ICC is neither a UN body nor a world court—it is a treaty-based, independent institution built on the principle of **complementarity**.

## Key Themes

- **Complementarity Principle**: The ICC only exercises jurisdiction when national courts are unwilling or unable genuinely to investigate or prosecute. This is intended to respect state sovereignty while creating a backstop against impunity. In practice, complementarity generates complex debates about what constitutes "unwillingness"—particularly when states conduct sham trials.
- **Jurisdictional Architecture**: The ICC has jurisdiction over crimes committed on the territory of a state party or by a national of a state party. The UN Security Council can also refer situations in non-party states (Darfur 2005, Libya 2011), and states may accept jurisdiction on an *ad hoc* basis. The crime of aggression requires a Security Council determination or a state-party opt-in—essentially immunising the P5.
- **Prosecutorial Record**: The ICC has opened investigations in 17 situations (mostly African), issued over 40 arrest warrants, and secured 10 convictions—a modest record given two decades of operation. Landmark cases include *Prosecutor v. Lubanga* (first conviction, child soldiers) and *Prosecutor v. Ongwen* (LRA commander convicted of 61 counts).

## Challenges

The ICC faces a **legitimacy crisis** driven by three interlocking issues. First, **great-power non-membership**: the US, Russia, China, and India are not parties, drastically limiting the Court's reach. The US has actively opposed the ICC (passing the American Service-Members' Protection Act—the "Hague Invasion Act") and sanctioned ICC officials over the Afghanistan and Palestine investigations. Second, the **African bias critique**: ten of the Court's first eleven situations were in Africa, fuelling charges that the ICC is a neo-colonial instrument targeting weak African states while ignoring Western crimes. The withdrawal threats by Burundi, the Philippines, and South Africa (later reversed) reflect this backlash. Third, **enforcement**: the ICC has no police force; arrest warrants depend on state cooperation. Sudan's Bashir travelled internationally for years despite an outstanding warrant, and Russia's Putin (warrant issued March 2023) remains un-arrestable without Security Council consensus.

## Key Questions

- Can the ICC achieve universal jurisdiction without great-power membership?
- Is the ICC selectively prosecuting the Global South?
- Does international criminal justice deter atrocity, or only punish the defeated?
- Can the ICC survive US and Russian active opposition?
