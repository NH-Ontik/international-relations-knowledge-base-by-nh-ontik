# European Union

## Overview

The European Union (EU) is the world's most advanced experiment in supranational governance, evolving from a six-member coal-and-steel community in 1951 to a 27-member political and economic union. Its trajectory—from the European Coal and Steel Community (ECSC, 1951) through the European Economic Community (EEC, 1957) to the Maastricht Treaty (1992) establishing the EU—represents the central case study in regional integration theory.

The EU's institutional architecture comprises four principal bodies: the **European Commission** (supranational executive, proposes and enforces law), the **Council of the European Union** (intergovernmental, member-state ministers), the **European Parliament** (directly elected, co-legislator), and the **Court of Justice of the European Union** (supreme arbiter of EU law). This quadripartite structure reflects the permanent tension between supranationalism—where authority is pooled and delegated to common institutions—and intergovernmentalism—where member states retain veto power and control over high politics.

## Key Themes

- **Supranationalism vs Intergovernmentalism**: The core axis of EU politics. Supranational dynamics dominate regulation, trade, and competition policy; intergovernmentalism prevails in foreign policy, defence, and taxation.
- **Integration Theories**: *Functionalism* (Mitrany) argued technical cooperation would spill into political integration; *Neo-functionalism* (Haas) added the concept of "spillover" and supranational entrepreneurship; *Intergovernmentalism* (Hoffmann, Moravcsik) insists integration advances only when member-state preferences converge.
- **Democratic Deficit**: Critics argue EU decision-making lacks accountability, transparency, and electoral connection compared to national parliaments.
- **Security Community**: Karl Deutsch's concept of a "pluralistic security community"—where war becomes unthinkable—fits the EU as its paradigmatic case.

## Challenges

The EU has faced a succession of existential crises. The **Eurozone Crisis** (2009–2015) exposed the fragility of a monetary union without fiscal union, forcing austerity on peripheral states. The **Migration Crisis** (2015–2016) revealed deep North-South and East-West divisions over burden-sharing and border control. **Brexit** (2016–2020) marked the first member-state withdrawal, validating intergovernmentalist predictions that integration has limits when core sovereignty is challenged. The persistent **Democratic Deficit**—low turnout, technocratic decision-making, and the growing power of the European Council—undermines legitimacy.

## Key Questions

- Can the EU survive as a federalising project without a demos?
- Does neo-functionalism explain integration through crises, or does intergovernmentalism better account for its limits?
- Is the EU a post-Westphalian polity or an advanced intergovernmental regime?
- Can strategic autonomy in defence and foreign policy be achieved without replicating great-power politics?
