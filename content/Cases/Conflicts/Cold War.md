# Cold War (1947–1991)

## Overview
Bipolar ideological and geopolitical rivalry between the United States (liberal-democratic capitalist bloc) and the Soviet Union (communist authoritarian bloc), shaping the entire structure of international relations during the second half of the 20th century.

## Key Themes
- Bipolarity vs unipolarity (post-1991)
- Nuclear deterrence and arms race
- Proxy wars in the Third World
- Ideological competition (capitalism vs communism)
- Containment policy
- Decolonisation and superpower competition

## Related Theories
- [[Realism]] — structural explanation of bipolar stability, security dilemma
- [[Liberalism]] — end of Cold War as triumph of liberal ideas (Fukuyama)
- [[Constructivism]] — Gorbachev's "New Thinking" as identity shift (Koslowski & Kratochwil)
- [[Marxism]] — Cold War as capitalist-imperialist project
- [[Strategic Studies]] — nuclear strategy, deterrence theory, proxy warfare
- [[Deterrence]] — MAD framework emerged from US-USSR rivalry

## Key Events / Turning Points
- Berlin Blockade (1948–49)
- Korean War (1950–53)
- Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)
- Vietnam War (1955–75)
- Détente (1970s)
- Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979)
- Fall of Berlin Wall (1989)
- Collapse of USSR (1991)

## Key Case Studies
- Postcolonialism uses Cold War as context for Berlin Conference/Sykes-Picot critique
- Liberalism uses Cold War to argue institutional cooperation succeeded
- Realism uses Cold War to argue bipolarity produced stability
- Marxism uses Cold War to highlight Global South exploitation
- Feminism: Carol Cohn's analysis of Cold War nuclear discourse

## Key Questions
1. Why did the Cold War end peacefully?
2. Was bipolarity more stable than multipolarity?
3. Did the Cold War benefit the Global South?
4. How should we periodise the Cold War?
