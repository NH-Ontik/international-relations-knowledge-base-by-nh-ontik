# Belt and Road Initiative

## Overview
The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) is China's global infrastructure and investment strategy, announced by President Xi Jinping in 2013. It aims to connect Asia, Europe, Africa (and increasingly the Middle East and Latin America) through a network of roads, railways, ports, pipelines, and communications infrastructure. The BRI is the largest infrastructure project in history, involving over 150 countries and international organisations.

## Structure
The BRI resurrects the imagery of the ancient Silk Road in two components:
- **Silk Road Economic Belt** — Overland corridors through Central Asia, the Middle East, Russia, and Europe (railways, highways, pipelines)
- **Maritime Silk Road** — Port and shipping networks across the South China Sea, Indian Ocean, East Africa, Persian Gulf, and Mediterranean

## Core Principles (Beijing)
China's official framing: win-win cooperation, mutual respect, non-interference, and "shared future for mankind." Emphasises:
- Infrastructure investment (China's competitive advantage — it builds fast and cheap)
- Policy coordination, trade facilitation, and financial integration
- People-to-people ties

## Alternative Development Model
The BRI positions itself as an alternative to the [[Bretton Woods Institutions]] approach to development:
- No political conditionalities (unlike the IMF and [[World Bank]])
- No requirements for governance reform, human rights standards, or environmental impact assessments before infrastructure financing
- State-owned enterprises lead implementation, not private sector actors
- Financing comes from China's policy banks (CDB, Exim Bank) rather than Western-dominated international financial institutions

This aligns with China's consistent foreign policy principle of non-interference in domestic affairs ([[Sovereignty]]).

## Debt-Trap Diplomacy Debate
A major controversy centres on whether the BRI deliberately creates debtor-client relationships:
- **Critics argue**: China lends to strategically important but credit-unviable countries (Sri Lanka's Hambantota port, Pakistan's Gwadar). When the country cannot repay, China takes ownership of strategically valuable assets (Hambantota — 99-year lease to China Merchants Port). This is "debt-trap diplomacy."
- **Defenders argue**: Every infrastructure project has repayment risk; China has restructured debts for many countries; the 99-year lease was a voluntary agreement; the BRI has produced real economic benefits (the Peshawar-Karachi motorway, Ethiopian-Djibouti railway)
- **Academic consensus**: Debt-trap is overclaimed as a framework — there are only a few clear examples, but Chinese lending does systematically increase leverage over borrower states

## Key Debates
- **Transparency** — BRI contracts are often confidential; debt terms are undisclosed
- **Environmental impact** — Dam projects, coal plants, and forest-clearing linked to BRI
- **Labour standards** — Chinese workers imported rather than local workers hired
- **Geopolitics** — Is the BRI an economic development program, or a geostrategic project rivaling the US?
