# Global Governance

## Definition
The sum of formal and informal rules, norms, institutions, and practices through which collective action is taken at the global level. Coined by **James Rosenau** as "governance without government" — governance exists in the absence of world government through overlapping authorities, issue-specific regimes, and networks of actors.

## Key Scholars
- **James Rosenau** — *Turbulence in World Politics* (1990); "governance without government" as the defining feature of globalised politics
- **Thomas Weiss** — UN reform, humanitarian intervention, global governance as practical problem-solving
- **David Held** — Cosmopolitan democracy, global social democracy
- **Anne-Marie Slaughter** — Networked governance: government networks (judges, regulators) as a new model

## Actors in Global Governance
| Actor Type | Examples | Role |
|------------|----------|------|
| States | US, China, India, Germany | Primary rule-makers, sovereignty holders |
| International Organisations | [[United Nations]], [[Bretton Woods Institutions]] | Convening, norm-setting, implementation |
| NGOs | Amnesty International, MSF, Oxfam | Advocacy, service delivery, monitoring |
| MNCs | Apple, Shell, Google | Economic governance, private regulation |
| Civil Society | Social movements, epistemic communities | Agenda-setting, accountability pressure |
| Private authorities | ISO, ICANN, rating agencies | Standard-setting, certification |

## Issue Areas
- **Trade** — WTO rules, regional agreements ([[Bretton Woods Institutions]])
- **Climate** — UNFCCC, Paris Agreement, IPCC
- **Health** — WHO, COVAX, pandemic response
- **Security** — UN Security Council, peacekeeping, arms control
- **Human Rights** — UN Human Rights Council, ICC, treaty bodies
- **Finance** — IMF, FSB, Basel Committee
- **Digital** — ICANN, IGF, GDPR as norm export

## Key Debates
- **Effectiveness** — Does global governance actually solve problems? (Climate negotiations stalled for decades; pandemic response uneven)
- **Legitimacy and accountability** — Who elected the WTO? Democratic deficit of global bodies
- **Enforcement** — Most global governance lacks coercive power; relies on compliance pull
- **Fragmentation** — Overlapping regimes, competing institutions (e.g., climate vs trade)
- **Power asymmetries** — Global governance reflects power (P5 veto, IMF weighted voting)
- **North-South divide** — Developing countries argue governance serves Western interests

## Key Questions
- Is global governance replacing the state or supplementing it?
- Can global governance address existential threats (climate, AI, pandemics) without world government?
- Who governs the governors?
