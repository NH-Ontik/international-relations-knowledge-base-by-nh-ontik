# Responsibility to Protect

## Definition
A global political commitment adopted by all UN member states at the 2005 World Summit to prevent genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity. R2P reframes sovereignty from *control* over people to *responsibility* for people.

## Origins
- Direct response to the failures of the 1990s: [[Rwanda Genocide|Rwanda]] (1994) and [[Kosovo War|Kosovo]] (1999) — one where the international community did nothing, one where it acted without legal authorisation
- **ICISS Report** (2001) — "The Responsibility to Protect" commissioned by the Canadian government; introduced the idea of sovereignty as responsibility
- **2005 World Summit Outcome Document** (UNGA Resolution 60/1, paras 138--140) — Endorsed by consensus, though in a narrower form than ICISS proposed
- **UN Secretary-General reports** (2009, 2010, etc.) — elaborated the "three pillars"

## The Three Pillars

### Pillar I: The protection responsibilities of the state
- Every state has the primary responsibility to protect its populations from the four crimes
- Sovereignty entails responsibility ([[Sovereignty]] redefined)

### Pillar II: International assistance and capacity-building
- The international community should help states meet their R2P obligations
- Includes early warning, mediation, capacity-building, peacekeeping

### Pillar III: Timely and decisive response
- If a state manifestly fails to protect its population, the international community has a responsibility to respond collectively
- Peaceful measures first (Chapter VI); if insufficient, coercive measures (Chapter VII) including military force — but only with UNSC authorisation
- **The most controversial pillar** — can R2P authorise military intervention without state consent?

## The Four Crimes
R2P applies exclusively to: genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, crimes against humanity. Originally intended to be narrow to build consensus, but critics say the scope is still dangerously elastic.

## Key Debates

### R2P as Progress
- [[Liberalism]] — R2P represents normative progress: human rights constraining sovereignty
- Shifts the debate from "right to intervene" (choosing whether to act) to "responsibility to protect" (obligation to act)
- Created institutional infrastructure: UN Joint Office, Special Adviser on R2P, annual UNGA dialogues

### R2P as Neo-imperialism
- [[Postcolonialism]] — R2P is "the white man's burden" rebranded; powerful states determine who needs protecting
- [[Marxism]] — R2P legitimises military intervention by Western powers to maintain capitalist world order
- Selectivity: Libya (2011) intervened, Syria (2011--) not — pattern follows geopolitical interests, not human suffering

## Case Studies

### Libya (2011) — The turning point
- UNSC Resolution 1973 authorised "all necessary measures" to protect civilians in Benghazi
- NATO air campaign rapidly expanded to regime change (bombing Gaddafi's forces, supporting rebels)
- Aftermath: state collapse, civil war, proxy conflict, migrant crisis, slave markets
- China and Russia concluded R2P was a cover for regime change
- Syria: no consensus ever reached; UNSC deadlocked (Russia, China veto)

### Syria (2011--present)
- R2P clearly applicable: crimes against humanity, war crimes, chemical weapons
- UNSC vetoes (Russia × 12+, China × 8+) blocked all enforcement
- The failure of Pillar III: when the Security Council is paralysed, R2P offers no mechanism to act
- Unilateral action (US/UK/France airstrikes 2018 on chemical weapons) — criticised as outside UN framework

### Rwanda (1994) — The thought experiment
- Had R2P existed, the international community would have had a framework — but would powerful states have acted differently?
- R2P addresses the *legitimacy* gap, not the *will* gap — a recurring critique

## Critiques
- **Three pillars, one problem** — Pillar III remains unenforceable when permanent members disagree
- **R2P as rhetorical** — No change in state behaviour; states intervene when they want and label it R2P ([[Realism]])
- **Narrow scope** — Excludes climate change, pandemics, structural violence, poverty
- **North-South divide** — Majority of Global South states remain suspicious; R2P is seen as a Western tool
- **Threshold problem** — What counts as "manifestly failing"? Who decides?

## Key Questions
- Does R2P constrain or enable great power intervention?
- Is R2P dead after Libya and Syria, or is it a norm in progress?
- Can R2P survive a Security Council that cannot agree?
