# Middle Powers

Middle powers are states that are neither great powers nor small states, possessing moderate material capabilities but disproportionate influence through diplomatic skill, niche specialisation, and institutional leadership.

## Defining Middle Power

The concept resists a single definition. Material approaches rank states by GDP, military spending, and population — usually the 10th to 30th ranked states qualify. Behavioural approaches emphasise **niche diplomacy**: middle powers concentrate resources in specific domains where they can lead (e.g. Canada on peacekeeping, Norway on mediation, Australia on trade). Constructivist approaches highlight **middle power identity** and status-seeking.

## Key Examples

**Canada** — pioneered peacekeeping under Lester B. Pearson; active in UN reform and human security. **Australia** — middle-power diplomacy in the Indo-Pacific, balancing US alliance with Asian engagement. **Norway and Sweden** — niche diplomacy in mediation, development, and disarmament. **South Korea** — bridging developed and developing worlds through middle-power activism. **Turkey** — assertively projecting middle-power status across multiple regions.

## Modes of Diplomacy

Middle powers typically pursue **multilateralism** and work through [[International Organizations]] such as the UN, G20, and regional bodies. They act as bridge-builders, coalition-formers, and norm entrepreneurs — states that cannot compel outcomes through force shape them through persuasion and institutional rules.

## Middle Powers and Bangladesh

[[Bangladesh Foreign Policy]] increasingly reflects middle-power aspirations. Bangladesh has carved niches in peacekeeping (one of the largest troop contributors), climate diplomacy, and the Rohingya humanitarian response. Scholars in [[Comparative Foreign Policy]] and [[Foreign Policy Analysis]] debate how and when middle powers can translate niche influence into structural power.

## Critiques

Critics argue "middle power" is a self-serving label deployed by states that lack coercive power but want status. The category may also be a product of Western, liberal internationalist thinking that downplays the role of geopolitics and coercion.
