# Soft Power

## Definition
The ability to get what you want through attraction rather than coercion or payment. A state's soft power rests on three resources: its **culture** (where it is attractive to others), its **political values** (when it lives up to them at home and abroad), and its **foreign policies** (when they are seen as legitimate and moral).

## Origins
- **Joseph Nye** — Coined the term in *Bound to Lead* (1990) and developed in *Soft Power* (2004)
- Nye was responding to the "declinist" debate: the US was not in absolute decline because its soft power (Hollywood, universities, democratic ideals, multilateral alliances) would sustain its influence
- Contrast with [[Hegemony|hard power]]: military might, economic sanctions, coercion

## Three Sources (Nye)

### 1. Culture
- High culture (literature, art, education) and popular culture (film, music, television, sport)
- US: Hollywood, Silicon Valley, jazz, universities — "American way of life" as magnetic
- Contemporary competitors: South Korea (K-pop, K-dramas), Japan (anime, gaming), China (Confucius Institutes, Belt and Road narrative, but weaker cultural pull)

### 2. Political Values
- Democracy, human rights, rule of law — where states genuinely embody them
- The appeal of liberal democracy to populations under authoritarian rule
- Erodes when values are violated (Guantánamo, Abu Ghraib, democratic backsliding under Trump)

### 3. Foreign Policies
- Multilateralism, alliance-building, foreign aid, diplomacy
- Policies perceived as legitimate and inclusive generate soft power
- Unilateralism, pre-emptive war, disregard for international law erode it ([[Iraq War]] 2003 significantly damaged US soft power)

## Smart Power
- Nye later argued for "smart power" — combining hard and soft power resources into an integrated strategy
- Neither pure coercion nor pure attraction is sufficient alone
- [[Liberalism]] provides the theoretical home for smart power: institutions + diplomacy + force where necessary

## Critiques

### Conceptual / Measurement
- **Vague and hard to measure** — How do you quantify "attraction"? Opinion polls? Box office receipts? Student visa numbers?
- **Soft power = influence?** — Not necessarily: attraction does not automatically translate into policy outcomes
- **Correlation vs causation** — Do countries follow the US because they admire it, or because they have no choice?

### Realist Critique
- [[Realism]] — Soft power is irrelevant when interests conflict; hard power decides
- China builds Confucius Institutes AND aircraft carriers — the carrier matters more
- "Nice" does not stop aggression; Putin was not deterred by US soft power

### Postcolonial / Asian Values Critique
- [[Postcolonialism]] — "Soft power" is just cultural imperialism rebranded; the West decides what counts as attractive
- **Asian values debate** — Soft power assumes the universal appeal of liberal democracy; authoritarian states may have their own model that competes on different terms (China's "sharp power": co-optation, censorship, influence operations rather than attraction)

### Constructivist Insight
- [[Constructivism]] — Soft power is about legitimacy, which is intersubjective: it depends on shared understandings that can change over time
- A state's identity and the legitimacy of its actions are socially constructed, not inherent

## Case Studies
- **US (1945--present)** — Unmatched soft power: Marshall Plan, Hollywood, academic dominance, democratic brand. Damaged by [[Iraq War]], Guantánamo, surveillance disclosures (Snowden)
- **China** — Growing investment in soft power (Belt and Road, Confucius Institutes, media expansion — CGTN, Xinhua), but limited by authoritarian values, censorship (Uighur repression), and "wolf warrior" diplomacy
- **EU** — "Normative power Europe": environmental standards, human rights diplomacy, development aid — soft power without significant hard power

## Key Questions
- Can authoritarian states generate genuine soft power, or is it inseparable from liberal democracy?
- Is soft power declining as a feature of international politics, or are its sources shifting (from states to corporations, social movements)?
- Does soft power matter less in an era of great power competition?
