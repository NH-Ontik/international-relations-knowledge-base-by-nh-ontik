# Hegemony

## Definition
Leadership or dominance of one state (the hegemon) over others in the international system. Hegemony can be **coercive** (based on military/economic force) or **consensual** (Gramsci: leadership by intellectual and moral authority, where subordinate groups accept the hegemon's worldview).

## Two Traditions

### Realist / Material Hegemony
- A single state possesses such overwhelming material preponderance that it can dominate the system
- Measured in: military spending, economic production, technological primacy
- **Hegemonic Stability Theory** (Kindleberger, Gilpin): A hegemon is necessary for stable international economic order (US after 1945, UK in 19th century)
- Crisis when hegemon declines → system becomes unstable
- **Mearsheimer**: Great powers seek regional hegemony (US in Americas, China in Asia)

### Gramscian / Ideational Hegemony (from [[Marxism]])
- Hegemony = dominance by a combination of coercion AND consent
- The dominant group's worldview becomes "common sense" — institutions, education, media, international organisations all normalise the hegemon's order
- Transnational capitalist class creates global hegemony
- **Cox**: "Hegemony is a structure of values and understandings about the nature of international order that permeates the system"

## Hegemonic Stability Theory
- A hegemon provides public goods: free trade, stable currency, security
- UK provided in 19th century (Pax; Britain); US provided post-1945 (Bretton Woods)
- **Decline** → instability, great power competition
- **Critique**: The theory is circular — hegemons create order; order attributed to hegemons

## Case Studies
- **US Hegemony (1945—present)** — Both material (45% of global GDP post-WWII) and ideational (democracy/human rights/market norms)
- **Pax Britannica (1815-1914)** — British naval dominance + global trade network
- **China** — Rising but not yet hegemonic; contesting US leadership in some domains
- **Soviet** — Had military hegemony over Eastern bloc but never achieved economic/ideational global hegemony

## Key indicators
- Largest economy, technology frontier
- Leading military, global force-projection
- Dominant cultural / ideological influence
- Controls international institutions (its rules, its design)

## Exam Questions
- "Is US hegemony really in decline?"
- "Can China become a hegemon without a liberal ideology?"
- "Does hegemony benefit the whole system or just the hegemon?"
