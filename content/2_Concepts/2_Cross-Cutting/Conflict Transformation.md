# Conflict Transformation

## Definition
Conflict transformation is the process of fundamentally changing the relationships, structures, and communications that sustain violent conflict. Developed most prominently by John Paul Lederach, it differs from conflict **resolution** (ending the immediate problem) and conflict **management** (containing the problem) by treating conflict as a **catalyst** — an opportunity for positive, long-term social change.

## Beyond Resolution and Management
| Approach | Goal | Timeframe | Key Question |
|----------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Conflict management | Contain, de-escalate | Short-term | How to stop the violence? |
| Conflict resolution | Solve the issue | Medium-term | What is the deal? |
| Conflict transformation | Transform relationships | Long-term (years) | How to rebuild society? |

Lederach argues that resolution often reproduces the same structures that produced conflict. Transformation asks harder questions: What relationships and patterns made this conflict possible? How can those relationships be rebuilt?

## Lederach's Four Dimensions
1. **Personal** — Changes in individual attitudes, emotions, and beliefs. Trauma healing, empathy, personal responsibility.
2. **Relational** — Changes in communication, interaction, and trust between the conflicting parties. Dialogue, reconciliation, forgiveness.
3. **Structural** — Changes in the systems, institutions, and political/economic arrangements that sustain conflict. Justice reform, power redistribution, institutional redesign.
4. **Cultural** — Changes in the cultural patterns (stories, symbols, rituals) that support violence. Cultural peace-building, public memory.

## Middle-Range Actors
A key strategic insight: top-level leaders (presidents, generals) are often locked into their positions and public postures. Grassroots actors (local communities) lack leverage. The **middle-range actors** — academics, religious leaders, NGO heads, business leaders, traditional authorities — are best positioned to lead transformation because they:
- Have access to both top and grassroots
- Are less publicly committed to hardline positions
- Can generate creative alternatives without losing legitimacy

## Related Approaches
- **[[Gandhi Satyagraha]]** — Nonviolent resistance as a transformational force that seeks to convert, not defeat, the opponent
- **Restorative justice** — Reconciliation rather than punishment of offenders (see [[Justice]])
- **Truth commissions** — South African TRC, Rwanda gacaca — truth-telling as transformation

## Case Studies
- **South Africa** (1994–99) — The TRC process aimed at transformation rather than retributive justice
- **Northern Ireland** (1998–present) — The Good Friday Agreement as managed resolution; ongoing transformation work in communities
- **Rwanda** — Post-genocide reconciliation through community courts (gacaca) and intergroup dialogue
