# Climate Change

## Overview

Climate change represents one of the most pressing global challenges of the 21st century, with far-reaching implications for international relations. Rising global temperatures, extreme weather events, and environmental degradation transcend national borders, requiring unprecedented levels of international cooperation.

## Securitization

Climate change has increasingly been framed as a security issue. The UN Security Council has debated climate-related security risks, including resource scarcity, displacement, and conflict. This securitization process — elevating climate from an environmental issue to a matter of international peace and security — reflects constructivist dynamics in global governance.

## Key Institutional Responses

The primary multilateral framework is the **United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)**, most notably operationalised through the **Paris Agreement (2015)**, which commits signatories to limiting global warming to well below 2°C. Challenges include enforcement gaps, free-riding, and tension between developed and developing states over historical responsibility.

## Related Concepts

Climate change intersects with [[Global Governance]] (institutional architecture), [[International Development]] (sustainable development, climate finance), and [[Postcolonialism]] (climate justice — the Global South bears disproportionate costs of emissions it did not cause).
