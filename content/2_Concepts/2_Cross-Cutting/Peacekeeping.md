# Peacekeeping

Peacekeeping is a tool of [[Collective Security]] in which impartial military and civilian personnel are deployed to help maintain or restore international peace. It sits between peaceful settlement under [[United Nations]] Chapter VI and enforcement action under Chapter VII.

## Core Principles

The UN's three bedrock principles: **consent** of the host state and parties, **impartiality** (not neutrality), and **non-use of force except in self-defence and defence of the mandate**. These principles, formalised under Secretary-General Dag Hammarskjöld, distinguish peacekeeping from war-fighting.

## Brahimi Report (2000)

The report was a watershed reform following catastrophic failures in the [[Rwanda Genocide]] (1994) and Srebrenica (1995). It recommended robust mandates, clear rules of engagement, larger and better-equipped forces, and a shift from observing to protecting civilians. It marked the transition from "traditional" to "multidimensional" peacekeeping.

## Evolution

**First generation** (1948–1988): Lightly armed observer missions monitoring ceasefires (e.g. UNTSO, UNMOGIP).

**Second generation** (1988–1999): Expanded mandates including election monitoring, human rights, and disarmament. The post–[[Cold War]] moment freed the Security Council from veto paralysis.

**Third generation** (1999–present): Robust peacekeeping under Chapter VII mandates — authorised to use force beyond self-defence. Missions in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (MONUSCO) and Mali (MINUSMA) exemplify this trend. The [[Kosovo War]] and subsequent KFOR deployment reflected this evolution.

## Critiques

Peacekeeping suffers from credibility gaps when mandates are not matched by resources, and from legitimacy problems when Western-led interventions are perceived as neo-imperial. [[Peace and Conflict Studies]] scholars debate whether peacekeeping primarily serves Northern security interests.
