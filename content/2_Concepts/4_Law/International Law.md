# International Law

## Definition
A body of rules and principles governing relations between states and other international actors. Unlike domestic law, there is no central enforcement authority — but states generally comply, making IL a real, if distinct, legal system.

## Sources of International Law (Art. 38, ICJ Statute)
1. **International conventions** (treaties) — Written agreements between states
2. **Customary international law** — State practice accepted as law (*opinio juris*)
3. **General principles of law** — Recognised by civilised nations
4. **Judicial decisions and scholarly writings** — Subsidiary sources

## Key Distinction: Treaty vs Custom
- Treaties: binding only on parties
- Custom: binding on all states (except persistent objectors)

## Types of International Law

| Domain | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| Jus ad bellum (right to war) | UN Charter Article 2(4) on use of force |
| Jus in bello (law of war/IHL) | Geneva Conventions, Hague Conventions |
| International Human Rights Law | UDHR, ICCPR, ICESCR |
| International Criminal Law | Rome Statute, ICC |
| Law of the Sea | UNCLOS |
| International Environmental Law | Climate agreements, Montreal Protocol |
| International Trade Law | WTO agreements |
| Diplomatic Law | Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations |

## Key Debates
- **Is international law "really law"?** — Realists argue it is merely politics because no enforcement
  - Positivist: Law is a command of a sovereign → no global sovereign → no law (Austin's 19th century critique)
  - Compliance: Why do states obey law without coercion?
- **Enforcement problem** — No international police; ICJ requires consent for jurisdiction; UN Security Council is political
- **Fragmentation** — Proliferation of courts, tribunals, regimes with no hierarchy
- **Compliance puzzle** — States generally comply with IL most of the time (Henkin: "almost all nations observe almost all principles of international law almost all the time")
- **Selective application** — Great powers often exempt while smaller states are held accountable

## Key Theorists
- **Hugo Grotius** — Father of international law; *De Jure Belli ac Pacis* (1625)
- **Hugo Grophus** — Dutch, claimed natural law basis for IL
- **H.L.A. Hart** — *The Concept of Law* — IR is "primary rules" without "secondary rules" of recognition
- **Anne-Marie Slaughter** — Liberal approach: domestic courts enforce IL
- **Beth Simmons** — *Mobilizing for Human Rights* — empirical evidence on why states comply with human rights treaties
