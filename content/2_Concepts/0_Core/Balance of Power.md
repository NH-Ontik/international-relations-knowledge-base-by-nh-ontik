# Balance of Power

## Definition
The distribution of power among major states such that no single state can dominate the international system. Balancing occurs when states align against the most powerful or threatening state to prevent hegemony.

## Key Ideas
- States "balance" not "bandwagon" (join the strong) — Waltz's central prediction
- Balancing can be **internal** (building military strength) or **external** (forming alliances)
- **Tension**: the concept is both descriptive (balance *does* occur) and normative (balance *should* occur)

## Key Theorists
- **Kenneth Waltz** — Structural realism predicts balancing against dominant powers
- **Stephen Walt** — *Balance of Threat* theory: states balance against threats, not just power
- **John Mearsheimer** — States balance against rising hegemons
- **Hans Morgenthau** — Balance as universal law of politics

## Key Debates
- **Does balancing still occur?** — Post-Cold War predictions: US unipolarity, some said balancing against US was inevitable. It didn't happen on realist scale.
- **Balance vs Bandwagoning** — Waltz predicts balancing; some states bandwagon (join the winner)
- **Soft Balancing** — Non-military balancing: diplomatic coalitions, institutional constraints, economic statecraft
- **Balance of Threat** (Walt) — States balance against the most *threatening* state, not the most *powerful*

## Types of Balancing
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Internal | Build up own military + economic capabilities |
| External | Form alliances |
| Hard | Military |
| Soft | Diplomatic, economic, institutional checks |

## Case Studies
- **Alliances against Napoleon** — Repeated coalitions to prevent French domination
- **Cold War NATO vs Warsaw Pact** — Classic bipolar balance
- **US-China** — Currently debated: is China "balancing" against the US, or rising within the system?
- **OPEC** — Balancing through economic leverage
