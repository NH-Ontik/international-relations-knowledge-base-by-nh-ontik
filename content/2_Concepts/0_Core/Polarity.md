# Polarity

## Definition
The number of major power clusters (poles) in the international system. Polarity describes how power is concentrated: **unipolarity** (one dominant power), **bipolarity** (two roughly equal powers), and **multipolarity** (three or more major powers). Polarity is a core variable for structural realists, who argue the distribution of power shapes system stability and great power behaviour.

## Key Scholars
- **Kenneth Waltz** — Bipolarity is most stable (two powers, no miscalculation, clear responsibility)
- **John Mearsheimer** — Multipolarity is unstable (many powers, shifting alliances, probable war)
- **William Wohlforth** — Unipolarity is durable (US unipolar moment, no balancing coalition)
- **G. John Ikenberry** — Liberal order makes unipolarity stable (institutions, legitimacy)

## The Stability Debate
| Polarity | Pro-Stability | Anti-Stability |
|----------|--------------|----------------|
| Bipolar (Waltz) | Clear hierarchy, no misperception, no chain-ganging | Simple but brittle; ideological rigidity increases stakes |
| Multipolar (Deutsch, Singer) | Flexibility, cross-cutting pressures | Miscalculation, over-reaction, chain-ganging (WWI) |
| Unipolar (Wohlforth) | No great-power rivalry, US provides public goods | Overextension, balancing backlash, legitimacy deficit |

- **Waltz (1979)**: Bipolarity is stable because there are no peripherals to drag powers into war (contrast: WWI chain-ganging). Two great powers manage the system directly.
- **Mearsheimer (2001)**: Multipolarity is the most war-prone. States cannot reliably gauge threats; uncertainty leads to security competition.
- **Deutsch & Singer (1964)**: Multipolarity reduces conflict by creating cross-cutting loyalties and reducing attention concentration.

## The Unipolar Moment
- **1990–2008**: US as sole superpower after Soviet collapse. Debated: is unipolarity a "moment" or an "era"?
- **Critiques**: Russia, China, and regional powers push back. Unipolarity erodes as others rise.
- **Current**: Debate on US-China bipolarity or "de facto bipolarity" — some argue return to bipolar structure with US and China as two poles.

## Key Debates
- **Does polarity determine stability?** — Structural realists say yes; liberals point to institutional factors; constructivists argue identity/interaction matter more
- **Measuring polarity** — Military? Economic? A combination? China is near-peer economically but not militarily
- **Transition danger** — Hegemonic war theory: the rising power challenges the dominant power (Thucydides's Trap)
- **Is the current system bipolar, multipolar, or multipolar with two leading poles?**

## Case Studies
- **[[Cold War]]** — Classic bipolarity. US-USSR competition with rigid alliance blocs, but no direct great-power war (Waltz's prediction holds)
- **[[Ukraine War]]** — NATO and US backing Ukraine; Russia acting against perceived unipolar encroachment. Unipolar decline accelerates
- **[[World War I]]** — Classic multipolar failure. Chain-ganging, miscalculation, rigid alliance system turned local crisis into continental war
- **Post-Cold War (1991–2008)** — The unipolar moment. US unchallenged in military, economic, and institutional terms
