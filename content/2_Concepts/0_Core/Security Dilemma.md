# Security Dilemma

## Definition
A situation where one state's efforts to increase its own security (e.g., building up its military) are perceived as threatening by other states, who then respond with their own measures, leading to a spiral of mutual suspicion and arms racing — ultimately leaving all parties less secure than before.

## The Paradox
Measures taken for defensive reasons are perceived as offensive threats. Each state *feels* defensive; other states *see* aggression.

## Key Theorists
- **John Herz** (1950) — First articulated the concept
- **Robert Jervis** (1978) — "Cooperation Under the Security Dilemma" — distinguishes offence-dominant vs defence-dominant worlds
- **Charles Glaser** — Rational theory of the security dilemma
- **Herbert Butterfield** — "Hobbesian fear" as structural rather than psychological

## Conditions That Intensify It
- Offence-dominant military technology (easier to attack than defend) — WWI
- Offensive doctrines and postures
- Geography (proximity intensifies)
- Inability to distinguish offensive vs defensive capabilities
- History of mistrust

## Conditions That Mitigate It
- Defence-dominant technology
- Arms control agreements 
- Transparency and confidence-building measures
- Democratic governance ([[Democratic Peace Theory]])

## Case Studies
- **WWI** — Classic case: mobilisation plans driven by fear cascaded into war (But in 1914 case)
- **Cold War** — US-USSR arms race following nuclear weapons development
- **South Asia** — India-Pakistan conventional and nuclear competition
- **NATO-Russia** — NATO expansion seen by Russia; threatening; NATO says defensive
