# Agent-Structure Problem

The agent-structure problem is a foundational metatheoretical debate in International Relations about whether international outcomes are best explained by the actions of agents (states, leaders) or the constraints of structures (anarchy, distribution of power, norms). It is rooted in the broader social-theoretic problem of structure and agency.

## The Core Puzzle

Do agents shape structures, or do structures constrain agents? If anarchy drives states to compete ([[Realism]], [[Neorealism]]), then structure is decisive and agents have little room to vary their behaviour. If states can change the character of the system through their interactions, then agency matters. The answer determines how we theorise change, identity, and possibility in world politics.

## Key Positions

**Kenneth Waltz ([[Neorealism]])** argues that the structure of the international system — its organising principle ([[Anarchy]]), the differentiation of units (functionally undifferentiated states), and the distribution of capabilities — constrains state behaviour so powerfully that outcomes can be explained without reference to state-level attributes or leaders. Systemic logic, not state choice, produces balancing and war.

**Alexander Wendt ([[Constructivism]])** rejects Waltz's materialism. He argues that structures are not material givens but are socially constructed by what states do. Anarchy is what states make of it — "self-help" is not a logical consequence of anarchy but a product of shared ideas. Agents and structures are *mutually constitutive*: agents reproduce or transform structures through practice, and structures enable or constrain agents.

## Implications for IR

If structures are dominant, [[Foreign Policy Analysis]] matters less than systemic theory. If agents co-constitute structures, then diplomacy, norms, identity, and leadership become central. The debate shapes how we study everything from the [[Cold War]] to humanitarian intervention.

## Critiques

Critics note that the debate is excessively abstract and that empirical research rarely engages it explicitly. Some argue for a pragmatic synthesis: structures set parameters, agents exercise choice within them, and the boundary shifts over time.
