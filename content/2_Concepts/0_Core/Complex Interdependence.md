# Complex Interdependence

## Definition
A theoretical model of world politics introduced by Robert Keohane and Joseph Nye in *Power and Interdependence* (1977) that challenges the core assumptions of [[Realism]]. Under conditions of complex interdependence, international relations are shaped not only by state power and security but by multiple channels of connection across societies, a lack of hierarchy among issues, and a declining utility of military force.

## Three Core Characteristics

### 1. Multiple Channels of Connection
- **Interstate** (formal diplomatic relations between states)
- **Transgovernmental** (networks of officials, judges, regulators across borders)
- **Transnational** (multinational corporations, NGOs, epistemic communities, diaspora networks)
- Non-state actors matter — a direct challenge to realist statism

### 2. No Hierarchy of Issues
- Military security does not consistently dominate foreign policy
- Economic welfare, environmental protection, human rights, health can be equally or more important
- "Low politics" (trade, finance, environment) can equal or surpass "high politics" (security, war)

### 3. Declining Role of Military Force
- Force is less useful against other advanced industrialised states
- Military power cannot resolve economic or environmental disputes
- Coercion is costly and counterproductive in interdependent relationships
- *Keohane & Nye acknowledged this was ideal-typical — the real world always mixes interdependence and realist politics*

## Challenge to Realism
Complex interdependence was the foundational empirical claim of [[Liberalism|neoliberal institutionalism]]:
- Against [[Anarchy]] → cooperation IS possible through institutions
- Against statism → non-state actors shape outcomes
- Against security as dominant → agenda-setting, welfare, environment matter independently
- Against force as ultimate arbiter → military power is irrelevant in many domains

## Key Concepts from *Power and Interdependence*

### Sensitivity vs Vulnerability Interdependence
- **Sensitivity** — How quickly do changes in one country affect another? (e.g., oil price shock)
- **Vulnerability** — How costly is it to adjust? The actor with fewer alternatives is more vulnerable even if less sensitive
- Vulnerability asymmetry is a source of power — Keohane & Nye's key insight

### International Regimes
- Complex interdependence makes regimes (principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures) necessary and likely
- Regimes reduce transaction costs, provide information, stabilise expectations
- See [[International Organizations]]

## Critiques

### Realist Responses
- [[Realism]] — Military force is *still* ultimate: the US dollar depends on US Navy, trade depends on secure shipping lanes, energy depends on Middle East stability
- **Mearsheimer** — Great powers cheat when interdependence threatens their security; economic interdependence can increase conflict ([[World War I]]: highly interdependent economies still went to war)
- Ukraine war proved realism right — security concerns overwhelmed trade interdependence

### Empirical Challenges
- Is complex interdependence describing a general condition or only relations among advanced democracies?
- Globalisation backlash (Brexit, Trump tariffs, trade wars 2018--) suggests states reassert control
- Weaponised interdependence (Farrell & Newman): states use centrality in networks to coerce (SWIFT sanctions, Huawei ban)

### Constructivist / Critical
- [[Constructivism]] — The model ignores how interdependence is *perceived* and *constituted* by identities and norms
- [[Feminism]] — Ignores how global care chains and gendered labour underpin economic interdependence
- [[Postcolonialism]] — Interdependence is structurally unequal: the global South is "dependent" more than "interdependent" — echoes of dependency theory

## Contemporary Relevance
- **Global supply chains** — COVID-19 and Ukraine war revealed vulnerabilities in complex interdependence
- **Climate change** — The defining interdependent problem: no hierarchy, no military solution
- **Economic coercion** — US-China decoupling, sanctions, and export controls are state strategies to *reduce* interdependence for security reasons
- **Cyber domain** — Multiple channels, non-state actors, no hierarchy of issues — the ideal-type complex interdependence world

## Key Questions
- Does interdependence produce peace or vulnerability? (Trade with China — peace or leverage?)
- Can states reverse interdependence (decoupling, deglobalisation)?
- Is complex interdependence a descriptive model or a normative aspiration?
