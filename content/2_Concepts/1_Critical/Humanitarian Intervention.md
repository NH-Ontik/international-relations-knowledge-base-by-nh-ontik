# Humanitarian Intervention

## Definition
The use of military force by a state or coalition of states against another state for the primary purpose of protecting civilians from widespread suffering or human rights abuses. Distinct from self-defence or strategic intervention — the stated motive must be humanitarian.

## Legal Framework
- **UN Charter** — Article 2(4) prohibits the use of force against the territorial integrity of states; Article 2(7) prohibits intervention in domestic affairs
- **Chapter VII** — UN Security Council may authorise force for international peace and security; has been stretched to cover humanitarian crises (Somalia 1992, Libya 2011)
- **Customary international law** — No clear right of humanitarian intervention exists without UNSC authorisation
- **R2P precursor** — The concept of "sovereignty as responsibility" emerged from the failures in [[Rwanda Genocide|Rwanda]] and [[Kosovo War|Kosovo]] to legitimate humanitarian action

## Key Debates

### For Humanitarian Intervention (Liberal case)
- [[Liberalism]] — Human rights are universal; sovereignty is conditional on protecting populations
- The Responsibility to Protect ([[Responsibility to Protect|R2P]]) reframed the debate from a "right to intervene" to a "responsibility to protect"
- Inaction in [[Rwanda Genocide|Rwanda]] (800,000 killed while UN watched) is the strongest moral argument

### Against Humanitarian Intervention
- **Sovereignty** — Violates the foundational norm of non-intervention ([[Sovereignty]])
- Selectivity — Great powers intervene where they have interests, ignore where they don't ([[Rwanda Genocide|Rwanda]] ignored, [[Kosovo War|Kosovo]] bombed — both 1990s, different outcomes)
- **Neo-imperialism** — [[Postcolonialism]] and [[Marxism]] argue humanitarian intervention is the new "civilising mission": Western powers imposing their values militarily on the global South
- **R2P as Trojan horse** — Humanitarian language masks regime change ([[Libya 2011]]: R2P authorised no-fly zone → NATO bombing → regime change → chaos)

## Case Studies

### Kosovo War (1999)
- NATO intervened without UNSC authorisation (Russia, China opposed)
- Justified as humanitarian necessity to stop ethnic cleansing
- Legal scholars divided: illegal but legitimate (Independent International Commission on Kosovo)
- Set precedent for intervention without Security Council blessing

### Rwanda Genocide (1994)
- The failure that drove the humanitarian intervention agenda
- UNAMIR peacekeepers present but not authorised to use force
- UN Security Council voted to *reduce* the mission during the genocide
- Debate: Was inaction a failure of will, law, or great power politics?

### Libya (2011)
- UNSC Resolution 1973 authorised "all necessary measures" to protect civilians under R2P
- NATO interpreted the mandate as authorising regime change (bombing Gaddafi's forces)
- [[Responsibility to Protect|R2P]] suffered reputational damage — China and Russia accuse the West of abusing R2P for regime change
- Follow-up: Syria — no consensus (vetoes by Russia, China)

## Critiques
- **Selectivity** — Intervention follows geopolitical interests, not human need ([[Realism]] vindicated)
- **Inconsistent application** — Darfur (2003--), Myanmar (Rohingya 2017), Yemen (2015--) — no robust intervention
- **No clear legal basis** — Without UNSC authorisation, intervention violates international law
- **Worse outcomes** — Libya after 2011 is more violent, Syria after 2011 is more violent; intervention can entrench conflict
- **Postcolonial critique** — The "humanitarian" claim echoes colonial discourse of bringing civilisation to backward peoples ([[Postcolonialism]])

## Key Questions
- Can military force ever genuinely be "humanitarian"?
- Is legal authorisation a necessary condition for legitimate intervention?
- Who intervenes, and who is intervened upon — does the pattern reveal a colonial logic?
